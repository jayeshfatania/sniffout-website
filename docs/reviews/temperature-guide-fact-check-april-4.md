# Fact Check: "Is it too hot to walk my dog? A temperature-by-temperature guide"

---

## 1. Overall Assessment

**PASS WITH TWO CORRECTIONS**

18 of 20 checks verified. Two corrections required before publication:

- **Check 3:** The WBGT-to-air-temperature conversion is stated as "20 to 22 degrees Celsius" but the research documents it as "roughly 20 to 25 degrees Celsius." The article narrows the range by three degrees, understating the upper end of the conversion. Correct to "20 to 25 degrees Celsius."

- **Check 5d:** The Pedigree UK quote is hardened from their published conditional ("22C may be too hot") to an unqualified statement ("too hot"). The qualifier must be restored. Additionally, the word "explicitly" in the lead-in is inaccurate given that Pedigree UK expressed the claim conditionally.

No factual errors on the VetCompass odds ratios, clinical data, survival rates, or pavement temperature figures. No brachycephalic data cross-contamination from other guides. No em dashes. No prohibited language.

**Check 3 - "median ambient temperature... equivalent to around 20 to 22 degrees Celsius" (VetCompass WBGT conversion)**

**NOT VERIFIED AS WRITTEN - CORRECTION REQUIRED**

The underlying VetCompass WBGT figure (16.9C) is correct, as is the acknowledgement that this is a WBGT measurement and not a standard dry-bulb air temperature reading. However, the conversion range stated in the article is inaccurate.

The article states: "equivalent to around 20 to 22 degrees Celsius."

The research states clearly in both the scoring-validation-april-3.md and temperature-guide-research-april-4.md: "roughly equivalent to a dry-bulb air temperature of around 20 to 25 degrees Celsius depending on humidity" and "A UK summer day with a WBGT of 16.9C likely corresponds to an air temperature in the 20-25C range depending on conditions."

The article narrows the range from 20-25C to 20-22C, cutting off three degrees at the upper end. This is not a minor discrepancy: the stated 20-22C range could lead readers to believe the 22-25C band is not implicated in median heatstroke conditions when the research says it is. The article's intent is to show that heatstroke happens at "what most people would call a pleasant afternoon" - this purpose is served equally well by the accurate "20 to 25 degrees Celsius" range, which is if anything more persuasive.

**Correction required:** On line 17, change "equivalent to around 20 to 22 degrees Celsius" to "equivalent to around 20 to 25 degrees Celsius."

The same narrowed range appears in the Myths section (line 150): "The VetCompass median heatstroke temperature is equivalent to roughly 20-22C dry-bulb." This must also be corrected to "20-25C dry-bulb" or the full sentence removed, as it repeats the inaccuracy.

**Check 5d - 22-24C: Pedigree UK "22C too hot for flat-faced"; Vets Now "20-23C 6/10"**

**PARTIALLY VERIFIED - CORRECTION REQUIRED**

**Vets Now:** The 6/10 rating and "moderate risk; rigorous exercise could cause heatstroke" description for the 20-23C band are confirmed in the temperature-guide-research-april-4.md. VERIFIED.

**Pedigree UK:** The article states: "Pedigree UK explicitly names 22C as 'too hot for flat-faced, young, elderly, obese, or thick-coated dogs.'"

The research records the Pedigree UK source as: "22C may be too hot for flat-faced, elderly, young, obese, thick-coated dogs" (scoring-validation-april-3.md) and "Pedigree UK: '22C may be too hot' for vulnerable dogs" (temperature-guide-research-april-4.md).

Two problems:

1. The word "may" is absent from the article's version. Pedigree UK expressed this conditionally. The article removes the conditional and presents it as an unqualified statement ("too hot"), which misrepresents the source.

2. The lead-in word "explicitly" is inaccurate. Pedigree UK's conditional phrasing is the opposite of an explicit statement. The word "explicitly" should be removed.

**Correction required:** Change "Pedigree UK explicitly names 22C as 'too hot for flat-faced, young, elderly, obese, or thick-coated dogs'" to "Pedigree UK names 22C as potentially too hot for flat-faced, young, elderly, obese, or thick-coated dogs."

The factual position the article is making (22C is a meaningful threshold for vulnerable dogs) is well-supported by the research and entirely sound. Only the representation of what Pedigree UK said specifically needs adjusting.

## 3. Corrections Required

**Two corrections required before publication.**

---

**Correction 1 - Check 3: WBGT conversion range understated (two instances)**

**Severity: Material factual error - the stated range contradicts the research.**

The research documents the WBGT 16.9C to dry-bulb air temperature conversion as "roughly 20 to 25 degrees Celsius depending on humidity." The article states "20 to 22 degrees Celsius" in two places.

**Instance 1 - Line 17:**

Current text: "the median ambient temperature when dogs developed heat-related illness was equivalent to around 20 to 22 degrees Celsius - what most people would call a pleasant afternoon in summer."

Corrected text: "the median ambient temperature when dogs developed heat-related illness was equivalent to around 20 to 25 degrees Celsius - what most people would call a pleasant afternoon in summer."

**Instance 2 - Line 150 (Myths section):**

Current text: "The VetCompass median heatstroke temperature is equivalent to roughly 20-22C dry-bulb."

Corrected text: "The VetCompass median heatstroke temperature is equivalent to roughly 20-25C dry-bulb."

Both instances require the same change: replace "22" with "25."

---

**Correction 2 - Check 5d: Pedigree UK quote removes conditional qualifier**

**Severity: Factual misrepresentation - a conditional source is presented as an unqualified statement.**

The research records Pedigree UK's published wording as "22C may be too hot for flat-faced, elderly, young, obese, thick-coated dogs." The article removes "may" and adds the word "explicitly," creating a harder claim than the source makes.

**Line 43:**

Current text: "Pedigree UK explicitly names 22C as 'too hot for flat-faced, young, elderly, obese, or thick-coated dogs.'"

Corrected text: "Pedigree UK names 22C as potentially too hot for flat-faced, young, elderly, obese, or thick-coated dogs."

Note: removing the quotation marks from the factual claim (rather than treating it as a direct quote) avoids any further discrepancy between the article and the source text. The article's broader argument (22C is a meaningful threshold for vulnerable dogs) is well-supported by multiple sources and does not depend on Pedigree UK's conditional phrasing being hardened.

## 4. Suggestions

**Suggestion 1 - Agria inline quote format (Check 5e):**

The article presents "any temperature over 25C is a very definite NO for walking" as a direct quote, with the words "for walking" added. The research source records the Agria quote as "Any temperature over 25C is a very definite NO" without "for walking." As a direct quote (signalled by quotation marks), the addition of unreported words is technically inaccurate. The Editor may wish to either remove the quotation marks and paraphrase ("Agria calls 25C a definite no for walking") or verify the exact Agria source text. This is not a factual error in substance but a quotation accuracy issue.

**Suggestion 2 - Met Office attribution (Check 17):**

The research source for the July temperature averages is primarily currentresults.com (citing Met Office records) rather than the Met Office's own published pages. Before publication, Tom or the Editor should confirm the three figures (London 24C, Birmingham 22C, Manchester 20.5C) are directly verifiable on a Met Office publication (e.g. the Met Office's regional climate pages at metoffice.gov.uk/research/climate/maps-and-data/uk-climate-averages) and amend the source footnote accordingly. This is a minor sourcing precision note, not a factual concern.

**Suggestion 3 - "overweight dogs" OR figure:**

The article states (line 85): "Overweight dogs have a substantially elevated risk of severe outcomes." The research records the VetCompass figure from PMC9144152 as approximately 1.5 times greater odds of HRI for overweight dogs. The article correctly avoids attaching a specific OR to this claim (which would be harder to verify), but the absence of a number in an otherwise data-rich section may prompt reader questions. The Editor may wish to add "(approximately 1.5 times greater, PMC9144152)" for consistency with the rest of the odds ratio section, or leave as is given the uncertainty around the exact figure.

**Suggestion 4 - "skin burns in approximately one minute" phrasing:**

Line 53 states "Skin burns in approximately one minute at 52C." The research source (Vets Now, citing burn pathophysiology) says "skin destruction can occur in just one minute at 52C" and "burns possible in approximately 60 seconds at 49C." The article's "burns in approximately one minute" is a slight simplification of "skin destruction can occur in under one minute" but is within acceptable range for a consumer article. The Editor may wish to add "can occur" for precision: "Skin burns can occur in approximately one minute at 52C."
