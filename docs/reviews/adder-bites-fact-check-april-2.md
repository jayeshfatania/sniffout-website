## 1. Overall Assessment

**PASS WITH CORRECTIONS**

One factual error requires correction before publication. Five non-blocking suggestions would strengthen the guide. All statistics are correctly reported. Medical advice is accurate and consistent with authoritative sources. The one factual error is a geographic claim about Hindhead Common that overstates what the research supports.

---

## 2. Statistics

### "Around 100 dog bites from adders each year"
**VERIFIED.**
Research key fact 2: "Around 100 dogs are bitten by adders in the UK each year, according to data from the Veterinary Poisons Information Service." Research section 2: "Approximately 100 dogs are bitten by adders in the UK each year, based on VPIS data." Source: VPIS retrospective study (Reid et al. 2011, PubMed 21868437).

### "69.2% of recorded bites fell within April through July"
**VERIFIED.**
Research key fact 3 and section 2 both confirm: "69.2% of bites occurred between April and July." Source: Reid et al. 2011 VPIS retrospective study.

### "Bites cluster most commonly between 3pm and 4pm"
**VERIFIED.**
Research key fact 3: "Bites were especially common between 15:00 and 16:00 hours." Research section 1 (time of day): "VPIS study data identified 15:00-16:00 as the most frequent time for dog bites." Source: VPIS study.

### "Antivenom used in around 56% of cases"
**VERIFIED.**
Research section 2 gives the precise figure: "Antivenom was used in 55.9% of VPIS-reported cases." The guide rounds this to "around 56%" which is accurate. Source: VPIS study.

### "Roughly halves the duration of swelling - from an average of 94 hours down to around 47 hours"
**VERIFIED.**
Research key fact 7: "cuts swelling duration roughly in half (from 94 hours to 47 hours on average)." Research section 2: "reduces swelling duration - from an average of 94 hours to 47 hours." Source: VPIS study.

### "The great majority of dogs who receive veterinary care make a complete recovery"
**VERIFIED - framing is accurate.**
Research section 2 gives the underlying data: "Mortality rate is 4.6% (approximately 1 in 22 dogs). Most dogs - around 96% - make a full recovery within 5 days with treatment." A 95.4% recovery rate is correctly characterised as "the great majority." The phrase "who receive veterinary care" appropriately signals that this outcome is conditional on prompt treatment, which is accurate. No correction required.

## 3. Medical Advice

### "Carry the dog, do not let it walk"
**VERIFIED.**
Research key fact 5: "Carry the dog - do not let it walk after a bite. Movement speeds venom circulation." Research section 3: "All major sources agree: carry the dog where possible. PDSA, Vets Now, and Medivet all state that carrying the dog reduces venom spread by minimising circulation." Sources: PDSA, Vets Now, Medivet.

### "Do not apply a tourniquet, ice, or attempt to suck out venom"
**VERIFIED.**
Research section 3 What NOT to do: "Do not apply a tourniquet or tight bandage" (confirmed with Scottish SPCA explanation of why), "Do not cut, or suck the wound," "Do not apply ice or cold packs." Sources: PDSA, Scottish SPCA, NFDOG, Vets Now.

### "Do not give human antihistamines"
**VERIFIED - guidance is correct. Reason given in guide is not precisely aligned with the research.**
The guidance not to give oral antihistamines is confirmed by NFDOG and Scottish SPCA (cited in research). The guide gives the reason as "they can mask symptoms and delay the right treatment." The research's stated reason is different: antihistamines "absorb too slowly to be of significant benefit and should not delay getting to the vet." The underlying instruction is correct and the outcome (delay vet treatment) is the same, but "mask symptoms" is not the framing used in any source cited in the research. This is a minor discrepancy rather than an error - the advice stands. Flagged as a suggestion below.

### "Keep the bitten area elevated above the heart" - this step is in the research but not in the guide
**OMISSION - flagged as suggestion.**
Research key fact 5: "Keep the bitten area elevated above the heart if possible." Research section 3: "Keep the bitten limb elevated above the heart level if a leg is bitten." This is cited from PDSA, Vets Now, and Medivet. The guide's first aid steps do not include this. It is not a safety-critical omission for most bites (the primary priority is carry and call vet), but it is a recommended action from multiple authoritative sources. Flagged as suggestion 1.

### Are there any important first aid steps missing?
The guide omits two steps present in the research:
1. Keeping the bite site elevated (above) - suggestion 1.
2. Photographing the snake from a safe distance if possible, to help the vet confirm species (research section 3, step 6). Not critical - the treatment is the same regardless - but helpful for confirmation. Flagged as suggestion 2.

The guide includes all major first aid steps: keep calm and still, carry, call vet, note bite time, no tourniquet/ice/suction, no antihistamines, clarify snake identity, antivenom not universally stocked. No safety-critical omissions.

## 4. Geographic Claims

### "Surrey has the second largest adder habitat by area in the UK"
**VERIFIED - minor omission noted.**
Research key fact 9: "Surrey has the second largest adder habitat area in the UK (after Devon)." Research section 1: "Surrey has the second-largest adder habitat area in the UK (after Devon)." The guide's phrasing is accurate but omits the clarifying parenthetical "(after Devon)." Not an error, but adding this would be more complete. Flagged as suggestion 3. Source attribution in the guide reads "(Surrey Wildlife Trust, ARC Trust)" - the research attributes this specifically to Surrey Wildlife Trust; ARC Trust is not cited as the primary source for this particular claim. Minor attribution issue only.

### "Hindhead and Frensham are confirmed sites for all six of the UK's native reptile species, including adders"
**FACTUAL ERROR - CORRECTION REQUIRED.**

The research is explicit that all six native reptile species are confirmed at Frensham only. Research key fact 9: "Frensham Common is one of only two Surrey sites where all six native reptile species including adders are found." Research section 5 (Frensham entry): "Frensham Common is one of only two Surrey sites where all six native reptile species are present." The second of these two sites is not identified in the research as Hindhead.

The research's Hindhead entry confirms adders are present at Hindhead as part of the Wealden Heaths NNR and describes it as primary adder habitat. It does not state that all six native reptile species are confirmed there.

Stating that Hindhead is confirmed for all six species is not supported by the research and may be factually incorrect. The "all six" claim should be restricted to Frensham.

## 6. Corrections Required

### Correction 1 (REQUIRED) - "all six species" claim extended incorrectly to Hindhead

**Current text (line 78):**
"Hindhead and Frensham are confirmed sites for all six of the UK's native reptile species, including adders."

**What it should say:**
"Frensham is one of only two Surrey sites confirmed for all six of the UK's native reptile species, including adders. Hindhead is a confirmed adder stronghold and part of the Wealden Heaths National Nature Reserve."

**Why:** The research explicitly and repeatedly states that the "all six native species" confirmation applies to Frensham Common specifically. Hindhead is confirmed for adders but the research does not support the claim that all six native reptile species are confirmed there. Extending the "all six" claim to Hindhead is not supported and may be incorrect.

## 7. Suggestions (non-blocking)

These are not factual errors. They would improve the guide but do not require correction before publication.

**Suggestion 1 - Add limb elevation to first aid steps**
Research key fact 5 and section 3 both include "keep the bitten limb elevated above the heart level" as a recommended action, cited from PDSA, Vets Now, and Medivet. The guide's first aid step 1 could be extended: "Keep your dog as calm and still as possible, carry them if you can, and if a limb is bitten, keep it elevated above the level of the heart."

**Suggestion 2 - Reason for avoiding oral antihistamines**
The guide states antihistamines "can mask symptoms." The research's cited reason is that they "absorb too slowly to be of significant benefit and should not delay getting to the vet" (NFDOG). The guide's reason is not supported by any source in the research notes. Consider aligning: "They absorb too slowly to be effective and should not substitute for or delay getting to the vet."

**Suggestion 3 - Clarify Surrey ranking**
"Surrey has the second largest adder habitat by area in the UK" would be more complete as "Surrey has the second largest adder habitat by area in the UK, after Devon." The research includes the Devon qualifier in both places it cites this fact.

**Suggestion 4 - Seven Sisters and Grasmere absent from walk section**
Seven Sisters is rated MODERATE risk in the research and Grasmere is LOW-MODERATE. Neither is mentioned in the guide's Sniffout walks section, which otherwise covers nine walks. Adding brief coverage would make the walk section consistent with the research. Suggested addition to the "lower risk" paragraph: "Seven Sisters is worth a mention - the chalk grassland and any scrubby south-facing banks along the route carry some adder risk, particularly in the lower valley sections near Cuckmere Haven, though the exposed clifftop itself is less typical habitat. Grasmere's lakeside and meadow terrain is low-risk but Cumbria does have adder populations."

**Suggestion 5 - Photograph the snake step**
Research section 3 step 6 recommends photographing the snake from a safe distance if possible, as it helps the vet confirm species. This step is absent from the guide. It is low-priority but adds practical value and reinforces the legal protection point naturally.
