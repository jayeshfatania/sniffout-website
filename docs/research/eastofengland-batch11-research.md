## WALK 1: Dunwich Heath and Beach

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Dunwich Heath and Beach
- **Location:** Dunwich, Suffolk
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** IP17 3DQ (National Trust car park, Coastguard Cottages)
- **Latitude:** 52.2519
- **Longitude:** 1.6291
- **Approximate distance:** 4.8 km (3 miles — main circular from Coastguard Tea Rooms; Woof Walk is approx 3.2 km/2 miles) (HIGH)
- **Difficulty:** Easy
- **Terrain type:** Sand and shingle beach, heathland paths (sandy/gravel), some grassy tracks. Largely flat. Can be very exposed in wind. No significant elevation. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- Off-lead is permitted on two specific areas only: (1) the beach year-round, and (2) the designated Woof Walk (a 3.2 km waymarked route running through the northern section of the heath). On the main heathland tracks and footpaths, dogs must be on lead from 1 March to 31 August. Outside this period (1 September to 28/29 February), dogs must be under close and effective control on the heath. Dogs on the Woof Walk can be off lead year-round, but must return to lead when leaving the trail back onto heath paths during the 1 March to 31 August period. (HIGH)
- The beach carries no seasonal lead restrictions. Owners should prevent dogs chasing seals or approaching birds on the beach. Grey seals haul out along this coastline, particularly in autumn and winter. (HIGH)
- **CRoW Act note:** Dunwich Heath is CRoW Act open access land. The statutory requirement is a 2m maximum lead from 1 March to 31 July for open access land. The National Trust applies a stricter in-house rule on the main heath: dogs on lead 1 March to 31 August (one month beyond the statutory minimum). The Woof Walk is a designated route where off-lead is permitted year-round as an exception. (HIGH)
- **Livestock present:** No livestock on the heath itself. Farm fields adjoin the northern boundary. Seals on the beach seasonally (autumn/winter). (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Dogs must stick to footpaths on the heath during the March-August lead period. The National Trust issues a "Woof Guide" (available from the visitor welcome hut) with site-specific dog rules. Dogs welcome in the outdoor cafe seating area but not inside the tearoom. (HIGH)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** National Trust car park at Coastguard Cottages, IP17 3DQ. Free for National Trust members; approximately £6 per day for non-members (confirm current charge before publishing). Cashless payment. Car park open from 9am. (MEDIUM — charge may change)
- **Toilets:** Yes, at the car park near Coastguard Cottages. Baby changing available. Accessible toilet (RADAR key required). (HIGH)
- **Cafe or pub:** Coastguard Cottages Tearoom (National Trust) — tea, coffee, cakes, snacks, light bites. Outdoor seating with views towards Minsmere Marshes. Dogs welcome at outdoor tables. (HIGH)
- **Public transport:** Poor. Nearest railway station is Darsham (East Suffolk Line, Greater Anglia), approximately 5 miles from Dunwich. No regular bus service to the heath. A seasonal community bus (Sole Bay Community Bus) operates approximately May to September, providing limited links from surrounding villages. The East Suffolk Line new transport initiative aims to improve links to RSPB Minsmere and Dunwich village but no fixed regular service as of 2025-2026. Visitors are strongly recommended to arrive by car. (HIGH)

### 4. Hazards

- **ticks** — March to October. Heathland and scrub habitat. Check dogs and owners after every visit. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to September. Dunwich Heath is classic adder habitat — dry heathland with gorse and bracken edges. Most active in warm sunny spells. Keep dogs close and on paths. (HIGH)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 August. Stonechat, nightjar, and other heathland species nest on the ground. Lead rule enforced across the main heath during this period. (HIGH)
- **livestock** — Not applicable on main heath. Seasonal presence: grey seals on beach from approximately October to March. Dogs must not approach or chase seals. (HIGH)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Sandy paths and shingle beach surface can heat significantly in direct sun in summer. (MEDIUM)
- **cliff** — Not applicable at this location. Beach is flat shingle. No cliff sections on this route. (HIGH)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** Dunwich Heath is one of England's finest fragments of lowland heathland — a completely different sensory environment from parkland or forest. Dogs get heather, gorse, sandy paths, salt air, and the beach all within a single walk. The Woof Walk gives a genuine off-lead circuit on the heath itself (rare for a National Trust heathland property). The beach is shingle and sand, directly accessible from the car park, and dogs can swim.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Year-round off-lead beach. Dedicated Woof Walk for off-lead roaming on the heath outside lead season. Dog water bowls at the visitor welcome hut. Outdoor seating at the tearoom welcomes dogs. Wide open space with sea views and the sound of the surf.
- **Honest practical note:** The main heathland paths are on-lead 1 March to 31 August, which covers most of the year's best walking weather. If you visit in summer and want off-lead, stick to the Woof Walk and the beach. Outside the lead season, the heath opens up but "close control" is still expected.

### 6. FAQ Suggestions

1. Can I let my dog off the lead at Dunwich Heath?
2. Are dogs allowed on Dunwich beach all year round?
3. What is the Woof Walk at Dunwich Heath?
4. Is the lost medieval city of Dunwich visible — and what happened to it?

**Note on FAQ 4:** Dunwich was a major medieval port, one of England's most significant, that was progressively swallowed by the sea following storms from the 13th century onward. The last church tower fell into the sea in 1919. It is now considered the largest submerged medieval town in Europe and has been dubbed "England's Atlantis." Historic England mapped the submerged remains in 2013. This drives substantial search interest and is highly FAQ-worthy. The ruins of Greyfriars Priory still stand on land above the cliff edge and are visible from the heath. (HIGH)

## WALK 2: Thetford Forest High Lodge

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Thetford Forest High Lodge
- **Location:** Santon Downham, Brandon, Suffolk/Norfolk border
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** IP27 0AF (High Lodge Forest Centre car park)
- **Latitude:** 52.4643
- **Longitude:** 0.6667
- **Approximate distance:** Variable. The main waymarked walking trails from High Lodge are: Heritage Trail (loops of 1 km, 3 km, 4.2 km), Pine Trail (1.5 km), Nature Trail (1.5 km), Fir Trail (5 km, 10 km, or 12 km options). For the website, recommend presenting the Fir Trail (10 km, 6.2 miles) as the featured walk distance. (HIGH)
- **Difficulty:** Easy
- **Terrain type:** Predominantly flat conifer plantation on sandy Breckland soil. Waymarked forestry tracks and softer natural forest floor paths. The Pine Trail is mostly firm-surfaced. The Nature Trail uses natural forest floor (no firm surface). Virtually no elevation gain throughout. The most distinctive flat walking terrain in this batch. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- Thetford Forest is Forestry England land (not National Trust) and dogs are broadly welcome. Off-lead is possible in general forest areas away from the mountain bike trails and the busy visitor hub. High Lodge has a dedicated off-lead dog exercise area within the site — described as an expansive fenced area with varied terrain including open grassland, pine trees, and small mounds. Dogs should be kept on lead: (1) in the busy areas around the car park, cafe, play areas, and Go Ape activity zones; (2) on and around the waymarked mountain bike trails (Shepherd, Poacher, Beater, Lime Burner) for safety reasons; (3) during any Forestry England ground-nesting bird protection initiatives. The wider forest beyond the visitor hub can generally be explored with dogs off lead where no cyclists are present, but be aware the MTB trails weave through the forest. (MEDIUM — Forestry England does not publish a detailed lead/off-lead map for Thetford online; this is compiled from multiple visitor sources)
- **Livestock present:** No livestock. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Dogs are not permitted on Go Ape courses or equipment. If you participate in Go Ape, you must have a non-participating companion to supervise your dog. Dogs are allowed in the cafe at High Lodge. Dogs must be kept under control around mountain bikers on the trail network. (HIGH)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** High Lodge Forest Centre car park, IP27 0AF (off B1107 between Brandon and Thetford, near A11). Cashless, ticketless, number plate recognition system. Charges: approximately £3.50 to £15.50 depending on duration; coaches £55, minibuses £30. Forestry England parking (not free for members). 12 accessible parking spaces. (HIGH)
- **Toilets:** Yes, at High Lodge. Open from 9:30am daily. Accessible toilet available. Brief daily closure 1:30pm-2pm for cleaning. (HIGH)
- **Cafe or pub:** High Lodge Cafe (Forestry England). Monday to Friday 10am-4pm, weekends 9am-4:30pm. Dogs allowed inside the cafe. (HIGH)
- **Public transport:** Possible but limited. Brandon railway station (Greater Anglia, Cambridge to Norwich line) is the nearest station — approximately 1 mile / 19-minute walk from the High Lodge entrance. Bus services 80 and 86 stop near High Lodge. Overall, car is the most practical option, but High Lodge is one of the few forest sites in this batch with a realistic train-and-walk option from Brandon. (HIGH)

### 4. Hazards

- **mountain-bikers** — Year-round. This is the principal hazard at Thetford for dog walkers. Four waymarked MTB trails run through the forest: Shepherd Trail (green, 8 km), Poacher Trail (blue, 16 km), Beater Trail (red, 9.9 km short route), Lime Burner Trail (red, 15 km with optional black-graded sections). Cyclists travel at speed and the trails cross and share forestry tracks. Keep dogs under close control and on lead near trail crossings. (HIGH)
- **deer** — Year-round. Thetford Forest holds four deer species: fallow, roe, red, and muntjac. Fallow are the most numerous (historically estimated at 4,000+). Encounter risk is high, particularly at dawn and dusk. Dogs should be under reliable recall near forest edges and clearings. Deer rut: fallow October-November, roe June-July. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Mixed woodland and heathland edges. Check dogs thoroughly after every visit. (HIGH)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — April to August. Thetford Forest is a Special Protection Area (SPA) partly because it holds approximately 20% of the UK's breeding nightjar population and 12% of breeding woodlark. Both are ground-nesting species in felled and restocked areas. Disturbance risk is highest in open clearings and young restock areas away from the main trails. Keep dogs on lead or under close control in these areas. Nightjars are active at dusk (May-August). (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to September. Heathland edges and open sunny clearings within the forest. Breckland habitat supports adder populations. (MEDIUM — no primary Forestry England source confirms adder presence at High Lodge specifically, but Breckland heathland context makes this credible)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Sandy forest tracks in direct sun can heat significantly. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The flatness is the defining character — an easy, legs-stretching forest walk with no hills and endless pine corridor. The scale is enormous: 18,730 hectares of forest to explore, giving dogs a genuine sense of big open space and deep woodland scent. Four species of deer in the forest mean sniffing is exceptional even when deer aren't visible. The sandy Breckland soil underfoot is soft on paws.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Dedicated off-lead dog exercise area at High Lodge. Dogs allowed inside the cafe. Broad forestry tracks mean plenty of room for dogs to move without being underfoot on narrower paths. Brandon station makes this one of the few forest walks accessible without a car.
- **Honest practical note:** Mountain bikers are a real and constant presence on the trail network. The waymarked MTB trails criss-cross the forest and cyclists travel at speed. Check the trail map before you go and stay away from the red and blue MTB routes if your dog has unpredictable recall around cyclists. The dedicated walking trails (Heritage, Pine, Nature, Fir) avoid the worst MTB conflict zones, but complete separation is not possible.

## WALK 3: Sheringham Park

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Sheringham Park
- **Location:** Upper Sheringham, Norfolk
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** NR26 8TL (National Trust car park, Lodge Hill)
- **Latitude:** 52.9294
- **Longitude:** 1.1721
- **Approximate distance:** Main featured walk: Woodland and Coastal Walk — 11.2 km (7 miles). Shorter option: Repton's Walk — approximately 4 km (2.5 miles). Active 5k trail also waymarked on site. (HIGH)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate (Woodland and Coastal Walk has some steep slopes, cliff-edge sections, and sections that can be very wet underfoot). Repton's Walk is Easy to Moderate. (HIGH)
- **Terrain type:** Mixed — parkland, deciduous and mixed woodland, fields, and clifftop paths above the North Norfolk coast. Some hard stone paths near the visitor centre; natural soil and grass paths across the wider estate. Slopes are present but not severe except near the cliffs. Can be very wet underfoot after rain. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- Dogs must be kept on lead throughout Sheringham Park from 1 March to 31 July to protect ground-nesting birds (skylarks, sand martins, and other species on the coastal cliffs and in the parkland). This is the National Trust rule and applies across the whole estate, not just specific zones. Outside this period (1 August to 28/29 February), dogs are welcome under "close control" — defined by the National Trust as: able to recall at first call, always visible, kept on footpaths when vegetation is too dense to maintain visibility, and not allowed to approach other visitors uninvited. (HIGH)
- Dogs must be kept on lead in the parkland area near the visitor centre and cafe due to cattle grazing. This lead requirement applies year-round (not just during the March-July bird season). When cattle approach, National Trust guidance advises letting the dog off the lead and calling it to you when safe. (HIGH)
- **Livestock present:** Yes, cattle graze the open parkland sections, year-round. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Dogs are welcome on all trails including the clifftop coastal sections. Two pawprint-rated NT property (water bowls and dog bins confirmed on site). Dogs welcome in the Courtyard Cafe outdoor seating area. Keep dogs on a short lead near cliff edges and deep water/ponds on the estate. (HIGH)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** National Trust car park, NR26 8TL (Lodge Hill, 2 miles southwest of Sheringham on the A148/B1157 junction). Free for National Trust members; £7 per car for non-members (coin-only parking machines — bring cash). (HIGH — confirm current charges before publishing; coin-only noted as of research date)
- **Toilets:** Yes, opposite the Courtyard Cafe. (HIGH)
- **Cafe or pub:** Courtyard Cafe (National Trust) — hot and cold drinks, sandwiches, cakes, light snacks. Adjacent bookshop in the barn. Dogs welcome in the outdoor seating area. (HIGH)
- **Public transport:** Good by National Trust standards. The nearest train station is Sheringham (Greater Anglia, Bittern Line from Norwich), approximately 2.1 miles from the park. Bus 9 runs direct to Sheringham Park entrance from Sheringham town centre (Railway Approach); journey approximately 5-10 minutes, no Sunday service. A bus stop is within approximately 150m of the park entrance on Lodge Hill. The North Norfolk Railway (heritage steam railway, the Poppy Line) also runs from Sheringham and can be used as part of a circular route with Holt. Walking from Sheringham station to the park is approximately 45 minutes. (HIGH)

### 4. Hazards

- **cliff** — Year-round. The Woodland and Coastal Walk includes a clifftop section along the North Norfolk coast. The cliff edges are exposed and unfenced in places. Keep dogs under close control and on lead near cliff edges. (HIGH)
- **livestock** — Year-round. Cattle graze the open parkland sections. Dogs on lead required in cattle areas. If cattle approach and charge, drop the lead. (HIGH)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Skylarks and sand martins on the coastal cliffs; other species in the parkland. Statutory lead requirement across the whole estate during this period (NT rule). (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Mixed woodland and bracken-edged paths throughout the estate. Check dogs and owners after every visit. (HIGH)
- **deer** — Year-round. Roe deer present in the woodland sections of the estate. Encounter risk is moderate. (MEDIUM — inferred from North Norfolk woodland habitat; no primary NT source confirming deer at Sheringham specifically)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Hard stone paths near the visitor centre and exposed clifftop paths can heat in direct sun. (LOW)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The clifftop walk is the standout — dogs get coastal air, sea views, and the texture and smell contrast of moving from deep oak woodland to exposed cliff edge. The estate spans 1,000 acres which means genuine variety within a single walk: formal parkland, ancient woodland, open fields, and the coast. The rhododendron Wild Garden in May-June is visually spectacular and the scent will be extraordinary for dogs (and owners). The gazebo treetop viewpoint on the Woodland and Coastal Walk offers views to Blakeney Point on a clear day.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Year-round cliff-top walking (with care). One of the most varied estates in the East of England for dogs — the range of habitats in a single circular walk is exceptional. Water bowls on site. Bus access from Sheringham means car-free visits are genuinely feasible. Shorter Repton's Walk (4 km) is a good option for less mobile dogs or hot days.
- **Honest practical note:** Dogs must be on lead throughout the whole estate from 1 March to 31 July — that is a long lead-only period that covers the rhododendron peak season. If showing off the park in May-June colours, all photos will be of a dog on lead. Cattle in the parkland mean leads are required near the visitor centre year-round regardless. This is a partial site even in the "off-season."

## RESEARCHER NOTES

### Significant findings and flags for the Editor

**WALK 1 — Dunwich Heath and Beach**

FLAG — Seasonal lead rule discrepancy: Sources are inconsistent. The National Trust's own dog walking page (confirmed via search results) states "on lead 1 March to 31 July" (matching the CRoW Act statutory minimum). A secondary source (beachesofsuffolk.co.uk) states "1 March to 31 August." A third summary from TripAdvisor says "March to September." The Woof Walk search result also confirms dogs "must return to lead when leaving the trail back onto heath paths during the March-August period" — suggesting August is included. The National Trust website was blocked by security verification during research; I was unable to directly confirm the current rule from primary source. RECOMMENDATION: The Editor or Developer should access the NT dog page directly (nationaltrust.org.uk/visit/suffolk/dunwich-heath-and-beach/bringing-your-dog-to-dunwich-heath) to confirm the current end date. For safety, this research pack uses "1 March to 31 August" as the conservative end date. If NT confirms 31 July, update accordingly. Confidence: (MEDIUM) on this specific date.

FLAG — Beach seal hazard: Grey seals haul out regularly along this section of the Suffolk coast, particularly on shingle at Dunwich and Minsmere. Dogs approaching seals is a risk from approximately October to March. This is not one of the 10 approved hazard types in the WALKS_DB schema, but it should be flagged in the Ailsa description as a practical note. It is not a hazard pill issue — it is a dog-management note.

NOTABLE: The lost medieval city of Dunwich (England's largest submerged medieval site) drives strong search interest and should be addressed in FAQ 4. Greyfriars Priory ruins on the cliff edge above Dunwich village are visible and walkable. This adds significant content depth and is a clear SEO opportunity. A brief explanation of the medieval city should be included in the walk page copy.

NOTABLE: The "Woof Walk" is a branded National Trust term for this property — it should be used in the copy. It is one of the few NT heathland properties that has a year-round off-lead walking route on the heath itself (most NT heathland is lead-only throughout the season). This is a genuine USP.

**WALK 2 — Thetford Forest High Lodge**

FLAG — Off-lead policy: Forestry England does not publish a clear, authoritative off-lead dog policy document for High Lodge online. The dedicated dog exercise area is confirmed by multiple visitor sources (including Tripadvisor reviews and the Rydale blog). However, the exact boundary and signage of this area could not be verified from a primary Forestry England source. The MEDIUM confidence rating on off-lead status reflects this. The safe copy position for Ailsa is: off-lead in the dedicated exercise area, and general good judgment in the wider forest away from MTB trails and busy zones. Do not overstate off-lead freedom across the whole forest.

FLAG — Adder confidence: No primary Forestry England source confirmed adder presence at High Lodge. Adders are credible given the Breckland heathland context and are appropriate to flag as a hazard, but confidence is MEDIUM only. The heathland edges within and around the forest are classic adder habitat.

FLAG — Go Ape dog policy: Confirmed dogs are not permitted on Go Ape courses but are allowed in the park and cafe. Participants must have a non-participating companion to supervise dogs. This is important FAQ content and should be in the walk page.

NOTABLE: Mountain bikers are a more significant and constant hazard here than at any other walk in this batch or previous batches. Thetford is one of the UK's most popular mountain biking venues (four waymarked trails including two red-graded). The hazard is real and the Editor should ensure it is clearly communicated — not alarmist, but honest. Dog owners with dogs that chase or are startled by fast cyclists need this information before they arrive.

NOTABLE: The nightjar and woodlark SPA designation is genuinely significant — Thetford holds approximately 20% of the UK's breeding nightjars. The ground-nesting-birds hazard is particularly important here, especially in open clearings and restock areas. This is stronger ecologically than most of the hazard flags in previous batches.

NOTABLE: The flat terrain is distinctive and should be emphasised. This is the only genuinely flat walk in Batch 11 and probably among the flattest walks on the whole Sniffout site. It is excellent for elderly owners, mobility-restricted walkers, and older or arthritic dogs. The Editor may want Ailsa to lean into this.

NOTABLE: Brandon station accessibility (approximately 1 mile walk) makes High Lodge one of the most train-accessible forest walks on the site. Worth emphasising.

**WALK 3 — Sheringham Park**

FLAG — Coin-only parking machines: Research source notes parking machines at Sheringham Park are coin-only (no card payment). This is operationally important for visitors and unusual in 2026. The Developer should note this in the practical information section. Recommend verifying this is still current before publishing (it may have been updated).

FLAG — Rhododendron season and lead requirement conflict: The rhododendron peak (mid-May to early June) falls squarely within the mandatory on-lead period (1 March to 31 July). Visitors arriving in May or June to see the famous rhododendrons will find they cannot let dogs off lead anywhere on the estate. This is an honest limitation that Ailsa should acknowledge. It does not reduce the appeal of the walk — the spectacle is enormous — but it sets correct expectations.

FLAG — "Partial" off-lead: Sheringham is technically partial only because dogs can be off lead from 1 August to 28 February outside the cattle areas. However, the cattle lead requirement is year-round and the parkland section (the area closest to the visitor hub) is always on-lead. In practical terms, the free-ranging off-lead zone is the woodland and coastal section in the August-February window only. The Editor should be aware the "Partial" classification is generous — the majority of the year is effectively on-lead throughout. Consider whether "On-lead only" would be more accurate for the WALKS_DB entry and discuss with PO.

NOTABLE: The North Norfolk coast cliff section is a genuine cliff hazard. The cliffs are not dramatic (comparable to North Norfolk in general — moderate height, eroding clay and sandy cliffs rather than dramatic limestone). But the edges are unfenced and the drops are real. Dogs should be on lead near the cliff edge. This is appropriately flagged as a cliff hazard.

NOTABLE: The 7-mile (11.2 km) Woodland and Coastal Walk is the longest walk in this batch and a substantial dog walking commitment. The shorter Repton's Walk (approx 4 km / 2.5 miles) is a more accessible option for shorter visits or less active dogs. Both should be mentioned in the walk page content.

NOTABLE: The North Norfolk Railway (Poppy Line steam railway) at Sheringham adds a tourism hook. A dog day out combining the steam railway and Sheringham Park is a content angle that could generate engagement. This is editorial territory for the Editor rather than walk page copy, but worth noting.

**General batch notes**

- All three walks are in the East of England. They cover three distinct terrain types: heathland and beach (Dunwich), flat conifer plantation (Thetford), and parkland/woodland/cliff (Sheringham). Good variety for a batch area page.
- Two of the three are National Trust properties (Dunwich, Sheringham). One is Forestry England (Thetford/High Lodge). The NT security verification system blocked direct page access throughout this research session; all NT-sourced facts were retrieved via secondary sources and search result snippets. Confidence ratings reflect this. The Editor should conduct a final spot-check on the NT dog access pages before publishing, particularly for Dunwich (lead date discrepancy) and Sheringham (cattle area extent).
- The East of England area page will need to be created as part of this batch (content/areas/east-of-england.md). Saoirse intro needed.
- SEO opportunity: "dog walk Thetford Forest" and "dog walk Sheringham Park" are both plausible medium-volume search terms. "Dog walk Dunwich Heath" is lower volume but faces less competition and benefits from the "lost medieval city" content hook.
