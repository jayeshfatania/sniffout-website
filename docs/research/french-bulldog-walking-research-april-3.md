## Key Facts for the Writer

1. **Daily exercise: 30-60 minutes total, split across two walks.** The most widely cited UK guidance is two walks of approximately 20-30 minutes each. A single walk should rarely exceed 30 minutes. Sources: PitPat exercise guide; search-extracted UK vet consensus.

2. **Distance limit: treat 3-4 km per walk as the practical maximum for a healthy adult.** Some sources suggest up to 5 km is possible for a very fit individual, but this is an upper ceiling, not a target. Under 3 km per walk is safer as a general rule. Source: multiple breed exercise guides including French Bulldog Owner and TomKings Kennel; consistent cross-source consensus.

3. **French Bulldogs have 6.49 times greater odds of heat-related illness than Labrador Retrievers.** This is a UK-specific, peer-reviewed figure from the VetCompass programme using clinical records from over 900,000 UK dogs. Source: O'Neill et al., PMC7303136, Scientific Reports 2020.

4. **BOAS affects approximately 50% of French Bulldogs when tested by respiratory function measurement** - and 30.9% are 30.89 times more likely to be diagnosed with BOAS by a vet than a non-French Bulldog dog. Both figures come from peer-reviewed UK research. Sources: RVC VetCompass (PMC8675495); Cambridge/RVC BOAS functional grading research.

5. **French Bulldogs cannot naturally swim.** Their top-heavy build, large head, short legs, and compressed airway mean they cannot keep their head above water under their own power. They will drown without a life jacket in open water. This applies to walks near rivers, lakes, ponds, and coasts. Source: multiple breed-specific safety guides consistent with anatomical reasoning.

6. **BOAS is graded 0-3 by the University of Cambridge/Kennel Club Respiratory Function Grading Scheme.** Grades 0 and 1 are clinically unaffected. Grades 2 and 3 indicate clinical disease requiring management or surgery. A grade 3 dog must not be exercised as a healthy dog would be. Source: University of Cambridge Veterinary Medicine Department, BOAS resources; Kennel Club RFG scheme.

7. **Flat, shaded, even terrain is the right match for French Bulldogs.** Steep climbs significantly increase respiratory effort and heat production. Rough, boggy or rocky terrain adds joint stress on top of breathing strain. Source: UK vet guidance cross-referenced across multiple sources.

8. **Harnesses are recommended over collars for all French Bulldogs.** Collar pressure on the trachea worsens airway compression and can trigger respiratory distress in BOAS-affected dogs. A Y-shaped harness distributes pressure across the chest instead. Source: veterinary consensus cited across multiple breed harness guides; K9 Magazine; BrachyDog Care.

9. **Start cautious at 20 degrees Celsius; do not walk in the hottest part of the day above that threshold.** The adjacent canine-weather-sensitivity research file (April 2) establishes a 4-5 degree Celsius downward adjustment for brachycephalic breeds, giving a caution threshold of approximately 15-16 degrees Celsius and a danger threshold of approximately 20-21 degrees Celsius. In UK summers this matters from May onwards. Source: canine-weather-sensitivity-april-2.md; PMC7303136.

10. **French Bulldogs were the second most registered breed by the Kennel Club in 2023** (26,704 registrations) and the most popular puppy breed among UK dogs under one year of age (7.0% of all puppies). They overtook Labradors for the first time in 2018 by registration count for that year. Sources: Kennel Club 2023 registration data; RVC VetCompass puppy study (rvc.ac.uk).

## 1. Exercise Needs

### How much daily exercise?

The cross-source UK consensus sits at 30-60 minutes of physical activity per day for a healthy adult French Bulldog. The most practical interpretation is two walks per day - one shorter (15-20 minutes in the morning) and one slightly longer (20-30 minutes in the evening). A single walk should rarely exceed 30 minutes, and during warmer weather or for dogs with any degree of BOAS, 15-20 minutes per walk is more appropriate.

Source: PitPat exercise guide (pitpat.com); Your Family Vets Harrogate (yfvets.co.uk); multiple breed-specific exercise articles consistent with UK vet guidance.

The Kennel Club breed standard for French Bulldogs does not specify a daily exercise duration or distance, but emphasises that "dogs showing respiratory distress or making excessive respiratory noise" are unacceptable and that "soundness of movement" is of "utmost importance." This places breathing health at the centre of any exercise assessment. Source: Royal Kennel Club breed standard (royalkennelclub.com).

Note on conflicting sources: some non-UK sources (particularly US-based) suggest up to 60 minutes of continuous daily exercise. This is unlikely to be appropriate for most French Bulldogs, particularly any affected by BOAS, and should be treated with caution for UK use.

### How does exercise need vary by age?

**Puppies:** The standard "5 minutes per month of age" rule applies to French Bulldogs, with the added caveat that this breed has additional vulnerability because of its brachycephalic conformation and its predisposition to spinal and joint problems. A 4-month-old French Bulldog puppy should have no more than 20 minutes of walking per session, up to twice per day. Growth plates do not mature until approximately 12 months. Source: PitPat (pitpat.com) and multiple breed puppy exercise guides; TrendingBreeds.com.

For brachycephalic puppies specifically, even the 5-minutes-per-month rule should be applied conservatively in warm weather or if the puppy shows any signs of respiratory effort. Source: general brachycephalic puppy care consensus.

**Adults (1-7 years):** Two walks per day totalling 30-60 minutes is the adult target. Individual fitness varies significantly depending on BOAS grade, weight, and conditioning. A French Bulldog with Grade 2 or 3 BOAS should be exercised more conservatively than a Grade 0 dog, regardless of age. Source: Cambridge BOAS grading scheme; vet consensus in multiple UK-linked sources.

**Seniors (from approximately 7-8 years):** French Bulldogs begin showing signs of age from around 7-8 years and are considered senior from approximately 10 years. Exercise volumes should remain similar to adult levels unless the vet advises otherwise, but pace should slow and any reluctance to walk taken as a warning sign. Vigorous exercise should be avoided entirely. Source: Animalife senior French Bulldog care guide (animalife.co.uk); multiple senior breed guides.

### What pace?

French Bulldogs are a leisurely walking breed. Brisk walks, jogging, or anything approaching running is unsuitable. The breed's exercise is best thought of as "ambling with sniff breaks" rather than purposeful cardiovascular exercise. Mental stimulation from sniffing is highly valuable and easier on the respiratory system than sustained forward movement. Source: general vet-aligned breed exercise guidance; consistent across all sources reviewed.

### Safe maximum distance?

A healthy adult French Bulldog with no diagnosed BOAS (Grade 0) can typically manage 3-5 km in ideal conditions (cool weather, flat terrain, slow pace, shade available). The practical working maximum for planning purposes - particularly on routes that involve any incline or warm weather - is 3 km per walk. This figure is consistent with the 30-minute-per-walk guidance at an average Frenchie walking pace of approximately 3-4 km per hour.

For BOAS-affected dogs (Grades 2-3) or overweight dogs, 1-2 km per walk is more appropriate.

Source: French Bulldog walking distance guides (frenchbulldogowner.com; tomkingskennel.com; frenchiechat.com). Note: no single peer-reviewed paper establishes a specific maximum distance figure for French Bulldogs; these distances are derived from vet-aligned secondary sources and walking time guidelines.

## 2. Heat Sensitivity

### Why are French Bulldogs particularly vulnerable?

French Bulldogs are brachycephalic - their skulls are shortened, compressing the entire upper airway into the same space. The key anatomical features that impair heat management are:

- **Stenotic nares (pinched nostrils):** Reduced nasal airflow from the outset. VetCompass data shows French Bulldogs are 42.14 times more likely to have stenotic nares than non-French Bulldogs (OR 42.14). Source: PMC8675495 VetCompass study.
- **Elongated soft palate:** Extends too far into the airway, causing turbulence and obstruction, especially under exertion.
- **Hypoplastic trachea:** Narrowed windpipe limiting total airflow volume.
- **Everted laryngeal saccules:** Tissue that turns outward and further obstructs the airway under respiratory effort.

Dogs cool primarily through panting - evaporating moisture from the tongue, palate and upper airway. A French Bulldog's compressed anatomy makes this process significantly less efficient, meaning body temperature rises faster during exercise and takes longer to fall afterwards.

Source: RVC VetCompass BOAS research; Cambridge University Department of Veterinary Medicine BOAS pages; search-extracted anatomical descriptions consistent across multiple veterinary sources.

### Temperature thresholds

The following thresholds are drawn from the adjacent Sniffout research file (canine-weather-sensitivity-april-2.md), which established these figures from primary sources:

- **15-16 degrees Celsius:** Caution begins for brachycephalic breeds (vs approximately 20 degrees Celsius for average dogs). Keep walks short, avoid midday, ensure shade and water.
- **20-21 degrees Celsius:** High-risk zone for French Bulldogs. Very short necessity walks only, early morning or late evening. Not during the hottest part of the day.
- **25 degrees Celsius and above:** Do not walk French Bulldogs outdoors. Indoors only with fans or air conditioning.

Source: canine-weather-sensitivity-april-2.md citing UK vet consensus, Barc London, Agria Pet Insurance UK guidance, and brachycephalic-specific sources. The adjustment of 4-5 degrees Celsius downward for brachycephalic breeds is referenced in that document.

The PDSA confirms dogs above 40 degrees Celsius body temperature are in heatstroke. Source: PDSA heatstroke page (pdsa.org.uk) - confirmed via fetch.

### VetCompass heat-related illness data

The VetCompass study (O'Neill et al., 2020, PMC7303136) analysed 905,543 UK dogs under primary veterinary care in 2016. French Bulldogs had **6.49 times the odds of heat-related illness (HRI) compared to Labrador Retrievers** (95% CI 3.62-11.63, p less than 0.001) in multivariable analysis. The one-year incidence for French Bulldogs was 0.18% (95% CI 0.12-0.25%).

Exercise (including walking) caused or contributed to 74% of all HRI cases in the study. 68% of exercise-triggered events were caused by walking alone, not running or vigorous exercise.

The fatality rate for HRI in UK dogs was 14.18% (approximately 1 in 7 affected dogs died).

Source: O'Neill et al., "Incidence and risk factors for heat-related illness (heatstroke) in UK dogs under primary veterinary care in 2016," Scientific Reports, PMC7303136 (fetched directly and confirmed).

Context: the figure of OR 6.49 was confirmed by direct fetch of the PMC full text. This figure was also cited in the adjacent canine-weather-sensitivity-april-2.md document.

### Signs of overheating in French Bulldogs

French Bulldogs may show heat distress at lower exertion levels than other breeds. Signs to watch for:

- Heavy or very loud panting (note: some Frenchie noise is normal; a marked increase is the warning sign)
- Slowing down significantly or stopping
- Sitting or lying down and refusing to continue
- Drooling more than usual
- Bright red gums or tongue
- Pale or very pale gums (severe sign)
- Foamy saliva
- Vomiting or dry retching
- Wobbly gait or disorientation

Source: PDSA heatstroke guidance (pdsa.org.uk - fetched directly); Kennel Club heatstroke guidance (royalkennelclub.com - fetched directly); multiple breed heat guides consistent with these signs.

A key difference from other breeds: French Bulldogs can show heat distress even at moderate temperatures (below 20 degrees Celsius) because their limited airway means any increased respiratory demand pushes them toward their physiological ceiling quickly.

### Cooling strategies

- Move to shade or indoors immediately at first signs
- Cool water poured over neck, armpits, groin and paws (not ice-cold - cold water can cause shock)
- Offer small amounts of water to drink
- Wet towels laid over the body
- Fan or air conditioning if available
- Contact a vet immediately if signs do not improve within a few minutes

Do not use ice or ice-cold water directly - this can cause peripheral vasoconstriction and trap heat centrally. Source: PDSA guidance (pdsa.org.uk); Kennel Club guidance (royalkennelclub.com) - both fetched directly.

## 3. Breathing and Exercise (BOAS)

### What is BOAS and how does it affect exercise?

Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome (BOAS) is the collective term for the set of anatomical abnormalities - stenotic nares, elongated soft palate, hypoplastic trachea, everted laryngeal saccules - that obstruct airflow in brachycephalic breeds. It causes exercise intolerance, noisy breathing, sleep disruption, and heat sensitivity.

Under exercise, dogs with BOAS face a compounding problem: increased demand for oxygen coincides with a structurally limited ability to supply it. As the dog breathes faster and harder, soft tissues vibrate and collapse more severely, further narrowing the already-restricted airway. This can become a dangerous positive feedback loop.

Source: RVC VetCompass BOAS research pages; Cambridge University Department of Veterinary Medicine BOAS pages (vet.cam.ac.uk).

An accelerometry study (PMC10936329, 2024) measured habitual physical activity in French Bulldogs with and without BOAS using accelerometers. French Bulldogs with BOAS had significantly lower total activity counts (p=0.001), spent significantly less time in high-intensity activities (p=0.012), and had increased sedentary time (p=0.012) compared to control dogs. French Bulldogs without BOAS showed activity levels similar to control dogs. This confirms that BOAS directly limits how much a French Bulldog can and will move.

Source: PMC10936329, "Comparison of habitual physical activity in French Bulldogs, Pugs and normocephalic dogs by accelerometry" (2024) - fetched directly.

### Prevalence in French Bulldogs

Two figures from different methodologies:

- **Approximately 50%** when assessed by whole-body barometric plethysmography (functional respiratory testing). This is the more sensitive measure. Source: RVC research cited in rvc.ac.uk news articles on BOAS.
- **30.9 times** more likely than non-French Bulldogs to be recorded with a BOAS diagnosis in veterinary clinical records (OR 30.89, 95% CI data in full paper). Source: PMC8675495 VetCompass study (O'Neill et al., 2021, Canine Medicine and Genetics) - fetched directly.

Note on conflicting figures: the 50% figure reflects functional testing of the airway; the clinical records figure reflects what is actually diagnosed and recorded by vets, which is likely an undercount. The true prevalence likely lies between these figures depending on how stringently it is assessed.

An additional note from the RVC: "breathing problems in flat-faced dogs generally worsen with age, if their dog is overweight and during hot weather." (Dr Dan O'Neill, RVC, quoted in RVC news article on noisy breathing in flat-faced dogs.) Source: rvc.ac.uk news article on noisy breathing, fetched directly.

### BOAS grading system

The University of Cambridge and Kennel Club jointly operate the Respiratory Function Grading (RFG) Scheme for French Bulldogs, Pugs, and Bulldogs. The grading involves listening to the dog's airway before and after a 3-minute trotting exercise tolerance test at approximately 4-5 mph.

- **Grade 0:** No BOAS signs. Annual health check suggested if under 2 years old.
- **Grade 1:** Clinically unaffected, mild respiratory signs. Annual health check suggested if under 3 years old.
- **Grade 2:** BOAS-affected, moderate signs. Clinical disease - requires management including weight loss and/or surgical intervention.
- **Grade 3:** BOAS-affected, severe signs. Thorough veterinary examination and surgical intervention strongly advised.

Grades 0 and 1 are clinically unaffected. Grades 2 and 3 require active management.

From January 2025, all French Bulldogs, Pugs and Bulldogs attending Crufts must hold a valid RFG assessment. Grade 3 dogs are excluded from the show. Source: University of Cambridge BOAS resources (vet.cam.ac.uk); Royal Kennel Club media centre (royalkennelclub.com) - both confirmed via search and fetch.

**Practical note for Tom's guide:** owners whose French Bulldog has not been formally assessed can ask their vet to listen to the dog's breathing and provide an informal assessment. Any dog making significant respiratory noise at rest or during light walking should be assessed before undertaking any walk beyond a short toilet walk.

### Harness vs collar

Veterinary consensus strongly favours harnesses over collars for French Bulldogs. A collar places direct pressure on the trachea when a dog pulls or the lead tightens. In a dog with an already-compromised airway, this can trigger or worsen a respiratory crisis during exercise.

Recommended: Y-shaped harness (also called a T-harness or Y-front harness) that sits on the breastbone rather than across the throat and allows full shoulder movement. Mesh or padded nylon fabric for breathability.

Source: BrachyDog Care (brachydogcare.com); K9 Magazine (k9magazine.com); multiple breed harness guides consistent with veterinary positioning.

### Warning signs during a walk

- Sudden loud increase in breathing noise
- Breathing with mouth wide open (French Bulldogs breathe primarily through the nose at rest)
- Coughing or gagging
- Refusing to continue walking
- Blue-tinged gums or tongue (cyanosis - emergency)
- Collapse

Any of these signs during a walk requires stopping immediately, moving to shade, cooling, and calling a vet if symptoms do not resolve quickly.

## 4. Terrain and Walk Type

### What terrain suits French Bulldogs?

**Best suited:** Flat, even surfaces with shade available. Tarmac paths, mown grass, packed gravel paths, flat woodland tracks. Urban parks, flat coastal paths (not sand dunes), flat lakeside paths.

**Manageable with care:** Gentle inclines (no more than a few metres of modest gradient at a time), light woodland with firm paths.

**Avoid or use with significant caution:** Steep climbs, rough moorland, rocky terrain, deep mud, sand dunes, exposed cliff paths with no shade. These challenge both breathing and joints simultaneously.

Source: consistent vet-aligned guidance across French Bulldog exercise resources including yfvets.co.uk; tomkingskennel.com; French Bulldog Owner.

### Can French Bulldogs swim?

French Bulldogs cannot naturally swim and are at serious risk of drowning in open water without assistance. The reasons are anatomical:

- **Top-heavy conformation:** Their large, heavy head and broad, muscular chest concentrate weight at the front. When they try to swim, the front sinks and the head goes underwater.
- **Short legs:** Insufficient paddling power to maintain a head-above-water position.
- **Compressed airway:** Even if they manage to paddle, breathing while swimming is extremely difficult. They tire and become breathless faster than other breeds.

The consensus across breed safety guides is: never allow a French Bulldog near open water (rivers, ponds, lakes, the sea) without either a properly fitted dog life jacket or keeping the dog on-lead well away from the water's edge.

Source: Pets4Homes UK water safety guide (pets4homes.co.uk); multiple breed swimming safety guides with consistent conclusions; OutwardHound UK (outwardhound.com). Note: French Bulldog Rescue Network (FBRN) in the US also confirms this, though that is a non-UK source.

**Implications for Sniffout walks:** Walk 4 (Frensham Great Pond) has significant open water. Walk 7 (Box Hill) has the River Mole nearby. Walk 8 (Seven Sisters) has coastal access. Walk 9 (Haytor/Hound Tor) has moorland pools and streams. Walk 10 (Stanage) has moorland streams. Walk 12 (Malham Cove/Gordale Scar) has waterfalls and streams. Walk 14 (Grasmere) is a lakeside circuit. All require on-lead discipline near water for French Bulldogs.

### Off-lead behaviour

French Bulldogs are generally sociable with other dogs and people. Their temperament is described as friendly, clown-like, and people-oriented, making them less likely than many breeds to pursue prey or roam far. Their physical limitations (short legs, limited stamina) also mean they are unlikely to cover ground quickly.

However, their recall reliability depends heavily on training and individual temperament. French Bulldogs can have an independent or stubborn streak that makes recall in the presence of distractions inconsistent. Early socialisation and positive recall training are recommended.

The primary concern with off-lead exercise for French Bulldogs is not aggression or prey drive but rather overexertion - a Frenchie may chase or play beyond what its respiratory system can safely handle, particularly in warm weather.

Source: breed temperament descriptions consistent across multiple sources; dogbizness.com.au recall training guide for French Bulldogs; northcalfrenchies.com breed behaviour guide.

## 5. Seasonal Considerations

### Summer

- Exercise before 8am or after 7pm when temperatures have dropped. In the UK, even spring and autumn days can reach caution thresholds on sunny afternoons.
- Never walk on a day where air temperature is forecast to exceed 20 degrees Celsius without first timing the walk for early morning.
- Carry water on every summer walk. Offer it every 10-15 minutes, not only when the dog seems thirsty.
- Choose routes with tree cover and shade. Exposed routes (heathland, cliff tops, open moorland) become dangerous quickly.
- Check tarmac or gravel temperature before walking - use the 7-second palm test (if it is uncomfortable to hold your palm flat on the surface for 7 seconds, it is too hot for paws).
- Watch for signs of heat distress from the very start of a walk, not just at the end.

Source: Kennel Club heatstroke guidance (royalkennelclub.com - fetched directly); PDSA heatstroke guidance (pdsa.org.uk - fetched directly); canine-weather-sensitivity-april-2.md; French Bulldog-specific heat guides.

### Winter

French Bulldogs feel the cold relatively quickly due to their single-layer, short coat and low body fat. Their flat faces also mean cold air is less warmed before reaching the lungs compared to longer-muzzled breeds.

- A well-fitting dog coat is recommended below approximately 7 degrees Celsius. This is consistent with the general short-coat breed guidance in canine-weather-sensitivity-april-2.md (short-coated breeds start struggling at 7 degrees Celsius).
- In wet, windy conditions, the effective temperature feels lower - the coat becomes more important.
- Shorten walks in very cold, wet or windy weather.
- Ears and paws are vulnerable to frostbite in sub-zero temperatures - dog boots are an option in extreme cold.
- Cold air can also worsen BOAS symptoms - some dogs breathe more noisily and with more effort in cold, damp conditions.

Source: French Bulldog cold weather guide (frenchbulldogowner.com); winter tips from French Bulldog Rescue Network (frenchbulldogrescue.org); canine-weather-sensitivity-april-2.md for the 7 degree Celsius short-coat threshold.

### Rain and mud

French Bulldogs do not need significant waterproofing beyond a basic dog coat in heavy rain, but deep mud should be avoided: their short stature means they sit lower to the ground and collect mud in skin folds, which can cause skin fold dermatitis - already a breed predisposition (OR 11.18 vs other dogs, per PMC8675495 VetCompass).

## 6. UK Context

### Registration data

French Bulldogs have been among the UK's most registered pedigree breeds for over a decade:

- **2018:** First year French Bulldogs exceeded Labrador Retriever registrations in any given year (36,785 vs 36,526). Source: Kennel Club media centre press release (thekennelclub.org.uk, 2018).
- **2023:** French Bulldogs had 26,704 Kennel Club registrations, placing them second behind Labradors (38,074). Source: Kennel Club 2023 registration data cited in search results.
- **Puppy popularity:** Among UK dogs under one year of age, French Bulldogs are the most popular specific breed at 7.0% of all puppies, ahead of Cockapoos (6.2%) and Labradors (5.8%). Source: RVC VetCompass puppy study (rvc.ac.uk - fetched directly).
- **Estimated UK population:** Approximately 503,798 French Bulldogs currently under veterinary care in the UK. Source: RVC VetCompass (cited in rvc.ac.uk news article on noisy breathing, fetched directly).

### Key health conditions affecting walking ability

The following conditions are all significantly more prevalent in French Bulldogs than in the general UK dog population:

**BOAS (Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome)**
OR 30.89 vs other dogs. Directly limits exercise tolerance and heat management. Primary concern for walking. Source: PMC8675495.

**Stenotic nares (pinched nostrils)**
OR 42.14. Often a component of BOAS; narrows airflow from the very first stage of the respiratory tract. Source: PMC8675495.

**IVDD (Intervertebral Disc Disease)**
French Bulldogs are chondrodystrophic (born with a genetic tendency to premature cartilage degeneration in spinal discs). IVDD in French Bulldogs causes back pain, hind-leg weakness, and in severe cases paralysis. Conservative management requires strict exercise restriction (toilet walks only, 5 minutes every 4-6 hours, on-lead). Owners should watch for any signs of back pain, reluctance to jump, or abnormal gait. Source: PDSA IVDD page (pdsa.org.uk); Fitzpatrick Referrals (fitzpatrickreferrals.co.uk); Walkin' Pets IVDD in French Bulldogs guide.

**Patellar luxation (slipping kneecap)**
French Bulldogs are listed as a predisposed breed. Mild grades (1-2) may only cause occasional skipping lameness; severe grades (3-4) cause persistent gait abnormality and pain. Strenuous exercise and jumping worsen the condition. Multiple short daily walks are preferred over single long walks to reduce joint loading. Source: VCA Animal Hospitals; AKC patellar luxation guide; VetCompass data notes breed predisposition.

**Skin fold dermatitis**
OR 11.18 vs other dogs. Relevant to walking in the sense that mud and moisture in skin folds requires post-walk cleaning on wet walks. Source: PMC8675495.

**Corneal ulceration and eye issues**
OR 4.38 vs other dogs. Exposed, dusty, or windy terrain can irritate French Bulldog eyes more than for other breeds. Eye protection is not typically required but owners should rinse eyes after dusty or heath walks. Source: PMC8675495.

### VetCompass French Bulldog study (PMC8675495)

Published in Canine Medicine and Genetics (2021). Compared 2,781 French Bulldogs with 21,850 non-French Bulldogs under UK primary veterinary care. Key finding: "French Bulldogs had significantly increased adjusted odds of 20/43 (46.5%) specific-level disorders" and differed from other dogs in disorder risk for 31 of 43 conditions analysed. The breed was younger (median 1.51 years vs 4.48 years for other breeds) and lighter (12.45 kg vs 13.80 kg).

Authors conclude French Bulldogs "no longer resemble a typical dog" given the scale of their health divergence from the general dog population.

Source: PMC8675495 (fetched directly).

### Kennel Club respiratory scheme

The Kennel Club and University of Cambridge launched the Respiratory Function Grading Scheme in 2019. From 2025, all French Bulldogs, Pugs and Bulldogs attending Crufts (if over 14 months) must hold a valid RFG assessment. The scheme grades dogs 0-3; Grade 3 dogs are excluded from the show. The scheme is also part of the French Bulldog health certificate (Bronze, Silver, Gold levels).

Source: Royal Kennel Club media centre (royalkennelclub.com); French Bulldog Club of England health scheme page (thefrenchbulldogclubofengland.com); Granta Veterinary Specialists RFG explainer.

## 7. Sniffout Walk Assessment

**Assessment criteria for French Bulldogs:**
- Distance: under 3 km is comfortable; 3-5 km is manageable in good conditions for a fit Grade 0 dog; over 5 km is not recommended.
- Terrain: flat and even is best; any significant steep section is a concern.
- Shade: important in any weather above 15 degrees Celsius.
- Water access: for cooling, not for swimming (French Bulldogs should not enter open water).
- Exposure: open, exposed terrain is a problem in heat and extreme cold.
- On-lead vs off-lead: on-lead only walks reduce the risk of overexertion.

### CHALLENGING

These walks have distance, terrain, or exposure characteristics that make them genuinely unsuitable for most French Bulldogs unless very significantly modified.

| Walk | Distance | Why this is challenging |
|------|----------|------------------------|
| 8. Seven Sisters Cliffs | 8.4 km | 8.4 km far exceeds the recommended maximum. Clifftop exposure means full sun with minimal shade in warm weather. Uneven chalk path with undulations. Coastal wind can be cold but there is no shelter from sun in summer. The risk of overheating on a summer day walk is severe. Very small sections near Birling Gap could be done as a 1-2 km out-and-back, but the route as described is not appropriate. |
| 9. Haytor and Hound Tor | 7.8 km | 7.8 km on open moorland, rated moderate with rough terrain. Rocky, boggy, and uneven ground stresses joints. No shade on open moorland sections. Dartmoor weather is unpredictable - can be hot and sunny or cold and wet. Moorland streams are drowning risks. Not appropriate for French Bulldogs as described. |
| 10. Stanage Edge and Hathersage Moor | 9.2 km | Longest walk on the list at 9.2 km. Gritstone edge and moorland terrain is rough and uneven. Rated moderate. The gritstone surfaces are hard on paws. Open moorland means no shade. Peak District can be cold and windy which partially mitigates heat risk, but the distance and terrain remain unsuitable. |

**Note for Tom:** Even the CHALLENGING walks could theoretically be entered for a very short distance at a flat, accessible point (e.g. parking at Birling Gap for a short flat walk, or the Haytor car park for a brief low-level stroll). But the walks as described on Sniffout are not appropriate for French Bulldogs. This distinction is useful for the article.
