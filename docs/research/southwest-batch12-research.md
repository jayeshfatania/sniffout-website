## WALK 1: Tehidy Country Park

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Tehidy Country Park
- **Location:** Camborne, Cornwall
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** TR14 0HQ (North Cliffs car park, main Cornwall Council car park off Tehidy Road; also TR14 9HB for Bassett's Combe car park on south side) (MEDIUM — multiple car parks serve the park; primary postcode requires verification against Cornwall Council current site)
- **Latitude:** 50.2330
- **Longitude:** -5.2540
- **Approximate distance:** Lake circuit approximately 3.2 km (2 miles); full woodland circuit approximately 6 km (3.7 miles); multiple linked paths allow extension. (MEDIUM — OS mapping confirms lake footpath exists; specific waymarked circuit distance not confirmed from primary source)
- **Difficulty:** Easy
- **Terrain type:** Mixed deciduous and conifer woodland, gravel and earth paths, lakeside tracks. Relatively flat with gentle undulation. Generally well-drained gravel paths around the lake; muddier woodland tracks in wet conditions. (HIGH — consistent with multiple visitor accounts and Cornwall Council park character)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- Cornwall Council manages Tehidy Country Park as a public park. Dogs are permitted throughout the park but must be kept on a lead in the formal garden areas, near the car parks, and in the picnic areas close to the visitor facilities. On the main woodland and lakeside paths, dogs may be off lead if under close control and owners must maintain line-of-sight. No formal designated off-lead zone comparable to some Forestry England sites. (MEDIUM — based on Cornwall Council general parks policy; Tehidy-specific lead rules not confirmed from primary source as Cornwall Council website URLs for Tehidy did not resolve during research)
- There is no application of CRoW Act seasonal lead restrictions at Tehidy as the park is not open access land — it is a managed Cornwall Council country park. Footpaths within the park are public rights of way and permissive paths. (HIGH — CRoW Act applies to open access land only; managed parks are exempt)
- **Livestock present:** No. No livestock grazing within the country park. The park is enclosed woodland and managed parkland. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Dogs must be kept on leads in car parks, picnic areas, and play areas. Fouling must be cleared. Dog bins present throughout. (MEDIUM — standard Cornwall Council parks policy; confirm Tehidy-specific signage on site)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Multiple Cornwall Council car parks serve the park. North Cliffs car park (main entrance, off Tehidy Road, near Camborne) is the primary car park and the suggested start for the lake circuit. Currently free to park (Cornwall Council managed). (MEDIUM — free parking confirmed via visitor sources; verify charges have not changed as Cornwall Council periodically reviews park car parking)
- **Toilets:** Yes, at the North Cliffs car park and at the café building. Seasonal opening hours apply for the main facilities. (MEDIUM)
- **Cafe or pub:** The Woodland Cafe at Tehidy Country Park — a Cornwall Council operated or concession cafe adjacent to the car park. Dog-friendly outdoor seating. (MEDIUM — cafe presence confirmed via visitor accounts; current operator and dog-inside policy requires direct confirmation)
- **Public transport:** Poor. Nearest towns with bus services are Camborne and Redruth. The park is approximately 2 miles from Camborne town centre. The 47/47A bus (First Kernow) serves Camborne and Redruth but does not stop at the park gates. Visitors are recommended to arrive by car or cycle. (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **ticks** — March to October. Dense deciduous and mixed woodland with leaf litter is ideal tick habitat. Check dogs and owners thoroughly after every visit. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to September. Heathland edge near the North Cliffs area adjacent to the park supports adder populations. Main woodland paths lower risk but hedgerow and scrubby edge habitats carry seasonal risk. (MEDIUM)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Woodland birds including tawny owl, greater spotted woodpecker, and various thrushes nest on and near the ground in dense scrub. Close control recommended during this period. (MEDIUM)
- **mountain-bikers** — Year-round. Tehidy Country Park has informal mountain bike use on some of the wider woodland tracks. The paths are not officially designated mountain bike trails but informal cycling occurs. Walkers should expect cyclists particularly on weekends. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** Tehidy is the largest area of ancient woodland in West Cornwall — a very different character from the county's famous coastal scenery. Dogs get tree canopy, leaf-litter sniffing, and the lakeside circuit without salt air or cliff risk. The lake is a genuine highlight for water-loving dogs, though swimming access from the main path is limited. The woodland floor is rich with scent year-round.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Relatively flat, well-maintained gravel paths make this accessible for older dogs or less-fit dogs. The lake circuit gives a satisfying loop without complexity. Free parking removes cost barrier. The woodland shade is a genuine advantage in summer heat.
- **Honest practical note:** Tehidy is popular with families, cyclists, and joggers at weekends. The paths can feel busy near the car park and cafe. For a quieter experience, visit mid-week or on a winter morning when the woodland atmosphere is at its best.

## WALK 2: Moors Valley Country Park

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Moors Valley Country Park and Forest
- **Location:** Ashley Heath, Ringwood, Dorset/Hampshire border
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** BH24 2ET (main Moors Valley car park, Horton Road, Ashley Heath)
- **Latitude:** 50.8540
- **Longitude:** -1.8260
- **Approximate distance:** Variable — the park has multiple waymarked walking trails. The main Forest Walk circuit is approximately 4.8 km (3 miles). The full network of park paths covers more than 25 km. For the website, recommend presenting approximately 5 km as the main featured walk distance. (MEDIUM — Forestry England and park literature reference extensive trail network; specific waymarked distances not confirmed in km from a single primary source during this research)
- **Difficulty:** Easy
- **Terrain type:** Mixed lowland woodland (Scots pine, oak, birch), gravel and packed-earth trails, heathland sections, lakeside path. Flat to gently undulating. Generally well-drained. A stream corridor (Moors River) runs through the park. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- Dogs must be kept on lead in: car parks, picnic areas, around Moors Lake, on the Play Trail, on paths beneath the Go Ape high ropes courses, in the Visitor Centre and restaurant areas, and in all play areas (dogs banned from play equipment zones entirely). Dogs cannot run freely across heathland or wooded heath from March to September due to ground-nesting bird protection. On standard woodland footpaths away from these zones, dogs may be off lead if they are "under close control, in sight of their owners and on the footpath at all times and respond to a call to return." (HIGH — confirmed from Moors Valley primary source, moors-valley.co.uk/dogs)
- Dogs on leads may walk beneath Go Ape courses and in the Nets Adventure area. Dogs cannot participate in Forest Segway sessions. Dogs are banned from the steam railway entirely (except guide/assistance dogs). (HIGH — confirmed from primary source)
- Two designated dog paddling areas at Crane Bridge and the north end of Crane Lake are the only authorised water access points for dogs. All other water areas are off-limits. (HIGH — confirmed from primary source)
- **CRoW Act note:** Moors Valley is a managed Forestry England site. CRoW Act seasonal restrictions apply to the heathland sections (1 March to 31 July statutory minimum; Moors Valley extends this restriction to 1 March to 30 September on heathland and wooded heath). (HIGH)
- **Livestock present:** No livestock within the park. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Golf course is completely off-limits to dogs. Steam railway — no dogs allowed except guide/assistance dogs. Self-service dog wash facility available at the park. 16 dog waste bins marked on trail maps. Water bowls at Visitor Centre, patio, and dog park. (HIGH — confirmed from primary source)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Main car park at Horton Road, Ashley Heath, BH24 2ET. Pay and display. Charges: weekends/bank holidays £5.40-£16.50 depending on duration; weekday standard £3.80-£14.00; weekday November to March super off-peak approximately £2.50 all day. 13 accessible parking spaces. (HIGH — confirmed from forestryengland.uk/moors-valley)
- **Toilets:** Yes, at the Visitor Centre. Baby changing available. (HIGH)
- **Cafe or pub:** Visitor Centre cafe/restaurant (Moors Valley). Dogs on leads are permitted inside the Visitor Centre restaurant and at outdoor patio seating. Water bowls provided. Open 9am-6pm (confirm seasonal hours). (HIGH — confirmed from primary source: "Dogs on leads are permitted inside the Visitor Centre restaurant and at outdoor patio seating")
- **Public transport:** Poor. Ashley Heath is a rural location. Nearest railway station is Bournemouth (approximately 10 miles) or Ringwood (no station). Yellow Buses route 38 provides limited service between Bournemouth and Ringwood and may allow access to a stop near Ashley Heath — confirm current routing. Most visitors arrive by car. (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **ticks** — March to October. Mixed woodland and heathland habitat is suitable tick habitat. Check dogs thoroughly after visits. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to June. Heathland sections of the park carry adder risk in spring/early summer warm spells. (MEDIUM)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 30 September (Moors Valley's extended restriction beyond the statutory CRoW Act minimum). Nightjar, woodlark, and Dartford warbler use the heathland sections. Lead rule enforced across heathland and wooded heath during this period. (HIGH)
- **mountain-bikers** — Year-round. Moors Valley has dedicated mountain biking trails. The park is a popular mountain biking destination. Dog walkers share some trail surfaces with cyclists. Exercise caution especially at weekends and at trail junctions. (HIGH)
- **flooding** — November to March. The Moors River corridor through the park can cause localised flooding of low-lying paths after heavy rainfall. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** Moors Valley is one of the best-managed dog-walking parks in southern England, with a genuine infrastructure for dogs including designated paddling areas, a dog wash facility, water bowls at multiple points, and a dog-walkers map with bin locations. The woodland is predominantly Scots pine over sandy heathland soil — springy underfoot and easy on paws year-round.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Two designated off-lead paddling spots at Crane Bridge and Crane Lake. Dogs welcome inside the restaurant (on lead). Dog wash station means muddy dogs don't have to travel home dirty. The trail network is extensive enough for variety across multiple visits.
- **Honest practical note:** The park is extremely popular with families, and weekends in school holidays can feel crowded. Mountain bikes share some paths. The heathland off-lead restriction (March to September) significantly limits free-running for most of the calendar year. If off-lead exercise is the priority, a midweek winter visit gives the most freedom.

## WALK 3: Golden Cap

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Golden Cap
- **Location:** Chideock, Bridport, Dorset
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** DT6 6JB (Langdon Hill National Trust car park, Muddyford Lane, off A35 at Chideock Hill). Alternative start from Seatown village — limited free roadside parking at Seatown, DT6 6JU. (HIGH — Langdon Hill car park grid reference SY412930 confirmed via OS Maps; Seatown postcode DT6 6JU confirmed via The Anchor Inn address)
- **Latitude:** 50.7212
- **Longitude:** -2.8520
- **Approximate distance:** 4 km (2.5 miles) for the main circular from Langdon Hill National Trust car park. Circular route from Seatown adds approximately 1 km to include the village, making approximately 5 km (3.1 miles). Summit elevation 191 m — the highest point on the entire south coast of England. (HIGH — 4.06 km confirmed via OS Maps primary source; 191 m elevation confirmed)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate (steep ascent to summit; some sections requiring care on descent)
- **Terrain type:** Coastal grassland, muddy field paths (winter), woodland steps, rocky summit section. Steep descent from summit. The route passes through fields and coastal scrub before the final summit approach. Paths can be slippery when wet — wooden steps to summit described as slippery in wet conditions. (HIGH — confirmed via OS Maps route description)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- The National Trust estate at Golden Cap is open access land for most of the open grassland and clifftop sections. CRoW Act applies: dogs on lead (max 2 m) on open access land 1 March to 31 July to protect ground-nesting birds. Outside this period, dogs under close control on open grassland sections. (HIGH — CRoW Act confirmed via gov.uk primary source)
- Fields along the route sometimes contain livestock. Dogs must be kept on lead when livestock are present. The OS Maps route description explicitly states: "keep all dogs on leads and stay well away from any farm animals" where the route passes through livestock fields. (HIGH — confirmed via OS Maps primary source)
- Cliff sections of the coastal path near the summit require close control. Golden Cap's summit involves a steep approach where a dog off lead could approach cliff edges. On-lead or close control strongly recommended near the summit and cliff edges. (HIGH — general cliff safety principle; confirmed by terrain character)
- **Livestock present:** Yes, seasonal. Fields adjacent to and along the approach route to Golden Cap carry livestock (cattle and sheep) on a seasonal basis. Specific months vary by year; treat as year-round possibility. (HIGH — confirmed via OS Maps route description: "sometimes contain livestock")
- **Specific dog restrictions:** National Trust management. Dog bags and dispensers may be available at Langdon Hill car park — verify on site. The coastal grassland is protected habitat; owners should prevent dogs damaging scrub or disturbing nesting habitat. (MEDIUM)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Langdon Hill National Trust car park, DT6 6JB (Muddyford Lane, accessed off A35 at Chideock Hill). National Trust members free; charge for non-members — typically £3-£5 (confirm current charge as National Trust periodically updates). Limited spaces; can fill at busy weekends. Alternative free parking in Seatown village (roadside, very limited) at DT6 6JU — adds 1 km to route. (MEDIUM — parking charge from National Trust not confirmed from primary source during this research due to NT website blocking; confirm before publishing)
- **Toilets:** No toilets at the Langdon Hill car park. Nearest toilets are in Chideock village (approximately 1 mile by road) or at the Anchor Inn in Seatown if visiting the village. (MEDIUM)
- **Cafe or pub:** The Anchor Inn, Seatown, DT6 6JU. Located at the beach in Seatown village, at the foot of Golden Cap. A beachfront pub open Monday-Sunday 12pm-10pm, kitchen 12pm-8pm. Cashless payments only. Dog-friendly confirmation not obtained from a primary source — this is a very common post-walk destination and is widely reported as dog-friendly in visitor accounts, but explicit confirmation requires a direct call or website check. Tel: 01297 489215. (MEDIUM — The Anchor Inn's existence and location confirmed from primary source; dog-friendly status based on general visitor reports but not confirmed from pub's own website during research)
- **Public transport:** Poor. The route is accessible from the A35 at Chideock Hill but there is no regular bus stop at Langdon Hill. The X51 bus (First Hampshire and Dorset) runs along the A35 between Bridport and Axminster and stops in Chideock village — from there it is a 1.5-mile walk to the car park. Most visitors arrive by car. (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **cliff** — Year-round. Golden Cap is 191 m — the highest point on the south coast of England. The summit plateau approaches cliff edges. The Jurassic Coast cliffs at this location are actively eroding — cliff edges are unstable and the coastal path approaches the cliff edge at several points. Dogs must be under close control near the summit and on cliff-edge sections. Keep dogs away from cliff edges at all times. (HIGH)
- **livestock** — Year-round (treat as year-round even if seasonal). Fields along the route carry livestock. Cattle and/or sheep present seasonally. Dogs on lead when livestock visible. (HIGH)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Coastal grassland and scrub support ground-nesting species including skylark and stonechat. CRoW Act seasonal lead restriction applies on open access grassland sections. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Bracken, scrub, and coastal grassland carry tick populations. Check dogs and owners after every visit. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to June. South-facing coastal grassland and scrub on the approach to Golden Cap is prime adder habitat. Most active in warm sunny spells April-June. (MEDIUM)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Rocky and chalky path surfaces on the exposed upper section of Golden Cap can heat significantly in direct summer sun. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** Golden Cap offers dogs the highest coastal viewpoint on the entire south coast — 191 m of open sky, grassland scent, and sea breeze. The approach through woodland and scrub is rich with sniffing opportunities before the open summit. The Seatown finish makes this a rare walk where the pub genuinely punctuates the route rather than being an optional add-on.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Open coastal grassland for off-lead running outside the March-July lead season. The summit plateau gives panoramic views from Portland to the Devon cliffs. Seatown beach at the foot of the hill for a post-walk paddle. The Anchor Inn in Seatown as a post-walk stop.
- **Honest practical note:** The cliff sections are not suitable for dogs that are not reliably responsive to recall. This is a working cliff landscape — the Golden Cap summit edges are real hazards. The fields below also carry livestock, meaning on-lead sections are a genuine feature of the route, not just a formality. Paths can be extremely muddy in winter.

## WALK 4: Exmoor - Watersmeet and the Lynmouth Gorge

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Watersmeet and the Lynmouth Gorge, Exmoor
- **Location:** Watersmeet, near Lynmouth, Devon
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** EX35 6NQ (Lynmouth village car park, the most accessible start point); or EX35 6NT (Watersmeet itself — drop-off only, no main car park at Watersmeet; narrow lane, mobility parking by arrangement only, tel 01598 753348). The walk typically starts from Lynmouth and follows the East Lyn River upstream to Watersmeet, returning the same way or via the ridge. (HIGH — EX35 6NT confirmed as Watersmeet postcode from primary National Trust/visit-exmoor source; EX35 6NQ for Lynmouth town car park confirmed via postcode data)
- **Latitude:** 51.2330
- **Longitude:** -3.8280
- **Approximate distance:** Lynmouth to Watersmeet return: approximately 4.8 km (3 miles) each way on the riverside path, making a 9.6 km (6-mile) return. The standard out-and-back riverside walk from Lynmouth to Watersmeet is approximately 4-5 km (2.5-3 miles) one way. Many visitors walk to Watersmeet and return the same way: approximately 8-9 km (5-5.5 miles) round trip. National Trust estate at Watersmeet covers 2,000 acres. (MEDIUM — distances estimated from terrain and visitor accounts; precise waymarked distance not confirmed from a single primary source)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate (the riverside path has some rocky sections; the return route via ridge involves a significant climb)
- **Terrain type:** Rocky riverside path (East Lyn River), ancient oak woodland, gorge sides. The path hugs the river on a narrow shelf in places with steep gorge walls above and the river below. Some stepping stones and rocky scrambles. Can be very slippery when wet. Not suitable for pushchairs. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- Watersmeet is a National Trust property on Exmoor. The woodland and paths are permissive paths on National Trust land; the gorge section is not generally open access land. Dogs are permitted throughout and "dogs welcome" is confirmed as a facility. On the main riverside path, dogs can be off lead if under close control, but the path is narrow with steep drop-offs to the river in places — owners should use discretion about off-lead given the terrain. The narrow riverside path sections and proximity to the river edge make close control advisable for most dogs. (HIGH — "dogs welcome" confirmed from primary source visit-exmoor.co.uk)
- On any sections that do fall under CRoW Act open access land (heathland and moorland sections on the ridge return route), the statutory 1 March to 31 July lead requirement applies. (HIGH — CRoW Act confirmed via gov.uk)
- **Livestock present:** No livestock on the main riverside path. The open moorland sections if taking the ridge route may have cattle and/or Exmoor ponies. (MEDIUM)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Dogs are welcome at the Watersmeet House tearoom's outdoor seating. Inside the tearoom dog policy not confirmed from primary source — "dogs welcome" on site generally but indoor policy requires direct confirmation. (MEDIUM)

**CRITICAL FLAG — FLASH FLOODING:** The East Lyn River gorge at Watersmeet is the site of one of the most catastrophic flash floods in British history. On 15-16 August 1952, extreme rainfall on Exmoor caused catastrophic flooding of the East and West Lyn Rivers, destroying Lynmouth and killing 34 people. The gorge acts as a funnel in extreme rainfall events. Flash flooding risk is a REAL and SIGNIFICANT hazard at this location. Do NOT walk in the gorge during or after heavy rain. The Environment Agency and Exmoor National Park Authority issue flood warnings for this catchment. This hazard must be flagged prominently on the walk page. (HIGH — 1952 Lynmouth Flood Disaster is historically documented; gorge flash flood risk is a known and documented hazard)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Lynmouth village car park (main start point). EX35 6NQ. Pay and display. Lynmouth is a small coastal village with limited parking — can fill very quickly in season (Easter, summer). The Lyn Valley Road car park in Lynmouth or Lynton (the hilltop town above) provides additional overflow parking. (MEDIUM — Lynmouth car park existence confirmed; current charges not verified from primary source)
- **Toilets:** Yes, in Lynmouth village. Also confirmed as available at Watersmeet area (accessible via drop-off, mobility parking only at Watersmeet itself). (MEDIUM)
- **Cafe or pub:** Watersmeet House Tearoom (National Trust), EX35 6NT. Open daily April to October, 10:30am-4pm. Famous for cream teas. Dogs welcome at the site. Tea, coffee, hot chocolate, cakes, cream teas. (HIGH — confirmed from primary source visit-exmoor.co.uk; "dogs welcome" confirmed) Indoor dog policy not confirmed — assume outdoor seating at minimum.
- Multiple pubs and cafes in Lynmouth village at the start/end of the walk. The Rising Sun Hotel and the Harbour House Cafe in Lynmouth are known visitor stops (dog-friendly status not individually confirmed — requires verification).
- **Public transport:** Limited. Quantock Motor Services operates seasonal bus routes in the Exmoor area. The nearest railway stations are Barnstaple (25 miles, Tarka Line) or Taunton (35 miles). The Exmoor Coaster bus service (route 300) runs Minehead-Lynmouth seasonally. (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **flooding** — Year-round, especially October to March and during summer thunderstorm events. CRITICAL HAZARD. The East Lyn River gorge is susceptible to flash flooding. The river can rise dramatically and rapidly after heavy rainfall on the Exmoor plateau. Do not enter the gorge during or after heavy rain. Check Environment Agency flood warnings before visiting in wet weather. The 1952 Lynmouth disaster demonstrates the extreme potential of this hazard. This hazard requires prominent placement on the walk page. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Ancient oak woodland along the gorge is prime tick habitat. High tick pressure throughout this environment. Check dogs and owners thoroughly after every visit. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to June. The moorland and heath sections on the ridge (if taking the return via high ground) carry adder risk. Less relevant on the main riverside path through closed woodland. (MEDIUM)
- **deer** — Year-round, peak October to November (rut). Red deer are present on Exmoor. Near the moorland sections of the Exmoor plateau, deer are regularly encountered. Less relevant on the main riverside gorge path but applicable if extending the walk to open moorland. Keep dogs under close control. (MEDIUM)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Applies to open moorland and heathland sections on any ridge extension. Pied flycatcher and other woodland birds nest in the gorge woodland; owners should prevent dogs disturbing dense ground cover. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** Watersmeet is one of the most dramatic river gorge walks in southern England. Dogs walk alongside the East Lyn River for the entire outward route — crystal-clear tumbling water with numerous pools and shallows where water-loving dogs can paddle. The ancient oak woodland above creates a canopy that makes this walk magical in all seasons: misty in autumn, loud with birdsong in spring.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** River access at multiple points for paddling and drinking. The Watersmeet tearoom is the classic reward stop with outdoor seating and dogs welcome. The gorge path is enclosed and intimate — ideal for dogs that find open moorland over-stimulating. Return the same way to add symmetry, or take the ridge route for open sky and moorland scent.
- **Honest practical note:** The flash flooding risk in this gorge is real and not to be dismissed. If rain is falling on the Exmoor hills, the river rises fast. The path also becomes very slippery in wet conditions — rocky surfaces underfoot. This is a walk that rewards pre-planning. Check the weather and river conditions before you go.

## WALK 5: Quantock Hills - Holford and Rams Combe

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Quantock Hills - Holford and Rams Combe
- **Location:** Holford, near Bridgwater, Somerset
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** TA5 1RY (Holford village car park, Holford, Somerset — roadside/village car park at the start of the main combe walks). Also TA5 1SE for the Plough Inn area in Holford which has limited parking. (MEDIUM — Holford village postcode confirmed; specific car park postcode requires OS verification)
- **Latitude:** 51.1880
- **Longitude:** -3.2010
- **Approximate distance:** The classic Holford circular via Rams Combe and return over the heathland ridge is approximately 8-10 km (5-6 miles). The simpler Holford Combe out-and-back to the top is approximately 4-5 km (2.5-3 miles). (MEDIUM — estimated from terrain and AONB documentation; specific waymarked distance not confirmed from a single primary source)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate (significant elevation gain over the ridge sections; the combe paths are easier)
- **Terrain type:** Oak woodland in the valley combes (Glen) giving way to open heathland and bracken on the ridgeline. Earth paths through the combes, increasingly open with gorse and bracken on the higher sections. Can be boggy on the moorland plateau in wet conditions. (HIGH — consistent with AONB character and multiple visitor accounts)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- The Quantock Hills are an AONB (Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty) and the open heathland is CRoW Act open access land. CRoW Act applies: dogs on lead (max 2m) on open access land from 1 March to 31 July for ground-nesting bird protection. (HIGH — CRoW Act confirmed via gov.uk primary source; Quantock Hills AONB is confirmed CRoW Act open access land)
- Red deer are present year-round on the Quantocks. During the red deer rut (typically late September to early November), dogs must be kept under very close control on the open heathland to avoid disturbing rutting stags. Natural England and the Quantock Hills AONB Authority strongly advise keeping dogs on lead during the rut season in areas where deer are known to be present. Chasing deer on open access land is an offence. (HIGH — red deer on Quantock Hills is well documented; rut advice consistent with Natural England guidance)
- In the valley combes (including Holford Combe and Rams Combe), the woodland paths are generally permissive paths rather than open access land. Dogs can be off lead in the valley woodland with owner discretion — but red deer also use the combes, particularly at dawn and dusk. (MEDIUM — distinction between CRoW Act land and permissive path not confirmed from AONB primary source; this is the standard interpretation for AONB combes)
- **CRoW Act note:** The open heathland ridgeline is CRoW Act open access land. The statute requires 2m lead 1 March to 31 July. Outside this period, close control required — particularly given deer presence. (HIGH)
- **Livestock present:** Yes, seasonal. Cattle and/or sheep graze the open heathland on the Quantock ridge on a seasonal basis. Commoners' grazing is a traditional feature of the Quantocks. Dogs on lead when livestock present. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Red deer are the primary dog management consideration. The Quantock Hills AONB Authority and Natural England issue guidance advising lead control near deer especially during the rut. (HIGH)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Holford village car park, TA5 1RY. The car park is small and free. Can fill on busy summer weekends. Alternative parking at Kilve or Nether Stowey for different approach routes. (MEDIUM — Holford parking confirmed via visitor accounts; charges/current status require verification)
- **Toilets:** No formal public toilets at the Holford village car park. Nearest public toilets are in Nether Stowey (approximately 3 miles). (MEDIUM)
- **Cafe or pub:** The Plough Inn, Holford, TA5 1RY. A traditional Somerset pub in the village itself, directly on the walk route. Dog-friendly status not confirmed from primary source — highly likely given rural pub location and walking clientele, but requires direct confirmation. Tel verification recommended. Also The Combe House Hotel (now Combe House) in Holford sometimes operates a tearoom or food service. (MEDIUM — The Plough Inn existence confirmed; dog-friendly status not confirmed from primary source)
- **Public transport:** Very poor. Holford is a small village with minimal bus service. The 15B/15C service (First Somerset and Avon) provides limited connections between Bridgwater and Holford via Nether Stowey — infrequent service. Nearest railway station is Bridgwater (11 miles). Visitors are strongly recommended to arrive by car. (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **deer** — Year-round; HIGH RISK October to November (rut). Red deer are the Quantock Hills' most significant wildlife hazard for dog walkers. Stags during the rut can be aggressive toward dogs. Dogs chasing deer on the open heathland is an offence and causes real wildlife disturbance. Keep dogs on lead near deer at all times, particularly during the rut (late September to early November). (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. The valley combes (dense bracken and woodland) are high-pressure tick habitat. The open heathland also carries ticks in season. Check dogs and owners thoroughly after every visit. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to June. The open heathland sections carry adder populations. Bracken, gorse, and rocky outcrops are prime basking habitat in spring. (HIGH — adders are well documented on Quantock Hills AONB heathland)
- **livestock** — Seasonal (spring to autumn typically). Commoners' cattle and sheep graze the open heathland. Keep dogs on lead when livestock are present. (HIGH)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Dartford warbler, nightjar, and other heathland species nest on the Quantock heathland. Lead restriction applies on open access land. (HIGH)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The Quantock Hills offer a rare combination in England: an ancient woodland combe leading directly up onto open heathland and moorland, all within a single walk. Dogs get the enclosed, scent-rich atmosphere of the oak woodland in the lower section, then the complete exposure of heathland and sky on the ridge. The transition is genuinely dramatic. The combes are green, sheltered, and quiet; the ridge is expansive and wind-blown.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** The valley floor paths in Holford Combe run alongside a stream — shallow enough for paddling and perfect for a drink in warm weather. The open heathland above provides genuine freedom for well-trained dogs outside the March-July lead period. The Quantocks are never heavily crowded — this is undervisited countryside with a real sense of wildness.
- **Honest practical note:** Red deer are the headline management consideration on the Quantocks. If you visit during the rut (October-November), lead your dog on the open heathland sections. A dog that chases deer in this landscape risks real harm — to the deer, to itself, and legally to you as the owner.

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Dartmoor - Burrator Reservoir Circuit
- **Location:** Yelverton, Devon (Dartmoor National Park)
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** PL20 6PE (Burrator Discovery Centre and main dam car park, Yelverton, Devon). Also PL20 6PF for Norsworthy Bridge car park; PL20 6PE for Quarry car park. (HIGH — PL20 6PE confirmed from South West Lakes Trust primary source at swlakestrust.org.uk)

- **Longitude:** -3.9990
- **Approximate distance:** The full Burrator Reservoir circuit is approximately 9-10 km (5.5-6.2 miles). The South West Lakes Trust's accessible wildlife trail in the Arboretum is 1.5 km. Self-guided walk leaflets (available from the Discovery Centre) detail the full reservoir circuit. (MEDIUM — the 1.5 km accessible trail is confirmed from primary source; full reservoir circuit distance estimated from terrain and visitor accounts as approximately 9-10 km; the reservoir perimeter is approximately 5 miles/8 km by road)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate (the main circuit has moorland sections with uneven terrain; the accessible Arboretum trail is Easy)
- **Terrain type:** Mixed — woodland (Arboretum section), open Dartmoor moorland, reservoir shoreline, moorland tracks. Some sections are on surfaced tracks; others are open moorland paths. Can be boggy in wet conditions. Historic features include remains of farms, tin working areas, and railway remnants. Sheepstor tor is accessible from the circuit. (HIGH — confirmed from South West Lakes Trust primary source)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- South West Lakes Trust manages Burrator Reservoir and has a clear dog access policy. Dogs must be kept on lead throughout the site EXCEPT in the designated off-lead dog park, which is located approximately 600 yards (540 m) northeast of the Discovery Centre. The off-lead area is fenced and contains native broadleaf saplings, with multiple paths within the enclosure. (HIGH — confirmed from South West Lakes Trust primary source swlakestrust.org.uk/dog-walking)
- On the main reservoir circuit and all woodland and water-edge paths, dogs must be on lead. (HIGH — confirmed: "Dogs must be kept on a lead" as the general rule at all SWLT sites)
- Dogs must be kept out of the water at all times across all South West Lakes Trust sites due to potential blue-green algae. (HIGH — confirmed from primary source: "Dogs must be kept out of the water at all times")
- **CRoW Act note:** The surrounding Dartmoor open moorland outside the reservoir estate (accessible via Sheepstor and the open moor above) is CRoW Act open access land. The statutory 2m lead requirement applies 1 March to 31 July on open access moorland. Outside this period, dogs under close control. Sheep are grazed on Dartmoor open moorland year-round — dogs on lead near livestock at all times. (HIGH — CRoW Act confirmed via gov.uk; Dartmoor livestock grazing is well documented)
- **Livestock present:** Yes. Sheep graze on the Dartmoor moorland sections. Dartmoor ponies may also be encountered on open moorland sections beyond the reservoir estate boundary. Dogs on lead near all livestock at all times. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Dogs must be kept out of the water at all times across all South West Lakes Trust sites. Fixed penalty charges apply for rule violations. Dog waste must be removed. (HIGH — confirmed from primary source)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Main dam / Discovery Centre car park (PL20 6PE). Donation boxes available — free parking, donations appreciated. Also: Norsworthy Bridge car park (PL20 6PF) and Quarry car park — both free with donation. No formal pay and display charges at any Burrator car park as of confirmed information. (HIGH — "Donation boxes are available" confirmed from primary source; no pay-and-display charges mentioned)
- **Toilets:** Yes. Public toilets at the main dam, open seven days a week. (HIGH — confirmed from primary source)
- **Cafe or pub:** Burrator Discovery Centre (PL20 6PE). Open 10am-3:30pm, subject to volunteer availability — hours and opening are not guaranteed. Ice cream van at main dam and Norsworthy Bridge car park on busy days. Nearest pub with food is the Royal Oak in Meavy village (approximately 1 mile from the reservoir, PL20 6PJ) — a classic Dartmoor village pub with outdoor seating; dog-friendly status widely reported but not confirmed from primary source during this research. (MEDIUM — Discovery Centre confirmed from primary source; Meavy Royal Oak existence confirmed; dog-friendly status requires direct confirmation)
- **Public transport:** Poor. Dartmoor is difficult to access without a car. The 48 bus service (First Devon and Cornwall/Go South West) runs from Plymouth to Yelverton — nearest bus stop in Yelverton village (PL20 6DH), approximately 2 miles from the reservoir. Nearest railway is Plymouth (15 miles). (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **ticks** — March to October. Dartmoor heathland, bracken, and woodland is very high-pressure tick habitat. One of the highest tick-risk environments in England. Check dogs and owners extremely thoroughly after every visit. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to June. Dartmoor moorland and heathland edges around the reservoir carry adder populations. Rocky, south-facing bracken and heather edges near Sheepstor are prime basking habitat. (HIGH — adders are well documented on Dartmoor)
- **livestock** — Year-round. Sheep are grazed across the Dartmoor moorland sections. Dartmoor ponies may be encountered on open moorland beyond the reservoir. Dogs on lead near all livestock at all times. (HIGH)
- **algae** — May to September. South West Lakes Trust requires dogs to be kept out of the water at all times due to blue-green algae risk. Burrator is a drinking water reservoir — algae bloom risk is taken seriously. (HIGH — confirmed from primary source: "Dogs must be kept out of the water at all times")
- **deer** — Year-round; October to November (rut). Red deer are present on Dartmoor. On moorland sections above the reservoir, deer may be encountered. Close control required. (MEDIUM)
- **flooding** — October to March. Dartmoor's high rainfall (one of the wettest parts of England) means the moorland sections can become very boggy and the valley paths flood seasonally. The reservoir itself rises in wet conditions. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** Burrator Reservoir is the most accessible Dartmoor circuit walk for dogs that don't suit the most exposed open moor. Dogs get the drama of Dartmoor — granite, moor, sky — without requiring navigation skills or fitness for a full moorland expedition. The reservoir itself is scenic in all conditions, from glassy reflections in still weather to grey waves in wind.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Free parking. Public toilets open every day. Enclosed off-lead dog park near the Discovery Centre. The circuit route through woodland and moorland gives dogs varied terrain and scent interest. Sheepstor tor adds height and a genuine moorland character to the walk.
- **Honest practical note:** The lead-on-water-edge rule is strictly enforced — South West Lakes Trust do not allow dogs in the water at any point due to blue-green algae risk. This is a drinking water reservoir. Off-lead freedom is limited to the fenced dog park near the Discovery Centre; the rest of the circuit is a lead walk. Plan accordingly.

## RESEARCHER NOTES

### Significant Findings

**1. Burrator Reservoir — Dogs in water PROHIBITED (critical)**
South West Lakes Trust have a hard rule: dogs must be kept out of the water at all times across all their sites, including Burrator. This applies to the reservoir, its inlets, and shoreline. Reason: potential blue-green algae. This is stronger than simply "algae risk" — it is a blanket water access prohibition at a drinking water source. The walk page must be explicit about this. The off-lead dog park (600 yards NE of the Discovery Centre, fenced) is the ONLY off-lead area. The main circuit is a lead walk. This significantly affects the walk's dog access rating — it is Partial off-lead at best, and in practice a predominantly on-lead circuit. (HIGH confidence — confirmed from primary source)

**2. Moors Valley — Excellent dog infrastructure, but more restricted than it appears**
Moors Valley has outstanding dog infrastructure (dog wash, designated paddling areas, water bowls, dog-walkers map). However, the restrictions are significant and numerous: no steam railway (any breed), mandatory lead around the lake, around Go Ape, in car parks and picnic areas, and on all heathland from March-September. The park is excellent for dogs but the marketing impression of freedom is somewhat misleading — the on-lead zones cover much of the park's most popular areas. The off-lead woodland path access is genuinely available but requires navigating a significant number of on-lead zones first. (HIGH confidence — confirmed from primary source)

**3. Watersmeet — Flash flooding is a CRITICAL safety hazard requiring prominent placement**
The 1952 Lynmouth Flood Disaster (34 deaths) demonstrates the extreme potential of the East Lyn River gorge in flood conditions. The gorge acts as a drainage funnel for the Exmoor plateau. This is not a theoretical hazard — it has killed people within living memory. The walk page must include a prominent, specific, not-alarmist but clear note about not entering the gorge during or after heavy rainfall, and checking Environment Agency flood warnings. This is the single most safety-critical piece of information in this batch. (HIGH confidence — 1952 disaster is historical fact; gorge flood mechanism is well documented)

**4. Quantock Hills — Red deer management is the lead finding**
Red deer on the Quantocks are not just a wildlife highlight — they are a dog management imperative. During the rut (late September to early November), stags can be dangerous to dogs and the chasing of deer on open access land is a wildlife crime. The walk page must clearly communicate this. The CRoW Act lead requirement (1 March to 31 July) applies to the open heathland. Combined with the rut period, the practical off-lead window on the heathland is approximately August to mid-September. (HIGH confidence — red deer on Quantocks is well documented; CRoW Act confirmed)

**5. Golden Cap — Cliff hazard is genuinely serious**
Golden Cap is the highest point on the south coast of England at 191 m. The Jurassic Coast cliffs are actively eroding. Dogs approaching cliff edges at this height on eroding cliff faces are a real risk. This is not standard "cliff nearby" territory — the summit plateau at 191 m is directly above sea cliffs. The walk page should not downplay this. (HIGH confidence — 191 m height and Jurassic Coast cliff character confirmed)
