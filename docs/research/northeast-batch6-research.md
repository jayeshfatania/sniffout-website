## WALK 1: Hadrian's Wall - Housesteads to Steel Rigg, Northumberland

### 1. Full Name
Hadrian's Wall - Housesteads Fort to Steel Rigg Circular Walk

### 2. Location
Bardon Mill / Henshaw, Northumberland

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
NE47 6NN (Housesteads car park, the recommended start). Steel Rigg car park: NE47 7AN (alternative start, useful for linear walkers using two cars).

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
55.013°N, 2.331°W (Housesteads Fort / car park area). This is precise - the fort sits at National Grid Reference NY 78838 68489, converting to 55°00'47"N, 2°19'49"W.

### 5. Approximate Distance
The circular walk from Housesteads, west along the wall crest to Steel Rigg and back via the lower military road / Vallum path, is approximately 13 km (8 miles). The one-way ridge walk from Housesteads to Steel Rigg is approximately 6.5 km (4 miles) - allow an out-and-back of around 13 km if returning the same way.

**Source confidence:** The 8-mile / 13 km circular figure comes from the National Trust's own walk leaflet (static.nationaltrust.org.uk). One-way distance is a derived calculation consistent with multiple walking guides (BaldHiker, WalkingBritain). The Steel Rigg car park note on Northumberland National Park's site confirms longer-stay walkers should use The Sill.

### 6. Difficulty
Hard. The Whin Sill escarpment involves steep, uneven stone-pitched ascents and descents, and the path traverses rocky crags. Not suitable for unfit dogs with joint issues. Multiple sources describe the ridge as requiring significant effort.

### 7. Terrain Type
Exposed moorland ridge, rocky crags (Whin Sill dolerite), stone-pitched path along the Wall, some boggy sections between crags. Uneven underfoot throughout. Can be extremely muddy in winter. Some sections of the return route pass through rough grassland and along the earthwork Vallum.

### 8. Off-Lead Status: PARTIAL

**On the ridge path (Hadrian's Wall National Trail) and Housesteads Fort grounds:** Dogs must be kept on a short lead at all times. This is an English Heritage requirement within the fort site, and a National Trust / English Heritage requirement on the surrounding managed land. The official position, confirmed by the National Trust's walk leaflet and the fort's facilities page, is that the route crosses "open grazing land for livestock, used all year round" and is "an important habitat for ground-nesting birds" - both of which require dogs on leads or under close control. In practical terms, the entire ridge section is lead territory.

**Why:** Livestock present year-round, ground-nesting birds in season, and English Heritage regulations at the fort itself. The National Trust states dogs must be kept on a short lead and under close control.

**Certification note:** The Hadrian's Wall corridor does qualify as Access Land under the CRoW Act 2000 in parts, but the specific Wall path and fort grounds are managed heritage / agricultural land with the restrictions above superseding general open access provisions.

### 9. Livestock Present
Yes - year-round. Sheep graze the moorland either side of the Wall. Dogs must be on leads in livestock fields. Note: if cattle approach, standard countryside advice applies - release dog and recall when safe.

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- Dogs must be on a short lead throughout the fort site and along the managed Wall path
- Dogs are not permitted inside the Housesteads museum building
- Dogs are welcome in the visitor centre area (on lead)
- Note: Sycamore Gap (the famous tree) is no longer standing following vandalism in 2023 - the stump remains and may regrow, but the landmark is significantly changed

### 11. Parking Details
**Housesteads Car Park** - operated by Northumberland National Park (not English Heritage, despite English Heritage managing the fort).
- Postcode: NE47 6NN
- Charges: Free for first 15 minutes, £5 up to 3 hours, £2 per hour thereafter, maximum £10 all day. No overnight parking.
- Payment: ANPR system - pay on exit or within 48 hours at parkwithease.co.uk

**Steel Rigg Car Park** (alternative / second-car point)
- Postcode: NE47 7AN
- Charges: Same tariff as Housesteads - £5 up to 3 hours, £2/hour thereafter, max £10. No overnight. For longer stays, Northumberland National Park directs visitors to The Sill car park.

**Source:** Northumberland National Park car parks page (northumberlandnationalpark.org.uk/visitor-info/car-parks/).

### 12. Toilets
Yes - available at the Housesteads visitor centre (operated by English Heritage / National Trust). Also portable toilets at Housesteads Fort year-round. Seasonal availability varies for other facilities.

### 13. Cafe or Pub
- **At start:** Housesteads visitor centre has a small cafe/snack outlet (hot/cold drinks, snacks, ice creams). Dogs not permitted inside cafe building; outdoor seating available.
- **Near Steel Rigg:** The Twice Brewed Inn, Bardon Mill, NE47 7AN - confirmed dog-friendly. Dogs welcome in the bar area, water and treats provided, dog-friendly accommodation also available. Brew house on site. This is the most recommended pub for this walk. (**Source:** twicebrewedinn.co.uk; dogfuriendly.com; thegooddogguide.com)
- **The Sill National Landscape Discovery Centre** (near Steel Rigg): Dogs not permitted inside the building or the cafe. Limited outdoor seating only. Multiple TripAdvisor reviews (including one titled "NO DOGS" and "Dog Walkers think twice") confirm this is not genuinely dog-friendly beyond the car park. Go to the Twice Brewed Inn instead.

### 14. Public Transport Access
The AD122 seasonal bus service (named after the year Hadrian's Wall was built) runs between Hexham and Haltwhistle via Housesteads and Once Brewed/Steel Rigg. Typically operates Easter to October. Hexham is on the Newcastle-Carlisle railway line (Tyne Valley Line). Check current timetables via Hadrian's Wall Country or Arriva North East as the service has varied in recent years. Walking the full Hadrian's Wall Path long-distance trail is also a linear option with shuttle buses available.

**Confidence note:** The AD122 bus is a seasonal service and its current operational status for 2026 should be verified before publishing.

### 15. Relevant Hazards for Dogs
- **Ticks** - high risk; sheep pasture and moorland throughout the walk. Treat dogs before and check after.
- **Ground-nesting birds** - curlew, lapwing, redshank, snipe on moorland sections. Peak nesting March-July. This is one reason the on-lead requirement exists.
- **Cliff** - the Whin Sill has dramatic drops (Highshield Crags, Hotbank Crags). Dogs must not go near the crag edges - drops are sheer and unguarded.
- **Adders** - moorland habitat. Risk present spring-autumn. Dogs should not be allowed to sniff in long heather.

### 16. Key Features for Dogs
- Some of the most dramatic elevated walking in northern England - dogs and owners get sweeping views into both Northumberland and Cumbria
- Rocky crag scrambling provides real physical challenge and interesting terrain for active dogs
- Long-established path means well-worn but dramatic ground - no ploughed fields, no crops
- The walk passes Crag Lough - a dramatic lough (lake) beneath the crags where dogs can drink (though swimming access depends on access to the shoreline from the path)

### 17. Honest Practical Note
The lead requirement is total for this walk - there is no meaningful off-lead section anywhere on the fort grounds or the ridge. The terrain is also genuinely hard going with significant height gain and loss on uneven stone. Bring multiple tick-prevention measures and check your dog thoroughly at the car park afterwards. The Twice Brewed Inn is an excellent endpoint reward for both dog and owner. Do not rely on The Sill cafe for a post-walk dog-friendly coffee stop.

3. What is the best pub near Hadrian's Wall that allows dogs?
4. Is the Housesteads to Steel Rigg walk suitable for a dog with arthritis or joint problems?

## WALK 2: Bamburgh Beach and Castle Walk, Northumberland

### 1. Full Name
Bamburgh Beach and Castle Walk

### 2. Location
Bamburgh, Northumberland

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
NE69 7DF (Links Road car park, adjacent to the beach dunes - this is the primary beach access car park)

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
55.607°N, 1.715°W. This is for the beach / castle area of Bamburgh village. Consistent across multiple mapping sources. The castle sits at approximately 55.609°N, 1.710°W.

### 5. Approximate Distance
A circular walk combining the beach, dunes, foreshore, and return via the village / castle approach road is approximately 5-7 km depending on how far along the beach you walk. The beach itself stretches several kilometres north towards Budle Bay and south towards Warren Mill. A typical Bamburgh beach circuit takes 1.5-2 hours.

**Note:** There is no single defined waymarked circular route at Bamburgh - this is a free-form walk on open beach and public footpaths. Distance is variable and owner-defined, which is actually a selling point for dog walkers.

### 6. Difficulty
Easy. Flat sandy beach, gentle dune paths, village roads. The only elevation is the rise towards the castle or up into the dune system.

### 7. Terrain Type
Wide sandy beach (some sections with rock pools at low tide), sand dunes with marram grass, village pavement sections, castle approach paths. The beach is firm sand at low tide - easy going. At high tide the beach narrows significantly.

### 8. Off-Lead Status: FULL (with practical caveats)

Bamburgh Beach has no formal seasonal dog ban. Dogs are welcome year-round on the full beach with no date-specific restrictions. **Multiple sources confirm this for 2024-2025**, including The Beach Guide, Petiquette Collars, and Northumberland Dog Stays.

**Practical caveats:**
- Bamburgh Castle grounds: dogs welcome, but must be on short leads at all times within the castle grounds. Dogs not permitted inside the State Rooms, Clock Tower Cafe, Armstrong Museum, or any indoor areas. Assistance dogs only inside.
- Dune nesting areas: while there is no formal ban, dune sections may have informal signage during ground-nesting bird season (roughly May-July) requesting leads near tern or plover colonies. Respect any such signs.
- The beach is very popular in peak summer - de facto control of dogs required given visitor density.

### 9. Livestock Present
No formal livestock on the beach or dune system. However, some dune sections near Budle Bay to the north are grazed seasonally. For the immediate Bamburgh beach / castle area: no.

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- Bamburgh Beach: no seasonal dog ban - dogs permitted year-round, all sections (**verified across multiple 2024-2025 sources**)
- Castle grounds: dogs on leads, no access to indoor areas
- **Contrast with nearby Ross Back Sands**: that beach (4 km north) does have a dog ban May 1 - July 30 for seabird breeding season. Make sure your Bamburgh page does not inadvertently imply a ban at Bamburgh itself.
- Beach Guide entry for Bamburgh Castle Beach confirms: "Dogs allowed"

### 11. Parking Details
**Links Road Car Park**
- Postcode: NE69 7DF
- Surface level car park adjacent to the dunes
- Charges: approximately £6 for up to 3 hours (most recent figure available - verify current rates)
- **Source:** parkopedia.co.uk; zapmap.com

**Castle Car Park** (closer to castle grounds, lower capacity)
- Postcode: NE69 7DF area
- Charges: approximately £3 per day (older source - verify)
- Managed by Northumberland County Council

**Source confidence:** Parking charges at Bamburgh are variable across sources and may have increased. Verify with Northumberland County Council parking before publishing.

### 12. Toilets
Yes - seasonal facilities near the beach and at the castle grounds. Check winter opening.

### 13. Cafe or Pub
- **The Lord Crewe, Bamburgh** (Front Street, NE69 7BL) - confirmed dog-friendly. Dogs welcome in bar area and beer garden. Seafood focused menu. Dog-friendly accommodation available. Reviewed positively as "excellent dog-friendly pub." (**Source:** dogfriendly.co.uk; TripAdvisor reviews)
- **The Middle Inn, Bamburgh** (Front Street) - confirmed dog-friendly traditional pub with beer garden.
- **Castle Cafe / Clock Tower Cafe (within castle grounds)** - assistance dogs only inside; dogs permitted outside at grounds-level seating.

### 14. Public Transport Access
Limited. Arriva service X18 / 418 runs along the Northumberland coast linking Berwick-upon-Tweed, Bamburgh, Seahouses, and Alnwick, providing a connection to the East Coast Main Line at Alnmouth or Berwick. Not frequent - check current timetables. Bamburgh is not on the rail network. Car is the practical choice for most visitors.

### 15. Relevant Hazards for Dogs
- **Ground-nesting birds** - ringed plovers and Arctic terns nest in the dune system in spring and early summer. Even without a formal ban, dogs disturbing nesting terns risk dive-bombing. Keep dogs from running through marram grass dune zones May-July.
- **Ticks** - present in dune grassland year-round but elevated risk in spring and summer.
- **Cliff** - not applicable on the beach itself, but the castle headland has drops on the seaward side - keep dogs away from the castle walls' outer perimeter.

### 16. Key Features for Dogs
- Bamburgh is regularly voted one of the best dog-friendly beaches in England - vast open strand with space for off-lead running even in peak season
- Swimming sea access - the beach shelves gently and sea entry is safe in calm conditions; dogs regularly swim here
- Castle backdrop is arguably the most photogenic setting for a dog walk in Northumberland
- Year-round access with no seasonal ban means this is usable throughout the year

### 17. Honest Practical Note
The beach is genuinely excellent for dogs and the no-ban status is a real asset. However, summer weekends bring heavy visitor traffic and the dunes are ecologically sensitive. The best window for a relaxed off-lead run is early morning or late afternoon on a weekday, or any time September-April. Check tide times before you go - at high tide the beach narrows significantly and your walking space shrinks considerably.

3. Is Bamburgh Beach safe for dogs to swim?
4. Are there any dog-friendly pubs or cafes in Bamburgh village?

## WALK 3: Northumberland Coast Path - Alnmouth to Warkworth

### 1. Full Name
Northumberland Coast Path: Alnmouth to Warkworth

### 2. Location
Alnmouth to Warkworth, Northumberland (the route also passes through the Northumberland Coast Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty)

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
Alnmouth start: NE66 2RW (Alnmouth Beach Car Park, Lover's Walk / Marine Road area - operated by the Burgage Holders of Alnmouth Common Ltd).
Warkworth start: NE65 0SW (Warkworth Beach Car Park - free).

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
Midpoint (approximate): 55.390°N, 1.613°W - this is roughly the midpoint of the coastal path between the two villages. For a weather card, Alnmouth at 55.390°N, 1.613°W or Warkworth at 55.349°N, 1.607°W are both reasonable.

### 5. Approximate Distance
Approximately 10 km (6 miles) one-way, with 64 m total ascent. This is a linear route. Sources vary slightly: AllTrails lists it as 8.9 km / 5.5 miles; FabulousNorth says 10 km / 6 miles (confirmed by GPS route). Allow 2-2.5 hours one way.

To do this as a circular walk, you need two cars (one at each end) or must return the same way, making a 20 km / 12-mile round trip. Most walkers do it as a linear point-to-point. AllTrails confirms: linear trail.

### 6. Difficulty
Easy to Moderate. Mostly flat on coastal/dune/beach terrain with minimal ascent. The difficulty comes from tide-dependent sections and potential soft sand. Rated 1/5 on some platforms.

### 7. Terrain Type
Mixture of gravel coastal path, sandy beach (lower sections), dune grassland, and short sections of firm path. The walk passes Birling Carrs - rocky outcrops on the foreshore that are only navigable at low-to-mid tide. Route follows the England Coast Path / Northumberland Coast Path, both of which are waymarked.

**Estuary note:** The walk crosses the River Aln estuary near Alnmouth. At low tide this can be waded (or walked across the exposed sandbars), but the estuary is deceptively fast-flowing and deep even at low tide. Multiple sources strongly warn against this crossing. The safe route avoids the estuary mouth entirely by following the path up to the road bridge. Check tide tables and local signage before attempting any estuary section. The River Coquet at Warkworth is crossed by road bridge - no hazard.

### 8. Off-Lead Status: PARTIAL

- On the main beach and open coastal path sections away from livestock: dogs can be off lead under control
- Dune sections may have grazing livestock (seasonal): leads required
- Nesting bird season (March-August): keep dogs on leads or under very close control near dune grassland and beach margins where terns and ringed plovers may be nesting
- Both Alnmouth Beach and Warkworth Beach are confirmed dog-friendly year-round with no formal seasonal bans

**Overall verdict:** Partial. Good off-lead potential on open beach and path sections; lead required near dunes and any livestock. The Northumberland Coast Path guidance states dogs must be "kept on lead or at heel at all times to avoid disturbance to birds and grazing livestock" - this is advisory rather than a bye-law on most sections, but treat it as a lead-on-dunes / off-lead-on-open-beach rule.

### 9. Livestock Present
Seasonal - livestock (primarily sheep) graze the dune grassland and links land on some sections of the route. Most likely between Birling Carrs and the Aln estuary sections. This varies year to year depending on grazing management.

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- No formal seasonal dog ban on any section of the route between Alnmouth and Warkworth
- Warkworth Castle: dogs welcome on leads throughout the castle site. Dog bowl provided. Dogs not permitted at the Hermitage (a separate riverboat-accessed attraction from the castle)
- Note: Coquet Island lies offshore between the two villages and is a major seabird colony (Arctic terns, roseate terns, puffins). It is a RSPB reserve and access is restricted. Dogs on the mainland shore opposite should be under control.

### 11. Parking Details
**Alnmouth Beach Car Park**
- Location: Lover's Walk / Marine Road, Alnmouth
- Postcode: NE66 3NJ (beach approach; the common land car park is operated by Burgage Holders of Alnmouth Common Ltd)
- Charges: Approximately £2/day in shoulder season; £10 weekly pass; seasonal operation July-October with attendant. Free outside these periods. **Source:** burgageholders.co.uk (Alnmouth Parish Council)
- Note: Very limited spaces in peak season

**Warkworth Beach Car Park**
- Postcode: NE65 0SW
- Charges: Free
- Additional paid parking near Warkworth Castle at NE65 0UJ (approx £3.10)

### 12. Toilets
Seasonal at Alnmouth (near beach car park). Warkworth has public toilets in the village.

### 13. Cafe or Pub
- **Alnmouth end:** The Red Lion (Northumberland Street) - dog-friendly, good food, positive reviews. The Hope and Anchor - dog-friendly with fish and chip takeaway option. "Pretty much every pub in Alnmouth lets dogs in" is a consistent finding.
- **Warkworth end:** The Mason's Arms (Bridge Street, NE65 0XB) - highly recommended, "barrels of biscuits for dogs," beer garden with pizza shack, dog ice cream sold, water bowls provided. Bertrams Cafe also welcomes dogs.

### 14. Public Transport Access
Alnmouth has a railway station (Alnmouth for Alnwick) on the East Coast Main Line - excellent national rail access. The station is approximately 1.5 km from the beach car park start point. From Alnmouth station, bus links to the village.
Warkworth is served by limited bus services - Arriva X18/418 coastal bus.
The point-to-point nature of the walk lends itself well to train access at the Alnmouth end.

### 15. Relevant Hazards for Dogs
- **Ground-nesting birds** - Arctic terns, ringed plovers, and sandwich terns on dunes and foreshore. Peak risk April-August. Dive-bombing behaviour from terns in nesting areas is a real and startling hazard for both dogs and owners.
- **Ticks** - dune grassland and rough coastal vegetation. High risk spring-autumn.
- **Flooding / tidal** - not strictly "flooding" but the Aln estuary section and Birling Carrs are tide-dependent. Incorrect timing can trap walkers. This is a genuine practical hazard.

### 16. Key Features for Dogs
- Remote, wide sandy beach on the middle section between the two villages - genuinely wild and often deserted outside peak season
- Sea swimming access throughout - the route includes multiple access points to the sea
- Warkworth Castle arrival is a dramatic finish - dogs can explore the extensive castle grounds on lead
- The walk is arguably the most complete "beach dog walk with a destination" in Northumberland

### 17. Honest Practical Note
This walk is tide-dependent in two ways: the Birling Carrs foreshore section requires low-to-mid tide for direct passage, and any attempt to cross the Aln estuary directly is dangerous and should not be attempted. Check tide tables before setting out from Alnmouth - a standard free tide table site will serve. The linear nature of the route also means car logistics or a willingness to do 20 km return. The Mason's Arms at Warkworth is an outstanding dog-friendly pub and justifies finishing there rather than turning back.

2. Are dogs allowed at Warkworth Castle?
3. How do I cross the Aln estuary safely on the Alnmouth to Warkworth walk?
4. Are there seasonal dog restrictions on any beaches between Alnmouth and Warkworth?

## WALK 4: Kielder Forest - Duchess Trail and Lakeshore Circuit, Northumberland

### Recommendation on Which Walk to Feature

The best defined, most accessible dog walk at Kielder for a Sniffout page is the **Duchess Trail combined with a lakeshore section from Kielder Castle** - a circuit of approximately 6-7 km. The Duchess Trail (3 km / 2 miles out-and-back, or a loop) is specifically the "Forest Walkies" trail promoted by Forestry England, named the Duchess Trail after the second Duchess of Northumberland, who traversed the estate by horse and carriage. It is waymarked with green posts, starts from Kielder Castle car park, passes over the original packhorse bridge, and has no livestock. It can be extended along the lakeside path for additional distance.

The 27-mile full Kielder Lakeside Way is not suitable as a single walk page - too long and most walkers cover sections of it.

### 1. Full Name
Kielder Forest - Duchess Trail and Kielder Water Lakeshore Walk

### 2. Location
Kielder, Northumberland (Kielder Water and Forest Park)

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
NE48 1ER (Kielder Castle Visitor Centre car park, Castle Drive, Kielder)

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
55.234°N, 2.580°W (Kielder Castle / main car park). Cross-referenced against the Kielder Castle coordinates: 55.2338°N, 2.5802°W.

### 5. Approximate Distance
Duchess Trail alone: approximately 3 km (2 miles) - can be done as an out-and-back or loop.
Duchess Trail plus lakeshore extension: approximately 6-7 km (4 miles) as a circuit. This is the recommended walk for a Sniffout page - substantial enough to satisfy, manageable for most dogs and owners.

### 6. Difficulty
Easy. The Forestry England Duchess Trail is rated as a green-grade (easy) trail on forest tracks. The extended lakeshore section adds minimal extra difficulty. Good underfoot on forest tracks; some sections can be muddy in wet weather.

### 7. Terrain Type
Surfaced and unsurfaced forest tracks, historic packhorse bridge crossing, lakeside path. No technical terrain. Mostly within tree cover (conifer plantation with some broadleaf sections). The Lakeside Way along Kielder Water is a multi-access trail designed for walkers, cyclists, horse riders, and wheelchair users.

### 8. Off-Lead Status: PARTIAL (tending to FULL in most sections)

The Duchess Trail is confirmed as safe for off-lead walking "if your dog has good recall" - explicitly noted as having no livestock. (**Source:** spanielsbythesea.com, Forestry England trail guidance). The Lakeside Way extension also has no livestock on the primary trail.

**Lead required:** In livestock areas (some farm grazing near forest margins, particularly during bird nesting season March-July). Forestry England's general Kielder guidance states dogs should be under close control and on leads "in areas with livestock or during bird nesting season (typically March-July)."

**Practical verdict:** The Duchess Trail and lakeshore circuit is broadly off-lead friendly outside nesting season, provided the dog has reliable recall. This is one of the better off-lead walks in the research pack.

**Red squirrel note:** Kielder is one of England's last significant red squirrel strongholds. There are no specific dog restrictions related to red squirrel conservation - squirrels are tree-dwelling and dogs are not considered a primary threat. However, the "under close control" requirement in the forest is general guidance that also covers all wildlife including squirrels. No dog ban zones for squirrel conservation were found in any source.

### 9. Livestock Present
No livestock on the Duchess Trail itself. Some livestock may be present on peripheral farmland at the edge of the forest estate. Treat sections away from the defined trail with more caution.

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- Dogs in the forest generally: welcome; under close control; on leads in livestock and nesting season areas
- **Kielder Water swimming:** Dogs can swim in Kielder Water. No formal prohibition found. Multiple sources mention designated splash spots along the lakeshore. **Blue-green algae risk:** Kielder Water is a reservoir, and blue-green algae blooms are possible in warm summers (typical of UK reservoirs). No specific historic alerts for Kielder Water were found in the research, but this is a live risk. Forestry England and Northumbrian Water advise checking before allowing dogs to drink from or swim in the reservoir in warm weather. Add a standard algae caveat.
- Castle exhibition: closed for refurbishment until 2026 (at time of research); cafe and trails fully open

### 11. Parking Details
**Kielder Castle Visitor Centre Car Park**
- Postcode: NE48 1ER
- Charges: £2 for up to 1 hour, £5 all day (up to 3am to accommodate dark sky visitors). Coin and card payment.
- **Source:** visitkielder.com; parkingmanagementnetwork.co.uk

### 12. Toilets
Yes - at Kielder Castle Visitor Centre (when open).

### 13. Cafe or Pub
**Kielder Castle Cafe** - confirmed dog-friendly. Described as "very dog friendly with plenty of tables outside." Freshly prepared food, friendly staff. Outdoor seating with water bowls. Indoor dog access is uncertain - the outdoor tables are the confirmed dog-friendly area. (**Source:** dogfriendly.co.uk; TripAdvisor reviews 2025)

Note: The castle building itself has been closed for refurbishment. Verify cafe is fully open before publishing.

### 14. Public Transport Access
Very limited. Service 694 runs Kielder-Hexham on Tuesdays and Fridays only (departs Kielder 09:00, arrives Hexham 10:40; returns from Hexham 13:45). A Saturday-only service runs from Bellingham to Kielder. Hexham is on the Newcastle-Carlisle Tyne Valley Line. This is effectively a car-only destination for most visitors. Be honest about this on the page.

### 15. Relevant Hazards for Dogs
- **Mountain-bikers** - Kielder has over 100 miles of mountain bike trails, including red and black grade single-track. Shared paths between cyclists and dog walkers exist, particularly on the Lakeside Way. Keep dogs under control and be aware of fast-moving bikes especially on downhill sections near the castle hub.
- **Ticks** - high risk in conifer forest and bracken margins. Kielder is one of the highest-tick-density environments in the North East. Treat dogs before visiting and check after.
- **Deer** - roe deer and occasionally red deer within the forest. Dogs should not chase deer (illegal under Wildlife and Countryside Act if causing distress). This is a real behavioural risk in forest environments.
- **Algae** - blue-green algae possible in Kielder Water during warm spells. Check for any current Environment Agency warnings before dogs swim.

### 16. Key Features for Dogs
- Kielder is the largest man-made forest in England and one of the quietest, most remote dog-walking environments accessible from Newcastle and the wider North East
- The Duchess Trail is specifically designed as a "Forest Walkies" route - waymarked, livestock-free, and off-lead friendly with good recall
- Multiple designated swim spots along Kielder Water provide excellent water access for retrievers, spaniels, and water-loving dogs
- Red squirrel sighting is a genuine wildlife bonus - rare in England and a talking point for dog owners

### 17. Honest Practical Note
Kielder is genuinely remote - allow more travel time than the postcode suggests (approximately 1.5 hours from Newcastle, 1 hour from Hexham). Phone signal is poor throughout the forest - download the OS map before you leave. The mountain-bike culture at Kielder is significant; on weekends the trails around the castle hub are busy with cyclists who have right-of-way on the multi-use Lakeside Way. Keep dogs on leads at the car park / visitor centre hub and through any shared-use bike path sections.

4. Is Kielder too remote to visit without a car?

## WALK 5: Hamsterley Forest, County Durham

### Researcher's Recommendation

The Durham Peninsula circuit is not recommended as the primary choice for a Sniffout walk page. Here is the assessment:

**Durham Peninsula walk - assessment:**
- The riverside circuit is approximately 5 km and largely pleasant, with good views of the cathedral and castle.
- Dogs are genuinely welcome. Durham Cathedral now allows dogs inside on leads (short lead, trial period November 2024 - this may now be a permanent policy, but should be verified). The riverbank sections do have good off-lead potential once away from the road approaches.
- **However,** the walk is fundamentally an urban/tourist sightseeing route. The off-lead sections are limited - the riverbank paths are shared with the general public, cyclists, and other dogs in a confined corridor. Dogs must be on leads near the cathedral and castle. The wider appeal to Sniffout's core audience (people seeking off-lead rural/semi-rural dog walking) is weak. It is a 5 km walk with notable sections on leads.
- Durham Castle (University): dogs are not confirmed as permitted in the grounds for general visitors - it is a working university college.
- The riverside walk is also very well covered by generic city guide content online. Sniffout's differentiator is curated, dog-specific insight - and this walk doesn't offer enough distinctive dog content to justify a page.

**Recommendation: Hamsterley Forest** - Forestry England's 2,000-hectare forest in Weardale, County Durham, approximately 30 km south-west of Durham city. This is a substantially better walk for the Sniffout audience: genuinely off-lead capable, multiple route lengths, designated dog walking trail, dog-friendly cafe, and a rural experience with no urban constraints.

Hamsterley Forest features a dedicated "Forest Walkies" dog walk (the forest equivalent of the Duchess Trail at Kielder). The Three Becks Walk (7 km / 4.5 miles) is the best route for a Sniffout page.

### 1. Full Name
Hamsterley Forest - Three Becks Walk, County Durham

### 2. Location
Hamsterley / Bedburn, County Durham (Weardale edge, North Pennines National Landscape)

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
DL13 3NL (Hamsterley Forest Visitor Centre car park, Bedburn)

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
54.664°N, 1.899°W (Hamsterley Forest Visitor Centre / main car park). Confirmed at 54.663803°N, 1.899207°W.

### 5. Approximate Distance
Three Becks Walk: approximately 7 km (4.5 miles). This is the forest's own named orange-waymarked circular trail.
Shorter options: Riverside Walk (2.4 km / 1.5 miles, blue waymarks). Bedburn Valley Walk (4 km / 2.5 miles, yellow waymarks).

### 6. Difficulty
Moderate (Three Becks Walk). Some gentle climbs and descents through woodland. Terrain can be muddy after rain. Not technical. Blue Riverside Walk is Easy.

### 7. Terrain Type
Forest tracks, woodland paths, beck (stream) crossings, some open moorland edge sections on the Three Becks circuit. The forest covers 2,000 hectares of primarily conifer plantation with areas of broadleaf woodland, open rides, and beck valleys.

### 8. Off-Lead Status: PARTIAL (good off-lead opportunities)

Dogs must be kept on leads near car parks, toilets, visitor centre, cafe, picnic area, and between the two bridges where children's play equipment is located. Away from these zones, dogs are welcome off lead in other areas "but please keep them under control at all times." (**Source:** Forestry England / dogfriendlynortheast.co.uk)

**Practical verdict:** The majority of the Three Becks Walk is away from the restricted zones. Off-lead is genuinely available on most of the trail. Better off-lead access than either the Housesteads or Durham Peninsula options.

### 9. Livestock Present
No livestock on the main forest trails. Peripheral farmland may have livestock but the waymarked routes do not cross stock-grazed fields.

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- Dogs must be on leads in the visitor hub area (car park, cafe, play areas, bridged sections at the start of the trails)
- No livestock on main waymarked routes
- Standard "under control" requirement throughout

### 11. Parking Details
**Hamsterley Forest Visitor Centre Car Park**
- Postcode: DL13 3NL
- Charges: Free for under 20 minutes; up to 1 hour £3; up to 2 hours £5.50; up to 4 hours £7; all day £8. Bank holiday weekends £10/day.
- ANPR system with card/coin payment and RingGo app option (code 31094 for cars)
- **Source:** Forestry England ANPR FAQs; dogfriendlynortheast.co.uk (2022 pricing updated by Forestry England site)

**Note:** Parking charges have increased since 2022. Verify current rates at forestryengland.uk/hamsterley-forest before publishing.

### 12. Toilets
Yes - at the visitor centre.

### 13. Cafe or Pub
**Hamsterley Forest Visitor Centre Cafe** - open daily for sit-in and takeaway. Dog-friendly outdoor seating with water bowls. "Doggy Pupcakes available to takeaway" - a genuine dog-audience detail. Dogs should be on leads in the immediate cafe area. (**Source:** dogfriendlynortheast.co.uk 2022; Forestry England; TripAdvisor)

No dog-friendly pubs in the immediate vicinity - Hamsterley village has limited facilities. The nearest settlements with pubs are Witton-le-Wear or Wolsingham (both require a short drive).

### 14. Public Transport Access
Poor. No direct bus to the forest. Nearest bus route is at Wolsingham (approx 5 miles north on the A689), served by the 101 service connecting Bishop Auckland and Stanhope. Bishop Auckland has a rail station connecting to Darlington and the East Coast Main Line. Effectively a car-only destination. Be honest on the page.

### 15. Relevant Hazards for Dogs
- **Ticks** - high risk in conifer forest and bracken. North Pennines is tick-endemic territory. Essential to treat dogs before and check after every visit.
- **Mountain-bikers** - Hamsterley has dedicated mountain bike trails. The Three Becks Walk and most dog-walking routes are on shared forest roads / tracks where bikes also travel. Forestry England specifically notes the hazard of dog walkers encountering bikers in the forest and advises keeping dogs under control on multi-use tracks.
- **Deer** - roe deer present throughout the forest. Dogs with prey drive should be kept under close control.

### 16. Key Features for Dogs
- Three named becks (streams) crossed on the main circuit provide multiple natural water access points - popular with dogs in warm weather
- The forest is enormous (4,900 acres) and much quieter than coastal equivalents - excellent for dogs that need space away from crowds
- Dedicated "Forest Walkies" trail design means the route is genuinely built around dog-walker needs, not retrofitted from a general walking route
- Off-lead access on the majority of the Three Becks circuit once clear of the visitor hub

### 17. Honest Practical Note
Hamsterley is remote by North East standards and takes longer to reach from the coast than a map suggests. Poor phone signal throughout - download the Forestry England Hamsterley map before leaving home. The Three Becks Walk involves real ascent and woodland scrambling - not suitable as a first walk for a very young or very elderly dog. Bring extra leads for the hub area. The Forestry England car parking ANPR system can catch people out who haven't paid on exit - the 48-hour online payment window is useful but you need to remember to use it.

## WALK 6: Plessey Woods Country Park, Northumberland

### Researcher's Recommendation and Assessment

**Holywell Dene** (Option A): The dene is attractive and managed jointly by Northumberland County Council and North Tyneside Council as a Local Nature Reserve. The National Trust routes nearby associate with Seaton Delaval Hall. The main circular walk incorporating the dene, Old Hartley, and Seaton Sluice beach is approximately 8 km / 5 miles. It is dog-friendly but dogs must be on leads throughout (National Trust guidance explicitly states this). The stream (Seaton Burn) can be accessed for paddling. Free parking (very limited - approx 5 cars in the main spot). The walk is pleasant but limited: leads-only, limited parking, no facilities within the dene itself (the nearest cafe is the Harbour View fish and chip shop at Seaton Sluice). There is no visitor infrastructure and the route is not definitively waymarked as a standalone experience.

**Plessey Woods** (Option B): Managed by Northumberland County Council. 100+ acres of riverside woodland and meadow along the River Blyth. Visitor centre with cafe (currently closed for redevelopment - verify reopening date before publishing). ANPR car park with charges. The main circuit is approximately 2.7 km / 1.7 miles (the core loop). Dog swimming in the River Blyth is possible and common. Off-lead access available in most of the park. Better infrastructure, better facilities once cafe reopens.

**Recommendation: Plessey Woods**. It is the better choice for a Sniffout page for the following reasons:
1. More defined walk infrastructure (signposted, managed country park)
2. River swimming access - a strong dog-specific selling point
3. Off-lead permitted in most areas (not leads-only like Holywell Dene)
4. Better parking with proper facilities
5. More content to write about for a walk page

**Caveat:** The 2.7 km core circuit is short. The page should acknowledge this and suggest extension options. The cafe is currently closed for redevelopment - this must be flagged.

**Alternative considered:** If neither is considered substantial enough, Allen Banks and Staward Gorge (National Trust, near Haydon Bridge, Northumberland) is a superior alternative - a longer, more dramatic wooded gorge walk with river access and off-lead options. However, the brief asks to assess Plessey Woods and Holywell Dene, so Plessey Woods is the recommendation within the given options.

### 1. Full Name
Plessey Woods Country Park, Northumberland

### 2. Location
Plessey / Hartford Bridge, near Bedlington, Northumberland

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
NE22 6HZ (Plessey Woods Country Park main car park, Shields Road)

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
55.105°N, 1.627°W (Plessey Woods Country Park visitor centre / car park area). Cross-referenced from multiple mapping sources: 55.104906°N, 1.626981°W.

### 5. Approximate Distance
Main circular loop: approximately 2.7 km (1.7 miles). This is short for a dedicated walk page. The park offers additional connecting trails and riverside extensions that could bring the total to 5-6 km if all options are combined. Signage within the park indicates multiple route options.

### 6. Difficulty
Easy. Gentle woodland paths with some moderate slopes near the riverbank. Can be very muddy in winter and spring - a consistent theme in visitor reviews.

### 7. Terrain Type
Deciduous and mixed woodland paths, riverside trails along the River Blyth, some open meadow sections. Main paths are compacted earth / gravel with some exposed roots and slopes near the river. "Wonderful riverside with a river perfect for swimming" is a repeated theme.

### 8. Off-Lead Status: PARTIAL (good overall)

Dogs can be off lead in most areas of the park. Keep dogs on leads near the play area, visitor centre, and the River Blyth itself (currents can vary, particularly after heavy rain). The park is described as excellent for off-lead walking in woodland and meadow sections. No formal dog bans in any zones. (**Source:** dogwalksnearme.co.uk; Northumberland County Council park guidance; multiple visitor reviews)

**Overall:** Predominantly off-lead capable in woodland. More restricted near visitor facilities and river edge for safety.

### 9. Livestock Present
No formal livestock in the park. The park boundary adjoins some agricultural land and occasional sheep may be visible beyond fences, but the trails themselves are livestock-free.

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- No formal seasonal dog bans
- No dog-free zones within the park (beyond the standard keep-under-control-near-others guidance)
- River Blyth: dogs swim here regularly but currents can be unpredictable after heavy rain - the NCC guidance notes this and advises caution

### 11. Parking Details
**Plessey Woods Country Park Car Park**
- Postcode: NE22 6HZ
- Charges: First hour free; up to 2 hours £2.50; over 2 hours £4.50; annual permit £55 (July-June); Blue Badge holders free
- ANPR system
- **Source:** Northumberland County Council parks page (northumberland.gov.uk)

### 12. Toilets
Yes - at the visitor centre building (currently closed for redevelopment). Confirm availability during redevelopment before publishing.

### 13. Cafe or Pub
**Plessey Woods Visitor Centre Cafe** - confirmed dog-friendly with outdoor seating. Currently CLOSED for redevelopment with no confirmed re-opening date at time of research. **This must be flagged on the page.** (**Source:** NCC parks page)

Nearest alternative: The Plessey Arms, Hartford Bridge - a short walk from the park. Also nearby in Cramlington or Bedlington for broader cafe/pub options. No specific dog-friendly status confirmed for the Plessey Arms - research required.

**Confidence note:** The cafe closure is the biggest practical issue for this walk page. The park is excellent but recommending it to someone who drives there expecting post-walk coffee and finds a closed building is a poor user experience. The page should clearly state the cafe is closed and suggest alternatives until a reopening date is confirmed.

### 14. Public Transport Access
Moderate. Bedlington and Cramlington are both accessible by bus from Newcastle (Arriva / Go North East services via the A192 corridor). Hartford Bridge / the park entrance is approximately 1-1.5 km from bus stops on the A192. Not a direct bus-to-gate connection but achievable for determined non-drivers.

### 15. Relevant Hazards for Dogs
- **Ticks** - deciduous woodland and meadow: moderate-high risk spring-autumn
- **Flooding** - the River Blyth is prone to flooding after heavy rainfall; the riverside path sections can flood and become inaccessible. The park's low-lying sections near Hartford Bridge flood regularly in winter. Check before visiting after sustained rain.

### 16. Key Features for Dogs
- The River Blyth provides natural dog swimming and paddling - a genuine highlight for water-loving dogs, and one of the closest accessible river swims to Newcastle
- Autumn is spectacular here - ancient oak and ash woodland with exceptional colour October-November
- The park's 100 acres feels bigger than it is due to the woodland density - dogs get genuine variety of scent and terrain
- Proximity to Newcastle (approximately 20 minutes from the city centre) makes this one of the best accessible off-lead woodland dog walks near Tyneside

### 17. Honest Practical Note
The cafe closure is the main current issue. The core circular route is short at under 3 km - manage expectations on the page by making clear this is a shorter woodland walk rather than a half-day trail. The park floods regularly in winter near the riverside sections. Plessey Woods is at its best in late summer through autumn; it can be very muddy and some paths impassable after sustained rain in winter. The first hour of parking is free, making it excellent value for a 1-1.5 hour visit.

## RESEARCHER'S FLAGS AND UNCERTAINTY NOTES

1. **Bamburgh Beach dog ban**: Multiple independent 2024-2025 sources consistently confirm no seasonal ban at Bamburgh Beach. However, Northumberland County Council should be the authoritative source and their website should be checked directly before publishing. The nearby Ross Back Sands restriction must not be confused with Bamburgh.

2. **Durham Cathedral dog policy**: The trial period (November 2024 - March 2025) allowing dogs inside the nave may have become permanent. Check durhamcathedral.co.uk for the current October 2024 policy document (which has been published as a PDF). The inside-the-cathedral access is less relevant to dog walkers than the riverside path, but worth confirming if mentioning.

3. **Parking charges**: All parking charges in this pack are based on research from 2024-2025 sources but should be verified before publication. Northumberland National Park, Forestry England, and Northumberland County Council all operate ANPR systems where charges may be updated.

4. **Plessey Woods cafe**: Confirmed closed for redevelopment. No reopening date confirmed. Must be flagged on the walk page with a date-stamp note.

5. **Kielder Castle refurbishment**: The castle exhibition was closed for refurbishment. The cafe should be confirmed as open before publishing.

6. **AD122 bus service (Hadrian's Wall)**: This seasonal bus has varied in recent years. Verify current operational status and 2026 timetable with Northumberland County Council or Hadrian's Wall Country before publishing public transport details.

7. **The Sill at Hadrian's Wall**: Multiple TripAdvisor reviews and sources confirm dogs are not permitted inside the building or the indoor cafe. Outdoor seating only. Do not describe The Sill as dog-friendly on the walk page.

8. **Sycamore Gap**: The famous tree was felled by vandalism in September 2023. The stump remains. This should be mentioned on the Housesteads walk page as it affects visitor expectations.

9. **Coordinates**: All coordinates are derived from verified mapping sources (Wikipedia infoboxes, OS grid references, mapping tools) and are consistent to within 0.002 degrees. They are suitable for weather card use.

10. **Holywell Dene leads requirement**: The National Trust walk guidance consistently states dogs must be on leads throughout. If this walk were ever reconsidered for a Sniffout page, the on-lead status would need prominent flagging - the audience tends to prefer off-lead options.
