## Key Facts for the Writer (Top 12)

1. **38% of UK vets have treated a dog for heatstroke after a walk - versus 9% from hot cars.** Walks kill more dogs than cars in summer. Source: BVA survey 2022.

2. **68% of exercise-triggered heatstroke in UK dogs is caused by walking, not running.** The dog does not need to be doing anything strenuous. A normal lead walk on a warm day is enough. Source: VetCompass PMC7459873.

3. **UK summer 2025 was the warmest on record (mean 16.10C), beating 2018 by 0.34C.** A summer as warm or warmer is now 70 times more likely than in a pre-industrial climate. The number of days above 25C has risen from 7 per year (1961-1990) to 11 per year (1991-2020) - a 57% increase. Source: Met Office 2026.

4. **Artificial grass is the hottest surface a dog can walk on** - hotter than asphalt in all trials. Ranking hottest to coolest: artificial grass, asphalt/tarmac, concrete, sand, grass in sun, shaded grass. Shaded natural grass is the safest surface. Source: Vets Now UK university experiment.

5. **Pavements cool significantly by late evening but not by 7pm.** Pavement at 10pm is approximately 30C, versus a peak of approximately 50C at 1-2pm. Air temperature peaks between 2pm and 4:30pm. The "before 8am, after 7pm" rule is a practical guideline from UK vets, consistent with the thermal data.

6. **Gum colour is the fastest visible emergency signal.** Pink to red: act now, move to shade. Pale, white, or grey: the dog is in shock - this is more serious than bright red, not less. Blue or purple: critical emergency, call vet immediately. Normal is salmon pink.

7. **Cool first, then transport.** Cooling before arrival at the vet improves survival from approximately 50% to approximately 80%. The RVC protocol: pour cool tap water (approximately 15C, not ice) over the whole body, create air movement, small sips of water, call the vet. Do NOT wrap in a wet towel - this traps heat.

8. **Heatstroke can develop within minutes.** A dog can go from "seems warm" to collapse in under 15 minutes in extreme conditions. Post-exercise, core temperature can continue rising for several minutes after the dog stops - about a quarter of heatstroke cases showed peak temperature after exercise ended.

9. **Double-coated breeds (Huskies, Samoyeds, Berners) are NOT heat-tolerant.** The double coat insulates in both directions. High-drive dogs (Huskies especially) will not self-limit in heat. The Siberian Husky Welfare Association UK recommends their temperature x humidity formula: if air temperature (C) x relative humidity (%) approaches 1,000, exercise should be stopped.

10. **UK heatwave threshold is 3 consecutive days above the regional limit.** London: 28C. Much of central and southern England: 27C. Wales and northern England: 26C. Scotland: 25C. First updated to reflect UK warming in March 2022.

11. **On 19 July 2022, the UK recorded 40.3C for the first time ever** - at Coningsby, Lincolnshire. Six stations exceeded 40C that day. The chance of a 40C+ day in the UK is now estimated at 4% per year - from under 1% in the 1960s.

12. **The 7-second test is a US-developed practical heuristic, not a clinical study finding.** It was developed by US veterinarians and adopted by UK vets. The principle is sound (if it burns your hand it burns dog paws), but it is not derived from a controlled burn time study. Tom should mention it as a "quick field check" rather than citing it as research.

## 1. Temperature Chart by Breed Type

This is the centrepiece of the article. All ranges are for walking exercise in UK conditions unless noted.

### Average/typical breeds
Labrador, Cocker Spaniel, Border Collie, Beagle, Boxer, Staffordshire Bull Terrier

| Zone | Temperature | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| Safe | Below 20C | No restriction for healthy adults |
| Caution | 20-25C | Shorten walk, morning/evening only, water essential |
| Danger | 25-28C | Very short necessity walk only |
| Avoid | 28C+ | Do not walk |

Source: Scottish SPCA, Agria Pet Insurance, Guide Dogs UK, VetCompass (all confirmed in scoring-validation-april-3.md)

---

### Brachycephalic breeds
French Bulldog, Pug, Bulldog, Shih Tzu, Cavalier King Charles Spaniel, Chow Chow, Boston Terrier, Dogue de Bordeaux

Adjusted down approximately 4-5C from average dogs. Justification: Bulldog 14x HRI risk vs Labrador; French Bulldog 6x; Pug 3x; Chow Chow 17x (highest in VetCompass data). Bulldog recorded overheating at 21C air temperature while standing still (PMC7459873).

| Zone | Temperature | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| Safe | Below 15-16C | Short walks acceptable |
| Caution | 15-20C | Very short walks, early morning only, monitor breathing closely |
| Danger | 20-22C | Necessity trips only, maximum 10 minutes |
| Avoid | 22C+ | Stay indoors; access to cool room or air conditioning |

Sources: VetCompass PMC7303136 (odds ratios), PMC7459873 (Bulldog at 21C), Pedigree UK, scoring-validation-april-3.md

Note for Tom: The specific 4C offset is a modelled estimate based on the evidence, not a published single threshold. State as "veterinary evidence suggests flat-faced dogs begin facing risk at temperatures around 4-5 degrees lower" rather than citing as a named study threshold.

---

### Double-coated and thick-coated breeds
Siberian Husky, Alaskan Malamute, Samoyed, Bernese Mountain Dog, Golden Retriever, German Shepherd, Akita

IMPORTANT MYTH TO BUST: The double coat does NOT provide heat protection. It insulates in both directions. These breeds can overheat at the same temperatures as average dogs - or sooner. High-drive breeds (Huskies, working dogs) will not self-limit.

| Zone | Temperature | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| Safe | Below 18-20C | Normal walks, water available |
| Caution | 20-25C | Shorten walk significantly, shade essential, water every 10 minutes |
| Danger | 25C+ | Serious risk; as dangerous as average breeds; avoid exercise |

Siberian Husky Welfare Association UK formula: temperature (C) x relative humidity (%) should stay well below 1,000. At 25C and 75% humidity = 1,875 (danger). At 20C and 60% humidity = 1,200 (elevated). At 18C and 50% humidity = 900 (near threshold).

Bernese Mountain Dog: black triple coat absorbs heat directly from sun. Above 25C requires extreme caution.

St Bernard: partially brachycephalic anatomy - treat similarly to brachycephalic animals, danger from 20-22C.

Sources: Siberian Husky Welfare Association UK (siberianhuskywelfare.co.uk); zoeta-dogsoul.com (Bernese Mountain Dog); AKC (Samoyed coat insulation)

Note for Tom: No UK veterinary school publishes a named Celsius threshold specifically for "double-coated breeds as a category." Use the Husky Welfare formula for Huskies; state the Bernese/St Bernard information as breed-specific.

---

### Small breeds
Chihuahua, Yorkshire Terrier, Miniature Dachshund, Pomeranian, Maltese, Toy Poodle

Small breeds do not face meaningfully different heat thresholds from average dogs but are more vulnerable to pavement burn (lower height = more radiated heat from surface) and to overheating faster in a small body mass.

| Zone | Temperature | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| Safe | Below 20C | Normal walks |
| Caution | 20-25C | Shorter walks; choose grass over tarmac; carry water |
| Danger | 25C+ | Small dogs heat up faster per unit time; very serious |

Key practical point: Small dogs walk much closer to the pavement surface. Air temperature at paw height on a hot day is meaningfully higher than at head height - a 5-8C difference in some measurements. The pavement burn risk is more acute for small dogs than any other group.

---

### Giant breeds
Great Dane, St Bernard, Newfoundland, Mastiff, Irish Wolfhound, Leonberger

Mechanism: larger body mass generates more metabolic heat during exercise and has a less favourable surface-area-to-volume ratio for heat dissipation.

| Zone | Temperature | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| Safe | Below 18-20C | Normal walks of appropriate length |
| Caution | 20-25C | Shorten significantly; watch for signs more carefully than with average breeds |
| Danger | 25C+ | Serious risk; avoid exercise |

St Bernards: partially brachycephalic anatomy - apply brachycephalic caution levels, danger from 20-22C.

Note for Tom: No published UK vet document names a Celsius threshold specifically for "giant breeds as a category." Explain the body-mass surface-area mechanism, note the St Bernard exception, and recommend owners treat giant breeds with at least as much caution as average breeds.

Sources: VetCompass PMC7303136 (dogs over 50kg OR 3.42 for HRI); Frontiers in Animal Science 2025.

---

### Elderly dogs (approximately 8 years and above)

VetCompass UK data (PMC9144152): dogs 12 years+ have 8.87 times greater odds of fatal heatstroke outcome vs healthy adults. Mechanism: reduced thermoregulatory capacity, reduced peripheral circulation, higher likelihood of underlying cardiac or respiratory conditions.

Note on "senior" definition: for giant breeds such as Great Danes, senior status begins around 5 years. For small breeds like Chihuahuas, typically from age 8.

| Zone | Temperature | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| Safe | Below 18-20C | Short, calm walks |
| Caution | 18-22C | Very short walks only; watch for any early signs |
| Danger | 22C+ | Avoid; garden or indoor activity only |

Sources: VetCompass PMC9144152 (OR 8.87 for fatal HRI in 12+ year dogs).

---

### Puppies (under 12 months)

Puppies cannot regulate body temperature as effectively as adult dogs. They are high-energy and will not self-limit.

| Zone | Temperature | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| Safe | Below 18-20C | Follow five-minutes-per-month-of-age rule for walk length |
| Caution | 18-22C | Very short, slow walks in shade; water available |
| Danger | 22C+ | Avoid exercise; garden only in cooler hours |

Note for Tom: No UK-published temperature threshold specifically for puppies as a standalone figure. They are listed by PDSA, RSPCA, and Blue Cross as a high-caution group alongside elderly, brachycephalic, and obese dogs.

---

### Overweight dogs

VetCompass UK data (PMC9144152): overweight dogs have approximately 1.5 times greater odds of HRI vs healthy-weight dogs. Adipose tissue insulates and traps heat; greater cardiovascular strain during exercise.

No published UK temperature threshold specifically for overweight dogs as a standalone figure. All major UK charities list them in the same high-caution category as elderly dogs, puppies, and brachycephalic breeds.

Guidance for the article: treat overweight dogs with the same caution as elderly dogs.

Sources: VetCompass PMC9144152 (1.5x OR); Frontiers in Animal Science 2025.

## 2. Signs of Heatstroke

### Early warning signs - act now

Move to shade immediately if you see any of these. These are signs the dog is struggling, before the situation becomes a medical emergency.

- Heavy panting significantly beyond their normal level
- Excessive drooling - thicker and stickier than usual
- Restlessness, agitation, pacing
- Actively seeking shade or lying down and refusing to continue
- Gums pinker or redder than their usual salmon pink
- Slowing pace, reluctance to walk
- Muscle weakness or slight wobble
- Drinking more water than usual

**Breed-specific notes for the article:**
- In brachycephalic dogs: normal noisy breathing masks early escalation. Watch for any change in breathing pattern or intensity above their individual baseline.
- In high-drive working breeds and Huskies: they will not self-limit. The absence of visible distress does not mean they are safe.
- In double-coated breeds: heavy panting is visible at the mouth but the dog's core may already be significantly overheated before it shows through the coat.

---

### Emergency signs - rush to vet

Call the vet immediately and begin first aid simultaneously if you see any of these.

- Bright red gums or tongue
- Pale, white, or grey gums (blood pressure drop, shock - more serious than bright red, not less)
- Blue, purple, or grey-blue gums (severe oxygen deficit - critical emergency)
- Very rapid heart rate
- Vomiting, particularly bloody vomiting
- Bloody diarrhoea
- Disorientation, confusion, glazed expression
- Staggering or inability to stand
- Seizures
- Collapse or loss of consciousness

**Gum colour guide (one of the most actionable things Tom can give readers):**
- Salmon/pink (moist): normal
- Pale pink (dry): mild dehydration
- Bright red: heatstroke in progress - act immediately
- White or pale: shock - life-threatening
- Blue/purple: critical oxygen deficit - emergency

**Body temperature (for owners with a thermometer):**
- Normal: 38-39C
- Above 40C: danger threshold (PDSA definition of heatstroke risk)
- Above 40.5C: emergency even if dog appears stable - go to vet
- Above 41.2C: multiple organ failure risk
- Above 43C: severe; high mortality risk

Sources: RVC heatstroke fact file; PDSA heatstroke guide; VCA Animal Hospitals; VetCompass PMC9144152; HotDogs research (heatstroke.dog)

---

### How fast does heatstroke develop?

- Core temperature can rise to dangerous levels within 15-30 minutes of exercise in heat
- In extreme conditions (very hot weather, brachycephalic dogs), collapse can occur in as little as 15 minutes
- **Critical point for the article:** core temperature can continue rising AFTER the dog stops exercising, as metabolic heat redistributes from muscles through the bloodstream. Approximately one quarter of UK heatstroke cases showed peak temperature recorded after exercise ended. A dog that stops and seems to be recovering may still be getting worse internally.
- VetCompass data: only 21.7% of UK dogs presenting with heatstroke had been cooled by owners before arrival at the vet. Most owners wait too long or do not act at all.

Sources: HotDogs research (heatstroke.dog); RVC "Cool First, Transport Second" guidance; VetCompass PMC data.

---

### First aid on a walk - step by step

**RVC protocol: "Cool First, Transport Second"**

Do not rush the dog to the car and drive. Cooling before arrival at the vet improves survival from approximately 50% to approximately 80%.

**Steps:**

1. Stop all activity immediately.
2. Move to shade and a well-ventilated area.
3. Pour cool tap water (approximately 15C - cold but not iced) over the whole body. Start at the paws, move to the neck, armpits, groin, and belly. Soak the coat thoroughly.
4. Create air movement: fan, open car doors/windows, position near a breeze. Evaporative cooling requires air movement - water alone is not enough.
5. Offer small sips of cool water. Do not force water into a panting dog's mouth; do not let them gulp while panting heavily.
6. Call the vet immediately, even if the dog appears to be recovering.
7. Transport in an air-conditioned vehicle; keep air moving during travel.

**Do NOT:**
- Place wet towels over the dog (RSPCA warns this traps heat). A wet towel under the dog is acceptable; over the dog is not.
- Use ice or ice-packed cold water (RVC guidance: risk of vasoconstriction at skin surface, reducing heat dissipation - use cool tap water).
- Force the dog to continue walking.
- Cuddle the dog (adds body heat).
- Apply alcohol.
- Leave in the car at any point.

**Survival rates (UK data):**
- Mild to moderate HRI treated promptly: approximately 95% survival
- Severe HRI presenting to emergency care: over 50% died
- Cooling before vet arrival: survival improves from approximately 50% to approximately 80%

Sources: RVC "Cool First, Transport Second" (rvc.ac.uk); RVC heatstroke fact file; HotDogs (heatstroke.dog); Full Fact (fullfact.org/online/overheating-dog-advice-water); RSPCA

## 3. Walk Timing

### Safe walking windows in UK summer

UK sunrise and sunset times (London, 2026, timeanddate.com):

| Month | Sunrise | Sunset |
|---|---|---|
| June | ~4:50am | ~9:20pm |
| July (early) | ~4:50am | ~9:20pm |
| July (late) | ~5:25am | ~8:50pm |
| August (early) | ~5:25am | ~8:50pm |
| August (late) | ~6:15am | ~7:50pm |

Edinburgh in June: sunrise approximately 4:26am, sunset approximately 10:03pm - nearly a full hour more daylight than London. The "before 8am" morning window is practical across all UK regions.

**Peak air temperature:** 2pm to 4:30pm is the typical peak in UK summer. Solar noon is around 1pm but air temperature lags solar input.

**The "before 8am, after 7pm" rule:** Cited by Vets Now and PDSA. A practical guideline consistent with pavement thermal data. Not derived from a named research paper - Tom should describe it as "vet-recommended guidance," not "research-based timing."

---

### Pavement cooling through the day

Pavement absorbs heat through the day and cools gradually after sunset:

- At 6am: approximately 24-25C (near overnight baseline)
- At 1-2pm (peak): approximately 50C+ in full sun when air is 25C
- At 10pm: approximately 30C (significantly cooled but still above air temperature)

The practical implication: pavements at 7pm are still materially hotter than air temperature. On very hot days, the 8pm guideline is safer than 7pm. Early morning is the coolest and safest time.

Sources: Civil engineering pavement thermal research; Vets Now university experiment data (2017).

---

### Shade - how much difference does it make?

Shade provides meaningful protection, primarily to surfaces and the dog's direct heat load:

- Concrete in full sun: approximately 57C. Same concrete in shade: approximately 27C. Difference: approximately 30C.
- Grass in full sun: approximately 35C. Shaded grass: approximately 26C.
- Woodland and grass surfaces: predicted maximum approximately 18C versus exposed non-transpiring city surfaces approximately 43C.

Air temperature is the same in sun and shade. The cooling benefit is to ground surfaces (paw safety), direct solar radiation on the dog's coat, and perceived heat load. A shaded woodland path on a 25C day is meaningfully safer than an exposed tarmac path.

PDSA specifically recommends "natural paths or the shaded side of the street" on hot days.

Sources: Trees and local temperature research (climate-woodlands.extension.org); surface temperature comparison (whas11.com).

## 4. Cooling Strategies

### What to carry

UK vet consensus (PDSA, RSPCA, Blue Cross):
- Travel water bowl and ample fresh water. Offer small amounts every 15-20 minutes.
- Know the route: confirm shade availability before setting out on hot days.
- Cooling coat (evaporative - wetted with water). Published clinical evidence confirms cooling coats reduce mean core body temperature by approximately 0.62C during exercise (PMC9494994, military working dog trial). Limitation: less effective in high humidity, which can occur in UK summer heatwaves.

### Mid-walk cooling - body areas

If dog shows early warning signs, cool these areas:
1. Paws (fast field access)
2. Neck and throat
3. Armpits
4. Groin (major blood vessels close to surface)
5. Belly and chest (less dense fur, direct contact)
6. Whole body by continuous pouring

**On wet coats:** Pour cool water freely over the whole body combined with air movement. The concern about "trapping heat in a wet coat" applies to static wet towels placed over the dog - not to active pouring with a breeze or fanning. Soaking the coat plus air movement accelerates evaporative cooling.

### Ice water - the nuance

**RVC official position:** Avoid ice-cold water - risk of vasoconstriction at skin surface, which reduces heat dissipation. Use cool tap water approximately 15C.

**Emerging UK evidence (The Balanced Canine UK, citing canicross field studies):** Cold water immersion is safe and faster than tepid water. Argues the greater danger is under-cooling, not over-cooling.

**Recommendation for the article:** Use cool tap water approximately 15C. This is the RVC-endorsed position. The critical message is that getting the dog wet and creating air movement is the priority - the exact water temperature matters less than acting at all.

Source: Full Fact (fullfact.org); The Balanced Canine UK; RVC heatstroke fact file.

### When to call the vet (even if dog seems okay)

Call the vet immediately after any heatstroke episode:
- Any seizure or collapse, even brief
- Vomiting or bloody diarrhoea
- Blue, pale, or grey gums at any point
- Any confusion or disorientation
- Brachycephalic, elderly, obese, or puppy dogs - higher risk of silent internal deterioration
- Any dog that has not clearly improved after 10 minutes of active cooling

Rationale: serious organ damage (kidney failure, clotting disorders, brain damage) can develop hours after the acute episode even in dogs that appear to recover fully.

## 5. Surface Temperature Comparison

Ranking from hottest to coolest in direct sun, approximately when air temperature is 25C:

| Surface | Approx temp in sun | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Artificial grass | 60C+ at peak | Hottest in all Vets Now UK trials. Plastic does not breathe. |
| Asphalt/tarmac | ~52C | Dark surface; absorbs 95% of solar energy. Confirmed 25C air = 52C surface (Vets Now). |
| Concrete/paving slabs | ~44-48C | Lighter colour than asphalt; still reaches burn threshold |
| Sand (beach, dry) | 43-54C | Light colour does not protect - still burns |
| Grass in full sun | ~35C | Significantly lower than hard surfaces |
| Gravel (pale, dry) | No specific figure - treat as concrete | Darker gravel approaches asphalt temperatures |
| Bark/wood chip | Cooler than concrete - no specific figure | Wood less thermally conductive |
| Shaded grass | ~26C | Safest surface for summer walks |
| Shaded concrete | ~27C | Dramatically cooler than same surface in sun |

**Key message for the article:** Grass and shade together create the safest summer walking environment. Artificial grass, tarmac, and concrete in full sun all reach burn-threshold temperatures (52C+) when air is 25C. The safest surface is shaded natural grass.

**Artificial grass warning (UK-specific):** Vets Now UK article specifically names artificial grass as the hottest surface in their university experiment - hotter than tarmac. Garden owners with artificial turf should be aware.

Sources: Vets Now UK 2017; whas11.com surface comparison; site.extension.uga.edu/climate; stablehandspcs.com (sand); trees-and-local-temperature research.

## 6. UK Summer Climate Context

### How often does the UK exceed walking danger thresholds?

**Days above 25C per year (Central England, HadCET data):**
- 1961-1990 average: approximately 7 days per year
- 1991-2020 average: approximately 11 days per year - a 57% increase in dangerous dog-walking days over 30 years

**Record years:**
- 1976 and 1995: 33 days above 25C each
- 2018 and 2022: 31 days each

**Regional July average maximum temperatures:**
- London and South East: approximately 24C
- Birmingham: approximately 22C
- Manchester: approximately 20.5C
- Newcastle: approximately 19C
- Scotland (far north): can be 10-15C cooler than south east England in summer

The practical implication: the 20C caution threshold is regularly exceeded across England from June to August, and sometimes in Scotland and the north. This is an annual, live risk for UK dog owners.

Sources: Met Office; Carbon Brief analysis of UK Summer 2022; currentresults.com UK temperature data.

---

### UK heatwave definition

A UK heatwave is declared when a location records at least **3 consecutive days** meeting or exceeding the regional maximum temperature threshold:

| Region | Threshold |
|---|---|
| London, SE England, East Midlands (including Herts, Surrey, Berks, Bucks, Beds, Cambs) | 28C |
| Most of central and southern England | 27C |
| Wales and much of northern England | 26C |
| Scotland and Northern Ireland | 25C |

These thresholds were updated in March 2022 to reflect the new 1991-2020 baseline, raised 1C in many areas from the previous 1981-2010 baseline - meaning heatwaves are being defined against a hotter norm.

Source: Met Office heatwave threshold update, March 2022.

---

### Is the UK getting hotter?

Yes - confirmed by Met Office data:

- **Summer 2025 was the UK's warmest on record:** mean 16.10C, beating 2018 by 0.34C. Also the UK's sunniest year on record.
- **A summer as warm or warmer than 2025 is now 70 times more likely** than it would be in a natural pre-industrial climate (rapid attribution study).
- **Days above 25C:** risen from 7 per year (1961-1990) to 11 per year (1991-2020) - 57% more dangerous walking days per summer.
- **40C+ days:** first recorded in the UK in July 2022. Estimated 4% annual probability now, from under 1% in the 1960s.

Sources: Met Office news releases 2025 and 2026; Carbon Brief 2022 analysis.

---

### The 2022 heatwave - useful context for the article

- **19 July 2022:** 40.3C at Coningsby, Lincolnshire - the first time 40C was ever officially recorded in the UK. Six stations exceeded 40C that day.
- **Summer 2022:** 31 days above 25C.
- **First ever Level 4 Heat Health Alert** (national emergency classification) was issued.
- **Estimated 4,507 heat-related deaths in England in 2022**, including approximately 2,803 excess deaths in those aged 65 and over.
- **BVA data:** five-fold increase in dog heatstroke cases presented to vets during heat-health alert periods in 2022. One in four affected dogs died.

Framing for Tom: These are the conditions UK dog owners are now experiencing, and the trend is upward. The 2022 summer was an anomaly that is becoming a precedent.

Sources: Met Office; UK Gov heat mortality monitoring report 2022; BVA news release 2022.

## 7. Sniffout App Integration Notes for Tom

These points support a natural mention of the Sniffout app in the article:

- The **bar chart** shows per-hour walk quality for today and tomorrow, so owners can see at a glance which hours are green (safe), amber (caution), and red/grey (avoid). It uses apparent temperature (feels-like), not raw air temperature - so humid warm days score worse than dry days at the same temperature, reflecting the real effect of humidity on panting.
- **Breed-specific heat alerts:** For owners who have set a brachycephalic breed in their dog profile, Sniffout shifts all heat thresholds down, so conditions show as amber or red earlier in the day than for average dogs.
- **The "best walk window" pill** identifies the longest consecutive green/good-to-great stretch in the day - typically early morning in summer. On very hot days it shows a specific narrow window ("6am-7:30am") rather than "all day."
- The app uses **Met Office-calibrated weather data** and updates weather scores with a silent background refresh when the phone is used - so the bar chart reflects the current forecast rather than one from several hours ago.
