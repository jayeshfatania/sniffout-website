## WALK 1: Bradgate Park, Leicestershire

### 1. Full Name
Bradgate Park and Old John Circular Walk

### 2. Location
Newtown Linford, Leicestershire

**Source confidence:** (HIGH - bradgatepark.org parking charges page, April 2025)

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
52.6900°N, -1.2139°W

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM - coordinates derived from multiple mapping sources and crosschecked; latitude.to gives 52.6900, -1.2139 for Bradgate Park; Newtown Linford car park area. Sufficient for weather card.)

### 5. Approximate Distance
The standard Bradgate Park and Old John Circular route (from Newtown Linford, across the deer park, up to Old John folly, and back) is approximately 6 km (3.7 miles), with approximately 100 m of elevation gain.

A longer route incorporating Swithland Wood can extend to approximately 10-12 km. The park itself covers 850 acres (344 hectares) and offers a network of informal paths that can be combined at any distance.

**Recommendation for Sniffout:** Use 6 km as the headline distance for the Newtown Linford-Old John circular. Flag that Swithland Wood can be added for a longer day.

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM - AllTrails and DogWalksNearMe data; 5.95 km cited in multiple sources for the Bradgate Park circular from Newtown Linford. 6 km rounded.)

### 6. Difficulty
Moderate. The approach to Old John involves a steady rocky ascent over granite outcrops with uneven ground; the main carriageway along the valley floor is flat and accessible. The mix of granite hillside, steep grassy slopes, and flat riverside sections makes this moderate overall.

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM)

### 7. Terrain Type
Heathland / mixed. Ancient deer park with exposed granite hilltops (Charnwood Forest geology), open heathland, rough grassland, the River Lin valley floor, and Swithland Wood if included (deciduous woodland). Main carriageway is tarmac and accessible; hillside paths are rocky, uneven and can be boggy in wet weather.

---

### 8. Dog Access

**Off-Lead Status: Partial**

Bradgate Park is a well-known and popular dog walking destination with clearly defined lead zones.

**Upper Park (majority of the park - approximately 75%):** Dogs are permitted off lead, provided they are under effective control and do not worry or approach the deer herd. The park defines effective control as: the dog must remain in sight at all times and return immediately when called. (HIGH - bradgatepark.org dog owners page)

**Lower Section (River Lin to Middle Wall):** Dogs must be kept on leads at all times. This is a clearly designated zone between the River Lin and the Middle Wall boundary. Signs mark the boundary on the ground. (HIGH - bradgatepark.org dog owners page)

**Car Parks:** Dogs must be on lead at all times in car parking areas. (HIGH)

**Near school/picnic groups:** Owners are asked to put dogs on lead when approaching school groups and picnicking families, even in the off-lead zone. (HIGH)

**Important deer warning:** The park contains approximately 550 red and fallow deer. The Trust states clearly: "ANY deer chase is potentially fatal" and can result in prosecution under the Dogs (Protection of Livestock) Act. A deer chase typically causes fatal stress injury to the deer rather than physical contact injury. This is a real and enforced risk. The Public Space Protection Order covers the estate; fines of up to £100 apply for non-compliance. (HIGH - bradgatepark.org)

**Swithland Wood:** Off lead under effective control. Horses and cyclists share some dedicated paths; owners must remain vigilant. (HIGH - bradgatepark.org)

### 9. Livestock Present
Deer only (no conventional livestock). Approximately 550 red and fallow deer roam the park year-round. No sheep or cattle confirmed on the main Bradgate Park estate. Swithland Wood: horses use dedicated paths but are not grazing livestock.

**Source confidence:** (HIGH - bradgatepark.org and multiple visitor sources)

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- On lead at all times in the Lower Park (River Lin to Middle Wall zone)
- On lead in all car parks
- Off lead in upper park only under effective control with reliable recall
- Deer must not be approached or chased under any circumstances; chase = prosecution risk
- PSPO in force: £100 fines for non-compliance
- Spring ground-nesting birds in meadow areas: close control advised (April-July)

### 15. Hazards Relevant to Dogs

Applicable hazard types from the approved list:

- **Deer** - year-round, approximately 550 red and fallow deer. This is the dominant hazard. Any deer chase is potentially prosecution-level serious. Autumn deer rut (October-November) increases aggression; give the herd wide clearance. (HIGH)
- **Ticks** - woodland (Swithland Wood), rough grassland and heathland; relevant March-October (HIGH - habitat confirmed)
- **Ground-nesting birds** - open heathland and meadow areas; relevant April-July (MEDIUM - habitat inference; close control required anyway for deer management)
- **Adders** - heathland and rough grassland; present April-September (MEDIUM - Charnwood heathland habitat suitable; not explicitly cited in Bradgate sources)

**Not applicable:** algae (River Lin is fast-flowing; no blue-green algae citations found), livestock (no sheep or cattle on main estate), cliff (rocky granite tops are steep but not sheer cliff), flooding (River Lin - note can flood in winter; stay on high paths if water levels high), paw-burn (exposed granite surfaces can warm in summer - seasonal note worth Ailsa mentioning), mountain-bikers

**Seasonal note on deer rut:** Late September to November. Particularly during the rut the stags are active and unpredictable. Dogs should be on lead or at absolute immediate recall when near the herd during this period.

### 16. Ailsa Description Notes

Key features for Ailsa to work with:
1. Old John folly is the visual centrepiece of the walk - a distinctive 18th-century stone tower on the park's highest point (at approximately 220 m), with panoramic views across the Charnwood Forest and, on clear days, towards the peak of the Wrekin in Shropshire. It is one of Leicestershire's most photographed landmarks.
2. The park is one of the oldest enclosed deer parks in England, dating from the 13th century. The Bradgate House ruins (home of Lady Jane Grey, the nine-day Queen) stand in the valley and give the walk a genuine historical weight that most country parks cannot match.
3. The River Lin meanders through the valley floor and provides a natural water source for dogs - the stony banks are accessible in multiple places and dogs love paddling in the shallow sections.
4. The scale and openness is exceptional for an English country park - 850 acres of ancient heathland, granite outcrops, and woodland with no road crossings once inside the park gates. On a quiet weekday morning the sense of wildness is genuine.

**Dog-friendly highlights:** Off-lead freedom across 75% of the park (with reliable recall), river paddling in the Lin, varied terrain from flat valley floor to rocky summit ridge

**Honest practical note:** Bradgate Park is extremely popular and can feel very crowded on summer weekends and bank holidays. The Newtown Linford car park fills before 10am on sunny Saturdays. For the best experience with dogs, visit midweek or arrive before 9am at weekends. The off-lead rules require a reliably trained dog - the deer are present across the whole park and a single chase incident is a serious enforcement matter.

Calke Abbey offers several waymarked walking options across its 600-acre parkland:

- **Purple Walk (recommended for Sniffout):** 3.5 km, approximately 1-1.5 hours. Waymarked route through the parkland.
- **Calke Abbey Circular:** 6.4 km, approximately 2-2.5 hours, 210 m elevation gain. The most complete exploration of the estate.

Easy. The parkland is gently undulating with no significant climbs. Paths vary from surfaced estate tracks to grass parkland paths. Some sections of the Tramway Trail are rougher and can be muddy. The main parkland walk is accessible to most dogs and owners year-round.

### 16. Ailsa Description Notes

Key features for Ailsa to work with:
1. Calke is famously the "un-restored" National Trust house - the Trust made the deliberate decision not to restore the crumbling mansion, leaving it as a remarkable time capsule. The peeling wallpaper, dust-covered furniture, and decaying grandeur of the house (visible from the courtyard even without a house ticket) give the estate an atmosphere completely unlike any other NT property.
2. The veteran trees in the parkland are exceptional - ancient oak pollards with enormous girths, some estimated to be over 500 years old. The parkland has never been ploughed or intensively managed, preserving a rare semi-natural wood-pasture ecosystem. Dogs (on lead) can walk among trees that predate the English Civil War.
3. The Portland sheep flock - a rare native British breed - are visible in the parkland and the encounter with livestock adds to the sense of a genuinely working estate rather than a managed visitor attraction.
4. The complete quietness and sense of forgotten time is Calke's most distinctive quality. Unlike many NT properties it draws relatively few visitors compared to its size, and midweek the 600-acre parkland can feel genuinely remote.

**Dog-friendly highlights:** Off-lead parkland exploration (under close control), veteran tree scenery, quiet estate character, free parking

**Honest practical note:** The off-lead opportunities at Calke require a dog with reliable recall and no prey drive towards deer or sheep, both of which are present in the parkland. Dogs without solid recall should stay on lead throughout. The Tramway Trail is rougher and passes working farmland - less suitable for families with young dogs.

## WALK 3: Cannock Chase Forest, Staffordshire

### 1. Full Name
Cannock Chase Forest, Birches Valley

The Birches Valley Forest Centre is managed by Forestry England and sits within the AONB. Use "Rugeley, Staffordshire" as the location identifier. Staffordshire (not West Midlands) is the correct county.

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
WS15 2UQ (Birches Valley Forest Centre car park - main visitor hub, Forestry England)

Cannock Chase Forest offers an extensive network of marked trails of varying lengths from Birches Valley:

- **Ladyhill Trail (green, family route):** approximately 4.8 km
- **Tackeroo Trail (blue, moderate):** approximately 8 km
- **Stile Cop Trail (red, more challenging):** approximately 12 km

**Recommendation for Sniffout:** Use the Tackeroo Trail at 8 km as the headline distance - this gives a proper woodland experience with good variety. Flag the shorter Ladyhill Trail for families and those wanting less distance. Note that free-roaming is possible across the AONB open access land.

### 6. Difficulty
Moderate. The terrain is largely gently rolling forest track and heathland with no extreme gradients. Some sections involve sandy or loose-surface paths that can be hard underfoot in dry weather. Mountain bike trails cross some paths (see hazards). Good for most dogs and owners with appropriate footwear.

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM)

### 7. Terrain Type
Heathland / woodland / mixed. A mosaic of Forestry England conifer and broadleaf plantation, open heathland (the surviving lowland heathland of Cannock Chase is nationally significant), sandy ridges, and valley streams. The heathland sections are the most distinctive terrain - open, breezy, with long sightlines and a different character from dense forest walks.

---

### 8. Dog Access

**Off-Lead Status: Partial (with mandatory seasonal restriction)**

**CRITICAL - CRoW Act seasonal lead requirement:**
Approximately 58% of Cannock Chase is designated as CRoW Act open access land. Under the CRoW Act 2000, dogs must be kept on a lead of no more than 2 metres on open access land from 1 March to 31 July (inclusive) every year. This is a legal requirement, not a recommendation. Cannock Chase AONB specifically confirms this applies here. (HIGH - cannockchase.org.uk primary source)

**Outside CRoW season (1 August to 28/29 February):** Dogs can be off lead on open access land under close control. Off lead also permitted on Forestry England waymarked trails in woodland outside the CRoW seasonal window, provided dogs are under close control.

**Mountain bike trail zones:** Dogs and walkers are not permitted on the designated technical singletrack mountain bike sections. Shared forest roads exist, but dogs must be kept close on any trail where cyclists may be present. (HIGH - dogwalksnearme.co.uk confirmed this specific rule)

**Livestock / grazing paddocks:** When entering any grazing paddock with cattle, dogs must be on lead. (HIGH - cannockchase.org.uk)

**General guidance:** Stay on main waymarked paths when in doubt; the AONB guidance emphasises path-keeping to protect sensitive heathland species.

### 9. Livestock Present
Yes - cattle graze in designated paddocks within the AONB (not on the main Forestry England forest tracks). Seasonal months not specified in sources reviewed. Approximately 800 fallow deer are present across the Chase year-round, with the highest densities around Brocton field, not at Birches Valley. (HIGH - cannock-chase.co.uk deer management page)

- On lead at all times near deer when encountered
- On lead in grazing paddocks with cattle

- Dogs must not disturb ground-nesting birds (see hazards section)

### 16. Ailsa Description Notes

Key features for Ailsa to work with:
1. The open heathland sections are the defining quality of Cannock Chase - in an era when lowland heathland has become extremely rare in England (97% has been lost since 1800), the Chase's surviving heath feels wild and spacious in a way that planted forest rarely does. On a bright morning the purple heather (August-September) is spectacular.
2. The scale of off-lead freedom available (outside the CRoW seasonal window) is exceptional - over 6,500 acres of predominantly open access land gives this walk a freedom of movement rare in the East/West Midlands.
3. The Grounds Cafe specifically caters for dogs with its dog ice cream and sausage menu - worth mentioning as a distinctive detail that sets this apart from generic "dogs welcome outside" cafes.
4. The mountain biking heritage gives the Chase an energy and activity level that differs from quieter country parks; on weekends the trails are busy with cyclists as well as walkers, making the whole place feel lively and purposeful.

### Significant Findings for Editor / Developer

**1. Cannock Chase - CRoW Act seasonal lead requirement (HIGH PRIORITY FLAG)**
Cannock Chase AONB's own website (cannockchase.org.uk) confirms that dogs must be kept on lead (max 2 m) on open access land from 1 March to 31 July. This is a legal requirement under the CRoW Act 2000, not a voluntary guideline. Approximately 58% of Cannock Chase is open access land. This significantly limits the off-lead experience during spring and early summer - the most popular walking season. Ailsa must communicate this clearly. The walk page must not describe Cannock Chase as an "off-lead" walk without the seasonal qualifier.

**2. Bradgate Park - weekend parking pressure (PRACTICAL FLAG)**
The Newtown Linford car park (ANPR-managed) operates a cashless number plate recognition system (no pay machines). Multiple visitor reviews confirm the car park fills before 10am on sunny Saturdays and summer bank holidays, causing congestion on Newtown Linford village road. The walk page should include a practical note about early arrival on weekends. The 2025 tariff increase to £5-6 for a full day at weekends (from April 2025) may also generate visitor comments.

**3. Bradgate Park - PSPO enforcement (NOTE)**
A Public Space Protection Order is in force at Bradgate Park. Fines of up to £100 apply for non-compliance (dogs not on lead in lead zones, failure to pick up). This is higher enforcement than most public parks and should be mentioned in the on-lead/off-lead section without alarming readers unnecessarily.

**4. Clumber Park - NT primary pages inaccessible during research (VERIFICATION FLAG)**
The National Trust Clumber Park dog walking page was blocked by a security verification (Radware) during this research session, preventing direct primary source extraction. The admission change notice for 2025 was also inaccessible for the same reason. The £6.80 vehicle charge and on-lead zone details in this report are sourced from secondary sources (dogwalksnearme.co.uk and TripAdvisor). **Before Ailsa writes the Clumber Park description or Developer builds the page, verify the following directly from nationaltrust.org.uk:**
  - Current admission/parking charge for non-members
  - Exact on-lead zones (particularly around the lake path)

**5. Sherwood Forest - Robin Hood Festival parking surge (SEASONAL FLAG)**
The Robin Hood Festival runs across multiple August weekends in 2026 (August 1-2, 8-9, 29-31). On these days parking charges rise from £7 to £15 per car. The site will be significantly more crowded. If the Sherwood Forest walk page goes live before August, include a note that event days have higher parking charges. The Robin Hood Festival is a very significant driver of searches for Sherwood Forest and the FAQ "Is Robin Hood's Sherwood Forest a real place?" is recommended explicitly to capture this traffic.

**6. Calke Abbey - walled garden dog access (UNCONFIRMED)**
Multiple sources describe dogs as welcome in the stable courtyard and gardens, but none confirmed dog access specifically to the enclosed walled garden. The NT primary page for Calke was inaccessible (security block). This should be verified before Ailsa writes any description that mentions the walled garden. Flag as (LOW) confidence until confirmed.

**7. Lickey Hills - conservation grazing verification needed**
The heathland at Lickey Hills may be managed with conservation grazing (cattle or ponies) as part of the SSSI heathland restoration programme. This was not confirmed or denied in sources reviewed. If conservation grazing is active, it changes the livestock status from "No" to "seasonal yes" and triggers the lead requirement near livestock. Recommend checking with Birmingham City Council Parks team before publication.

**8. Cannock Chase - maintenance works at Birches Valley (CURRENT STATUS FLAG)**
As of the research date, Forestry England noted ongoing maintenance work near the visitor hub and Ladyhill Trail at Birches Valley, with short diversions in place. Verify current status before publication.
