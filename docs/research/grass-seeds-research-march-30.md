## Section 1 - UK-Specific Context

### Grass Species

UK veterinary sources (Blue Cross, PDSA, Vets Now, Kennel Club) do not name specific grass species in their guidance. They refer generically to "grass seeds," with Blue Cross noting the characteristic appearance as seeds that "look like barley ears." The grass species most commonly cited in academic literature on this hazard (Meadow Foxtail - Alopecurus pratensis, wild barley grasses, brome grasses) are not named in accessible UK consumer-facing veterinary guidance.

**Flag for Tom:** Do not attribute the hazard to specific named species in the article unless you have a UK veterinary source that confirms named species. "Grass seeds with pointed, barbed awns" is the verified framing. The "barley ear" descriptor from Blue Cross is safe to use as a visual identifier.

### Peak Season - Verified and Precise

The most authoritative data comes from the SAVSNET study: Brant et al., "Seasonality and risk factors for grass seed foreign bodies in dogs," Preventive Veterinary Medicine, Vol. 197, 2021. This is a peer-reviewed study using SAVSNET data from 245 UK veterinary practices, covering 940+ recorded cases between 2014 and 2020.

Key findings:

- **90.6% of all grass seed foreign body cases occur between June and September**
- **July is the highest-risk single month: risk in July is approximately 176 times greater than in January**
- **May onwards: exponential rise begins.** From the University of Liverpool blog on the study: "May is the month it starts to increase exponentially"
- Cases are recorded year-round but at very low levels outside the May-September window
- Peak danger coincides with seeds drying out in warm weather, which hardens the awn and makes penetration easier

**Verified season summary:** May-September. Peak: June-July-August. July is the single worst month. Seeds become most dangerous as they dry and harden from late spring through summer.

Source: Brant et al. Preventive Veterinary Medicine (2021); University of Liverpool SAVSNET blog (March 2022) https://www.liverpool.ac.uk/infection-veterinary-and-ecological-sciences/blog/march2022posts/ives-blog-my-first-paper-bethaney-brant/

### Regional Risk - Verified

From Brant et al.:

- **Highest risk region: East of England**
- **Lowest risk region: Wales**
- Geographical location was confirmed as a statistically significant risk factor

**Flag for Tom:** The full regional breakdown (South East, South West, Midlands etc.) is not available in the accessible publication summaries. Only East of England (highest) and Wales (lowest) are explicitly confirmed. Do not state that South East or South West are high-risk without the full paper data. The safe framing is: "Incidence is highest in the East of England and lower in Wales, though cases are recorded across all UK regions."

Source: Brant et al. Preventive Veterinary Medicine (2021)

### High-Risk UK Terrain

Confirmed by Blue Cross, PDSA, Vets Now, and Kennel Club:

- Long grass (meadows, field margins, verges)
- Heathland
- Paths through crops and farmland
- Woodland edges where grasses grow at path margins
- Any unmown grassland during May-September

The more specific Blue Cross framing: "Grass seeds most commonly lurk in long or overgrown grass and dried-out meadows in summer."

## Section 2 - How Grass Seeds Harm Dogs

### Mechanism of Harm

Confirmed by PDSA and Blue Cross:

- Seeds have sharp, pointed ends designed (evolutionarily) to anchor into soil and animal fur for dispersal
- Once in contact with fur, they work towards the skin through movement
- "Their pointy shape enables them to pierce through skin, enter the body and travel around causing problems" (PDSA)
- Blue Cross: "Grass seeds have very sharp ends, so although they're tiny, if they become trapped in a dog's fur, they can burrow down and pierce their skin"
- The barbed structure means seeds can only travel forward through tissue - they cannot reverse. Once sub-dermal, the body's own movement drives them deeper

**Flag for Tom:** The "forward only, cannot travel backwards" mechanism is implied by clinical evidence (seeds travel deeper, not back) and the barbed structure, but no UK source states it in exactly these words. It is accurate based on the veterinary literature. The explanation can be included with this framing: "Once under the skin, the barbed structure means the seed can only travel forward through tissue - it cannot work its way back out on its own."

### Entry Points and Risk Level

Confirmed from PDSA, Blue Cross, Vets Now, and Kennel Club. Listed in approximate order of frequency and/or seriousness:

| Location | Signs | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| Between toes / paw pads | Licking, swelling, limping, abscess | HIGH - most common entry site |
| Ears | Sudden violent head shaking, pawing at ear, head tilt, pain | HIGH - can reach eardrum |
| Eyes | Squinting, discharge, pawing at eye, sudden onset | HIGH - Kennel Club notes blindness risk |
| Nose | Sudden violent sneezing, bloody nasal discharge, pawing at nose | HIGH - requires immediate vet |
| Armpits | Swelling, lameness, abscess | MEDIUM-HIGH - common in active dogs |
| Groin / inner thighs | Swelling, discomfort, abscess | MEDIUM |
| Under collar | Seeds trapped by collar against neck | MEDIUM - check after every walk |
| General skin | Abscess, red lump, heat, discharge | Variable - often secondary migration |
| Chest/thorax (migrated) | Coughing, breathing difficulty, lethargy | CRITICAL - rare but serious |

PDSA explicit advice: "Always check them for grass seeds afterwards, and remove any that you find, especially in between the toes, round their armpits and near their eyes."

### Consequences if Untreated

Confirmed by Blue Cross and PDSA:

- Infection and abscess formation at entry point
- Migration through tissue planes (forward-only due to barbed structure)
- "Once a grass seed has worked its way into the body, they are much harder to find, don't often show up on x-rays, and can start travelling around causing inflammation, infection and abscesses" (Blue Cross)
- Thoracic migration (into chest) is the most serious documented outcome
- Willows Referral Hospital (UK specialist centre) documents cases requiring CT scanning and bronchoscopy for lung-migrated seeds
- Pyothorax (infection in the chest cavity) is documented as a complication

**Flag for Tom:** UK consumer-facing sources do not use the word "fatal" or give fatality statistics for grass seed cases. The honest framing is: "In the most serious cases - where a seed has migrated into the chest cavity - the outcome can be life-threatening without specialist treatment." Do not state "grass seeds can be fatal" without this qualifier. Willows and other referral centres do document life-threatening thoracic cases.

Sources: Blue Cross; PDSA; Willows Referral Hospital https://www.willows.uk.net/grass-seed-season/

### Breeds Most at Risk

From Brant et al. (SAVSNET peer-reviewed study, 2021):

- **Spaniel types: 7.7 times more likely to have a grass seed foreign body than retriever types**
- The study identifies "hairy ears and feet" and "busy, ground-level foraging behaviour" as the likely reasons
- Long-haired breeds generally are at elevated risk due to fur trapping seeds before they reach skin
- Male dogs showed elevated risk compared to female dogs (exact multiplier not available in summary data)
- Working dogs and hunting dogs: elevated risk due to behaviour in long grass

Kennel Club insurance data (referenced in MRCVS News): "A third of Kennel Club insurance claims against grass seed injuries are made in July. 76% of all claims were made between July and September."

Sources: Brant et al. Preventive Veterinary Medicine (2021); Kennel Club insurance data

## Section 3 - Shar Pei Specific Research

### Skin Folds and Grass Seed Risk

No UK veterinary source specifically addresses grass seeds as a documented particular hazard for Shar Peis. The following is based on logical inference from verified facts about Shar Pei anatomy.

**What is confirmed (UFAW, PDSA on Shar Pei health):**

- Shar Pei skin folds trap moisture, dirt, and debris
- Reduced air circulation in folds creates conditions for bacterial and fungal infection
- Skin fold dermatitis is one of the most common health conditions in Shar Peis
- The RVC VetCompass research confirms entropion (in-turned eyelids) occurs in 17.9% of Shar Peis annually - significantly elevating eye hazard risk for this breed, including from grass seeds

**Inference for Tom to use carefully:**

The skin folds around the face, neck, and body of a Shar Pei create crevices where grass seeds could become lodged and concealed from view. A seed trapped in a fold would be harder to spot on post-walk inspection than one visible in short fur. This is logical and clinically plausible but not directly evidenced by a specific UK study.

**Safe framing:** "Shar Peis need particular attention after walks in grass seed season. The skin folds around the face, neck, and body create concealed crevices where seeds can become lodged out of sight, and the breed's predisposition to ear and eye issues means any contact with seeds in these areas should be treated as urgent."

Flag: Do not state that Shar Peis are clinically proven to have higher grass seed incidence than other breeds. The inference is reasonable but unverified.

### Bearcoat Shar Pei Coat Type

No UK source specifically addresses grass seed risk in bearcoat Shar Peis. What is confirmed:

- Bearcoat Shar Peis have a noticeably longer, softer coat than the standard horse coat or brush coat types
- The longer coat is more like a Chow Chow in texture
- The bearcoat type is not Kennel Club-registered in the UK (it is not an accepted variety under the breed standard)
- One source (referenced in the second agent's findings) suggests bearcoats may actually have fewer skin problems than standard coats - their skin fold structure may be less extreme

**Inference for Tom:** A longer, fluffier coat (bearcoat) is more likely to catch and hold grass seeds before they reach the skin - this is consistent with the established finding that long-haired breeds generally are at higher risk. However, seeds caught in the longer coat may also be more visible on inspection. This is reasonable to include as a practical note with appropriate hedging.

**Safe framing:** "Luna is a bearcoat Shar Pei, with a longer, fluffier coat than the standard breed. That coat catches grass seeds more readily than a short horse coat - which means the post-walk check matters even more in summer."

## Section 4 - Signs to Watch For

All confirmed by PDSA, Blue Cross, Vets Now, Kennel Club.

### By Body Location

**Paws and between toes:**
- Persistent licking or chewing at one paw
- Swelling between the toes (may be hard and hot)
- Limping or reluctance to put weight on the leg
- Abscess or red lump that appears between the toes

**Ears:**
- Sudden onset of violent, repeated head shaking
- Pawing at one ear
- Holding the head tilted to one side
- Apparent pain when the ear is touched
- Note: seeds can travel down the ear canal to rest against the eardrum - do not probe the ear

**Eyes:**
- Sudden squinting in one eye
- Excessive watering or discharge from one eye
- Pawing at the eye
- Visible redness

**Nose:**
- Sudden, violent, repeated sneezing (often the first sign)
- Pawing at the nose
- Bloody or persistent nasal discharge from one nostril

**General / skin:**
- Sudden unexplained lameness with no obvious cause
- A hot, swollen area of skin anywhere on the body
- An abscess or lump that appears rapidly and discharges

**Chest migration (serious):**
- Persistent coughing
- Difficulty breathing
- Lethargy combined with the above

### Timeline

No UK source provides a specific hour-by-hour or day-by-day timeline for seed migration. What is stated:

- Seeds can penetrate skin very quickly (within the duration of a walk in some cases)
- Once sub-dermal, migration rate depends on activity, location, and the individual seed
- Abscess formation typically takes days
- Internal migration (to thorax) takes longer - exact timing not available in accessible sources
- PDSA and Blue Cross both emphasise: "The sooner you get it removed, the less problem it's likely to cause"

**Safe framing for Tom:** "Grass seeds can work their way under the skin during a single walk. Once under the skin, they migrate deeper over days or weeks. The sooner the seed is located and removed, the simpler the treatment."

## Section 5 - Removal and Treatment

### What Owners Can Do

Confirmed from Vets Now and Kennel Club:

- **Seeds visible in outer coat but not yet piercing skin:** Owner can attempt gentle removal with tweezers, providing the dog is calm and cooperative
- **Seeds where the awn is above the skin surface:** Vets Now: "If the awn is mostly above the surface of the dog's skin, you can use tweezers to gently and slowly pull it out"
- **Critical warning (Kennel Club):** "If a grass seed is embedded in the skin, don't try to pull it out yourself as this can cause it to break apart and travel further under the skin"
- Any swelling, discharge, or discomfort means the seed has penetrated - go to vet

**Must go to vet immediately (all sources agree):**
- Any seed in or near the ear canal
- Any seed in or near the eye
- Any seed that has entered via the nose
- Any seed where the skin is already broken or swollen
- Any situation where the seed cannot be cleanly retrieved

### Veterinary Treatment

Confirmed by Vets Now, Willows, PDSA:

- **Sedation or general anaesthetic** almost always required for removal from sensitive areas or embedded seeds
- **Ultrasound** used to locate migrated seeds (X-rays often cannot detect grass seeds)
- **Surgery** for deeply embedded or internally migrated seeds - ultrasound used to guide and minimise tissue damage
- **CT scan and bronchoscopy** for suspected lung migration (Willows referral centre data)
- **Antibiotics** prescribed when infection is present or suspected. Full course required.
- **Cost note (not for article):** These procedures can be expensive; pet insurance claims for grass seeds are substantial and common in summer months

## Section 6 - Prevention

### Post-Walk Check Body Map

Confirmed from PDSA, Blue Cross, Vets Now, Kennel Club. Check after every walk in long grass during May-September:

1. Between every toe and under each paw pad
2. Inside and around both ear flaps
3. Around and in the eyes
4. Around the muzzle and in the mouth
5. Both armpits
6. The groin and inner thighs
7. Under and around the collar
8. Any skin folds (Shar Pei specific)
9. Run hands through the full coat, parting the fur

### Coat Trimming

Confirmed by Blue Cross and veterinary sources:

- Trimming hair between the toes and around the paw pads is recommended during seed season
- Trimming ear fur in breeds with heavily feathered ears (Spaniels) reduces trapping risk
- "Where appropriate, it may be better to keep the coat shorter in those breeds who appear more prone to picking up grass seeds, such as Spaniels"

This is particularly relevant for Tom's article given the Spaniel risk finding.

### Protective Boots

Confirmed as useful by Vets Now and Kennel Club:

- Dog boots prevent seeds entering between toes during walks in long grass
- Noted as lightweight, breathable options are available
- Not all dogs tolerate boots - introduce gradually

### Terrain Avoidance

All sources advise:

- Stick to mown paths and maintained footpaths during peak season (June-August especially)
- Avoid walking through fields with long or drying grass where possible
- "Avoid long grass in the warmer months" (Blue Cross)
- Check the path ahead and redirect if the grass is long and seeding
