## WALK 1: Formby Beach and Red Squirrel Reserve, Merseyside

### 1. Full Name
Formby Beach and Red Squirrel Reserve

### 2. Location
Formby, Merseyside

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
L37 2EB (Lifeboat Road car park - currently the only open car park, see closure note above). Victoria Road car park postcode L37 1LJ is closed until Spring 2026.

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
53.5504°N, -3.0966°W (Lifeboat Road car park / reserve entrance area)

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM) - coordinates derived from Driving with Dogs listing; crosschecked against Formby town coordinates from latlong.net. May need OS map verification for weather card precision.

### 5. Approximate Distance
The site offers several self-guided routes of different lengths. The standard Asparagus and Squirrel circular trail through the reserve is approximately 4.5 km. A shorter Red Squirrel Trail only loop is approximately 3.1 km. A longer coastal route combining beach, dunes, squirrel woodland, and asparagus fields reaches approximately 9.3 km.

**Recommendation for Sniffout:** Use the mid-length 4.5 km Asparagus and Squirrel circular as the headline distance. Flag that the site can be walked at different lengths.

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM) - AllTrails listed routes for Formby Asparagus Squirrel Trails Circular (9.3 km) and Red Squirrel Trail and Asparagus Circular Walk (3.1 km); 4.5 km figure from Komoot smart tour route.

### 6. Difficulty
Easy to Moderate. The terrain is generally flat through the pinewoods and asparagus fields, but the sand dunes require effort to cross and can be tiring for dogs in soft sand. Suitable for most dogs and owners.

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM)

### 7. Terrain Type
Coastal / woodland / mixed. Sand dunes, pinewood, asparagus fields, firm sandy beach, dune slacks (wet pools). The path surface through the woods is stone and compressed mud; generally firm but can become muddy in wet weather.

---

### 8. Dog Access

**Off-Lead Status: Partial**

Dogs are welcome across the site year-round, but access is split across three distinct zones:

**Zone A - The beach (firm sand):** Dogs are permitted off lead on the beach. However, under the Sefton Coast PSPO (Public Space Protection Order), there is a **seasonal dog ban on a section of the beach from 1 May to 30 September**. The restricted section runs from Cafe Cove to the end of the sea defences at Kingsway. Outside this section and outside these months, dogs can run off lead on the beach. (HIGH - Sefton Council PSPO)

**Zone B - Squirrel Walk (pinewood trail):** Dogs must be kept **on lead at all times** along the designated Squirrel Walk. The National Trust requires this to protect red squirrels and improve visitor sighting chances. (HIGH - National Trust primary page)

**Zone C - Dune slacks and pools:** Dogs must be kept well away from the dune pools throughout the site, particularly between March and August when natterjack toads lay spawn. Great crested newts also breed in the dune pools March to June. No formal on-lead requirement for the dune area beyond the pool margins, but close control is strongly advised. (HIGH - National Trust primary page)

**Zone D - Open pinewoods, asparagus fields, general trails:** Dogs welcome under close control. Off lead is possible in these areas outside the Squirrel Walk, but the National Trust advises keeping on lead if unsure. (MEDIUM)

### 9. Livestock Present
No livestock present on the National Trust site itself. (MEDIUM - no mention of livestock in any source reviewed)

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- Dogs on lead at all times on the Squirrel Walk (red squirrel conservation)
- Dogs banned from the Cafe Cove to Kingsway section of beach, 1 May to 30 September (Sefton PSPO)
- Dogs must be kept clear of all dune pool areas to protect natterjack toads (March-August), great crested newts (March-June), and sand lizards (May onwards)
- Ground-nesting birds including skylark and meadow pipits are present in dune and slack areas; close control advised during nesting season (approximately April-July)
- Victoria Road car park currently closed until Spring 2026; only Lifeboat Road car park open

Applicable hazard types from the approved list:

- **Ticks** - woodland, dune grassland and heathland habitat; relevant March-October
- **Ground-nesting birds** - skylark, meadow pipits, whitethroat, reed bunting; nesting season approximately April-July
- **Adders** - dune and heathland habitat; risk present April-September (MEDIUM - habitat type confirms risk; not explicitly cited in sources reviewed)

**Not applicable:** deer, algae, livestock, cliff, flooding (tidal flooding possible in extreme weather but not a walking hazard), paw-burn (sand can get warm in summer - worth a brief seasonal note), mountain-bikers

**Seasonal note on paw-burn:** Dry compacted sand and dark dune surfaces can become very warm in direct sun during summer months. Worth flagging in Ailsa notes as a brief practical point.

---

### 16. Ailsa Description Notes

Key features for Ailsa to work with:
1. One of the only places in England to reliably spot red squirrels in the wild - the pinewoods give an almost certain sighting on a quiet morning
2. The beach itself is vast, wild, and sandy - superb off-lead running on firm sand outside the restricted section and outside summer months; a dramatically different feel from manicured dog parks
3. The asparagus fields are historically distinctive - Formby was famous for its asparagus crop for centuries; the field trails are now part of the walking route and give the walk a character you won't find elsewhere
4. The dunes themselves are dramatic and ever-shifting; dogs love the physical challenge of crossing them, and the elevated dune tops give long views along the Sefton coast towards Southport

**Dog-friendly highlights:** Off-lead beach running (conditions and section permitting), pinewood exploration, open trail network with varied surfaces

**Honest practical note:** The Victoria Road car park is currently closed (until Spring 2026), the remaining Lifeboat Road car park is small and fills on sunny days, and parking can be genuinely difficult on summer weekends. Arriving by train is the most reliable option and is actively recommended by the National Trust.

## WALK 2: Beacon Fell Country Park, Lancashire

### 1. Full Name
Beacon Fell Country Park

### 2. Location
Goosnargh, Preston, Lancashire

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
PR3 2NL (main visitor centre and car park, Beacon Fell Road). Alternative: PR3 2ES (Carwags Picnic Area car park, recommended for horse riders; also used by dog walkers wanting a quieter start).

**Source confidence:** (HIGH - Lancashire County Council primary page gives PR3 2ER; PR3 2NL cross-referenced against multiple mapping sources as the more precise visitor centre postcode. Recommend verifying in sat nav before publication.)

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
53.8785°N, -2.6634°W

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM) - derived from multiple listing sources. Grid reference SD 565 427 is given by Lancashire County Council directly. Cross-reference confirms coordinates above.

### 5. Approximate Distance
Beacon Fell Country Park offers multiple self-guided routes:
- Outer Circular: 2.7 km (easy, under 1 hour)
- Summit Circular: 3.4 km with 102 m elevation gain (1-1.5 hours)
- Longer fell-edge circuit: approximately 9.7 km (1.5-2 hours)

**Recommendation for Sniffout:** Use the Summit Circular (3.4 km) as the headline distance - this reaches the 266 m summit viewpoint which is the key landmark, and is the most rewarding route for the effort. Flag that shorter and longer options exist.

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM - AllTrails and GPS-Routes data; no primary Lancashire County Council route distances found for the specific circular routes, only the map leaflet download referenced)

### 6. Difficulty
Easy. Gentle ascent of 102 m to the summit, well-surfaced paths, clearly waymarked. Accessible to most dogs and owners. The longer fell-edge circuit involves more sustained walking but remains easy in terms of terrain and footing.

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM)

### 7. Terrain Type
Woodland / moorland / mixed. The park covers conifer and mixed woodland on the fell flanks, with open moorland/fell at the 266 m summit giving panoramic views. Well-surfaced paths throughout; holds up well in wet weather, making this a reliable winter option compared to boggier moorland routes nearby.

- Dogs must be kept out of the **wildlife ponds** at all times
- Sheep graze some areas of the park seasonally; leads required when livestock are present. (MEDIUM - exact months not specified in sources reviewed; recommend checking signage on arrival)

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM - Lancashire County Council page does not specify off-lead areas in detail; detail derived from MyPawfectPlace and WildPackCollective dog-walking specific sources)

### 9. Livestock Present
Yes, seasonal. Sheep graze parts of the park at certain times of year. Exact months not confirmed in sources reviewed. Dogs must be on leads when sheep are present. (MEDIUM)

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- On lead near livestock (seasonal, check signage)
- On lead on park fringes adjacent to farmland
- Dogs out of wildlife ponds at all times
- No specific breed restrictions or blanket lead rules for the majority of the park

### 16. Ailsa Description Notes

Key features for Ailsa to work with:

**Dog-friendly highlights:** Off-lead woodland running, fell-top views, well-surfaced paths for year-round reliability

**Honest practical note:** The cafe is only open Wednesday to Sunday, and closes at 3:30pm. If visiting on a Monday or Tuesday, there are no onsite facilities. Plan accordingly, especially in winter when the park can feel very remote.

## WALK 3: Rivington Pike and Lower Rivington Reservoir, Lancashire

### 1. Full Name
Rivington Pike and Lower Rivington Reservoir Circular Walk

### 2. Location
Rivington, Chorley, Lancashire

**Note on county:** Rivington sits administratively in Lancashire (Borough of Chorley), though it borders Greater Manchester. Some sources list it as Greater Manchester. The postal address and local council are Lancashire. Use "Rivington, Chorley, Lancashire" for consistency.

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
BL6 7SB (Rivington Hall Barn car park - the most recommended start point, large car park, free). Alternative: BL6 7SA (Pigeon Tower car park area on Belmont Road, smaller).

**Source confidence:** (HIGH - multiple primary walking sources confirm BL6 7SB for Hall Barn; GPS-Routes confirms BL6 7SA for Pigeon Tower)

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
53.6193°N, -2.5413°W (Rivington Pike summit / general area)

**Source confidence:** (HIGH - latitude.to and multiple coordinate sources confirm 53.6196, -2.5411 for the Pike itself. Hall Barn car park is approximately 1 km north of the summit. For weather card, use 53.618°N, -2.541°W as a good area centre point.)

### 5. Approximate Distance
The full circular walk incorporating Rivington Pike summit and the shores of Lower Rivington Reservoir is approximately **9 km** (5.6 miles), with approximately 362 m of elevation gain. This is the AllTrails-listed "Rivington Pike and Lower Rivington" route.

A shorter route to the Pike summit only and back (from Hall Barn, not incorporating the full reservoir loop) is approximately 3 km (1.9 miles) from the Pigeon Tower car park.

**Recommendation for Sniffout:** Use the full 9 km circular as the headline distance. Flag the shorter out-and-back option for those wanting a quicker visit.

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM - AllTrails 9 km figure consistent across multiple sources; shorter 3 km route from GPS-Routes)

### 6. Difficulty
Moderate. The climb to Rivington Pike (363 m) is short but genuinely steep and uneven in places, with stone steps and cobbled sections. The surrounding reservoir and parkland sections are gentle. Overall moderate rather than hard due to the limited total distance.

**Note for Ailsa:** Flag this as a short but steep walk. The effort comes in a concentrated burst to the Pike summit, not over a sustained distance. Good for fit dogs; the step sections may not suit elderly dogs with joint issues.

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM)

### 7. Terrain Type
Moorland / woodland / riverside (reservoir). The route combines open moorland access land ascending to the Pike, woodland paths through Lever Park and the Rivington Terraced Gardens, and lakeside paths along Lower Rivington Reservoir. Surfaces vary: stone-pitched and cobbled near the Pike, gravel and compacted paths near the barn and reservoir, muddy in places after rain.

---

### 8. Dog Access

**Off-Lead Status: Partial**

The walk passes through multiple distinct land management areas, each with different rules:

**Rivington Moor / Pike summit area (CRoW open access land):** The moorland section approaching and surrounding the Pike is designated open access land under the CRoW Act 2000. Dogs are permitted, but under CRoW Act provisions, dogs must be kept on a lead of no more than 2 metres from **1 March to 31 July** on open access land (not on footpaths). Outside this window, dogs can be off lead on the open moor under close control. Sheep are present on the moor - dogs must be under control near sheep at all times, regardless of season. (HIGH - CRoW Act statutory requirement)

**Lever Park (around the barns and reservoir):** Dogs can be mostly off lead. This is managed public open space. Close control required. (MEDIUM)

**Rivington Terraced Gardens:** Dogs are permitted. On-lead policy applies within the formal garden areas. The gardens are managed by Rivington Heritage Trust. (MEDIUM - sources suggest on-lead required; recommend verification with Rivington Heritage Trust before publication)

**Road crossing - Rivington Lane:** Dogs must be on lead when crossing Rivington Lane, which bisects the route in places. (MEDIUM)

**Sheep:** Present on the moor sections. Dogs must be on lead or under immediate close control when sheep are visible. (HIGH - multiple walking guides confirm sheep on moors)

### 9. Livestock Present
Yes - sheep on moorland sections. (HIGH - confirmed by multiple sources)

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- Dogs on lead on open access (CRoW) moorland 1 March to 31 July (maximum 2 m lead)
- Dogs under close control near sheep at all times
- On lead when crossing Rivington Lane
- On lead in formal sections of Rivington Terraced Gardens (verify before publication)

### 16. Ailsa Description Notes

Key features for Ailsa to work with:
1. Rivington Pike is one of Lancashire's most recognisable landmarks - the stone tower at the summit has been drawing walkers for centuries, and the panoramic views on a clear day take in the West Pennine Moors, Manchester skyline, and even the sea
2. The contrast on this walk is its appeal: steep, rugged moor to reach the Pike, then a sudden shift to the woodland paths and parkland of Lever Park and the ornamental gardens

## WALK 4: Lyme Park and Lyme Cage, Cheshire

### 1. Full Name
Lyme Park and Lyme Cage Circular Walk

### 2. Location
Disley, Stockport, Cheshire

**Note:** While Lyme Park is in the Borough of Stockport (Greater Manchester), it sits within the landscape of the Peak District fringe and is strongly associated with Cheshire. For Sniffout purposes, use "Disley, Cheshire" as the county identifier - this reflects how most visitors search for it and is the historic county designation.

### 3. Nearest Postcode for Parking / Start Point
SK12 2NR (main Lyme Park car park)

**Source confidence:** (HIGH - National Trust and multiple primary sources confirm SK12 2NR)

### 4. Latitude and Longitude
53.3538°N, -2.0544°W

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM - derived from postcode coordinate lookup; crosschecked against Lyme Park Disley location data. Sufficient for weather card.)

### 5. Approximate Distance
The standard Lyme Cage circular walk (taking in Lyme Hall, The Cage, and Lantern Wood) is approximately 4.8 km (3 miles), with 128 m of ascent. (HIGH - PeakDistrict.org walk description)

A longer option incorporating the parkland moorland and Sponds Hill extends to approximately 7.2 km (4.5 miles). (MEDIUM - GPS-Routes and WalkingWildflower sources)

**Recommendation for Sniffout:** Use the 7.2 km (4.5 mile) figure as the headline route - this gives a more complete experience of the park, the Cage, and the moorland. Flag the shorter 4.8 km option for those wanting a quicker visit.

### 6. Difficulty
Moderate. The park combines formal parkland paths with open moorland sections that include some uneven ground and gradient. The ascent to The Cage and surrounding moorland involves sustained climbing. Some muddy sections in Carter's Slack field and approaching Lantern Wood.

**Source confidence:** (MEDIUM)

### 7. Terrain Type
Mixed: formal parkland, ancient woodland (Knightslow Wood, Pursefield Wood, Lantern Wood), open moorland, estate tracks. The terrain transitions between manicured parkland near the Hall and rougher moorland on the upper estate. Some road sections.

---

### 8. Dog Access

**Off-Lead Status: Partial**

Lyme Park is a National Trust three pawprint rated property. The off-lead situation is more complex than many NT sites due to the deer herd and seasonal livestock.

**Knightslow Wood:** Designated off-lead area, available **all year**. Dogs are permitted off lead in Knightslow Wood under close control throughout the year. (HIGH - National Trust dog walking page confirmed this directly)

**Pursefield Wood:** A route through Pursefield Wood is also available for off-lead walking. (HIGH - National Trust dog page)

**Deer park and open parkland:** Dogs must be kept on a short lead **at all times** when deer, sheep, or cattle are visible in any area of the estate. Given the size of the deer herd (approximately 380 red and fallow deer across 1,400 acres), deer are likely to be visible across most of the open parkland at most times. In practical terms, the open parkland should be treated as on-lead territory. (HIGH - National Trust primary page)

**Seasonal restriction (April-July):** Between approximately 12 April and 31 July, dogs must be kept on a short lead at all times throughout the estate, with the **exception of Knightslow Wood**. This is the most restrictive period due to calving, nesting, and the CRoW Act open access land requirements on the moorland sections. (HIGH - confirmed from NT dog walking page detail in search results)

**The formal gardens near Lyme Hall:** Dogs are welcome in the formal gardens and grounds on a short lead every day. (HIGH - NT primary page)

**Lyme Hall itself (interior):** Only assistance dogs are permitted inside the Hall. (HIGH)

**Timber Yard Cafe and Hall Shop:** Only assistance dogs permitted inside. Dogs welcome in the Muddy Paws Engine House (indoor seating area in the Timber Yard) and in the top half of the Ale Cellar. (HIGH - NT dog page detail from search results)

### 9. Livestock Present
Yes - year-round. The estate has a herd of approximately 380 red and fallow deer, plus sheep and cattle grazing in areas of the park. (HIGH - multiple sources confirm deer herd; livestock confirmed by NT lead requirements)

### 10. Specific Dog Restrictions
- Dogs on lead at all times in all areas where deer, cattle or sheep are visible
- Dogs on lead throughout the estate 12 April to 31 July (except Knightslow Wood off-lead area)
- Off-lead only permitted in Knightslow Wood (year-round) and Pursefield Wood route (verify seasonal status)
- Dogs not permitted inside Lyme Hall, Timber Yard Cafe, or Hall Shop
- Dogs welcome (on lead) in formal gardens and grounds every day

### 15. Hazards Relevant to Dogs

Applicable hazard types from the approved list:

- **Deer** - year-round, large herd. This is the dominant hazard at Lyme Park. Dogs must be kept away from deer at all times. During the autumn rut (late September to November) stags are aggressive and unpredictable; give the herd very wide clearance.
- **Livestock** - cattle and sheep year-round in various parts of the estate
- **Ticks** - ancient woodland and moorland; relevant March-October
- **Ground-nesting birds** - moorland sections; relevant April-July (one reason for the seasonal on-lead requirement)
- **Adders** - moorland habitat on upper estate; relevant April-September (MEDIUM - habitat inference)

**Seasonal note on deer rut:** Late September to November. Stags are highly aggressive during this period. Even with dogs on leads, owners should maintain significant distance from the herd and be prepared to leave the area. This is a genuine safety consideration at Lyme, not just a wildlife protection note.

**Not applicable:** algae (no open still water cited as risk), cliff, flooding, paw-burn, mountain-bikers

### 16. Ailsa Description Notes

Key features for Ailsa to work with:
1. Lyme Park is one of the most dramatic National Trust estates in northern England - 1,400 acres of moorland, ancient woodland, and formal gardens, with the Hall sitting in the valley as a centrepiece. The scale is impressive even before the walk begins
2. The Cage is the walk's highlight landmark - a sixteenth-century stone hunting tower on a moorland spur with far-reaching views across Greater Manchester and the Cheshire Plain. The history of the tower (built 1580, used to imprison poachers) gives it a character beyond a simple viewpoint
3. The deer herd is a defining feature of the park and creates a genuinely memorable walking experience - encountering a herd of red deer in ancient parkland is rare in England and gives Lyme a quality that most dog walks cannot match
4. For fans of period drama: Lyme Hall served as Pemberley in the 1995 BBC adaptation of Pride and Prejudice. The lake scene - Colin Firth's famous emergence from the water - was filmed in the grounds. This drives significant independent search interest and is worth a brief FAQ mention

**Dog-friendly highlights:** Knightslow Wood off-lead area, Muddy Paws indoor dog-friendly seating at the Timber Yard, vast estate to explore, dramatic moorland terrain

**Honest practical note:** The off-lead opportunities at Lyme are limited compared to other walks in this batch. Knightslow Wood is the main off-lead area. The rest of the estate is effectively on-lead territory due to the deer and livestock. Owners wanting extensive off-lead running should temper expectations; the reward here is the dramatic landscape and wildlife encounter, not off-lead freedom.

### Significant findings

**Formby Beach - Victoria Road car park closure (CRITICAL FLAG):**
The Victoria Road car park, the main car park for Formby Beach and the squirrel reserve, is closed from 1 May 2025 until Spring 2026 for a major sand dune restoration project. The Lifeboat Road car park remains open but is small and fills rapidly. The National Trust is actively directing visitors to use Formby railway station. At time of publication (April 2026), this closure may still be in effect or may have just reopened - **verify current status before the page goes live.** The walk page should reference Lifeboat Road as the parking option but flag train access prominently.

**Formby Beach - dog ban section confirmed:**
The seasonal dog ban (1 May to 30 September) applies specifically from Cafe Cove to the Kingsway sea defences, under the Sefton Coast PSPO. This is a section of beach, not the entire National Trust site. The squirrel reserve woodland and other parts of the site remain accessible year-round (with the lead requirement on the Squirrel Walk). Confidence on exact section boundaries is MEDIUM - I found the Cafe Cove / Kingsway description from a secondary source (Driving with Dogs / TailBlazr search result); recommend verifying directly against the Sefton Council PSPO document before publication.

**Lyme Park - seasonal on-lead escalation:**
The National Trust has a specific seasonal escalation at Lyme: between approximately 12 April and 31 July, dogs must be on lead throughout the entire estate except Knightslow Wood. Outside this window, the lead requirement still applies wherever deer or livestock are visible, which in practice means most of the open parkland. A news article ("Dog walkers unhappy at new rule brought in at Lyme Park" - Buxton Advertiser) was found during research but was inaccessible (403). This suggests there has been a relatively recent tightening of rules. The current position is sourced from the NT dog walking page and is described above. Worth noting for the Editor that dog walker sentiment at Lyme Park includes some frustration with the restrictions.

**Lyme Park - deer herd size:**
Multiple sources confirm a herd of approximately 380 red and fallow deer. One source described these as "descended from native forest deer" with fallow deer reintroduced in the 1990s. This is good colour for Ailsa but the deer herd is also the primary safety/access constraint on the walk.

**Rivington Pike - county and admin note:**
Rivington technically sits in Lancashire (Chorley Borough) but the AllTrails and some other listing sites categorise it under Greater Manchester due to its location on the West Pennine Moors near Bolton. Use Lancashire / Chorley for consistency with the walk brief and official administrative geography.

**Rivington - Terraced Gardens access:**
The Rivington Terraced Gardens are managed by Rivington Heritage Trust (not a national body) and public access rules are less clearly documented online than NT or LCC properties. Dogs appear to be permitted on lead in the formal garden areas, with public footpath access through parts of the grounds, but I was unable to verify this from a primary Rivington Heritage Trust source. Recommend verifying directly with the Trust before Ailsa writes the description or the page is built: rivingtonterracedgardens.org.uk.

**Beacon Fell - cafe opening hours:**
The Lancashire County Council primary page confirms the cafe is open Wednesday to Sunday, 10am to 3:30pm only. This is a meaningful practical constraint - Monday and Tuesday visitors get no onsite catering. Worth flagging clearly in Ailsa notes and FAQ.

**Beacon Fell - distance:**
Lancashire County Council do not publish specific route distances on their website (they offer a downloadable map leaflet instead). Route distances used in this report are sourced from AllTrails (MEDIUM confidence). Recommend downloading and reviewing the LCC map leaflet (lancashire.gov.uk/media/944126/beacon-fell-map-leaflet.pdf) for exact trail names and distances before Developer builds the page.
