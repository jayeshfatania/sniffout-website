## WALK 1: Pen y Fan and Corn Du Circuit

Location: Pont ar Daf, Libanus, Powys (Bannau Brycheiniog National Park)
Welsh: Pen y Fan, Corn Du, Bannau Brycheiniog
App data: 5.8 miles (9.3km), hard, off-lead none, livestock true

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 5.8 miles | BROADLY CORRECT - some sources cite 8km (5 miles) for the standard Pont ar Daf circular; the 9.3km / 5.8-mile figure is plausible for a slightly extended circuit. No significant conflict. |
| Difficulty | Hard | CONFIRMED - 350-400m of elevation gain, exposed ridge walking |
| Off-lead | None | CONFIRMED CORRECT - legally mandated 1 March to 31 July under CRoW Act; year-round livestock means practical advice is leads at all times |
| Livestock | True | CONFIRMED - Welsh Mountain sheep graze year-round; Welsh mountain ponies in wider Beacons area |

### 2. The Name Change

The park officially adopted the name Bannau Brycheiniog on 17 April 2023 (the park's 66th anniversary). The National Park Authority now uses Bannau Brycheiniog exclusively in both Welsh and English. The informal shorthand "the Bannau" is used.

Despite the official rebrand, "Brecon Beacons" remains widely used colloquially and by many local businesses and tourism organisations.

For Sniffout pages: use "Bannau Brycheiniog (Brecon Beacons)" to cover both search terms.

### 3. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Pen y Fan and Corn Du are on CRoW open access land. Under the CRoW Act 2000, dogs on open access land must be kept on a lead no longer than 2 metres from 1 March to 31 July to protect ground-nesting birds (curlew, skylark, and wheatear breed on these hills). Failure to comply is trespass with fines up to £1,000.

Outside this period, close control is still required near livestock. Given sheep graze year-round, the practical advice is to keep dogs on leads at all times on this route. The National Trust (which manages the Pont ar Daf car park and much of the surrounding land) states explicitly: "keep every dog on a short lead."

The "off-lead none" designation is correct. It should be qualified in the walk description as partly a legal CRoW Act requirement and partly practical necessity.

### 4. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Weather: Rapid changes are the primary hazard. Pen y Fan sits at 886m and Corn Du at 873m. The summit can be in cloud and cold while the base is warm and clear. Hypothermia risk even in summer.
- Navigation: Navigation errors are common on the open ridge in cloud. Compass and map (or GPS) essential.
- Terrain: The Pont ar Daf path is heavily eroded and stony. The steepest section is below Corn Du. Paths are boggy in wet weather.
- Dog-specific: This route is not suitable for elderly dogs, young puppies, or brachycephalic breeds in warm weather. Several fatalities on Pen y Fan from heat exhaustion in recent years - dogs face the same risk. Natural streams run on the lower sections near the Blaen Taf Fawr reservoir area but can run low in late summer. Carry water for dogs. No facilities on the mountain.
- Winter: Crampons and ice axes may be needed in icy conditions.

### 5. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Pont ar Daf (National Trust managed): LD3 8NL. £7.50 per vehicle flat rate. Free for NT members. Pay by cash at pay-and-display or Pay By Phone. Staff on site 8am-4pm for card payments. Over 180 spaces.
- Storey Arms (alternative): On the A470 approximately 1km north of Pont ar Daf. Free roadside/layby parking, very limited spaces. No facilities.

**Facilities:** Toilets at Pont ar Daf (new amenity building). No on-site cafe.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- Tai'r Bull Inn, Libanus, Brecon, LD3 8EL - approximately 4.5km from Pont ar Daf along the A470 towards Brecon. Confirmed dog-friendly with free sausages for dogs, water bowls, and treats. Traditional Welsh country inn.
- Storey Arms burger van: seasonal food van on the A470 at Storey Arms. Popular with walkers. Outdoor operation - dogs present as a matter of course.

### 6. FAQ

**Q: Can I let my dog off the lead on Pen y Fan?**
A: No, and in spring and summer this is a legal requirement. From 1 March to 31 July, the CRoW Act requires dogs on open access land to be kept on a lead no longer than 2 metres to protect ground-nesting birds including skylarks and curlew. Outside these dates, sheep graze the hillsides year-round so close control or a lead is required anyway. The National Trust states dogs should be kept on a short lead throughout.

**Q: Is Pen y Fan suitable for all dogs?**
A: No. This is a demanding mountain walk with approximately 400m of ascent over rough, stony terrain. Not suitable for elderly dogs, young puppies, or flat-faced breeds in warm weather. Fit medium to large dogs with good paw pads will cope well. Carry water as streams can be unreliable in summer.

**Q: Where should I park for Pen y Fan?**
A: The main car park is Pont ar Daf (LD3 8NL), managed by the National Trust. It costs £7.50 per vehicle (free for National Trust members). There is also limited free layby parking near Storey Arms on the A470 roughly 1km north, but it fills quickly on weekends.

**Q: Is the park still called Brecon Beacons?**
A: The park rebranded to Bannau Brycheiniog on 17 April 2023. "Bannau Brycheiniog" is now the official name in both Welsh and English. "Brecon Beacons" is no longer used officially, though it remains widely used colloquially.

### 7. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Lambing - extra vigilance. CRoW lead requirement starts 1 March. Paths boggy. Weather variable.
- Summer (June to August): Peak season. Pont ar Daf fills by 8am on weekends. Heat risk for dogs on the exposed ridge. Streams may be low.
- Autumn (September to November): Generally good conditions. Fewer crowds. Heather on lower slopes. Weather deteriorates from October.
- Winter (December to February): Serious mountain conditions possible. Ice, snow, and high winds common. Crampons may be needed.

## WALK 2: Llyn Padarn Lake Circuit

Location: Llanberis, Gwynedd (Eryri National Park - Parc Cenedlaethol Eryri)
Welsh: Llyn Padarn, Parc Padarn, Eryri
App data: 5.6 miles (9.0km), easy, off-lead partial, no livestock

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 5.6 miles | CONFIRMED - approximately 9km for the full circuit, consistent with multiple sources |
| Difficulty | Easy | CONFIRMED - primarily flat lakeside and woodland paths with minimal elevation change |
| Off-lead | Partial | CONFIRMED - correct. Leads required on open access sections (CRoW 1 Mar-31 Jul) and near any livestock; close control required near slate museum grounds and railway crossings. |
| Livestock | No livestock | BROADLY CORRECT for the main circuit. Minor caveat: a small community above the track keeps livestock and sheep may occasionally be on higher woodland sections. Not a constant presence. |

**IMPORTANT CURRENT NOTICE:** A section of the standard Llyn Padarn circular walk is temporarily closed as of 2024-2025 due to National Grid works installing electricity cables under the Llanberis Lake Railway track along the southern lakeside. This closure is expected to remain until early 2027. An alternative route through Coed Dinorwig woodland is marked but adds distance and may be steep and muddy. The app walk description must flag this diversion.

### 2. Land Management

Padarn Country Park is managed jointly by Gwynedd Council and Eryri (Snowdonia) National Park Authority. The lake circuit uses a combination of public footpaths, permissive paths through the country park, and the Wales Coast Path.

**Welsh language and naming note:** The park officially rebranded from Snowdonia National Park to Eryri National Park in February 2023 (the Welsh Government amended regulations to make Eryri the sole official name in both Welsh and English). Sniffout pages should use "Eryri" as the primary name with "(Snowdonia)" in brackets.

- Llyn Padarn: the lake (Welsh name, no English equivalent widely used)
- Eryri National Park (official name; formerly Snowdonia National Park)
- Yr Wyddfa: Snowdon (both names now used officially; Yr Wyddfa is the primary Welsh name)
- Parc Padarn: Padarn Country Park

### 3. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

The CRoW Act applies to open access sections. On open access land, dogs must be on a lead (under 2 metres) between 1 March and 31 July. In the country park itself (managed public access), the park rules apply - close control required throughout.

The route passes through sections of the National Slate Museum grounds where dogs should be on leads near entrances and exhibition areas.

### 4. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Slate quarry edges: The route passes near Dinorwig Quarry, the second-largest slate quarry ever worked in Wales. Stay strictly to waymarked paths. Quarry edges have sudden drops and unstable waste underfoot. Slate fragments can cut dogs' paws badly on unmaintained sections.
- Lake water quality: Llyn Padarn has official Bathing Water Status and is regularly monitored by Natural Resources Wales. Consistently rated good to excellent. Dogs swimming is well-established at the Y Glyn lagoon (eastern end), which has a shallow sandy entry and is popular and safe.
- Llanberis Lake Railway: The railway runs along the southern shore. Take care at level crossings.
- Route diversion: The current National Grid closure (southern lakeside, until early 2027) means the standard route is partially unavailable. The alternative via Coed Dinorwig woodland is steep and muddy.
- Yr Wyddfa (Snowdon) note: Llanberis is the base for the Llanberis Path up Yr Wyddfa. Dogs are subject to separate restrictions on Yr Wyddfa (leads during lambing and from May to September on summit paths). This is separate from the Llyn Padarn circuit.

### 5. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Gilfach Ddu car park (by National Slate Museum): LL55 4TY. Approximately £5-6/day (card and PayByPhone). Multiple car parks in the country park complex.
- Y Glyn car park (lagoon end): LL55 4EL. Similar charges.
- Dolbadarn car park: near Dolbadarn Castle ruins.
- Parking charges should be verified on arrival or via Gwynedd Council's website before publishing.

**Facilities:** Toilets at the National Slate Museum (free access). Cafe at the National Slate Museum. Dog waste bins on circuit.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- Pete's Eats, 38-40 High Street, Llanberis, LL55 4EU - reopened November 2025 after refurbishment. Confirmed dog-friendly. Welsh produce-led menu, coffee, cakes, local beers.
- The Heights Bar and Kitchen, Llanberis - confirmed dog-friendly by multiple visitor reviews.

### 6. FAQ

**Q: Can my dog swim in Llyn Padarn?**
A: Yes - Llyn Padarn is one of the most dog-friendly lakes in Wales for swimming. The Y Glyn lagoon area at the eastern end has a shallow, sandy entry and is popular and safe. The lake has official Bathing Water Status and water quality is consistently rated good to excellent by Natural Resources Wales.

**Q: Is the full circular walk around Llyn Padarn currently open?**
A: Not entirely. As of 2024, a section of the standard route along the southern shore is temporarily closed due to National Grid works. This closure is expected to last until early 2027. Alternative paths through Coed Dinorwig woodland are marked but are steep and may be muddy. Check signage at the car park before setting out.

**Q: Are there any slate quarry dangers near the walk?**
A: Yes - the route passes near Dinorwig Quarry. Always follow waymarked paths and do not allow dogs or children to explore off-path near the quarry. Slate fragments can cut paws and quarry edges have sudden drops. The waymarked route itself is safe when followed correctly.

**Q: Does Eryri National Park have specific dog rules?**
A: Eryri follows the CRoW Act (same as England). On open access land, dogs must be on a lead no longer than 2 metres from 1 March to 31 July. On Yr Wyddfa (Snowdon), the National Park recommends leads from May to September during the main walking season. For the Llyn Padarn circuit, close control is required and leads recommended near any livestock.

### 7. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): CRoW lead requirement active from 1 March on open access land. Lake swimmable for hardy dogs.
- Summer (June to August): Peak tourist season. Car parks fill early. Lake swimming conditions best. Watch for boat traffic.
- Autumn (September to November): Quieter. Woodland colours excellent in the Coed Dinorwig section.
- Winter (December to February): Paths can be icy and very muddy (especially the woodland alternative during National Grid closure). Lake swimming not advisable.

## WALK 3: Rhossili Bay and Worms Head

Location: Rhossili (Welsh: Rhosili), Swansea (Gower AONB)
App data: 4 miles (6.4km), moderate, off-lead full, no livestock

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 4 miles | PLAUSIBLE for the headland circuit without the full Worms Head crossing. If crossing the causeway to Worms Head is included, the round trip adds approximately 3-4km. The 4-mile figure appears to reflect the headland circuit only. |
| Difficulty | Moderate | CONFIRMED - uneven coastal terrain, cliff-top walking. The causeway crossing (if attempted) is significantly harder. |
| Off-lead | Full | INCORRECT - CHANGE TO PARTIAL. Beach: off-lead year-round. Headland: leads required near livestock (year-round). |
| Livestock | No | INCORRECT - CHANGE TO TRUE. Cattle, ponies, and sheep graze the headland common land year-round under traditional common land grazing rights. |

**This is one of the most significant data errors across all batches. Both fields need correcting.**

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

The Rhossili headland and surrounding land is National Trust-owned common land. Commoners exercise traditional grazing rights, meaning cattle, ponies, and sheep can graze across the headland throughout the year with no fence boundaries to contain them.

- Headland: dogs must be on leads near livestock. CRoW Act lead requirement (1 March to 31 July) also applies on open access sections.
- Rhossili Bay beach: dogs welcome off-lead year-round, no seasonal ban. One of the few unrestricted dog beaches on the Gower.
- Worms Head island: dogs can technically access the island via the causeway during the permitted tide window, but leads are required during seabird nesting season (approximately April to August).

**Worms Head causeway crossing:** Safe crossing window is approximately 2.5 hours either side of low water - a total window of approximately 5 hours centred on low tide. Safe crossing times are posted daily on a tide board at the National Coastwatch Institution (NCI) lookout station at the old coastguard building on the headland. Always check the board before crossing. Never estimate the tide yourself.

**Dogs on the causeway:** Multiple sources warn that the causeway surface is extremely jagged, covered with sharp seashells and protruding rocks. Dogs can suffer cut paws or worse. Short-legged, older, or arthritic dogs should not attempt the crossing. Assess your dog's paws and agility carefully before proceeding.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Tidal causeway: Being cut off on Worms Head is the most common emergency on Gower. The tide comes in faster than most visitors anticipate. People are stranded every year, requiring RNLI and Coastguard response. If cut off, you wait 5+ hours on the island in all weathers. Always check the NCI tide board before crossing.
- Cliff edges: The headland path passes close to cliff edges. Dogs on leads near cliff tops.
- Causeway surface: Jagged rocks, sharp seashells, and slippery weed. Serious paw injury risk for dogs.
- Seabird disturbance: Worms Head hosts nesting kittiwakes, guillemots, razorbills, and fulmars. Dogs must be on leads on the island during nesting season.
- Beach currents: Rhossili Bay is generally safe for swimming but rip currents can be present.

**URGENT NOTICE - Worms Head Hotel:** The Worms Head Hotel, directly adjacent to the car park, was destroyed by fire on 14 July 2025 and demolished from September 2025. It is not currently operational. The owners have stated an intention to rebuild but no timeline is confirmed. Do not list this as the nearest pub/cafe.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- National Trust Rhossili Car Park: SA3 1PR. £6/day, £3 for 2 hours. Free for NT members. Coin-only pay-and-display machines (no card reader at machines). Open year-round.
- St Mary's Church car park (further from the bay): approximately £5/day cash donation box. Very limited spaces.

**Facilities:** National Trust Visitor Centre with tide table and Worms Head crossing information. Toilets at the car park.

**Management:** The Rhossili headland, Rhossili Down, and much of the South Gower Coast is managed by the National Trust (approximately 700 acres of this coastline).

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe (Worms Head Hotel closed):**
- King's Head Inn, Llangennith, SA3 1HX - approximately 4km north via the B4247. Confirmed dog-friendly, dogs welcome in the bar. Good food and local ales. Currently the best dog-friendly option near Rhossili.
- King Arthur Hotel, Reynoldston, SA3 1AD - dogs welcome in the beer garden.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Is Rhossili Bay dog-friendly year-round?**
A: Yes - Rhossili Bay beach allows dogs off-lead throughout the year with no seasonal restrictions, making it one of the most dog-friendly beaches in Wales. However, on the headland above the beach, dogs must be under close control and on leads near the cattle, ponies, and sheep that graze the common land year-round.

**Q: How do I cross to Worms Head safely?**
A: You can cross the rocky causeway for approximately 2.5 hours either side of low tide - a total window of about 5 hours. Check the daily safe crossing times on the tide board at the National Coastwatch Institution lookout at the old coastguard station on the headland (approximately 1.3km from the car park). Never estimate the tide yourself - people are stranded every year.

**Q: Can my dog cross the Worms Head causeway?**
A: Dogs can technically cross but assess your dog carefully first. The 700m causeway is covered with extremely jagged rocks, sharp seashells, and slippery weed. Dogs with sensitive paws are at risk of serious cuts. Short-legged, older, or arthritic dogs should not attempt it. If your dog does cross, check their paws carefully on return.

**Q: Are there livestock on the Rhossili headland?**
A: Yes - cattle, ponies, and sheep graze the Rhossili headland common land throughout the year under traditional common land grazing rights managed by the National Trust. There are no fences to contain them. Keep dogs on leads when livestock are present.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Lambing on the headland - strict lead control essential. CRoW lead requirement active from 1 March. Seabird nesting begins on Worms Head from approximately April.
- Summer (June to August): Very busy. Car park fills by mid-morning on sunny weekends. Full seabird nesting season on Worms Head.
- Autumn (September to November): Crowds reduce. Grey seals may be visible on rocks below the headland. Good walking conditions.
- Winter (December to February): Wild and exposed. Beach largely empty. Livestock still on the headland. Tidal windows can coincide with short daylight - plan crossings carefully.

## WALK 4: Bute Park and Taff Trail

Location: Cardiff (Caerdydd), Wales
Welsh: Parc Bute, Taith Taf
App data: 3 miles (4.8km), easy, off-lead partial, no livestock

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 3 miles | PLAUSIBLE - Bute Park is approximately 56 acres (23 hectares); a park loop plus Taff Trail section reaching 4.8km is consistent. |
| Difficulty | Easy | CONFIRMED - flat urban park and riverside path |
| Off-lead | Partial | CONFIRMED CORRECT - off-lead in most of the park; on-lead in designated zones (signposted), near park entrances (adjacent to busy roads), and on the shared Taff Trail cycle path sections |
| Livestock | No livestock | CONFIRMED CORRECT - urban park in central Cardiff |
| Management | - | Cardiff Council and Bute Park Advisory Group. Grade I listed landscape. |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Bute Park is managed by Cardiff Council. Dogs are welcome throughout the park but owners must:
- Keep dogs on leads in designated on-lead areas (signposted throughout the park).
- Keep dogs on leads near park entrances adjacent to busy roads.
- Always keep dogs in sight.
- Pick up after dogs (bins provided throughout).

The Taff Trail through Bute Park is a shared-use path (National Cycle Route 8). There is a designated cycle route crossing the park between the Millennium Footbridge and the Royal Welsh College of Music and Drama. Pedestrians have priority over cyclists. The Taff Trail Code of Conduct (Sustrans Cymru, 2011) requires cyclists to give way to pedestrians and travel at an appropriate speed.

Dogs on the Taff Trail section should be on leads, particularly on busy weekends when cycling speed and volume increases.

**River Taff:** Dogs do swim in the River Taff within Bute Park. No formal ban. However, the river is tidal in the Cardiff stretch and runs fast after rain. Water quality is variable due to urban runoff. Caution advised.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Cyclists on the Taff Trail: Commuter and recreational cyclists travel at speed on this shared path. Keep dogs on short leads on the shared cycle path sections. Extending or flexi-leads are particularly dangerous here.
- River Taff: Tidal in lower reaches near Cardiff Bay. Can rise quickly after heavy rain. Dogs swimming should be supervised.
- Urban hazards: Litter, BBQ residue (officially not permitted but occurs), broken glass on hot summer weekends.
- Park entrances: North Road and Castle Street entrances adjoin busy roads. Always leash dogs before approaching gates.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking (no parking within Bute Park itself):**
- Castle Mews Car Park, Castle Street, Cardiff: CF10 3ER. Pay and display, Cardiff Council.
- North Road Car Park: CF10 3EA. Pay and display.
- Sophia Gardens Car Park: CF11 9LJ. Adjacent to Sophia Gardens, short walk across the river.
- Cardiff Council car park charges approximately £3-6 for 2-4 hours in this area (verify current rates).

**Facilities:** Dog waste bins throughout the park. Toilets by the Visitor Centre. No formal dog water bowls but the Summerhouse Cafe provides them.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- Summerhouse Cafe, within Bute Park - confirmed dog-friendly, water bowls, dog treats, and dog ice creams in summer. Open daily 8am-5pm (9am Sundays). Most convenient option, inside the park.
- Blackweir Tavern, 35 North Road, Cardiff, CF10 3DX - directly on the Taff Trail at the north edge of the park. Confirmed dog-friendly. CAMRA-listed. Open Mon-Thu 12pm-11pm, Fri-Sat 12pm-midnight, Sun 12pm-10:30pm.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Can I let my dog off the lead in Bute Park?**
A: Dogs can be off-lead in most of Bute Park, but there are designated on-lead zones which are signposted. Keep your dog on a lead near the park entrances (adjacent to busy Cardiff roads) and follow any lead signs you encounter.

**Q: Is the Taff Trail safe for dogs?**
A: The Taff Trail section through Bute Park is a shared-use path used by cyclists and pedestrians. Keep your dog on a short lead on the shared path sections, especially on busy weekends. Extending leads are particularly hazardous here as cyclists may not be able to stop in time.

**Q: Can my dog swim in the River Taff in Bute Park?**
A: There is no formal ban on dogs entering the River Taff in Bute Park. However, the river is tidal in Cardiff, can run fast after heavy rain, and water quality is subject to urban runoff. Exercise caution, supervise dogs closely, and avoid the river after heavy rainfall.

**Q: Is there a dog-friendly cafe inside Bute Park?**
A: Yes - the Summerhouse Cafe is inside the park and is explicitly dog-friendly, providing water bowls, dog treats, and dog ice creams in summer. Open daily from 8am (9am Sundays) to 5pm. The Blackweir Tavern at 35 North Road is on the Taff Trail at the north edge of the park and is also dog-friendly.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Park begins to get busier. Blossom on the arboretum. Cycling traffic increasing on the Taff Trail.
- Summer (June to August): Very busy on weekends and fine evenings. BBQs can leave hot coals (paw burn hazard). Heavy cycling traffic. River Taff generally lower and calmer.
- Autumn (September to November): Outstanding autumn colour in the arboretum. Quieter. Leaf mulch can make paths slippery.
- Winter (December to February): Open year-round. Paths muddy after rain. River Taff can flood in severe weather. Christmas events may temporarily restrict some park areas.

## WALK 5: Penllergare Valley Woods and Waterfall

Location: Penllergaer, Swansea
Note on spelling: the village is Penllergaer; the woods are Penllergare Valley Woods.
App data: 2.5 miles (4.0km), easy, off-lead partial, no livestock

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 2.5 miles | CONFIRMED - the main circuit to the waterfall and lakes is approximately 3-4km. 4.0km / 2.5 miles is consistent. |
| Difficulty | Easy | QUALIFIED - main carriage drive is easy. The path down into the Llan valley near the waterfall is steep and very slippery in wet conditions. "Easy with one moderate section" is more accurate. |
| Off-lead | Partial | CONFIRMED but requires specific qualification - leads required March to August (the Trust's policy is one month stricter than the CRoW Act minimum which ends 31 July) |
| Livestock | No livestock | CONFIRMED CORRECT |
| Management | - | Penllergare Trust (charitable organisation). Not National Trust, not Cadw, not local authority. |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Penllergare Valley Woods is managed by the Penllergare Trust. The Trust's own rules apply:

- Dogs on leads in visitor centre, coffee shop, gardens, and car park areas at all times.
- Dogs on leads near lakes, rivers, and waterfall at all times (safety).
- Dogs on leads during nesting season March to August (inclusive) - this is one month longer than the CRoW Act statutory minimum (which runs 1 March to 31 July). August is an additional month per Trust policy.
- No dogs in the water (lakes, rivers, or at the waterfall) - the Trust explicitly prohibits this.
- Off-lead permitted in broader woodland areas outside the nesting season and away from water.

**Car park note:** The car park barriers lock at 5pm every day. If you are inside the woods when the car park closes, your vehicle will be locked in. This is a genuine practical hazard.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Waterfall area: The River Llan waterfall is a Victorian man-made structure of massive masonry blocks with iron ties, cascading in three channels. The Trust warns the waterfall, lakes, and river can be "very dangerous" with "hidden hazards." Dogs must not enter the water near the waterfall.
- River Llan: Can rise quickly after heavy rain. Dogs must not enter any water on site (Trust rule).
- Steep paths: The path down into the valley towards the waterfall and upper lake is steep and can be very muddy and slippery in wet weather. Older dogs or those with joint problems may struggle.
- 5pm car park closure: Non-negotiable year-round. Plan accordingly.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Penllergare Valley Woods car park: SA4 9GS (off Junction 47 of the M4). £3 for up to 3 hours; £4 all day. Card and coin. Season tickets available (£100/year from the coffee shop). Open 9am-5pm daily. Barriers lock at 5pm.

**Facilities:** Visitor Centre and Coffee Shop open 10am-4pm daily (closed Christmas Day). Toilets. The coffee shop welcomes dogs in certain areas (lead required; check signage for dog-free zones).

**Access from M4:** Exit J47, signposted from the roundabout. Approximately 5 minutes from the motorway.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Coffee Shop at Penllergare Valley Woods - most convenient, dogs allowed in certain areas with leads.
- No prominent dog-friendly pub immediately adjacent. Nearest options are in Swansea (approximately 8km east) or Gorseinon (approximately 3km north). The site is most easily accessed from the M4.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Can my dog go off-lead at Penllergare Valley Woods?**
A: Dogs can be off-lead in the woodland areas outside the nesting season. However, leads are required from March to August (inclusive) to protect nesting birds - note this is one month longer than the statutory CRoW Act requirement (which ends 31 July). Leads are also required at all times near the waterfall, lakes, and river, and in the visitor centre, coffee shop, garden, and car park areas.

**Q: Can my dog go in the water at Penllergare?**
A: No - the Penllergare Trust explicitly prohibits dogs from entering the lakes, river, or waterfall area. These areas can be very dangerous with hidden hazards. Keep your dog on a lead near all water features on site.

**Q: Who manages Penllergare Valley Woods?**
A: The site is managed by the Penllergare Trust, a charitable organisation that has been restoring the Victorian estate since the 1990s. It is not managed by the National Trust, Cadw, or any local council.

**Q: What is the waterfall at Penllergare like?**
A: A Victorian-era man-made structure built by the Dillwyn Llewelyn family using massive masonry blocks held together with iron ties. The River Llan cascades in three separate channels over the structure. Genuinely impressive, particularly after heavy rainfall. There is a viewing point, but the surrounding area is wet and slippery and dogs must be kept on leads and out of the water.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Lead restriction starts 1 March. Bluebells and spring wildflowers make this one of the best seasons to visit. Paths can be muddy.
- Summer (June to August): Woodland canopy provides shade. Leads still required throughout August (Trust policy). Good for shaded walking on hot days.
- Autumn (September to November): Lead restriction lifts from 1 September (Trust end date is August). Autumn colour in the mixed woodland. Often muddy on valley floor paths.
- Winter (December to February): Open daily but paths very muddy. Waterfall most dramatic after heavy rain. Note the 5pm car park closure is non-negotiable.

## WALK 6: Llansteffan Castle and Estuary Walk

Location: Llansteffan, Carmarthenshire (Sir Gaerfyrddin)
Welsh: Castell Llansteffan, Afon Tywi, Bae Caerfyrddin
App data: 4.6 miles (7.4km), moderate, off-lead partial, no livestock

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 4.6 miles | CONFIRMED - consistent with the 7.7km / 4.7-mile Llansteffan long walk (Discover Carmarthenshire) |
| Difficulty | Moderate | CONFIRMED - steep castle ascent, coastal cliff sections, farmland paths that can be muddy |
| Off-lead | Partial | CONFIRMED but needs qualification: northern beach dog ban (1 May to 30 September) and Cadw lead requirement at the castle |
| Livestock | No livestock | FLAG - the route passes Lord's Park Farm and farmland sections. "No livestock" as an absolute statement is overconfident. Livestock possible. |
| Castle management | - | Cadw (Welsh Government's historic environment service). Free entry. |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

- Castle (Castell Llansteffan): Managed by Cadw. Free entry. Dogs welcome on short leads on the ground floor level only. Upper levels and tower stairs are not accessible to dogs. Cadw requires leads and fouling to be removed.
- Beach: Llansteffan Beach has a seasonal dog ban at the northern end from 1 May to 30 September. The southern and middle sections remain open to dogs year-round.
- Estuary: The Tywi Estuary must not be crossed even at low tide. Strong tidal currents and extensive shifting sandbanks make crossing extremely dangerous. Do not allow dogs to attempt estuary crossings.
- Farmland: The route passes Lord's Park Farm and alongside farmland. Dogs on leads when on or near farmland sections.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Tidal estuary: Swimming in the Tywi Estuary channels is dangerous due to strong tidal currents and shifting sandbanks. Dogs should not swim in the estuary channels. Beach paddling in shallow sandy sections near the shoreline is fine.
- Castle hazards: Cadw warns of falling masonry, uneven surfaces, and heights. The path to the castle is steep and slippery when wet. Dogs on leads throughout.
- Quicksand note: Quicksand as a specifically named hazard at Llansteffan is not confirmed by sources. The hazard to communicate is the tidal currents in the Tywi Estuary and the warning against attempting to cross. The estuary has shifting sandbanks which reinforce the no-crossing advice.
- Beach type: Llansteffan Beach is a sandy beach backed by grass. Two free car parks at either end with toilets.
- Farmland/mud: Routes can be very muddy on farmland sections in wet weather.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Llansteffan Beach Car Park (The Green): SA33 5LW. Free. Public toilets adjacent. Main start/finish for the walk.
- Second beach car park at the far end of the beach. Also free.

**Castle access:** Free entry. Managed by Cadw. Opening hours approximately 10am-4pm; closed 24-26 December and 1 January. The castle is reached via a steep footpath from the car park area (approximately 15-minute ascent).

**Facilities:** Public toilets at the beach car park. No on-site cafe at the castle. The village has a pub and a shop.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Castle Inn, High Street, Llansteffan, SA33 5JG - confirmed dog-friendly traditional Welsh pub. Tel: 01267 612727. Good pub grub, locally sourced.
- The Inn at the Sticks, High Street, Llansteffan, SA33 5JG - gastropub with rooms next door to the Castle Inn. Confirmed very dog-friendly. Locally celebrated food with Welsh ingredients. Dog-friendly rooms available.
Both are in the village centre, a short walk from the beach car park.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Is Llansteffan Castle free to visit?**
A: Yes - Castell Llansteffan is managed by Cadw and is free to enter. Dogs are welcome on short leads on the ground floor areas. Upper levels, tower stairs, and some interior areas are not accessible to dogs. Open approximately 10am-4pm; closed 24-26 December and 1 January.

**Q: Can my dog go on Llansteffan Beach all year?**
A: Not on all sections. There is a seasonal dog ban at the northern end of Llansteffan Beach from 1 May to 30 September. The southern and middle sections remain open to dogs year-round. Check signage on arrival.

**Q: Is it safe to let my dog swim in the Tywi Estuary?**
A: Do not allow dogs to swim in the estuary channels or attempt to cross the river, even at low tide. The Tywi Estuary has strong tidal currents and shifting sandbanks. Beach paddling in the shallow sandy sections at the shoreline is fine, but keep dogs away from the main estuary channels.

**Q: Are there livestock on the walk route?**
A: The route passes alongside Lord's Park Farm and farmland sections. While not a heavily livestock-grazed circuit, dogs should be on leads when passing farmland. The beach and castle sections have no livestock.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Good walking conditions. Wildflowers on the coastal path. Northern beach dog ban starts 1 May.
- Summer (June to August): Popular family destination. Northern beach dog-free. Southern beach remains accessible. Village gets busier.
- Autumn (September to November): Northern beach opens to dogs again from 1 October. Views across the estuary particularly striking in autumn light. Farmland paths can be very muddy.
- Winter (December to February): Quiet and wild. Castle closed 24-26 December and 1 January. Free parking remains. Excellent for dog walking with no crowds.

## WALK 7: Rhossili Down Ridge Walk

Location: Rhossili (Welsh: Rhosili), Gower, Swansea
App data: 4 miles (6.4km), moderate, off-lead partial, livestock true

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 4 miles | CONFIRMED - National Trust Rhossili Down, Hillend and Beach Walk is cited at approximately 6.5km (4 miles), consistent with the app. |
| Difficulty | Moderate | CONFIRMED - steep ascent onto the Down, exposed ridge, steep steps on descent |
| Off-lead | Partial | CONFIRMED - beach section off-lead year-round; Down has livestock year-round and CRoW Act applies on open moorland 1 March to 31 July |
| Livestock | True | CONFIRMED - semi-feral Welsh Mountain ponies and sheep graze Rhossili Down year-round |

**Confirmed as a genuinely separate walk from Walk 3.** Walk 3 (Rhossili Bay and Worms Head) heads south and west from the car park toward Worms Head. Walk 7 (Rhossili Down Ridge) climbs north from the car park onto the Down ridge (The Beacon, 193m) and descends north to Hillend, returning along the beach. Both share the same car park but cover completely different terrain.

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Rhossili Down is CRoW open access land. Under the CRoW Act 2000, dogs must be on a lead no longer than 2 metres from 1 March to 31 July. This is a legal requirement.

Outside 1 March to 31 July, dogs must still be kept under close control near the semi-feral ponies and sheep that graze year-round. The National Trust guidance states "keep under close control as livestock are present throughout the year."

On the beach section (returning along Rhossili Bay), dogs can be off-lead year-round with no restrictions.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Exposed ridge: Rhossili Down at 193m is the highest point on the Gower Peninsula. Completely exposed with no shelter. High winds are common year-round and extreme in winter. Wind-chill can be severe. Dogs and walkers can be knocked off balance in strong gusts.
- Semi-feral ponies: The Welsh Mountain ponies on Rhossili Down are not domesticated. Keep dogs on leads and do not approach the ponies. Dogs chasing ponies is a serious risk - ponies can become distressed and kick. Even a reliably recalled dog must be on a lead here during the CRoW Act period.
- Steep descent: The return involves a steep set of steps down to the beach at the Hillend end. Can be slippery in wet conditions. Dogs may need assistance on steps.
- Water on ridge: No reliable water access on the ridge. Carry water for dogs, especially in summer.
- Ticks: Bracken in summer can be tall and harbour ticks. Check dogs and yourself after the walk. Peak season April to October.
- Terrain: Heathland - heather, bracken, rough grass.

**The Beacon:** At 193m, the Beacon is the highest point on the Gower Peninsula and is also the site of a Bronze Age cairn approximately 4,000 years old.

**URGENT NOTICE - Worms Head Hotel:** Same closure applies as noted in Walk 3. The Worms Head Hotel was destroyed by fire on 14 July 2025 and demolished September 2025. Not currently operational.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- National Trust Rhossili Car Park: SA3 1PR. Same car park as Walk 3. £6/day, £3 for 2 hours. Free for NT members. Coin-only pay machines.
- Both Walk 3 and Walk 7 start from this car park but head in opposite directions.

**Facilities:** National Trust Visitor Centre and toilets at the car park (same as Walk 3).

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe (Worms Head Hotel closed):**
- King's Head Inn, Llangennith, SA3 1HX - approximately 4km north via the B4247. Confirmed dog-friendly, dogs welcome in the bar. Currently the best nearby option.
- King Arthur Hotel, Reynoldston, SA3 1AD - dogs welcome in the beer garden.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Is the Rhossili Down Ridge Walk different from the Rhossili Bay and Worms Head walk?**
A: Yes - they are genuinely separate walks that happen to share the same National Trust car park (SA3 1PR). The Down Ridge Walk climbs north onto the ridge (reaching The Beacon at 193m, the highest point on Gower) and returns via the beach at Hillend. The Worms Head walk heads south and west along the headland. Different terrain, different landmarks, different distances.

**Q: Are the ponies on Rhossili Down dangerous to dogs?**
A: The semi-feral Welsh Mountain ponies are not domesticated and can kick if a dog approaches or disturbs them. Keep your dog on a lead near the ponies (also a legal CRoW Act requirement from 1 March to 31 July). Do not let your dog chase or approach the ponies. Maintain a wide berth when ponies are on the path.

**Q: Do I need to keep my dog on a lead on Rhossili Down?**
A: Yes, during the legal period. Under the CRoW Act 2000, dogs must be on a lead no longer than 2 metres on open access land from 1 March to 31 July. Rhossili Down is CRoW open access land. Outside this period, dogs must still be under close control due to the year-round ponies and sheep. The beach return section is off-lead year-round.

**Q: Is there water for dogs on the ridge?**
A: There is no reliable water source on Rhossili Down. Always carry water for your dog, especially in summer. The beach section on the return allows dogs to access the sea (salt water should not be drunk in quantity). Bring your own supply.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): CRoW lead requirement starts 1 March. Lambing on the Down - extra care essential. Good visibility for ridge views.
- Summer (June to August): Exposed ridge can be hot and sunny with no shade. High tick risk in bracken. Strong UV on the open ridge. Carry water. The Beacon is popular in good weather.
- Autumn (September to November): Lead restriction ends 31 July. Heather and bracken colours excellent in September. Fewer visitors. Check dogs for ticks.
- Winter (December to February): Exposed and potentially very cold and windy on the ridge. Short days limit time. The return via the beach is dramatic in winter. Ponies graze the Down year-round.
