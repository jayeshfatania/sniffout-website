## WALK 1: Stiperstones and the Devil's Chair

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Stiperstones and the Devil's Chair
- **Location:** Stiperstones, Shropshire Hills AONB
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** SY5 0NL (Knolls Car Park)
- **Latitude:** 52.5735
- **Longitude:** -2.9319
- **Approximate distance:** 8.4 km (5.2 miles circular from Knolls Car Park)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate
- **Terrain type:** Quartzite ridge with dramatic rocky outcrops, heather and gorse heathland, some boggy sections after rain. Extremely rocky underfoot — good footwear essential. Exposed on the ridge. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** On-lead only
- Dogs must be kept on a lead or under very close control at all times across the reserve. This is confirmed by multiple sources including Natural England and the dogfriendlydestinations.com listing: "will need to be kept under close control or on leads at all times." (HIGH)
- Stiperstones is both a National Nature Reserve (NNR) and CRoW Act open access land. On open access land the CRoW Act mandates dogs on a short fixed lead (maximum 2 metres) from 1 March to 31 July due to ground-nesting birds. At all other times on open access land, dogs must be under close control near livestock. Since sheep graze year-round and ground-nesting birds include skylarks and meadow pipits, the practical guidance from the reserve is leads at all times. (HIGH)
- A Natural England open access restriction consultation was held in 2020 (case numbers 2014107424–2014107431) relating to seasonal restrictions at Stiperstones NNR — outcome not extractable from public-facing pages, but the reserve's standard guidance remains leads throughout. (MEDIUM)
- **Livestock present:** Yes, year-round. Sheep graze throughout the NNR. The circular route via Hollies Farm also passes through cattle fields. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** CRoW Act lead requirement 1 March to 31 July (ground-nesting birds). Livestock present means effective year-round lead requirement. The reserve is a NNR — heightened wildlife protection applies. Red grouse are present: associated with upland management. No confirmed formal grouse moor shooting management found, but red grouse and curlew are recorded breeding species.

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Knolls Car Park, SY5 0NL. Free with suggested voluntary donation of approx £1.50/hour. Approximately 30-40 unmarked spaces plus 2 disabled bays. Narrow single-track approach road. Alternative: The Bog Visitor Centre (larger, also free with donations). (HIGH)
- **Toilets:** Seasonal. The Bog Mine Visitor Centre has a volunteer-run facility open approximately April to October. Nothing at Knolls Car Park. (MEDIUM)
- **Cafe or pub:** The Stiperstones Inn, Stiperstones, SY5 0LZ (01743 791327). Dog-friendly confirmed: dogs welcome in one of the two dining rooms and in the beer garden. Open 11:30am–11pm (midnight Fri/Sat). Food served 12pm–9pm. Full menu including veggie/vegan options. (HIGH) The Bog Visitor Centre has a volunteer-run cafe when open. The Mytton Arms, Habberley Road, SY5 0TP is also mentioned as dog-friendly. (MEDIUM)
- **Public transport:** Minsterley Motors bus 552/553 runs Shrewsbury to Stiperstones (stopping at Stiperstones Inn), journey approx 39 minutes, operates Monday to Friday with approximately 4 services per day. Shropshire Hills Shuttle Bus (seasonal, Easter to October weekends) connects Church Stretton, Long Mynd and Stiperstones. (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **adders** — April to September. Well documented at Stiperstones. Heather and gorse heathland is prime adder habitat. Dogs nosing through vegetation at the rocky outcrops are at particular risk in spring when adders are emerging. This is flagged on multiple sources as a notable hazard. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Heather and long grass throughout the reserve. (HIGH)
- **livestock** — Year-round. Sheep throughout NNR. Cattle in fields on the Hollies Farm section of the circular route. (HIGH)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Skylarks, meadow pipits, curlew and red grouse all confirmed breeding species. CRoW Act lead requirement applies on open access land during this period. (HIGH)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Exposed rocky quartzite surfaces heat in direct sun. (MEDIUM)
- **mountain-bikers** — Year-round. Bridleways on the lower flanks of the reserve used by cyclists. (LOW)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The Devil's Chair quartzite outcrop is an extraordinary sensory environment — wind-blasted, atmospheric, unlike anything in the Midlands. The ridge walk connects multiple named tors (Shepherd's Rock, Devil's Chair, Manstone Rock) with constant wide-open views. The heather moorland gives dogs huge amounts to sniff even on lead. The Bog Mine area adds industrial history interest (old lead mining workings) with interesting smells and textures.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Wide, open ridge with space to walk alongside without crowding. Dramatic sky and views over Long Mynd and into Wales. The Stiperstones Inn is a genuinely welcoming dog pub — one of the better rural pubs in Shropshire for dogs, with indoor dining option. Varied terrain including woodland sections on the descent.
- **Honest practical note:** Dogs must be on lead throughout this reserve due to year-round livestock and ground-nesting bird protection. The terrain is very rocky and uneven — small dogs may struggle on the approach to the outcrops themselves. Adders are well documented here: keep dogs out of deep heather especially in spring.

## WALK 2: The Long Mynd and Carding Mill Valley

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** The Long Mynd and Carding Mill Valley
- **Location:** Church Stretton, Shropshire
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** SY6 6JG (National Trust Carding Mill Valley car park)
- **Latitude:** 52.5458
- **Longitude:** -2.8224
- **Approximate distance:** 8.0 km (5 miles, Burway Loop from Carding Mill Valley — a good representative circular. Shorter options: Lightspout Waterfall 2.4 km; longer: full Long Mynd circular 13.4 km)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate (Burway Loop). Easy options in valley. Hard for the full 13 km circuit.
- **Terrain type:** Mixed. The valley floor is a well-surfaced path alongside a stream. The moorland plateau is open heather, grass and bog — can be very wet. The Burway road is a tarmac single track used as access. Steep ascent from valley to plateau on most routes. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- The Long Mynd is CRoW Act open access land. Dogs must be on a short lead (max 2m) from 1 March to 31 July on the open moorland plateau due to ground-nesting birds. At all other times, dogs must be under close control near livestock. (HIGH)
- National Trust guidance explicitly states: keep dogs on lead at all times due to sheep and wild horses. This reflects the practical reality — livestock is unpredictable and present year-round. The Carding Mill Valley floor itself is narrower and the NT asks for leads near the tea room, car park and facilities. (HIGH)
- The valley stream offers an off-lead swimming opportunity in practice, though the NT guidance of leads throughout is the official position. Dogs do swim in the stream regularly according to visitor accounts. (MEDIUM)
- **Livestock present:** Yes, year-round. Sheep managed by Longmynd Commoners graze the plateau. Wild ponies (Long Mynd breed) also graze the common land in groups — they can be encountered anywhere on the plateau. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** CRoW Act 1 March to 31 July lead requirement on open access land. Lambing particularly acute February to April — NT asks for extra care. Wild ponies present at all times on plateau.

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** National Trust Carding Mill Valley car park, SY6 6JG. £5/day, free for NT members. Approximately 150+ spaces. Gets very busy on weekends and school holidays — arrive before 10am or use overflow. Blue Badge bays available. (HIGH)
- **Toilets:** Yes. At the Chalet Pavilion visitor centre by the tea room. (HIGH)
- **Cafe or pub:** National Trust Chalet Pavilion Tea Room — dogs welcome inside with water bowls provided. Serves light lunches, cakes, locally made ice cream. (HIGH) Church Stretton town (15-min walk or short drive) has additional dog-friendly cafes and pubs. Jemima's Kitchen in Church Stretton noted as dog-friendly. (MEDIUM)
- **Public transport:** Excellent. Church Stretton station (Shrewsbury–Hereford line, Transport for Wales) is a 15-minute walk from Carding Mill Valley. Regular trains from Shrewsbury, Ludlow, Hereford. Shropshire Hills Shuttle Bus (seasonal, Easter to October weekends) runs to Carding Mill Valley from Church Stretton station. (HIGH)

### 4. Hazards

- **livestock** — Year-round. Sheep and wild ponies on the plateau. Ponies can approach dogs. (HIGH)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. CRoW Act lead requirement on open access moorland. Skylarks, curlew, red grouse all confirmed breeding species on Long Mynd. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Long heather and grass on the moorland plateau. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to September. Heather moorland is adder habitat. Less prominently flagged here than at Stiperstones but present in moorland habitat. (MEDIUM)
- **flooding** — October to March. The valley stream can overflow after heavy rain. The Carding Mill Valley path is prone to flooding at its lower end. (MEDIUM)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Exposed rock and the tarmac Burway road heat up in direct sun. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The stream running the full length of Carding Mill Valley is the standout dog feature — most dogs end up in it. The contrast between the sheltered valley and the wild, open moorland plateau is dramatic. Wild ponies on the plateau give dogs something genuinely unexpected to encounter. The Lightspout Waterfall (small but impressive) is a natural focal point for a shorter walk.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** One of the few National Trust sites where the tea room actively welcomes dogs inside with water bowls. The stream is a reliable swimming spot in warmer months. The open moorland gives genuinely big sky walking. Well-maintained path in the valley makes it accessible for less confident dogs on lower sections.
- **Honest practical note:** Leads required in the car park and at the tea room regardless of season. On the moorland plateau, the CRoW Act requires a lead from 1 March to 31 July. Wild ponies are not dangerous but can unsettle dogs — give them space and don't let dogs chase them.

## WALK 3: Wenlock Edge

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Wenlock Edge
- **Location:** Much Wenlock, Shropshire
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** TF13 6EN (National Trust car park west of Much Wenlock, near Much Wenlock) or TF13 6DQ (Presthope car park)
- **Latitude:** 52.5933
- **Longitude:** -2.5728
- **Approximate distance:** 8.0 km (5 miles circular — the standard Blakeway Hollow to Major's Leap to Presthope circuit). Shorter NT-guided options: Jenny Wind walk 3.2 km; Major's Leap loop 2 miles (3.2 km). (HIGH)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate (full circular). Easy for the NT-guided shorter routes.
- **Terrain type:** Ancient woodland with limestone escarpment. Paths through oak and ash woodland, old quarry workings, grassy limestone clearings. Can be muddy after rain. Some moderate climbing on the full circular. No open moorland — very different character from Stiperstones and Long Mynd. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- Wenlock Edge is managed woodland and NT land, not CRoW Act open access moorland. The CRoW Act seasonal lead requirement (1 March to 31 July) does not apply to the woodland paths here in the same way as the open moorland walks. (MEDIUM)
- National Trust guidance: dogs welcome, "keep under close control," clean up after your dog. No livestock grazing in the woodland sections confirmed. The NT rates this a one-pawprint site (dogs welcome, facilities limited). (HIGH)
- In practice the woodland tracks allow dogs off lead where no livestock are present. The car parks require close control. No confirmed livestock in the main woodland sections. (MEDIUM)
- **Livestock present:** No confirmed livestock in the woodland sections. The Edge is agricultural land adjacent to fields but the walk itself passes through woodland managed by NT where no grazing is confirmed. (MEDIUM)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Keep under close control at all times. Clean up required. No formal off-lead prohibition in the woodland itself — but close control is the NT standard.

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** National Trust car park west of Much Wenlock, TF13 6EN (also noted as TF13 6AR in some sources — use TF13 6EN for sat nav to NT car park). Free for all visitors. Presthope car park TF13 6DQ also free, open 24 hours. (HIGH)
- **Toilets:** No confirmed on-site toilet facilities at the car parks. Much Wenlock town has public toilets. (MEDIUM)
- **Cafe or pub:** Wenlock Edge Inn, on the B4371 on Wenlock Edge itself — dog-friendly, confirmed reopened 2022 after 8-year closure, serves food, situated right on the escarpment with views. (HIGH) The George & Dragon, Much Wenlock — dog-friendly confirmed. Tea Junction & More, Much Wenlock — named Shropshire's most dog-friendly cafe venue. (HIGH)
- **Public transport:** Bus 436 (Select Bus Services) and 437 (Tanat Valley Coaches, launched September 2024) connect Much Wenlock with Shrewsbury and Bridgnorth. Journey from Shrewsbury approx 27 minutes. Services Monday to Saturday. The car parks are approximately 1 mile from Much Wenlock town centre. (HIGH)

### 4. Hazards

- **ticks** — March to October. Ancient broadleaf woodland is prime tick habitat. This is the highest tick-risk walk of the four. (HIGH)
- **mountain-bikers** — Year-round. The Edge has cycling trails. The full circular uses some bridleways shared with cyclists. (MEDIUM)
- **deer** — Year-round. Roe deer present in the woodland. Can excite dogs. Rut October to November. (MEDIUM)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Exposed limestone rock surfaces on the ridge get warm in direct sun. (LOW)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** This is a woodland walk first — very different from the open moorland of the other three Shropshire walks. The limestone escarpment gives sudden viewpoints opening through the trees over the Shropshire Hills. The old quarry workings are interesting and unusual — dogs pick up all sorts of scent in and around the old limekilns and quarry floors. Wild garlic carpets sections of the woodland in late spring. The 18-mile edge of limestone means the views and terrain shift throughout.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Good off-lead potential in the woodland sections with no confirmed livestock on the paths. Plenty of shade in summer. The Wenlock Edge Inn is right on the escarpment, making it genuinely convenient for a mid-walk stop. Varied terrain with interesting smells — limestone grassland, woodland floor, quarry debris.
- **Honest practical note:** Ticks are the main hazard to flag here — ancient broadleaf woodland is prime tick habitat from March to October. Check your dog thoroughly after every visit. The car parks are small and the lane to Presthope is narrow.

## WALK 4: Clent Hills

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Clent Hills
- **Location:** Clent, Worcestershire / West Midlands border
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** B62 0NL (Nimmings Wood car park, National Trust, Hagley Wood Lane near Romsley)
- **Latitude:** 52.4243
- **Longitude:** -2.0926
- **Approximate distance:** 8.1 km (5 miles, Walton Hill, Clent Hill and Four Stones circular from Nimmings Wood — the main full circuit). Shorter option: 4.5 km National Trust-guided circular. (HIGH)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate
- **Terrain type:** Open hillside grassland, mixed woodland, well-maintained footpaths and some grassy tracks. Moderate elevation gain to Walton Hill (316m). Views across Birmingham and into Wales. Not as remote as Stiperstones or Long Mynd — good infrastructure on site. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** Partial
- The National Trust states dogs can run off lead in large areas of the park but must be on a lead in: the car park, the cafe area, the Viewpoint path, and all areas grazed by livestock. (HIGH)
- From March to May during lambing season, dogs must be on lead around the Horse's Mane and Walton Hill areas specifically. (HIGH)
- Cattle also graze the hills — if cattle approach, NT recommends releasing the lead and calling the dog back when safe. (HIGH)
- Clent Hills is not CRoW Act open access land in the same way as the moorland walks — it is National Trust managed country park land with its own rules. (HIGH)
- **Livestock present:** Yes, seasonal. Sheep lamb March to May on Horse's Mane and Walton Hill. Cattle also graze the hills. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Lead required in car park, cafe area, Viewpoint path, and livestock areas. Strong recall essential. Lead during lambing season around Horse's Mane and Walton Hill specifically.

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Nimmings Wood car park, B62 0NL (National Trust, Hagley Wood Lane near Romsley). £4.80 all day, free for NT members. Open 9am to 4pm daily. Additional free parking available 24/7 at Walton Hill and Adams Hill. (HIGH)
- **Toilets:** Yes. Male and female ambulant toilets at Nimmings Wood car park entrance. (HIGH)
- **Cafe or pub:** Nimmings Wood Cafe at the car park — dog-friendly outdoor seating, serves sandwiches and hot drinks. (HIGH) The Fountain, Clent village, DY9 9PU — dog-friendly outdoor seating. (MEDIUM) The Bell and Cross, Holy Cross, DY9 9QL — well-regarded dog-friendly pub nearby. (MEDIUM)
- **Public transport:** Bus 192 (Birmingham to Kidderminster via Hagley) stops at Hagley, approximately 20-minute walk to Nimmings Wood. Bus 147 from Halesowen to Clent (Dark Lane) — 8 minutes journey but infrequent (approx every 4 hours). Bus 318 Stourbridge to Bromsgrove stops near Clent village (0.5 miles from hills). (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **livestock** — Seasonal: sheep lambing March to May on Horse's Mane and Walton Hill. Cattle year-round in some sections. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Mixed woodland and grassland provide tick habitat. Roe deer present. (MEDIUM)
- **deer** — Year-round. Roe deer confirmed in woodland sections. Rut October to November. (MEDIUM)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Open grassland habitats on hilltops. (MEDIUM)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Exposed hillside paths in direct sun. (MEDIUM)
- **mountain-bikers** — Year-round. Mountain biking trails exist at Clent Hills. Shared-use routes. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The Four Stones folly at the summit is the distinctive landmark — erected in 1763 by Lord Lyttleton of Hagley Hall, they sit on the hilltop with a toposcope and 360-degree views across to the Malvern Hills, Shropshire Hills and on clear days into Wales. The open ridgeline between Walton Hill and Clent Hill gives dogs big running space. The woodland descent through Nimmings Plantation provides a good contrast to the open hilltop. This is the most accessible of the four walks for Birmingham dog owners.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Large areas for genuine off-lead running on the open hilltop away from livestock. Good cafe at the car park with outdoor seating. Well-maintained paths throughout. Very family and dog-friendly atmosphere. The openness and views make it feel like bigger walking country than the elevation might suggest.
- **Honest practical note:** Very popular at weekends — arrive early or expect a full car park by mid-morning. During lambing season (March to May) dogs must be on lead around Horse's Mane and Walton Hill, which are the most open and scenic sections of the walk. The car park closes at 4pm — plan your return accordingly if using Nimmings Wood.

## RESEARCHER NOTES

### Significant Findings

**1. Stiperstones — Confirmed NNR lead requirement (HIGH confidence)**
Dogs must be on lead throughout Stiperstones NNR in practice. Livestock (sheep) present year-round. CRoW Act mandates lead from 1 March to 31 July on open access land. A Natural England consultation (2020) on access restrictions exists but outcome not publicly detailed on accessible pages — the in-practice position is leads throughout as confirmed by multiple sources. This is effectively an on-lead-only walk.

**2. Stiperstones adders — Well documented, flag prominently (HIGH confidence)**
Multiple independent sources confirm adders are present and well documented at Stiperstones. The heather and quartzite rock habitat is prime adder territory. This should be called out clearly in the walk description and hazard notices — it is the most significant dog health hazard on this walk after livestock control.

**3. Long Mynd wild ponies — Distinctive hazard not in standard hazard list (MEDIUM confidence)**
Wild ponies (Long Mynd breed) graze the common land and are encountered unpredictably on the plateau. They are not in the approved hazard pill list but should be mentioned in the description. The approved "livestock" hazard covers this.

**4. Long Mynd — Partial off-lead possible in practice (MEDIUM confidence)**
The National Trust guidance says leads throughout. However, the CRoW Act framework technically permits dogs off lead outside the 1 March to 31 July window and away from livestock on open access land. In practice, given wild ponies and sheep are encountered throughout, the NT's leads-throughout guidance is the safe position to communicate. The offLead field should be "Partial" to reflect the legal position while the description makes clear leads are expected on the plateau.

**5. Wenlock Edge — Off-lead partial in woodland (MEDIUM confidence)**
Unlike the moorland walks, Wenlock Edge is managed woodland without confirmed livestock grazing on the paths. The CRoW Act seasonal restriction applies to open access land, not managed woodland. NT guidance is close control rather than mandatory lead. This makes it the most genuinely off-lead-capable walk of the four, especially in the woodland sections. Flagged as Partial with good off-lead potential in woodland away from car parks and viewpoints.

**6. Clent Hills — Managed by National Trust (not Worcestershire County Council) (HIGH confidence)**
Clent Hills land was given to the National Trust by Worcestershire County Council in 1959 and is now managed by NT. The task brief referred to Worcestershire County Council management — this should be noted. Current parking charges, facilities and dog access rules are set by the National Trust, not the council.

**7. Clent Hills — Warrants its own page, distinct from Lickey Hills (HIGH confidence)**
Clent Hills and Lickey Hills are geographically and characteristically distinct. Clent Hills: open countryside, National Trust managed, Four Stones folly landmark, views to Shropshire Hills and Malvern Hills. Lickey Hills: closer to Birmingham suburban fringe (Rednal), Bromsgrove District Council/Birmingham City Council managed, different character. Independent page warranted.

**8. Clent Hills car park closing time — Practical flag (HIGH confidence)**
Nimmings Wood car park closes at 4pm. This is an unusual closing time for a popular day-walk destination. Worth flagging in the practical note so dog owners do not get caught out in winter when daylight extends past 4pm in summer but the car park gates close regardless.

**9. Long Mynd tea room — Dogs welcome inside (HIGH confidence)**
The National Trust Chalet Pavilion Tea Room at Carding Mill Valley explicitly welcomes dogs inside with water bowls provided. This is confirmed by the NT's own dog-walking page and multiple visitor accounts. Worth highlighting in Ailsa descriptions as it is relatively unusual for NT indoor facilities.

**10. Wenlock Edge Inn — Reopened 2022 after 8-year closure (HIGH confidence)**
The Wenlock Edge Inn was closed for 8 years and reopened August 2022. It is dog-friendly and sits right on the escarpment — an excellent mid-walk or post-walk option. Worth mentioning this is a relatively recent reopening as it may not appear on older listings.

### Critical Flags for Developer

- All four walks are in Shropshire or on the Shropshire/West Midlands border. A single Shropshire area page should cover all four, with a note that Clent Hills sits on the Worcestershire side of the border.
- offLead values: Stiperstones = "On-lead only"; Long Mynd = "Partial"; Wenlock Edge = "Partial"; Clent Hills = "Partial"
- All distances above are in km for the website. Convert to miles for WALKS_DB (divide by 1.609).
- Coordinates confirmed via park4night (Stiperstones), findlatitudeandlongitude.com (Long Mynd), search results (Wenlock Edge, Clent Hills).
- Clent Hills postcode B62 0NL confirmed for Nimmings Wood. Note: B62 is technically a Halesowen postcode (West Midlands) even though Clent is in Worcestershire — this is a known postcode boundary anomaly. The walk is in Worcestershire but uses a West Midlands postcode for the car park.
