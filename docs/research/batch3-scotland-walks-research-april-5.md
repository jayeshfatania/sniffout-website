## SCOTTISH OUTDOOR ACCESS CODE vs CRoW ACT - REFERENCE SUMMARY

The Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003 and the Scottish Outdoor Access Code (SOAC) grant everyone a statutory right of responsible access to almost all land and inland water in Scotland. This is broader and more permissive than the Countryside and Rights of Way (CRoW) Act 2000, which applies in England and Wales.

For dog walkers, the differences are significant:

Under CRoW in England and Wales: dogs on open access land must be kept on a short fixed lead (under 2 metres) near livestock and must be on a lead at all times between 1 March and 31 July during the bird nesting season on open country. These are legal requirements, not guidelines.

Under SOAC in Scotland: the standard is that a dog must be under "proper control" rather than necessarily on a lead. In practice, "proper control" means the dog must not disturb livestock or wildlife. When near livestock, SOAC guidance strongly recommends a short lead, but the law does not impose a fixed lead requirement in the same prescriptive way CRoW does.

Scotland also gives access rights across virtually all unenclosed land - mountain, moorland, woodland, and lochside paths - whereas CRoW access in England and Wales is largely restricted to designated open country and specifically mapped access land.

Key practical difference for app users: in Scotland, a well-trained dog under vocal control near livestock may technically comply with SOAC. In England and Wales, a physical lead is legally required near livestock on open access land regardless of training.

Sources: Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003; Scottish Outdoor Access Code (outdooraccess-scotland.scot); CRoW Act 2000.

| Manager | - | Historic Environment Scotland (HES) manages Holyrood Park as a Crown Park. NOT the City of Edinburgh Council. |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

The park is managed by Historic Environment Scotland (HES) under the Scottish Outdoor Access Code via the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003. HES publishes guidance that dogs must be under close control at all times.

- Near the three lochs (St Margaret's Loch, Dunsapie Loch, Duddingston Loch): dogs on leads. Swans and wildfowl present year-round. St Margaret's Loch swans are known to be aggressive when dogs approach.
- Salisbury Crags: dogs on leads strongly advisable. The path along the top (Radical Road) is unfenced in places with sheer vertical drops.
- Breeding season (approximately April to July): seasonal signs posted for bird restrictions. Peregrine falcons nest on Salisbury Crags - a nesting pair is confirmed at the site.
- HES Rangers patrol the park and have powers under the Historic Environment Scotland Act 2014 to manage access.
- Duddingston Loch is a SSSI and Local Nature Reserve - extra care required on the southern edge of the park.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Salisbury Crags: sheer vertical faces, partly unfenced. Dogs off-lead here face genuine fall risk.
- Arthur's Seat summit (251m): fully exposed. Wind speeds regularly exceed those in the city. Loose scree on descent routes.
- Swans at St Margaret's Loch and Dunsapie Loch: aggressive when provoked. Can injure dogs.
- Winter: grassy slopes below the summit freeze and become icy. Traction aids advisable for owners.
- Summer crowds: park is very busy Easter to October. Early morning (before 8am) strongly recommended for dog walkers.
- Breeding season (April to July): peregrine falcons, curlews, and skylarks nest. Off-lead dogs can cause serious disturbance.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking (all within or adjacent to Holyrood Park):**
- Broad Pavement car park (near Holyrood Palace): EH8 8DX. Pay-and-display, approximately £1/hour, 8:30am to 5:30pm.
- St Margaret's Loch car park: off Queen's Drive, often free, limited spaces.
- Dunsapie Loch car park: off Queen's Drive, EH16 5JT area. Limited spaces. Popular with dog walkers for the easier summit approach.
- Duddingston Loch car park: southern approach, quieter. EH15 3PX.
- Street parking on Queen's Drive (road through the park): free but limited.

**Facilities:** No cafe in the park itself. Public toilets near the Scottish Parliament / Holyrood entrance.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Sheep Heid Inn, 43-45 The Causeway, Duddingston, Edinburgh, EH15 3QA - on the Duddingston side of the park. Reputedly Scotland's oldest pub, dating to the 14th century. Dogs welcome. Outstanding post-walk option.
- Holyrood 9A, 9A Holyrood Road, Edinburgh, EH8 8AE - city-side option, dogs welcome with water bowls provided.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Can I let my dog off-lead anywhere in Holyrood Park?**
A: Yes, on the open grassy hillsides away from the lochs and under close control. However, near St Margaret's Loch, Dunsapie Loch, and Duddingston Loch, dogs should be on leads due to swans and wildfowl. On Salisbury Crags, keep your dog on a lead near the cliff sections. In breeding season (April to July), seasonal signs indicate areas where extra caution is needed.

**Q: Is Arthur's Seat suitable for all dogs?**
A: The main summit routes involve steep, rocky ascent with some scrambling. Dogs with joint problems may find the descent hard. The Dunsapie Loch approach from the east side car park is more gradual and suitable for most dogs. Small or elderly dogs may need carrying on the scrambling sections near the summit.

**Q: Who manages Holyrood Park and does the Scottish Outdoor Access Code apply?**
A: Historic Environment Scotland (HES) manages Holyrood Park as a Crown Park. The Scottish Outdoor Access Code applies, giving you the right to responsible access. HES Rangers can advise on current conditions (rangers@hes.scot).

**Q: Is there livestock in Holyrood Park?**
A: Permanent sheep grazing ended in 1977. However, HES and the Scottish Wildlife Trust occasionally bring in heritage breed animals for short-term conservation grazing to manage grassland habitats. Check current park notices before visiting with reactive dogs.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Breeding birds arrive. Keep dogs on leads near the crags (peregrine falcons nest on Salisbury Crags). Park gets busy from Easter.
- Summer (June to August): Busiest period. Arrive before 8am or after 6pm. Summit path heavily eroded in places.
- Autumn (September to November): Best season for dog walkers. Quieter, excellent views. Conservation grazing animals may be present.
- Winter (December to February): Quiet. Ice hazard on steep sections. Lochs attract large numbers of wintering wildfowl - keep dogs well clear.

## WALK 2: Threipmuir and Harlaw Reservoirs

Location: Balerno, Edinburgh
App data: 4.2 miles (6.8km), easy, off-lead partial, livestock true

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 4.2 miles | PLAUSIBLE - WalkHighlands lists the full Harlaw and Threipmuir circuit at approximately 5 miles (8km) for the full loop including Bavelaw Marsh. A tighter reservoir circuit comes in at 4-4.5 miles. The 4.2-mile figure is consistent with a route circling both reservoirs without extending to the higher Pentlands. |
| Difficulty | Easy | CONFIRMED - flat to gently undulating reservoir tracks |
| Off-lead | Partial | CONFIRMED and appropriate |
| Livestock | True | CONFIRMED - both sheep and cattle present. Sheep year-round on hillsides; Highland cattle reported on tracks in various areas. |
| Management | - | Reservoirs owned and operated by Scottish Water. Surrounding land and paths managed by Pentland Hills Regional Park, administered by the City of Edinburgh Council in partnership with Midlothian, West Lothian, and South Lanarkshire Councils. |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

The Scottish Outdoor Access Code applies throughout. Pentland Hills Regional Park guidance states dogs must be under "proper control" at all times. "If you cannot see your dog, it is unlikely to be under proper control."

- Near sheep and lambs: short lead or close heel required.
- The park's own guidance explicitly notes that dogs are shot every year in the Regional Park for worrying livestock. This should be communicated in the app.
- Scottish Water does not permit swimming or water activities in the reservoirs (public water supply). Dogs should not be encouraged to enter the reservoir water.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Livestock throughout the route. Sheep lambing approximately March to May - highest-risk period.
- Cattle can be present on or near the tracks. Pentland Hills Regional Park has warned about cattle intimidating walkers.
- Reservoir edges: Harlaw and Threipmuir reservoir banks are accessible but water is cold and drops away sharply. Dogs that enter the water face difficulty exiting. Dogs should not swim in these reservoirs (Scottish Water supply).
- Ticks: The Pentland Hills are a known and significant tick habitat. The combination of long grass, bracken, deer, and sheep creates ideal conditions. Use tick prevention before visiting and check the dog thoroughly after every visit. Peak season April to October but year-round vigilance needed.
- Track surfaces can be muddy in wet weather, particularly on the western side of Threipmuir.
- Wind exposure increases on the higher paths if extending beyond the reservoir circuit onto the open hills.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Harlaw car park (Harlaw House Visitor Centre), Threipmuir Road, Balerno. EH14 7JT. Charges apply (introduced after 2021 community consultation). Facilities include public toilets and visitor information point.
- Threipmuir car park, Mansfield Road, Balerno. EH14 7JT area. Closer to Threipmuir Reservoir. Smaller, fewer facilities. Charges also apply.
- Exact current charge rates were not confirmed in available sources. Check Pentland Hills Regional Park website (pentlandhills.org) before visiting.

**Facilities:** Toilets and visitor information at Harlaw House. No food outlet at the car parks.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Balerno Inn, 4 Main Street, Balerno, EH14 7EQ. Modern dining pub, family-run, CAMRA listed. 5-minute drive or walk from the car parks. Confirm dog policy by calling ahead.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Can my dog swim in Threipmuir or Harlaw Reservoir?**
A: No. Scottish Water owns and manages both reservoirs as drinking water supply. Swimming is not permitted for humans or dogs. Dogs should not be encouraged to enter the water. The reservoir banks are accessible but the water is cold, drops away sharply, and a dog that enters may struggle to get out. Keep dogs on a lead near the reservoir edges.

**Q: Are there ticks in the Pentland Hills?**
A: Yes - the Pentlands are significant tick habitat. Sheep, deer, and dense vegetation create ideal conditions. Use a vet-approved tick prevention treatment before visiting. Check your dog immediately after the walk - between toes, around ears, under the collar. Peak season is April to October but ticks can be active in mild winters.

**Q: What livestock will I encounter and when are restrictions tightest?**
A: Sheep and cattle both graze the hills adjacent to the reservoir paths. Sheep lambing typically runs March to May. During lambing, even a dog on a lead can stress a ewe enough to cause her to abandon a lamb. Pentland Hills Regional Park states explicitly that dogs have been shot in the park for worrying livestock. Keep your dog on a short lead whenever livestock are visible.

**Q: Who manages this area and what access rights apply?**
A: The Pentland Hills Regional Park is managed by the City of Edinburgh Council in partnership with neighbouring authorities. The Scottish Outdoor Access Code applies. The reservoirs are owned by Scottish Water - access rights under the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003 allow you to walk the paths, but Scottish Water restrictions on the water itself still apply.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Lambing season - dogs on leads throughout the route. Ticks active. Bluebells in woodland sections.
- Summer (June to August): Busiest period. Ticks at peak activity. Long days make evening walks feasible.
- Autumn (September to November): Excellent colours. Fewer crowds. Ticks reducing but still present into October. Paths muddy after rain.
- Winter (December to February): Quieter. Paths can freeze. Reservoir surfaces may partially freeze in cold spells - a hazard for dogs that might attempt to walk on ice.

| Difficulty | Easy | CONFIRMED - wide well-maintained woodland path, some stepped sections near Ossian's Hall |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

NTS states dogs are welcome at The Hermitage and must be kept under control at all times. The Scottish Outdoor Access Code applies throughout.

- No specific seasonal dog restrictions for red squirrel protection at this site. However, dog owners are asked to keep dogs under close control in the woodland to reduce disturbance to red squirrels, which are present in the Douglas fir canopy.
- The gorge section at Black Linn Falls is physically dangerous for off-lead dogs. The River Braan drops through a deep gorge with sheer rock walls. Ossian's Hall overlooks the gorge directly above the drop. A dog that falls here cannot be rescued safely.
- FLS-managed Craigvinean sections are open under SOAC. Dogs may be off-lead in the forest away from the gorge.

**App correction: change "off-lead full" to "off-lead partial" and add specific safety note about the falls gorge.**

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Black Linn Falls gorge: The River Braan falls through a deep gorge with sheer rock walls. This is the most significant safety hazard on the walk. Keep dogs on leads at the falls viewpoint and along all gorge-edge sections.
- River Braan swimming: dogs can swim in calmer sections upstream from the falls. The water is clear and cold. Strong currents exist near and below the falls - swimming only in calm, shallow sections well away from the gorge.
- Douglas firs: the giant Douglas firs on this site are among the tallest trees in Britain. In high winds, branches and debris fall from these enormous trees. The car park and main path pass beneath them. Avoid in high-wind conditions.
- Path can be slippery in wet conditions (leaf litter on boardwalk sections).

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- The Hermitage NTS car park, A9 approach road, Dunkeld. PH8 0JR (approximately 2 miles west of Dunkeld town centre on the A9). Pay-and-display, approximately £5 per vehicle. NTS members park free. An accessible lower car park is available near the railway bridge with disabled parking.

**Facilities:** Toilets in the car park. No on-site cafe at the Hermitage.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Taybank Hotel, Tay Terrace, Dunkeld, PH8 0AQ - riverside location, large beer garden, wood-fired pizzas. Dogs welcome. Highly rated.
- Palmerstons Cafe, 27 Atholl Street, Dunkeld, PH8 0AR - dog-friendly cafe, consistently recommended by dog walkers.
- Cafe Adventure, Dunkeld - outdoor seating with Tay views, dogs welcome outside.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Can my dog swim in the River Braan at the Hermitage?**
A: Yes, in the calmer upstream sections away from the falls and gorge. The Braan is clear and cold and dogs enjoy swimming here. Do not allow your dog into the water near Black Linn Falls - the current is extremely fast, the gorge walls are sheer, and a dog swept into the falls section cannot be rescued safely. Keep dogs on leads along the gorge path and at Ossian's Hall.

**Q: Are there red squirrels at the Hermitage?**
A: Yes. Red squirrels are present in the woodland and can often be seen in the Douglas fir canopy. The Hermitage is within a red squirrel conservation area. Dogs are not subject to specific legal restrictions for squirrel protection here, but keeping dogs under close control reduces disturbance.

**Q: How tall are the Douglas firs at the Hermitage?**
A: Among the tallest trees in Britain. The tallest specimen reached 63 metres (207 feet) before it was blown down in January 2017 storms. Other specimens stand at 50-60 metres - roughly the height of a 15-storey building. They were planted in the 19th century by the Dukes of Atholl from seeds imported from North America.

**Q: Who manages the Hermitage?**
A: The core site including Ossian's Hall and the riverside path along the Braan is managed by the National Trust for Scotland (free entry; car park charges apply). The Craigvinean woodland, used for longer route options, is managed by Forestry and Land Scotland. Both allow responsible access under the Scottish Outdoor Access Code.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Snowdrops and bluebells along the riverbank. Red squirrels active. Paths can be wet from snowmelt.
- Summer (June to August): Dense canopy keeps the walk cool even in hot weather. Car park fills early at weekends - arrive before 9am. River swimming for dogs in the calmer sections is popular.
- Autumn (September to November): Spectacular autumn colours. Douglas firs turn the woodland golden-red. Fungi on the forest floor. Arguably the best season for this walk.
- Winter (December to February): Falls at their most dramatic with high water levels. Path can be icy. The falls in spate are extraordinary but the gorge banks become even more dangerous.

## WALK 4: Tentsmuir Forest and Beach

Location: Leuchars, Fife
App data: 4.8 miles (7.7km), easy, off-lead partial, no livestock

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 4.8 miles | CONFIRMED - FLS Ice House Trail is 3.75 miles (6.2km). The longer circuit combining forest and beach reaches 4.8 miles (7.7km). The app figure is plausible for the extended route. |
| Difficulty | Easy | CONFIRMED - almost entirely flat coastal pinewood on sand, no elevation gain |
| Off-lead | Partial | CONFIRMED and important - leads required throughout the National Nature Reserve sections |
| Livestock | None | CONFIRMED - forest and beach environment, no grazing livestock |
| Management | - | Dual: forest managed by Forestry and Land Scotland. Tentsmuir National Nature Reserve (Tentsmuir Point, Morton Lochs, Tayport Heath) managed by NatureScot. |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

NatureScot explicitly requires dogs to be kept on leads throughout the NNR sections to protect the internationally significant seal colonies and diverse bird populations. This applies year-round but is most critical during:

- Common seal pupping season: June to July. Pups are born on the beach and can be abandoned by their mothers if disturbed.
- Grey seal pupping season: October to December.
- Bird nesting season: April to July.

FLS-managed forest sections follow SOAC. Dogs may be off-lead under close control in the main forest interior paths. In practice: leads in the NNR beach sections near Tentsmuir Point and around Morton Lochs; more freedom in the main forest paths. Signs in the NNR mark the boundaries.

NatureScot recommends staying at least 100 metres from hauled-out seals.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Tidal flats: The coastline at Tentsmuir is characterised by tidal sandflats extending well offshore. The tide comes in quickly here and walkers can become cut off if they venture too far north onto the tidal flats toward Tentsmuir Point. Check tide times before visiting (UKHO tide predictor). Safest 2-3 hours either side of low tide.
- RAF Leuchars (Leuchars Station) is immediately adjacent. Military aircraft and helicopter movements are frequent and can be loud and sudden. Some dogs find this distressing.
- Seal disturbance: Approaching seals too closely (with or without dogs) can cause the entire colony to stampede into the water, injuring pups. Keep 100 metres minimum from hauled seals.
- Deep sand on forest tracks can make progress slow.
- Open beach sections offer no shade in hot weather - dogs can overheat.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Kinshaldy Beach car park, Tentsmuir Forest, Leuchars. KY16 0DR. Operated by Forestry and Land Scotland. Charge: approximately £4/day (barrier system, coin and card accepted). Seasonal pass approximately £65. Blue Badge holders free. Summer 8am-9pm; winter 8am-dusk.
- Access: Follow brown "Kinshaldy Beach" signs from the church in Leuchars village centre. Approximately 1.5 miles on a narrow single-track road from the B945.

**Facilities:** No cafe or toilets at the car park. The beach and forest are entirely facilities-free.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Larick Cafe, The Larick Centre, Tayport, DD6 9AJ - award-winning community cafe, dog-friendly, open 7 days 10am-4pm. On the Fife Coastal Path. Approximately 4 miles from the car park.
- The Rhynd, Leuchars - dog-friendly pub, home-cooked food, 1.6 miles from Tentsmuir Forest, consistently well-rated locally.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Can I let my dog off-lead on the beach at Tentsmuir?**
A: On main beach sections away from the seal haul-out area, dogs may be off-lead in less sensitive sections. Within the National Nature Reserve - including Tentsmuir Point and its surroundings - NatureScot asks that dogs be kept on leads at all times to protect seals and nesting birds. Signs in the NNR mark the boundaries. In the forest interior, off-lead under proper control is generally acceptable.

**Q: Is the tide dangerous at Tentsmuir?**
A: Yes, caution is warranted. The tidal flats north of the beach extend well out to sea and the tide comes in rapidly. Do not walk far onto the tidal sands, especially with a dog that may run ahead. Always check tide times before visiting. Best visited 2-3 hours either side of low tide.

**Q: Are there seals at Tentsmuir all year round?**
A: Yes. Tentsmuir Point hosts both common seals and grey seals - one of the few places in Britain where both species can be seen together. Common seals are present year-round (pupping June to July). Grey seals arrive from autumn (pupping October to December). NatureScot asks visitors to keep at least 100 metres from hauled seals.

**Q: Is the noise from Leuchars military base a problem?**
A: Leuchars Station (formerly RAF Leuchars) is directly adjacent to the approach road. Military aircraft fly frequently and low. The walking route is entirely safe from a military access perspective - there is no restricted firing range on the route. However, the noise can be sudden and loud, which may startle anxious dogs. Worth noting for nervous dogs.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Best for forest birdsong. Red squirrels active. Begin of bird nesting season - increase lead use near dune and scrub.
- Summer (June to August): Common seal pupping (June to July). Strict NNR lead rules apply. Busy beach at weekends. Hot weather beach hazard for dogs.
- Autumn (September to November): Grey seal season begins October. Migrant birds on the shoreline. Quieter. Best photographic conditions.
- Winter (December to February): Grey seal colony remains. Very quiet. Storms create dramatic conditions on the beach. Forest provides good windbreak for walking.

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

The Scottish Outdoor Access Code applies throughout. The Loch Lomond Byelaws 2023 (in force November 2024) relate to water vessels and safety on the loch - they do not restrict dog walking on land. SOAC gives the right to responsible access.

- Lochside beach and shingle sections: dogs may be off-lead under proper control.
- West Highland Way section through the village: dogs should be on or near-lead due to other walkers and proximity of habitation.
- Conic Hill extension: dogs must be on leads in the enclosed fields at the base of the ascent. During lambing (approximately late April to mid-May), dogs are entirely prohibited from those fields. The open hill above is fine for off-lead under proper control.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Loch Lomond swimming: generally safe for dogs near Balmaha. Milarrochy Bay (a short distance south on the lochside path) is a popular and calm dog swimming spot. Water is cold but clean. Watch for boat traffic in summer (May to September) - keep dogs clear of the main channel.
- Midges: severe in the Balmaha area from May to September in still, humid conditions. For dogs and owners alike. Smidge or DEET products recommended.
- Crowds: The village of Balmaha is a major West Highland Way waypoint and popular family destination. The lochside path can feel very crowded between 10am and 3pm on peak summer days.
- Exposed tree roots in the woodland sections become slippery when wet.
- Lochside path can flood near Milarrochy in very wet spells.

**Conic Hill Extension Note:** Conic Hill (361m) is directly accessible from Balmaha village via the West Highland Way and adds approximately 3 miles (4.8km) and 330 metres of ascent to the walk. It sits directly on the Highland Boundary Fault Line. The summit views across Loch Lomond and the islands are exceptional and it is strongly worth mentioning in the app as an extension option. Dog note: leads required in enclosed fields at the base of the ascent; dogs prohibited from those fields during lambing (late April to mid-May).

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- National Park Centre car park, Balmaha, G63 0JQ. Managed by Stirling Council. Capacity approximately 177 spaces. Charge approximately £2.10 for up to 2 hours (full day rate also available). Payment by cash or RingGo app. Toilets open 9:30am to 5:30pm. Fills quickly from 10am on summer weekends - arrive before 9am.

**Facilities:** National Park Visitor Centre at the car park with information and toilets. St Mocha Coffee Shop and Ice Cream Parlour in the village (dog-friendly outside seating).

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Oak Tree Inn, Balmaha, G63 0JQ - Scotland's Top Pub award winner. Dogs welcome in the outdoor areas (beer garden). Serves pizza, burgers, fish and chips, steak pie. Dog-friendly accommodation available (additional charge). Directly at the start/end of the walk - ideal for post-walk refreshments.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Can my dog swim in Loch Lomond at Balmaha?**
A: Yes. Loch Lomond is generally safe for dog swimming near Balmaha. Milarrochy Bay, a short distance south on the lochside path, is a popular and calm spot. The water is clean and cold. Be aware of boat traffic in summer (May to September). Always supervise - loch water can be colder than expected even in summer.

**Q: Are there dog restrictions in Loch Lomond and Trossachs National Park?**
A: The Loch Lomond Byelaws 2023 (in force November 2024) relate to water vessels and loch safety - they do not restrict dog walking on land. The Scottish Outdoor Access Code applies throughout the National Park, giving the right to responsible access. Dogs should be under proper control, on leads near livestock, and poo picked up and removed.

**Q: Is Conic Hill worth adding to this walk?**
A: Yes, strongly. Conic Hill (361m) is a short but rewarding extension - approximately 3 extra miles (4.8km) and 330m of ascent. The summit views across Loch Lomond and the islands are exceptional. The hill sits directly on the Highland Boundary Fault Line. Note: dogs must be on leads in the enclosed fields at the base, and during lambing (late April to mid-May) dogs are not permitted in those fields at all.

**Q: How busy does Balmaha get in summer?**
A: Very. It is a major West Highland Way staging point and a popular day trip from Glasgow (approximately 45 minutes by car). The car park (177 spaces) fills by mid-morning on sunny summer weekends. Arrive before 9am or park in Drymen (3 miles away) and walk/use the bus. Early morning or evening visits with a dog are far more enjoyable.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Beautiful but busy from Easter. Lambing on Conic Hill fields (late April to May) - restrictions apply. Midges begin from May.
- Summer (June to August): Peak season. Midges severe. Boat traffic on the loch. Very busy paths. Best visited early morning.
- Autumn (September to November): Excellent. Midges gone by October. Outstanding autumn colours on oak and birch woodland. Quietest dog-walking season.
- Winter (December to February): Very quiet. Loch views dramatic in low winter light. Lochside path can be flooded in very wet spells near Milarrochy.

## WALK 6: Kinnoull Hill and Tay Valley Views

Location: Perth, Perth and Kinross
App data: distance not confirmed, difficulty not confirmed

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | Not confirmed | SUGGESTED: 3 miles (4.8km) for the standard Kinnoull Hill and Tower circuit. WalkHighlands and AllTrails both confirm approximately 4.7km for the woodland circular. The Deuchny Hill extension adds approximately 1.5 miles (2.4km). |
| Difficulty | Not confirmed | SUGGESTED: Moderate - cliff section near Kinnoull Tower is a genuine hazard requiring particular care; steep Quarry car park approach on the south side |
| Off-lead | Not confirmed | Set to partial - leads required near Kinnoull Tower cliff section |
| Livestock | Not confirmed | None on the route |
| Management | - | Jointly managed by Forestry and Land Scotland (229.2 hectares) and Perth and Kinross Council (76.2 hectares). Designated Scotland's first Woodland Park in 1991. Total area 305 hectares. |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Scottish Outdoor Access Code applies. No livestock on the paths. Dogs may generally be off-lead in the woodland. However:

- Near Kinnoull Tower and the cliff edge section: dogs must be on leads. The cliff on the south face drops approximately 150 metres (some sources cite 500 feet / 152 metres) and is partly screened by trees - walkers can be very close to the edge without fully appreciating the exposure.
- The path near the tower passes close to unfenced cliff sections. This is a genuine safety risk for off-lead dogs.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Kinnoull Tower cliff: The folly tower sits at the very edge of approximately 150-metre sheer drops on the south face. The cliff is partly obscured by mature woodland, creating a false sense of security. This section must be flagged clearly as dangerous for off-lead dogs. Walkers with young children should also exercise extreme caution.
- Viewpoint context: Kinnoull Hill is consistently cited as one of the finest accessible urban viewpoints in Scotland. The view over the meandering River Tay, with the Ochil Hills, Strathearn, and Tayside visible on clear days, is exceptional for a viewpoint requiring so little effort. VisitScotland promotes it as a Perthshire highlight.
- Red squirrels and roe deer present in the woodland.
- Boggy ground on lower paths in wet weather.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Jubilee car park, Muirhall Road, Perth, PH2 7LN. Free. North (gentle) side of the hill. Approximately 1.5 miles from Perth city centre.
- Corsiehill Quarry car park, off Muirhall Road area. Free. Steeper south approach.
- Directions from Perth: Cross the Tay on West Bridge Street (A85), continue on East Bridge Street, turn right on Muirhall Road, follow approximately 1.5 miles to the Jubilee car park.

**Facilities:** No cafe on site. Toilets in Perth city centre (15-minute walk or short drive).

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- Willowgate Cafe, Willowgate, Perth - riverside outdoor seating on the Tay, dogs welcome outside. Approximately 1.5 miles from the car park back into Perth.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Is the cliff near Kinnoull Tower genuinely dangerous?**
A: Yes. The south face of Kinnoull Hill drops approximately 150 metres. Kinnoull Tower sits at the very edge and the path passes close to unfenced cliff sections. The drop is partly screened by trees, which can create a false sense of security. Dogs must be kept on leads for the entire cliff-edge section near and around the tower.

**Q: Is Kinnoull Hill genuinely one of Scotland's best viewpoints?**
A: It is widely regarded as one of the best accessible urban viewpoints in Scotland. The view from the tower over the broad meandering arc of the River Tay, with the Ochil Hills and Strathearn visible on clear days, is exceptional for a viewpoint requiring so little effort to reach. VisitScotland promotes it as a Perthshire highlight.

**Q: Is there water access for dogs at Kinnoull Hill?**
A: No river or loch access on the main circuit. The River Tay is visible far below the cliffs but inaccessible from the hill path. Dogs can access the Tay on the Perth North Inch and South Inch riverside paths in the city, which connect to the Kinnoull area via Bridgend.

**Q: Who manages Kinnoull Hill and is there a charge to visit?**
A: Jointly managed by Forestry and Land Scotland and Perth and Kinross Council. Designated Scotland's first Woodland Park in 1991. No entry fee. Both car parks are currently free. Scottish Outdoor Access Code applies.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Bluebells in the beech woodland. Roe deer with young. Red squirrels active. Tay valley views particularly clear before the canopy closes in.
- Summer (June to August): Full canopy provides cool shade. Viewpoint busy on fine weekends. Long evenings make this excellent for after-work walks.
- Autumn (September to November): Outstanding. Beech and oak turn gold and orange. Most popular season. Red squirrel sightings more frequent. Fungi on the forest floor.
- Winter (December to February): Leafless canopy opens views through the woodland. Very quiet. Ice on steep paths and near cliff sections in cold spells - extra caution required.

## WALK 7: Elterwater and Langdale Valley

### LOCATION: LAKE DISTRICT, CUMBRIA, ENGLAND - NOT SCOTLAND

Elterwater is a village in the Great Langdale valley, approximately 4 miles west of Ambleside in the Lake District, Cumbria, England. Its name comes from Old Norse meaning "swan lake." This walk is in the Lake District National Park.

This walk must be moved from the Scotland batch to a separate England / Lake District batch. The Scottish Outdoor Access Code does NOT apply here. The Countryside and Rights of Way (CRoW) Act 2000 applies, with stricter lead requirements than SOAC.

---

Location: Great Langdale, Cumbria (Lake District)
App data: distance not confirmed, difficulty not confirmed, off-lead unknown, livestock unknown

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | Not confirmed | SUGGESTED: 4 miles (6.4km) for the standard Elterwater valley circuit combining Elterwater village, the tarn shore, Skelwith Bridge, and Baysbrown Wood. A shorter loop is approximately 3.7 miles (6.0km). Longer extensions reaching Chapel Stile run to 5-6 miles. |
| Difficulty | Not confirmed | SUGGESTED: Easy for the valley floor circuit. The route only becomes Moderate if extended to higher ground (Loughrigg Fell or Side Pike). |
| Off-lead | Unknown | Set to partial/restricted - extensive livestock throughout Great Langdale, CRoW Act requires leads near livestock and on open access land 1 Mar to 31 Jul |
| Livestock | Unknown | Set to true - extensive sheep throughout the valley including on the Elterwater village common |
| Management | - | National Trust owns approximately 12,170 acres (4,925 hectares) of Great Langdale and manages ten working farms. Lake District National Park Authority manages access rights and planning. Part of the Lake District UNESCO World Heritage Site. |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

The CRoW Act 2000 applies. In England, on open access land:
- Dogs must be kept on a short lead (not more than 2 metres) near livestock.
- Dogs must be on a short lead at all times between 1 March and 31 July during the bird nesting season on open country.
- Rights of way through farmland require dogs under close control.

Great Langdale has sheep throughout - including on the Elterwater village common. Dogs should be on leads for significant portions of this walk. The National Trust rates the Langdales as a "three pawprint" dog-friendly destination (welcome, but lead use frequent due to livestock).

This is more restrictive than the Scottish Outdoor Access Code for dog walkers.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Livestock throughout. Sheep on the village common at Elterwater, in fields, and on the open fell. Lambing approximately March to May.
- Great Langdale Beck: accessible for dog swimming at several points in the lower valley where the Beck broadens and slows. Avoid the upper, faster-flowing sections after heavy rain.
- Skelwith Force (waterfall near Skelwith Bridge): accessible but unguarded drop into the gorge. Keep dogs on leads near the falls.
- Stiles: several on the route may require dogs to be lifted or to use adjacent dog gates. Check route ahead for large dogs.
- Very busy in summer and autumn. Elterwater village and the Old Dungeon Ghyll hotel are major walker destinations. Car parks fill early.
- Langdale Pikes create fast-channelled winds in the valley in poor weather.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Elterwater car park, National Trust, Elterwater village. LA22 9HU. Pay-and-display. NT members park free. Approximately 40 spaces. Open-air, gravel.
- Stickle Ghyll car park: LA22 9JU. Larger, closer to upper valley. Pay-and-display, NT members free.

**Facilities:** Outdoor tap for dogs at the Elterwater car park area. No dedicated dog washing facilities.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Britannia Inn, Elterwater village, LA22 9HP - directly in the village. Dogs welcome in the bar (not the restaurant). Historic pub, open fires, real ales, outdoor seating. One of the most reliable dog-friendly pubs in the Lake District.

### 5. FAQ

**Q: Is Elterwater in Scotland or England?**
A: England - specifically Cumbria, in the Lake District National Park. The Scottish Outdoor Access Code does not apply. The CRoW Act (England and Wales) applies instead, with stricter lead requirements near livestock and during bird nesting season.

**Q: Do CRoW Act rules mean my dog must be on a lead in Langdale?**
A: Near livestock, yes - a physical lead under 2 metres is legally required. On open access land between 1 March and 31 July, dogs must also be on a short lead during the bird nesting season. Great Langdale has sheep throughout the valley including on the village common. In practice, your dog will need to be on a lead for significant portions of this walk.

**Q: Can my dog swim in Great Langdale Beck?**
A: Yes, in the safer sections of the lower valley where the Beck is broader and shallower. Avoid the upper, faster-flowing sections after heavy rain. Keep dogs on leads near Skelwith Force (near Skelwith Bridge) where the falls drop into a fast gorge.

**Q: Who manages the walks around Elterwater?**
A: The National Trust owns most of Great Langdale (approximately 12,170 acres) and manages the farms, footpaths, and car parks. The Lake District National Park Authority manages access rights and planning. The area is part of the Lake District UNESCO World Heritage Site.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Spring (March to May): Lambing. Dogs on leads throughout. Beautiful valley with fresh green. CRoW bird nesting rules apply from 1 March.
- Summer (June to August): Peak season. Valley car parks fill by 9am on summer weekends. Arrive early or use the bus from Ambleside. Beck swimming popular. CRoW nesting rules continue to 31 July.
- Autumn (September to November): Outstanding colours. The Langdale Pikes with autumn oak and birch are classic Lake District scenery. Busy but manageable on weekdays.
- Winter (December to February): Quiet. Valley can be wet and muddy. The walk is beautiful in frost. The Britannia Inn is welcoming in winter with open fires.
