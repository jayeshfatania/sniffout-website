## 1. Richmond Park
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are welcome throughout Richmond Park but mandatory leads apply during the deer birthing season (1 May to 31 July) and year-round in several specific zones.

**Lead rules:**
- Off-lead permitted across most of the park outside restricted periods, provided dogs stay a safe distance from deer
- Leads mandatory at ALL times in skylark protection zones: Lawn Field, Crown Field, and The Bog
- Leads mandatory around all lakes and ponds, including Pen Ponds, year-round
- Leads mandatory throughout the ENTIRE park from 1 May to 31 July (deer birthing season) - no exceptions
- Leads strongly advised near deer during the rutting season (September to November); the Royal Parks ask for a minimum 50m distance from deer and 100m if a stag is active
- Dogs on leads are permitted inside the Isabella Plantation
- Dogs (even on leads) are NOT permitted in Pembroke Lodge Gardens
- Maximum 4 dogs per walker for commercial operators (licence required)

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- 1 May to 31 July: all dogs must be on leads throughout the entire park (deer birthing season - compulsory, not advisory)
- September to November: leads strongly advised near deer during rutting season; dogs should maintain minimum 50m distance from deer
- Skylark zones (Lawn Field, Crown Field, The Bog): leads required year-round to protect ground-nesting skylarks

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Approximately 630 red and fallow deer roam freely throughout the park - the primary hazard for dogs
- It is a criminal offence for a dog to chase deer; prosecution can follow
- Deer are most dangerous during rutting season (September to November, stags) and birthing season (May to July, hinds with fawns)

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Deer: risk of injury if dog approaches, especially during rut and birthing season
- Pen Ponds and other water bodies: restricted access, leads required at water's edge
- Roads: car traffic enters the park; Richmond Park roads are open to vehicles
- Skylark nesting areas: dogs disturbing nests can cause nest abandonment

**Practical info:**
- Parking: multiple free car parks across the park (Kingston Gate, Broomfield Hill, Pen Ponds, Roehampton Gate, Robin Hood Gate, Ham Gate, Pembroke Lodge). Parking is currently free of charge; proposals for charges require Parliamentary approval and are not imminent as of early 2026. Disabled parking bays in all car parks; additional disabled-only car park at Isabella Plantation
- Facilities: Pembroke Lodge Cafe (no dogs inside), Roehampton Cafe, refreshment kiosks at Broomfield Hill, Pen Ponds, and Kingston Gate. Dogs not permitted inside cafes but outdoor seating available. Dog waste bins at Isabella Plantation gates and car parks. Free volunteer minibus (RP1) operates Mondays, Wednesdays, Fridays from 16 March to 13 November 2026

**Sources:** royalparks.org.uk/visit/parks/richmond-park/faqs; royalparks.org.uk press releases on deer birthing season lead rules; Royal Parks Dogs Policy PDF (December 2025)

## 2. Isabella Plantation
**Dog access summary:** Dogs on leads are permitted inside the Isabella Plantation at all times; this is the same lead rule that applies to the wider Richmond Park during restricted periods.

**Lead rules:**
- Dogs must be on a lead at all times inside the Isabella Plantation - this is a permanent requirement regardless of season
- During the deer birthing season (1 May to 31 July) the mandatory park-wide lead rule also applies, reinforcing the existing Isabella Plantation rule
- Dog waste bins are located outside the main gates and at the Isabella Plantation car park
- Dogs are NOT permitted in Pembroke Lodge Gardens (a separate enclosed garden within Richmond Park)

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- The wider Richmond Park's mandatory lead period (1 May to 31 July) applies to all areas including Isabella Plantation
- The permanent lead rule inside the plantation means there is no seasonal change specifically for this location - leads are required year-round inside

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Deer from the wider park can be encountered on paths approaching the Plantation
- The Plantation itself is enclosed; deer do not typically graze inside, but deer may be visible through the fence
- Rare and protected plants and habitat inside the Plantation

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Deer on approaches to the Plantation, especially during rut (September to November) and birthing (May to July) seasons
- Pen Ponds nearby - leads mandatory at the water
- The Plantation contains ornamental ponds; leads prevent disturbance to waterfowl

**Practical info:**
- Parking: dedicated disabled-only car park at Isabella Plantation; general park car parks (Broomfield Hill nearest). All currently free
- Facilities: no cafe inside the Plantation itself. Broomfield Hill kiosk nearby (open 9am to 3:30pm). Dog waste bins at main gates. Water available from park drinking fountains

**Sources:** royalparks.org.uk/visit/parks/richmond-park/faqs; royalparks.org.uk gardens page; Friends of Richmond Park

## 3. Wimbledon Common
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are generally off-lead across Wimbledon Common, but seasonal lead restrictions apply around all ponds (1 March to 31 August) and on The Plain (1 March to 31 August) to protect ground-nesting birds and nesting wildfowl.

**Lead rules:**
- Off-lead permitted across the majority of the Common outside restricted areas and seasons
- Leads required on The Plain from 1 March to 31 August (ground-nesting bird protection); dogs must stay on mown paths and out of long grass during this period
- Leads required around Queensmere Pond and all ponds from 1 April to 31 August (nesting wildfowl protection)
- Dogs must not enter any ponds or water from 1 April to 31 August; outside nesting season dogs may enter water within reason
- Maximum 4 dogs per walker in any group regardless of how many people are present
- Dog walkers asked to ensure dogs do not stray onto the golf course or disturb horses or cyclists

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- 1 March to 31 August: leads required on The Plain; dogs must stay on mown paths and out of long grass
- 1 April to 31 August: leads required around all ponds; dogs not permitted in water
- Current date (2 April 2026) means ALL of the above seasonal restrictions are currently in force

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- No grazing livestock on the Common
- Ground-nesting birds including skylarks and other species on The Plain
- Wildfowl nesting at Queensmere and other ponds
- Horses are ridden on the Common - dogs should not disturb riders

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Wimbledon Common Golf Club operates on the Common; dogs can wander onto fairways
- Water bodies - some ponds are restricted
- Horses: common use of bridleways by riders
- No roads cross the main common but busy roads border the site

**Practical info:**
- Parking: Windmill Car Park (main car park) - free, open Monday to Sunday 6am to 7pm; donation scheme in place to support the Conservators
- Facilities: Windmill Tearooms adjacent to the Windmill Museum - dog friendly with large outdoor seating area, water bowls provided, serves breakfast, hot food, cakes and ice cream including a dog-friendly Jude's ice cream option. Windmill Museum (free entry) on site. Public toilets at the Windmill. Dog waste bins throughout

**Sources:** wpcc.org.uk/activities/dogs; wpcc.org.uk/things-to-do/important-information-for-dog-owners-and-dog-walkers; wpcc.org.uk frequently asked questions

## 4. Hampstead Heath
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are welcome across most of Hampstead Heath and may be off-lead in much of the open space, but there are designated zones restricting dog access, leads are required in formal landscapes and sports areas, dogs are banned from the swimming ponds (except one designated area), and professional dog walkers require a City of London licence.

**Lead rules:**
- Off-lead permitted in large areas of open heath; the City of London has published a dog walking zones map dividing the Heath into seven areas (primarily for managing professional dog walkers, but indicative of where dogs are expected)
- Leads required in Golders Hill Park formal landscapes and on all sports pitches
- Dogs not permitted in the men's bathing pond (Highgate no. 2) or women's bathing pond (Highgate no. 5) at any time
- One designated dog swimming area exists on the Heath (commonly referred to as the dog pond - unofficial designation); dogs are not permitted to enter the mixed bathing pond
- Between 1 March and 31 July, the Countryside and Rights of Way Act requires dogs on leads of no more than 2 metres on any Open Access land (this applies to relevant parts of the Heath)
- Maximum 4 dogs per walker; dog walkers must not congregate in groups exceeding 4 dogs total
- Professional dog walkers must hold a City of London licence (cost approximately 300 pounds per year), be allocated one of seven designated zones, and may not walk more than 4 dogs at once

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- 1 March to 31 July: dogs must be on leads (maximum 2m fixed lead) on Open Access land areas of the Heath to protect ground-nesting birds
- Current date (2 April 2026) means the spring lead restriction is currently in force on Open Access areas

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- No grazing livestock on Hampstead Heath
- Ground-nesting birds in heathland sections during spring and summer
- Note: a 2025 study (Imperial College London) found pesticide contamination in ponds where dogs swim; the City of London held a committee discussion in January 2025 on dog swimming and pesticide introduction - this is an evolving issue

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Swimming ponds: dogs prohibited from human bathing ponds; risk of contamination noted in 2025 research
- Roads surrounding the Heath including East Heath Road
- Cyclists use paths throughout the Heath
- Parliament Hill lido and formal areas - no dogs

**Practical info:**
- Parking: three paid car parks serve Hampstead Heath. East Heath Car Park (NW3 1TH) - charges apply, payment via RingGo (phone: 020 7110 0000 or app). Blue Badge holders park free at East Heath Car Park. Millfield Lane near Highgate Men's Pond is pay and display Monday to Friday 10am to 12pm (90 minutes maximum), otherwise free
- Facilities: Lakeside Cafe at Parliament Hill, Golders Hill Park cafe and bandstand cafe, refreshment facilities at Parliament Hill Lido. Dog water bowls available at some facilities. Public toilets at Parliament Hill, Golders Hill Park, and Kenwood. Dog waste bins throughout

**Sources:** cityoflondon.gov.uk dog walking at Hampstead Heath page; cityoflondon.gov.uk/assets/Green-Spaces/hampstead-heath-dog-walking-code.pdf; cityoflondon.gov.uk/assets/Green-Spaces/hampstead-heath-dog-walking-zones.pdf; Camden New Journal (professional dog walker licensing); Imperial College London news (pesticides in ponds, 2025)

## 5. Box Hill
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are welcome throughout Box Hill and the surrounding estate; leads are required only when livestock are actively grazing in an area, which is seasonal and varies year to year. Dogs on leads are welcome inside the cafe.

**Lead rules:**
- Dogs do not need to be on leads across the open chalk downland and woodland paths outside of livestock grazing areas
- Leads required in any areas where livestock are actively grazing - these areas are signed; grazing is seasonal and varies by year (not fixed calendar dates)
- Dogs on short leads welcome inside the National Trust cafe at Box Hill summit
- Water bowls available inside and outside the cafe

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- Livestock grazing periods are not fixed to specific calendar dates; areas affected are signed on site. Visitors should watch for signs at access points
- No confirmed fixed seasonal closure dates from official sources for 2025 to 2026; contact Box Hill directly (01372 220643 or boxhill@nationaltrust.org.uk) for current grazing schedule

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Livestock used for conservation grazing on chalk downland; specific type and timing varies
- Chalk downland supports rare butterflies and flora that benefit from grazing management

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Steep chalk escarpment - significant drop on the cliff side of the North Downs Way; dogs should be kept away from the edge
- North Downs Way and other paths cross the A24 dual carriageway; extreme care needed at road crossings
- Stepping Stones car park is off the A24 which is fast and busy

**Practical info:**
- Parking: National Trust car park at Box Hill summit (main and east car parks, over 200 spaces total) and Stepping Stones car park off the A24. Charges: approximately 1.50 pounds per hour for up to 2 hours, 4 pounds up to 4 hours, 6 pounds for 5 hours and above. National Trust members park free
- Facilities: National Trust cafe at the summit - dogs on leads welcome inside, outdoor seating available, water bowls provided. Gift shop and toilets at summit. No facilities at Stepping Stones car park

**Sources:** nationaltrust.org.uk/visit/surrey/box-hill (via search); dogstaycation.co.uk National Trust dog-friendly places; wandereroftheworld.co.uk National Trust directory; getsurrey.co.uk Box Hill parking guide

## 6. Leith Hill
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are welcome across the estate and inside Leith Hill Place; leads are required around grazing livestock and within all fenced areas during ground-nesting bird season (1 March to 31 July).

**Lead rules:**
- Dogs may be off-lead in open woodland and estate areas when livestock are not present and outside the nesting season fenced areas
- Leads required around ALL grazing livestock at any time of year
- Leads required within fenced areas from 1 March to 31 July to protect ground-nesting birds
- Dogs on short leads welcome inside Leith Hill Place (downstairs seated areas and outside in the garden, but not in the kitchen)
- Water bowls available at Leith Hill Place courtyard and just inside the front door
- Dog ice cream available to purchase from the Kitchen

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- 1 March to 31 July: leads required within fenced areas across the estate (ground-nesting bird protection)
- Current date (2 April 2026) means this restriction is currently in force

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Grazing livestock present on parts of the estate at various times; exact schedule not confirmed from official sources - look for on-site signage

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Leith Hill Tower is at 294 metres, the highest point in the South East; steep paths in places
- Busy car park access roads

**Practical info:**

- Facilities: The Kitchen at Leith Hill Place serves cakes, scones, hot and cold drinks. Kiosk at the base of Leith Hill Tower serves hot and cold drinks and snacks. Toilets and baby-changing at Leith Hill Place during house opening times

## 7. Frensham Great Pond
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are welcome on the heathland paths but must be on leads during bird nesting season (March to September); dogs are banned from the beach and from entering the water at all times throughout the year.

**Lead rules:**
- Dogs must be on leads on the heathland during bird nesting season (March to September) - this is currently in force as of 2 April 2026
- Dogs are NOT permitted on the beach at Frensham Great Pond at any time of year, regardless of whether a lead is used
- Dogs are NOT permitted to enter the water at the Great Pond at any time of year
- Outside bird nesting season (October to February), dogs may be off-lead on heathland paths away from the beach
- No confirmed designated off-lead areas from official sources; check on-site signage

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- March to September: leads required across heathland to protect ground-nesting birds; this is the primary bird nesting protection period for a Site of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI), Special Protection Area (SPA), and Special Area of Conservation (SAC)
- Current date (2 April 2026) means heathland lead restriction is currently in force

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- No confirmed grazing livestock from official sources; check on-site signage
- Internationally important heathland for rare and endangered species including ground-nesting birds
- Note: blue-green algae alerts have occurred at Frensham Great Pond in summer (most recently summer 2025); this is toxic to dogs and can be fatal. Check current Waverley Borough Council alerts before visiting in warm weather

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Blue-green algae in warm weather - potentially fatal if dogs drink affected water; check alerts before visiting (waverley.gov.uk)
- Beach restriction - risk of prosecution if dogs taken onto beach
- Heathland fire risk (no BBQs or fires permitted on site)
- Note: Frensham Great Pond is managed by Waverley Borough Council, not the National Trust (National Trust manages nearby Frensham Little Pond and surrounding commons)

**Practical info:**
- Parking: pay-and-display car park at Frensham Great Pond. Charges: 1.60 pounds up to 1 hour, 3.30 pounds for 1 to 2 hours, 4.40 pounds for 2 to 4 hours, 6.50 pounds all day. Card payment only via RingGo - no cash accepted. Open 8am to 9pm every day except Christmas Day. Car park frequently full by 10am on weekends
- Facilities: cafe on site, toilets including wheelchair-accessible facilities. Dog waste bins. The car park is near the Visitor Centre and cafe

**Sources:** waverley.gov.uk/services/greenspaces/frensham-great-pond; altonherald.com new parking charges article; frensham-great-pond.wheree.com; dogfriendlydestinations.com/listings/frensham-little-pond

## 8. Hindhead Common and Devil's Punch Bowl
**Dog access summary:** Well-controlled dogs are welcome but should be kept on a short lead throughout the site, particularly from April to September; only assistance dogs are permitted inside the cafe.

**Lead rules:**
- Dogs should be kept on a short lead throughout the site at all times; the National Trust recommends leads on site to protect heathland wildlife and livestock
- Specific confirmed seasonal rule: dogs must be kept on a lead from April to September on site (ground-nesting bird protection and livestock grazing period)
- Off-lead walking is not explicitly offered at this location; the National Trust suggests asking about local off-lead alternatives if needed
- Only assistance dogs are permitted inside the Devil's Punch Bowl Cafe; all other dogs must remain outside

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- April to September: leads required on site (confirmed from search results)
- Current date (2 April 2026) means this seasonal restriction is currently in force
- Note: one source referenced March to July, another referenced April to September; the April to September period is from National Trust-specific guidance for this site; the broader CRoW Act lead requirement on Open Access land (1 March to 31 July) also applies

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Cattle and wild ponies graze on the heathland commons for conservation purposes
- The site is part of the Wealden Heaths National Nature Reserve (NNR)
- Rare ground-nesting birds present during breeding season

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Free-roaming cattle and ponies - dogs can cause stress to livestock and risk of injury if cattle charge
- Devil's Punch Bowl: steep-sided valley; paths can be muddy and uneven
- The A3 runs through a tunnel beneath the site but surface roads in the area can be busy
- Heathland fire risk in dry conditions

**Practical info:**

Kiosk for ice cream open weekends and daily during school holidays from 12pm. Changing Places accessible toilet at the back of the cafe. Dog waste bins in car park

## 9. Seven Sisters Cliffs
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are welcome at Seven Sisters Country Park; they should be kept on leads near livestock, wildlife, the cliff edge, and open water. Ground-nesting bird season (March to September) requires particular care. The cliffs themselves are an active hazard.

**Lead rules:**
- No blanket lead requirement across the entire park, but leads are required or strongly recommended: near livestock (sheep, cows, and ponies graze throughout the year), near the cliff edge (safety), near the Cuckmere River and other open water, and when signs indicate leads required (these are posted during ground-nesting bird season)
- Dogs should be kept under close control on the concrete path through the valley
- Ground-nesting bird season (March to September): signs posted requiring leads in specific areas; visitors should comply with all signed instructions
- Current date (2 April 2026) means nesting season is in force

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- March to September: ground-nesting bird season; specific areas will be signed for leads; visitors must follow posted signs
- The site also received designation as part of King Charles III England Coast Path and was declared a National Nature Reserve (date not confirmed in search results but recently announced)
- Livestock grazing occurs year-round (chalk grassland management) but is heavier in autumn and winter when cattle are brought in to control rank grass

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Sheep, cows, and ponies graze throughout the park year-round for chalk grassland management
- Cattle are present in autumn and winter on chalk grassland areas
- Adders are present in the park and are most active April to July; dogs should be kept to paths in warm weather (see Sniffout adder research for relevant first-aid guidance)
- Ticks are present in spring and autumn; regular checks after woodland and grassland walks are advised

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Cliff edge: CRITICAL hazard. The chalk cliffs erode unpredictably with 30 to 40cm lost per year; rockfalls happen without warning. Keep at least 5 metres from the cliff edge and base of cliffs - this applies to dogs equally
- Cuckmere River: tidal river; water levels and currents can be dangerous
- Adders: most active April to July, particularly in grassland and scrub areas
- Ticks: spring and autumn peaks
- Cattle: can charge if dogs approach, especially cows with calves

**Practical info:**
- Parking: two pay-and-display car parks at Seven Sisters Country Park (near Visitor Centre and Saltmarsh Cafe). Accept coins, cards, or RinGo app. Open 7am to 9pm from 31 March to 26 October; 7am to 6pm October to March. Car parks are locked overnight with a release fee payable. Four EV charging points (up to 7kw) in the north car park. Parking charges apply but specific amounts not confirmed from official sources - check sevensisters.org.uk for current charges
- Facilities: Visitor Centre, Saltmarsh Cafe (independently run, check for dog-friendly access), Grab and Go kiosk open daily from 10am (closed Christmas Day) with seasonal hours. Toilets at Visitor Centre. Dog waste bins on site

**Sources:** sevensisters.org.uk/plan-your-visit/dogs (not accessible but search results extracted content); southdowns.gov.uk/seven-sisters; sevensisters.org.uk/plan-your-visit/car-parking; sevensisters.org.uk/cows-seven-sisters (grazing FAQs)

## 10. Haytor and Hound Tor, Dartmoor
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are welcome on Dartmoor's open moorland; a mandatory short lead rule (maximum 2 metres) applies on all Open Access land from 1 March to 31 July. Outside this period, dogs should be under effective close control. Livestock and adders are the primary hazards.

**Lead rules:**
- 1 March to 31 July: dogs must be on a fixed short lead of no more than 2 metres on all Open Access land on Dartmoor (this includes Haytor Down and Hound Tor area). This is a legal requirement under the Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000
- Outside 1 March to 31 July: dogs must be under effective and close control at all times; if livestock are present, dogs must be put on a lead
- Dogs must be on leads when using paths through farmyards and domestic gardens
- Current date (2 April 2026) means the mandatory Open Access lead rule is currently in force

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- 1 March to 31 July: mandatory short lead (max 2m) on all Open Access land (legal requirement under CRoW Act)
- Current visit on 2 April 2026 falls within this mandatory lead period
- Dartmoor NPA is also in the process of revising its byelaws; check dartmoor.gov.uk for any updates

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Free-roaming cattle, sheep, and ponies graze extensively across the moorland including around Haytor and Hound Tor
- Livestock worrying is a serious offence; farmers are legally permitted to shoot dogs caught chasing livestock
- Dogs (Protection of Livestock) (Amendment) Act 2025 came into force 18 March 2026: unlimited fines now apply for livestock worrying (up from previous 1,000 pound maximum); police can seize dogs without the owner being present; law now applies on roads and paths, not just in fields
- Ground-nesting birds including skylarks, meadow pipits, and curlews nest on open moorland (protected by the March to July lead rule)

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Livestock (cattle, ponies, sheep) throughout the moorland - risk of injury if dog approaches
- Adders: present in moorland undergrowth; risk increases in warm weather; keeping dogs on leads and to footpaths reduces risk
- Marshy/boggy ground: dogs can sink or become injured in boggy terrain
- Steep granite tors: Haytor and Hound Tor are rocky outcrops with significant drops; dogs should not be allowed to scramble unsupervised
- River crossings and streams: can be in spate after heavy rain

**Practical info:**
- Parking: three pay-and-display car parks at Haytor (upper, lower/Visitor Centre, and a free middle car park). The Haytor Visitor Centre is at the lower car park. The same parking ticket covers movement between Haytor car parks. Dartmoor NPA increased parking charges from 17 April 2025; check dartmoor.gov.uk for current rates
- Facilities: Haytor Visitor Centre (lower car park, open seasonally) has information and accessible facilities. No cafe at the Visitor Centre; the hotel behind (Haytor Vale area) offers food and drinks. Ice cream van at the upper car park in season. EV charging at the Visitor Centre car park. No specific dog water facilities confirmed from official sources

**Sources:** dartmoor.gov.uk/enjoy-dartmoor/planning-your-visit/visiting-with-your-dog; dartmoor.gov.uk/enjoy-dartmoor/planning-your-visit/car-parks; dartmoor.gov.uk fees and charges 2025/26 PDF; dartmoor.gov.uk/about-us/Our-Blog/blog-posts/dogs-day-on-dartmoor; Dogs (Protection of Livestock) (Amendment) Act 2025 (legislation.gov.uk)

## 11. Stanage Edge, Peak District
**Dog access summary:** Dogs must be on a short lead at all times on the Stanage-North Lees Estate managed by the Peak District NPA. Additional access restrictions apply to areas north and west of the main edge under the CRoW Act, with permanent and seasonal dog restrictions protecting moorland birds including the red-listed Ring Ouzel.

**Lead rules:**
- Dogs must be on a short lead at ALL TIMES anywhere on the Stanage-North Lees Estate - this is a blanket rule with no off-lead areas confirmed on the estate
- On Open Access land within the estate (and the wider moorland), dogs must be on a fixed short lead of no more than 2 metres from 1 March to 31 July (CRoW Act requirement for ground-nesting bird protection)
- Areas north and west of Stanage Edge (including Bamford Edge, Moscar, Derwent and Hallam Moors) have a permanent dog restriction in addition to CRoW Act seasonal rules; these are due to land management requirements
- Additional seasonal closures may apply in areas around active Ring Ouzel nest sites - signage is posted and current closures are listed on the Natural England open access maps website; search for "Stanage" and check the restrictions section
- Current date (2 April 2026) means CRoW Act mandatory leads are in force AND Ring Ouzels have arrived from North Africa (arrive late March to early April) meaning Ring Ouzel protection restrictions are particularly relevant right now

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- 1 March to 31 July: mandatory short lead (max 2m) on Open Access land
- Late March to late summer: Ring Ouzel nesting season; climbing routes and paths near nest sites may have temporary restrictions shown by clear signage
- CRoW Act closures can also be applied for land management reasons (up to 28 days per year); check Natural England open access maps or PDNP website for current closure dates
- Public rights of way and concessionary paths remain open even when Open Access land is closed

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Sheep and cattle may be present on the estate; dogs must be on leads at all times near livestock
- Ring Ouzel: nationally red-listed bird (72% decline between 1991 and 2012); nests among rocks and vegetation of Stanage Edge; dogs near nest sites cause abandonment even without direct contact. Ring Ouzels arrive late March to early April and stay until late summer
- Other ground-nesting moorland birds present during spring and summer

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Gritstone edge: Stanage Edge stands approximately 25 metres high in places and is at 458 metres above sea level; dogs should not be allowed near the edge
- Boggy moorland: uneven terrain, potential for dogs to injure legs in rocky or boggy ground
- Sheep and cattle on the estate

**Practical info:**
- Parking: Stanage Plantation Car Park and Hollin Bank Car Park are the main pay-and-display options. Hooks Carr Car Park is free and closest to the top. Car parks fill early on weekends and bank holidays
- Facilities: no permanent food or drink facilities at the main car parks or on the estate. Nearest cafes and pubs are in Hathersage village: cafe above the Outside shop (next to the petrol station), and The Scotsman's Pack pub which is dog-friendly with food. Dog waste bins in car parks

**Sources:** peakdistrict.gov.uk/visiting/places-to-visit/stanage-and-north-lees; peakdistrict.gov.uk/visiting/planning-your-visit/dogs; peakdistrict.gov.uk/visiting/places-to-visit/stanage-and-north-lees/ring-ouzels; thingstodopeakdistrict.co.uk/stanage-edge; peakplaces.co.uk/stanage-edge

## 12. Grasmere Lake
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are welcome around Grasmere Lake but leads are essential near sheep and farmland (lambing is underway in April); some lakeside sections are fenced, providing off-lead opportunities, but unfenced areas near livestock require leads. The Lake District NPA urges dogs on leads near livestock year-round.

**Lead rules:**
- Leads required near sheep and livestock at all times; fields in the Grasmere area are full of sheep and dog walking in enclosed fields is not permitted
- Fenced sections of the lakeside track provide areas where dogs can be let off-lead without risk of accessing farmland; unfenced sections require leads or very close control if livestock are nearby
- Around Allan Bank (National Trust property near Grasmere Lake): dogs must be on leads from the bottom of the driveway to protect red squirrels and sheep. Dogs on leads are welcome inside Allan Bank downstairs and in the garden; dog water available by the front door
- The Lake District NPA, National Trust, NFU and Cumbria Constabulary have all urged dog owners to keep dogs on leads near livestock, particularly during lambing season (spring - currently in season on 2 April 2026)
- Guided walks on the fells: dogs welcome but must remain on a short lead throughout

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- Lambing season (broadly February to May, varying by farm): heightened lead requirement near all sheep; ewes with new lambs are particularly vulnerable and stress from dogs can cause lamb and ewe deaths
- Ground-nesting bird protection: leads advised on open access land 1 March to 31 July (CRoW Act applies to relevant open access areas around the lake)
- Current date (2 April 2026) is peak lambing season - leads near all sheep are essential

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Herdwick and other sheep extensively farmed on fells surrounding Grasmere Lake - some in fields directly beside the lake path
- Sheep worrying is a criminal offence; approximately 50 dog attack incidents per year are reported to Cumbria Constabulary
- Red squirrels at Allan Bank - dogs must be on lead to protect this protected species
- Cattle may be present on lowland fields around the lake

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Livestock throughout the valley and on surrounding fells
- Grasmere Lake itself: popular swimming spot but currents and cold water can be a hazard for dogs
- Roads through Grasmere village are busy with tourist traffic in season

**Practical info:**
- Parking: Broadgate Meadow Car Park (81 spaces, pay and display, 7 days a week, charges from 9am) and Red Bank Road Car Park (77 spaces including 6 disabled, open 7 days). Stock Lane Car Park near village entrance is pay and display. Approximate charges (verify locally as these may have been updated): up to 1 hour approximately 1.80 pounds, up to 2 hours 3.20 pounds, up to 4 hours 5.40 pounds, all day approximately 8.00 pounds
- Facilities: multiple dog-friendly pubs and cafes in Grasmere village including Tweedies Bar, The Good Sport, and The Poet's Cafe and Bar. Allan Bank (National Trust) has dog water and is dog-friendly with dogs on leads welcome inside. Dog waste bins in village car parks. Public toilets in the village

**Sources:** lakedistrict.gov.uk countryside code call for dog owners; nationaltrust.org.uk/visit/lake-district/allan-bank-and-grasmere/visiting-allan-bank-with-your-dog; visitlakedistrict.com/plan-your-visit/travel/broadgate-meadow-car-park; dogfriendlylakedistrict.com; keswickreminder.co.uk plea for leads (April 2025)

## 13. Burley Village, New Forest
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are welcome throughout the New Forest open Forest around Burley, but must be under effective control at all times; leads are required in car parks and alongside roads, and dogs must never chase, approach, or jump up at the free-roaming ponies, cattle, donkeys, deer, and pigs. There is no blanket lead requirement across the open Forest away from roads.

**Lead rules:**
- Leads required in ALL car parks in the New Forest
- Leads required alongside roads - dogs must not run loose near roads where free-roaming animals may also be present
- On main tracks in open Forest: dogs may be off-lead if the owner can reliably recall them; if recall is uncertain, leads should be used
- Through heathland during bird nesting season (March to August): dogs must be kept to main tracks; off-lead exercise through heathland during this period should be limited to reduce risk to ground-nesting birds
- Dogs must not approach or chase ponies, cattle, donkeys, deer, or pigs under any circumstances - on or off lead
- If threatened by livestock, the Forest code recommends dropping the lead so dog and owner can escape separately
- Note: Public Spaces Protection Orders (PSPOs) prohibit feeding or petting free-roaming animals; this was extended to run until 2029 (previously due to expire June 2026)

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- March to August: dogs must be kept to main tracks through heathland (ground-nesting bird protection)
- September to approximately late November: Pannage season - pigs are released onto the Forest to eat fallen acorns. Dogs must be kept under control near pigs; pigs can be aggressive. In 2025, the season ran from 15 September
- Year-round: cattle numbers peak in summer (several thousand on the Forest); numbers fall in autumn and winter when many return to owners' holdings or are removed to avoid acorn poisoning
- Current date (2 April 2026): heathland bird nesting season is in force; stick to main tracks with dogs on the heathland

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Free-roaming New Forest ponies, cattle, and donkeys year-round (varying numbers by season)
- Pigs during Pannage season (September to approximately November/December)
- Fallow and roe deer throughout the Forest
- Ponies and cattle are UNPREDICTABLE; mares with foals and cows with calves are particularly dangerous
- Feeding or petting ponies and donkeys is prohibited by PSPO and can result in a 100 pound fixed penalty or up to 1,000 pound fine if prosecuted

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Free-roaming livestock throughout the entire route - the primary hazard
- Ponies can bite and kick; cattle can charge; do not allow dogs to approach
- Roads: New Forest roads are unfenced with animals roaming freely; animal-vehicle collisions are common, especially at night
- Adders and ticks present in heathland areas
- Boggy ground in woodland areas
- Note: the Dogs (Protection of Livestock) (Amendment) Act 2025 (in force from 18 March 2026) applies in the New Forest; unlimited fines for livestock worrying offences

**Practical info:**
- Parking: public car park behind the Queens Head Pub in Burley village. Forestry England also manages various free and pay car parks in the wider area around Burley
- Facilities: The White Buck Inn (Burley) - dog-friendly with prior arrangement; The Burley Inn - dogs welcome in a separate area, serves food all day; Old Station Tearooms at Holmsley (accessible from a walk from Burley along a former railway line) - dog-friendly with breakfasts, lunches, and afternoon teas. Dog waste bins in village and car parks

**Sources:** newforestnpa.gov.uk/things-to-do/walking/dog-walking/dog-walking-code; newforestnpa.gov.uk/visiting/help-care-for-the-forest/new-forest-code; newforestnpa.gov.uk news (PSPOs extension); newforestnpa.gov.uk/things-to-do/walking/new-forest-walks/burley-village; realnewforest.org/pannage-season-2025; burleynewforest.co.uk/info

## 14. Malham Cove
**Dog access summary:** Dogs are welcome at Malham Cove and in the surrounding Yorkshire Dales National Park; they should be kept on leads or under very close control near livestock (which are everywhere on this walk), and on leads during the bird nesting season (1 March to 31 July). The limestone pavement at the top of the Cove has physical hazards for dogs.

**Lead rules:**
- Leads required, or dogs under very close effective control, near all livestock throughout the walk - Swaledale sheep and Belted Galloway cattle are present in fields along the route
- Leads strongly advised on the approach to and at Malham Cove itself - the path passes through gated fields with livestock
- During nesting season (1 March to 31 July): leads required on open access land (CRoW Act); dogs should be on a short lead or under close control
- Near Malham Tarn (if visiting): dogs are not allowed to swim or paddle in the water to prevent spread of waterborne pests and diseases; only assistance dogs are allowed on the boardwalk of the National Nature Reserve at Malham Tarn
- Current date (2 April 2026) means CRoW Act lead requirements on open access land are in force

**Seasonal restrictions:**
- 1 March to 31 July: leads required on Open Access land (CRoW Act)
- Peregrine falcons nest at Malham Cove each year (nesting since 1993); during active nesting the base of the Cove and parts of the top may be roped off or restricted to prevent disturbance. In 2025, the peregrine restriction was lifted on 19 May as the nest unfortunately failed. Restrictions for 2026 nesting season are not yet confirmed (nesting typically begins late March to April); check yorkshiredales.org.uk for current status. Drone flying at the Cove is prohibited during nesting
- Lambing: Malham area sheep (Swaledale) lamb in spring; heightened caution around sheep from February through May
- Current date (2 April 2026): lambing is active, CRoW Act leads are in force, and peregrine nesting season is likely beginning - leads are essential

**Livestock and wildlife:**
- Swaledale sheep in virtually all fields on the approach walk from Malham village
- Belted Galloway cattle also present on some sections
- Peregrine falcons nesting at the Cove cliff face (seasonal - see above)
- Typical Yorkshire Dales ground-nesting birds on moorland sections above the Cove

**Hazards for dogs:**
- Limestone pavement at the top of Malham Cove: deep grykes (crevices) between limestone clints (blocks) can be several feet deep and can trap a dog's leg; dogs should be kept on short leads or carried across pavement sections
- Shake holes and sink holes in the area around the Cove and on the walk to Malham Tarn
- Steep ascent via steps up the side of Malham Cove; dogs must be capable of the climb
- Peregrine falcon nesting area at the base of the Cove face - restricted access during nesting

**Practical info:**
- Parking: Yorkshire Dales NPA car park in Malham village (charges apply, approximately 8 pounds all day; Blue Badge holders also charged; payment via Milk Churns on site or online link). Additional village parking at busy times. National Park Visitor Centre is in the car park (open 7 days a week from 1 April 2025, 10am to 5pm)
- Facilities: National Park Visitor Centre with toilets (including RADAR-accessible facilities) and exhibition. Several dog-friendly pubs and cafes in Malham village including The Buck Inn (dog-friendly, Grade II listed, Thwaites pub acquired summer 2025; dogs welcome with prior acknowledgement). Multiple dog-friendly cafes in the village. Dog waste bins in the car park and village. Public toilets in the car park

**Sources:** yorkshiredales.org.uk/places/malham_cove; yorkshiredales.org.uk/be-inspired/what-to-do/to-enjoy-wildlife/peregrine-falcons-at-malham-cove; nationaltrust.org.uk/visit/yorkshire/yorkshire-dales/visiting-the-yorkshire-dales-with-your-dog; accesshub.yorkshiredales.org.uk/places/malham-national-park-visitor-centre; zoegoesplaces.com/malham-cove-yorkshire-dales; stayinapub.co.uk (Buck Inn)

### Recent rule changes relevant to all 14 walks

**Dogs (Protection of Livestock) (Amendment) Act 2025** - This came into force on 18 March 2026, just two weeks before this research date. Key changes affecting all rural walks:
- Unlimited fines now apply for livestock worrying (previously capped at 1,000 pounds)
- Police can seize dogs without the owner present
- Law applies on roads and paths, not just in enclosed fields
- Camelids (alpacas, llamas) now included in definition of protected livestock
- New owner exemptions if dog was in someone else's charge at the time

This is a significant recent change that should be mentioned in FAQ content for any walks near livestock (all rural walks on this list).
