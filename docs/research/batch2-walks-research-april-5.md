## WALK 1: Cuckmere Haven and Litlington

Location: Seaford, East Sussex
App data: 4.6 miles (7.4km), easy, off-lead partial, livestock true

### 1. Verified Data

| Off-lead | Partial | CONFIRMED CORRECT - most of the route is on-lead due to year-round livestock across the majority of fields |
| Livestock | True | CONFIRMED CORRECT - detail below |

### 2. Land Management

The Cuckmere Haven area is split across multiple managers:

- Seven Sisters Country Park (Exceat, the river valley, the meanders and beach approach): owned and managed by the South Downs National Park Authority (SDNPA), which took full ownership from East Sussex County Council on 30 July 2021. Covers 280 hectares of chalk downland, floodplain meadow and wetland.
- National Trust manages two properties in the valley: Chyngton Farm (a working farm at the mouth of the Cuckmere river) and Frog Firle Farm (on the riverbank).
- Seaford Head Local Nature Reserve: managed separately by Seaford Town Council.
- The Litlington section (village, church and Plough and Harrow pub) falls within the South Downs National Park but is not NT or SDNPA managed land.

### 3. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Off-lead partial is confirmed correct. Section by section:

- On lead in all areas where sheep and cattle are grazing. Chyngton Farm runs a commercial herd of around 25 Sussex beef cattle and 700 ewes in the fields for most of the year (brought inside only briefly during lambing). Dogs must be on lead in all grazed areas.
- On lead near cliff edges on Seaford Head. The Seven Sisters Country Park guidance explicitly requires on-lead near wildlife, cliffs and open water.
- On lead near the tidal river estuary and beach.
- Off lead possible on open chalk path sections away from livestock, though "under close control" is the standard requirement throughout the park.
- Ground-nesting bird season (March to September): visitors asked to stay on main paths. Skylarks and other species nest on the chalk grassland. Some areas may be roped off.
- If threatened by cattle, the official Seven Sisters Country Park guidance says to release your dog from the lead and move to safety.

In practice, this is a predominantly on-lead walk with some stretches of open chalk path where off-lead is possible under close control.

### 4. Livestock Detail

- Type: Sheep (domestic) and cattle. Chyngton Farm runs a 700-ewe flock (present most of the year, only briefly inside during lambing) and approximately 25 Sussex beef cattle. Foxhole Farm grazes young bullocks on additional fields around the park.
- Where: Throughout the park fields. The valley floor meadows and Chyngton Farm fields are the primary livestock areas.
- When: Sheep year-round. Cattle seasonally on pastures.

### 5. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Cliff edges: Seven Sisters chalk cliffs erode by 30-40cm annually. Cliff falls are unpredictable and can happen at any time. Stay at least 5 metres from the cliff edge and base. Do not approach cliff tops from below.
- Tidal risk at the estuary: The Cuckmere estuary is tidal. At high tide the river can be 6-7 metres deep with a strong current capable of sweeping a person or dog out to sea. Check tide times before visiting. The estuary splits the beach in two - at most states of tide it is too high and the current too strong to cross safely.
- Flooding: The Cuckmere floodplain floods regularly in winter after heavy rainfall. Valley floor paths and riverside sections are the most affected - typically November through February. Sections can be waterlogged or fully flooded during wet winters.
- Adders: Present, most active April to July. Seek immediate veterinary attention if a dog is bitten.
- Ticks: Check dogs carefully after visits, particularly spring and autumn.
- No lifeguards at Cuckmere Haven beach.

### 6. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Seven Sisters Country Park car parks (North and South): BN25 4AD. Pay and display. Accepts coins, cards and RinGo app (location code 40750). Charges: up to 2 hours £3.50, up to 4 hours £5.00, over 4 hours £7.00. Open 7am-9pm (March to October), 7am-6pm (October to March). Car parks locked overnight.
- Free alternative: South Hill Barn car park, Seaford - BN25 4JQ. Free parking from the Seaford Head side. Approximately 1km to the beach from here.

**Facilities at Seven Sisters Country Park:** Visitor centre, toilets, cafe (Grab and Go), dog water bowls on site.

**Beach:** Cuckmere Haven beach is shingle (pebbles) with rock pools at low tide. Dogs welcome all year round - no seasonal ban. No dog-free zones. The beach is reached by a 2km walk from the Seven Sisters car park or approximately 1km from South Hill Barn car park.

**Dogs in the river:** Dogs can swim in the Cuckmere river in the calmer, non-tidal meandering section near Litlington. The estuary near the beach mouth is tidal and dangerous for swimming.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Cuckmere Inn, Exceat Bridge, Seaford, BN25 4AB - directly on the route, Vintage Inns pub, dogs welcome in the bar. 20-30 minutes upstream walk from the beach.
- The Plough and Harrow, The Street, Litlington, BN26 5RE (tel: 01323 870632) - 17th century country pub in the heart of the route. Dogs welcome inside and in the beer garden. Doggy menu, real ales, home-cooked food. Open 7 days.

### 7. FAQ

**Q: Can I let my dog off the lead at Cuckmere Haven?**
A: Only on sections clear of livestock and cliff edges. Significant sections of this walk pass through actively grazed fields - the Chyngton Farm fields alone host around 700 ewes for most of the year. Near the cliff tops on Seaford Head, dogs must also be on lead. In practice this is a predominantly on-lead walk with some stretches of open chalk path where off-lead is possible under close control.

**Q: Is the beach dog-friendly all year?**
A: Yes. Cuckmere Haven beach has no seasonal dog ban. Dogs are welcome on the shingle beach year-round. However, be aware of the tidal estuary that splits the beach - check tide times before attempting to cross it.

**Q: Is it safe for dogs to swim in the Cuckmere river?**
A: In the calmer meandering sections upstream near Litlington, yes - dogs swim here regularly. Avoid the tidal estuary section near the beach mouth, where currents are strong enough to be dangerous.

**Q: Does the walk get flooded?**
A: The valley floor and riverside sections are prone to seasonal flooding, typically November through February after heavy rain. The floodplain meadows alongside the river meanders are the worst-affected areas. In very wet winters, sections can be impassable. Check conditions before visiting in winter.

### 8. Seasonal Notes

- Best time: Late spring (May to June) and autumn (September to October). Spring brings chalk downland wildflowers and the fields fill with lambs in May. Autumn gives clearer sea views.
- Summer: Can be very busy at the car parks and on the beach. Arrive early. Livestock present across most fields.
- Winter: Beautiful but paths can flood badly. Valley floor sections become waterlogged. Check before visiting November to February.
- Ground-nesting season (March to September): Stay on main paths on the chalk grassland. Skylarks breed above the valley.
- Worst conditions: Strong onshore winds with high tide. Cliff erosion risk increases in storms. The estuary crossing becomes impossible.

During the ground-nesting bird season (approximately late February to August), dogs should also be kept on leads on the chalk grassland where skylarks nest. **Q: Is there any water or shade for dogs?**

- Worst conditions: Hot summer days with full sun - minimal shade and no water. Strong wind on the exposed hilltop at any time of year.

### 2. Land Management

Managed entirely by the National Trust. The estate spans Hertfordshire, Buckinghamshire and Bedfordshire. Key features: Dockey Wood (prime bluebell area), the Bridgewater Monument, Ivinghoe Hills, commons and ancient woodland. Dockey Wood is also on the Woodland Trust Ancient Woodland inventory.

From 1 September 2025, professional dog walkers require a licence to operate at Ashridge Estate as part of the National Trust's Protecting Our Roots project.

### 3. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Dogs are welcome and can generally be off lead in the woodland areas on the bluebell route, with important exceptions:

- Dockey Wood during bluebell season (mid-April to early May): dogs must be on lead. Bluebell plants contain toxic glycosides - all parts are poisonous to dogs. Low-level fencing has been installed along visitor routes in Dockey Wood. Dog damage to bluebells is also a conservation concern.
- Ivinghoe Hills: dogs must be on lead at all times. Approximately 300 sheep graze here. Signs are posted. The standard bluebell walk to Dockey Wood does not typically pass through Ivinghoe Hills, but walkers who extend their route toward the hills must be aware.
- Visitor centre and shop: dogs on leads inside buildings.
- General estate woodland: off lead under close control is permitted.

**Recommended app setting: off-lead partial.**

### 4. Livestock and Deer

**Livestock:**
- Approximately 300 sheep graze on Ivinghoe Hills. Dogs on lead at all times in this area.

**Deer:**
- Fallow deer: an integral part of the estate. The herd traces back to the medieval deer park created around the monastery - continuously present since that period. Fallow deer rut occurs in October when bucks make loud groaning calls and "fallow rings" (circles of hoof prints) appear in the woodland. Spectacular wildlife spectacle and a popular reason to visit in autumn.
- Muntjac deer: also present throughout the estate. Small Asian deer, originally thought to have escaped from Woburn in the late 19th century. Now widespread. Described as damaging to low-growing flora including bluebells.
- Dogs should be under close control around all deer. During the fallow rut in October, on lead is strongly advised as bucks can be aggressive.

**Recommended app setting: livestock true** (300 sheep on Ivinghoe Hills; deer throughout estate year-round).

### 5. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Bluebell toxicity: All parts of the bluebell plant are toxic to dogs (toxic glycosides). Keep dogs away from eating plants in Dockey Wood during bluebell season.
- Ticks: Ashridge woodland is tick habitat. The estate's own guidance mentions ticks as a hazard. Check dogs thoroughly after every visit, especially spring and autumn. Peak season April to October.
- Muntjac deer: May startle and bolt unpredictably - dogs may chase.
- Fallow deer rut (October): Bucks can be aggressive. Keep dogs on lead where deer are active in October.
- Mud: Woodland paths become muddy after rain. Beech root network can be slippery underfoot.
- Bluebell season crowds: The flowering window is short (late April to early May). Significant visitor pressure - arrive early on weekdays.

**Parking:**
- Monument Drive car park (main visitor car park): HP4 1LT (Moneybury Hill, Ringshall, Berkhamsted). Currently free. A planning application for potential car park charges was submitted March 2025 and validated April 2025 - charges for non-members may be introduced. Check the National Trust website before visiting as this may change. Car parks locked 10pm-6am.
- Ling Ride car park: alternative on the estate, useful for avoiding bluebell crowds while still accessing good bluebell areas.
- Approximately 25 smaller informal car parks across the wider estate.

**Terrain:** Wide woodland paths through beech and oak woodland. Generally good in dry conditions. Muddy in wet weather - beech root systems create uneven ground. Largely level with gentle gradients.

**Shade:** Excellent. The route is almost entirely in woodland. Very good shade for dogs in warm weather.

**Water access:** No natural streams on the standard bluebell route. Water bowls at the visitor centre. Bring water for dogs.

**Facilities at Visitor Centre (HP4 1LT):** Cafe, toilets, National Trust shop. Dogs on lead inside buildings.

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Greyhound Inn, 19 Stocks Road, Aldbury, HP23 5RT - traditional country pub at the foot of the Ashridge Estate in Aldbury village. Dogs welcome in the bar and outside in the covered courtyard. Hall and Woodhouse (Badger Ales) pub. Note: conflicting sources on dog access to the restaurant - call ahead to confirm current policy.
- The Trooper, Aldbury - also at the foot of the estate, described as a modern country pub.

### 7. FAQ

**Q: Can my dog be off lead during the bluebell walk?**
A: In general woodland sections, yes - off lead under close control is permitted. However, in Dockey Wood during bluebell season (mid-April to early May), dogs must be on lead to protect the flowers and because all parts of the bluebell plant are toxic to dogs. If your route takes you toward Ivinghoe Hills, dogs must be on lead at all times due to the 300 sheep grazing there.

**Q: When is the best time to see the bluebells?**
A: The bluebells at Dockey Wood typically start flowering in mid-April and are at their best in the last week of April and first week of May. The exact timing varies with spring weather. Visit early on a weekday to avoid peak crowds - the car park fills quickly at peak weekends.

**Q: Are the fallow deer dangerous to dogs?**
A: Generally the deer will flee from dogs. However, during the fallow rut in October, bucks can become territorial and aggressive. Keep dogs on lead in areas where deer are active during October. Muntjac deer are also present and may trigger a chase response.

**Q: What hazards are there for dogs at Ashridge?**
A: Ticks are the main year-round hazard - check dogs thoroughly after every visit. Bluebell toxicity (all parts of the plant) is a specific risk in spring - do not allow dogs to eat the plants. Mud on woodland paths can be significant after rain. The 300 sheep on Ivinghoe Hills require dogs on lead in that section.

### 8. Seasonal Notes

- Best time: Late April to early May for bluebells (the specific reason for this walk). Arrive early on a weekday.
- October: Fallow deer rut. Spectacular - keep dogs on lead near active deer.
- Spring and summer: Ticks most active. Check dogs after every visit.
- Winter: Bare woodland, good visibility, fewer visitors. Paths can be very muddy. Deer still present.
- Summer: Woodland provides excellent shade. The estate is beautiful but bluebells are long gone - not the season for this specific walk.
- Worst conditions: Peak bluebell weekends in late April (very crowded). Winter mud on beech roots.

### 2. The Farley Mount Monument - Full Story

This is the most distinctive feature of the walk and an important detail for the walk description and app copy.

The pyramid-shaped monument on top of Farley Mount marks the resting place of a remarkable horse. In 1733, Paulet St John (1704-1780), a local landowner from Farley Chamberlayne, was out fox-hunting when his horse misjudged the terrain and leapt into a chalk pit 25 feet deep. Remarkably, neither horse nor rider was injured.

The following year, 1734, St John entered the horse in the Hunters' Plate at Winchester races - under the memorably self-explanatory name "Beware Chalk Pit." The horse won.

To commemorate this, St John built a small monument over the horse's grave when the animal eventually died. The original structure was cube-shaped. In 1870 it was restored by Sir William Heathcote of Hursley and the current elongated pyramid was built in its place. A 2014 renovation kept the monument in good order. It is a Grade II listed structure. An archaeological investigation found no trace of the horse's remains but confirmed the building was constructed in two phases consistent with the history.

The monument stands at the highest point of the park and is visible from a considerable distance.

### 3. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Farley Mount is generally an excellent off-lead destination. Multiple visitor accounts confirm it as a popular off-lead park: "Great space to let the dogs safely off the leash."

- Main exception: keep dogs on lead if cattle are encountered on the chalk downland sections (see livestock, below).
- No formal on-lead zones other than near livestock.

**Recommended app setting: off-lead full (with cattle caveat).**

### 4. Livestock

Cattle graze in the park as part of Hampshire County Council's land management strategy for maintaining the chalk grassland habitat. The grassland sections including Pitt Down (unimproved chalk downland) are managed through controlled grazing.

Visitor guidance confirms: "Keep dogs on a lead if you come across cattle."

The woodland sections (West Wood, Crab Wood, Parnholt Wood) do not typically have cattle. Livestock presence is seasonal and confined to the downland sections.

**Recommended app setting: livestock true (seasonal cattle grazing on chalk downland sections).**

- Narrow access roads: Sarum Road heading towards the monument is single-track in places. Not suitable for large vehicles.
- Cattle: Keep dogs on lead if encountered on downland sections.
- No facilities in the park: No cafe, no dog water taps on the route. Bring water for your dog.
- Woodland paths can be muddy in wet weather.

### 6. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Main car park near the monument: SO21 2JG. Free, no charges. Open dawn to dusk.
- Several car parks within the park, all accessed off Sarum Road (single-track in places - not suitable for large vehicles, coaches or motorhomes).

**Terrain:** Mixed - gravelled woodland trails, chalk downland grassland and forest paths. West Wood and Crab Wood are ancient broadleaf woodland (hazel coppice with oak and ash). Parnholt Wood is beech. Pitt Down is chalk grassland. West Wood has bluebells in spring.

### 7. FAQ

**Q: Can dogs be off lead at Farley Mount?**
A: Yes - Farley Mount is an excellent off-lead destination. The woodland paths and open areas are well suited to off-lead walking. The main exception is if you encounter cattle on the chalk downland sections - keep dogs on lead around livestock.

**Q: What is the pyramid on top of the hill?**
A: A monument built by local landowner Paulet St John in the 1730s to commemorate his horse, which survived a 25-foot fall into a chalk pit while fox-hunting in 1733 - and then went on to win a race at Winchester the following year under the name "Beware Chalk Pit." The horse is buried beneath the monument. The current pyramid structure dates from an 1870 restoration and is Grade II listed.

**Q: Are there ticks at Farley Mount?**
A: Yes - consistently described as one of the tick-heaviest sites in the area. Use tick prevention before visiting and check your dog thoroughly after every walk. Carry tick removal tools. Ticks are most active spring through autumn.

**Q: Is there parking and are there facilities?**
A: Parking is free in several car parks off Sarum Road (SO21 2JG for the main car park near the monument). There are no facilities within the park - no cafe, no toilets, no dog water taps. Bring water and food. The nearest pub is The Plough Inn in Sparsholt (SO21 2NW).

- Spring: Bluebells in West Wood. Ticks most active - use prevention.
- Summer: Excellent shade in the woodland. Good for hot days. Ticks still active.
- Autumn: Clear views from the monument. Leaf colour in the beech woodland.
- Winter: Quiet and peaceful. Woodland paths can become muddy but gravelled sections remain passable.
- Worst conditions: Very muddy in prolonged wet weather. Ticks active from early spring through autumn.

### 2. D-Day History

A compelling walk description asset:

On 6 June 1944, Lepe was one of the primary departure points for the D-Day landings in Normandy (Operation Overlord). The scale of the operation was enormous: every day 12,000 tons of stores and 2,500 vehicles were processed at Lepe ready for shipment to France.

Six massive concrete caissons (type B2 Phoenix breakwaters) were constructed at Lepe, then towed across the English Channel to form part of the Mulberry harbours used to land supplies after D-Day. These were temporary portable harbour structures that allowed the Allies to unload men and materiel directly on the beaches of Normandy without needing a functioning port.

Tanks and heavy vehicles were loaded directly from the beach. To prevent them sinking into the shingle, large concrete reinforcing mats were laid over the foreshore. The remains of this infrastructure are still visible at Lepe today - within half a mile of the car parks. Hampshire County Council has celebrated the park's D-Day connections with a dedicated festival weekend.

### 3. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Dogs are welcome in the country park generally. However, there is a seasonal beach restriction:

- Family beach: closed to dogs from approximately May to September (some sources specify April to September). Hampshire County Council's official guidance states no dogs on the family beach between these months. The boundaries are marked on site by a flagpole at the western end and a signed cable pole at the eastern end.
- Year-round alternative: There is an alternative coastal route for dogs when the family beach is closed. Dog walkers can access the shoreline and coast path away from the restricted section throughout the year.
- Woodland section: Dogs are not permitted in the woodland section.
- Children's play area: Dogs not permitted.
- Country roads forming part of the route: Dogs on lead.
- Country park open areas: Dogs welcome.

**Recommended app setting: off-lead partial** (year-round on the coastal path alternative; beach access restricted May to September; on lead near roads).

### 4. Livestock - New Forest Ponies

No formal livestock graze within Lepe Country Park itself.

However, the park is within the New Forest National Park. New Forest ponies, cattle, donkeys and pigs roam freely throughout the New Forest and can be encountered near the park's boundaries and on nearby roads. New Forest animals are semi-wild and unpredictable - do not feed them and keep dogs under control nearby.

On the extended Lepe Loop walk (which takes walkers inland on country lanes), encounters with roaming New Forest animals are likely.

**Recommended app setting: livestock false** (no livestock within the country park itself). However, note in app copy that New Forest ponies may be encountered on the approach roads and on the extended Lepe Loop route.

### 5. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Tidal currents - serious hazard: Currents at Lepe are extremely strong, particularly at Lepe Spit and Stansore Point. The National Coastwatch Institution (NCI) warns that currents at these points can be "too strong even for an Olympic swimmer" and strong enough to sweep a child off their feet. There are no lifeguards at Lepe at any time.
- Safe swimming area: The family beach in front of The Lookout cafe is the most gently shelving section and is relatively safer, though currents can still be strong here. "Respect the Water" guidance applies year-round.
- Dogs swimming: Calmer sections of the family beach provide reasonable dog swimming in settled conditions at low tide. Do not allow dogs to swim near Lepe Spit or Stansore Point.
- MOD land: There is MOD land in the wider Solent area. This does not directly affect the country park walk - stay on designated paths.
- Beach type: Primarily shingle with sand in some sections. Wide foreshore at low tide.
- Views: Clear views across the Solent to the Isle of Wight throughout the walk.

### 7. FAQ

**Q: Can dogs go on the beach at Lepe all year round?**
A: Not on the main family beach. Dogs are banned from the family beach section from approximately May to September. The boundaries are marked by a flagpole and a signed cable pole. From October to April, dogs are welcome on the family beach. Year-round, there is an alternative coastal path where dogs are welcome, running alongside the restricted area.

**Q: Is it safe for dogs to swim at Lepe?**
A: In the calmer sections in front of The Lookout cafe, swimming is possible in settled conditions at low tide. However, the currents at Lepe - particularly at Lepe Spit and Stansore Point - are extremely dangerous. The National Coastwatch Institution warns that currents at these spots can be too strong even for an Olympic swimmer. There are no lifeguards on duty at any point. Do not let dogs swim near Lepe Spit.

**Q: What is the D-Day connection at Lepe?**
A: Lepe was a major departure point for the D-Day landings of June 1944. Twelve thousand tons of supplies and 2,500 vehicles were processed here daily. Six massive concrete caissons built at Lepe were towed across the Channel to form the Mulberry harbours used in Normandy. The concrete mats laid on the beach to support tanks are still visible less than half a mile from the car park - best seen at low tide.

**Q: Are New Forest ponies a concern on this walk?**
A: The ponies do not graze within the country park itself. However, the park is within the New Forest National Park and ponies roam freely through the surrounding area, including on the approach roads. On the extended Lepe Loop (5 miles, takes you inland on country lanes), encounters with roaming ponies and cattle are likely. Keep dogs under control and on lead near all New Forest animals - they are semi-wild and unpredictable.

### 8. Seasonal Notes

- Best time: Autumn and winter (October to April) for full beach access with dogs. Spring and autumn for Solent views.
- Spring and summer (May to September): Dogs restricted from the main family beach. The alternative coastal route is available year-round. Very busy in summer. The Lookout cafe open.
- Autumn and winter (October to April): Full beach access for dogs. Quieter. Lower parking charges. The Solent can be dramatic in winter. D-Day remains are best visible at low tide when the beach is wider.
- Year-round: Strong tidal currents are a constant hazard. No lifeguards at any time.
- Worst conditions: High summer bank holidays - extremely busy car park, restricted beach for dogs, very hot shingle, strong tidal currents.
