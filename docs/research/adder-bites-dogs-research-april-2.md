## Key Facts for the Writer

1. The adder (Vipera berus) is the UK's only venomous snake. It is the only snake a UK dog walker needs to worry about. Grass snakes and smooth snakes are harmless. (ARC Trust, Wildlife Trusts)

2. Around 100 dogs are bitten by adders in the UK each year, according to data from the Veterinary Poisons Information Service. The mortality rate is 4.6% - so roughly 1 in 22 bitten dogs dies. 97% of bitten dogs show clinical symptoms. (VPIS retrospective study, PubMed 21868437)

3. The highest risk months are April to July - 69.2% of reported bites occurred during this window. Bites were especially common between 15:00 and 16:00 hours. Spring is particularly dangerous because venom is more potent after hibernation and the snakes are still sluggish, making escape less likely and accidental encounters more common. (VPIS study, Medivet)

4. Dogs are most commonly bitten on the face/nose (especially when sniffing an adder) and on the legs/paws (when stepping on one). Facial and neck bites are more serious than limb bites due to proximity to the airway and blood supply. (Vets Now, PDSA, New Forest vet Alice McLeish)

5. Carry the dog - do not let it walk after a bite. Movement speeds venom circulation. Keep the bitten area elevated above the heart if possible. Get to a vet immediately - this is a veterinary emergency. Do not cut, suck, or bandage the wound. (PDSA, Vets Now, Scottish SPCA)

6. Symptoms typically appear within two hours of a bite. They include painful dark swelling with two puncture wounds, pale gums, vomiting, lethargy, and in severe cases tremors, collapse, and breathing difficulties. Full recovery with treatment averages five days. (VPIS study)

7. Antivenom is used in roughly 56% of cases and cuts swelling duration roughly in half (from 94 hours to 47 hours on average). There is no licensed antivenom for animals in the UK - vets must import the human product via a Special Treatment Certificate from the VMD. Not all practices stock it. The VPIS ToxBox service (call 020 7305 5055) can locate the nearest stocking practice. (VPIS global, VPIS study)

8. Small dogs face greater risk than large dogs. A smaller body mass means venom reaches a higher concentration faster. Dogs bitten on the face or neck are at higher risk than those bitten on a limb. (Medivet, New Forest Dog Owners Group, ManyPets)

9. Surrey has the second largest adder habitat by area in the UK (after Devon). Frensham Common is one of only two Surrey sites where all six native reptile species including adders are found. Stanage Edge in the Peak District, the New Forest, and Dartmoor are all confirmed adder habitats. (Surrey Wildlife Trust, VPIS study geographic data, New Forest NPA, Dartmoor NPA)

10. Adder populations are declining across the UK. 90% of surveyed populations are declining. They could be restricted to just a handful of sites within 10 to 20 years. Legally protected under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 - it is illegal to kill or injure an adder. (ARGUK, Froglife)

## 1. Adder Basics for UK Dog Walkers

### What is an adder?

The European adder (Vipera berus) is Britain's only venomous snake. It grows to around 60-80 cm in length. Males are typically grey-white with a dark brown or black zigzag stripe running the full length of the back. Females are brown to copper coloured with a darker zigzag. Both sexes have a distinctive V-shaped or X-shaped marking on the back of the head. Occasionally all-black (melanistic) individuals are found, making the zigzag harder to see. Adders have vertical slit pupils, which distinguishes them from harmless grass snakes. (ARC Trust, Vets Now, ARGUK)

The two other native UK snakes - grass snake and smooth snake - are harmless. Smooth snakes are very rare and restricted mainly to heathlands in Dorset, Hampshire, Surrey and Sussex.

### Where are adders found?

Adders are found throughout mainland Britain including England, Scotland, and Wales, but are absent from Northern Ireland, Ireland, the Isle of Man, the Outer Hebrides, and the Channel Islands. (Wildlife Trusts, ARC Trust)

**Preferred habitats:**
- Lowland heathland (primary habitat)
- Upland moorland
- Open woodland and woodland edges/rides
- Coastal dunes and sea cliffs
- Chalk downland and grassland
- Rocky hillsides and south-facing slopes
- Rough grassland with dense sward
- Bracken-covered hillsides
- Disused quarries and railway embankments

Adders require a mosaic of habitat - open, south-facing ground for basking alongside dense cover (heather, gorse, bramble, bracken) for shelter and hibernation. They are typically found on habitat edges and transitional zones - for example on the edge of a woodland ride, tucked under a hedgerow, or by a dry stone wall. (Kent Wildlife Trust, ARGUK)

**Key UK strongholds:**
- Surrey and Hampshire (Surrey has the second largest adder habitat area in the UK by area, after Devon)
- West Wales and southwest England (Devon, Dorset, Cornwall)
- The New Forest (common and widespread in open areas)
- The Scottish Highlands (vast moorland and woodland habitat)
- Dartmoor (characteristic moorland species)
- The Peak District Eastern Moors (main local stronghold, estimated 300 individuals)
- Yorkshire heathland and moorland (Yorkshire Dales, North York Moors)

Adders are absent or very rare in central England (already locally extinct in Nottinghamshire and Oxfordshire). (ARC Trust, ARGUK, Surrey Wildlife Trust, Peak District CCVA)

### When is adder season?

Adders are active from approximately March to October, though the exact timing varies by region and year - males can emerge from January in southern England and West Wales in mild years, or as late as March in Scotland. They hibernate from October, spending winter in dry, sheltered spots such as old rodent burrows, hollow trees, and leaf piles.

**Monthly risk breakdown:**

- **January to February:** Hibernating in most of the UK. Very low risk. Males may emerge briefly on exceptionally warm days in southern England.
- **March:** Emergence and basking season begins. Adders are sluggish and concentrated near hibernation sites. Risk begins, especially in southern England.
- **April:** Risk increases significantly. Males actively seeking mates. Venom at its most potent post-hibernation. VPIS data shows bites begin to rise sharply this month.
- **May to July:** Peak risk period. 69.2% of all reported dog bites in the VPIS study occurred between April and July. Adders are fully active and widely distributed across habitat. May to July is the single highest-risk window.
- **August to September:** Still active. Young adders born August-September (6-20 per litter). Adults begin to wind down activity.
- **October:** Activity declines and hibernation begins. Risk drops but encounters still possible on warm autumn days.
- **November to December:** Hibernating. Minimal risk.

(VPIS retrospective study, Kent Wildlife Trust, ARGUK, Wildlife Trusts, ARC Trust)

**Why is spring especially dangerous?**
After months of hibernation, adders emerge with a full venom load and heightened hunger. Their venom is at its most potent in spring. They are also relatively sluggish immediately after emergence - which means they are less able to escape quickly, making accidental close encounters more likely and bites more probable when they do occur. (Medivet, ARGUK, search data)

### What time of day are adders most active?

VPIS study data identified 15:00-16:00 as the most frequent time for dog bites - likely because adders that have been basking all day begin moving back towards cover in the afternoon. This makes them more visible and active at path level.

In spring and autumn, adders typically bask from around 9 to 11 in the morning and again from around 16:00 to 19:00. During very hot summer days, they may be largely inactive at midday and shift to more nocturnal activity in the south of England.

There is little point looking for adders when temperatures are below 10 degrees C or above 17 degrees C. At below 10 degrees C they do not venture out. At above 17 degrees C they are alert, fast-moving, and likely to escape before a dog encounters them. The most dangerous temperature window - when adders are warm enough to be active but may not move away fast enough - is roughly 10-17 degrees C. (Dog Friendly Cornwall, ARGUK, Surrey ARG)

### Weather and encounters

Adders are far more likely to be encountered on sunny days when they are basking. South-facing slopes, log piles, patches of bare ground, and path edges on sunny spring and summer mornings are classic basking spots. They use surrounding vegetation for cover and return to it if disturbed. After rain on a warm day, adders may emerge to bask more actively.

### Are adders on or off the path?

Both. Adders bask in sunlit patches including path edges and occasionally on the path itself - particularly if the path surface is warm and south-facing. The greater risk for dogs is off-path in long grass, heather, bracken, and bramble - dogs that rush into undergrowth or push their nose into dense vegetation are at higher risk. (New Forest NPA, ARGUK, Vets Now)

## 2. Dog-Specific Risk

### How common are adder bites in dogs?

Approximately 100 dogs are bitten by adders in the UK each year, based on VPIS data. This is relatively rare given the tens of millions of dog walks taking place annually. However, the figure likely underestimates actual incidence since not all cases are reported to VPIS.

The VPIS retrospective study (Reid et al., published 2011, covering 1985-2010) is the primary source of clinical data on canine adder bites in the UK. It found:
- 97% of bitten dogs were symptomatic
- 4.6% of bitten dogs died
- 69.2% of bites occurred between April and July
- Bites most frequent between 15:00-16:00
- Most bites reported in south-east England, particularly Surrey

A 2019 Vets Now report documented an 86% year-on-year increase in adder bite consultations, with at least 39 dogs treated across their clinics in a single spring period. Geographic spread included Bournemouth, Tunbridge Wells, Ipswich, Colchester, Swansea, and Farnham.

(PubMed 21868437, Vets Now 2019 press release)

### Which body areas are most commonly bitten?

- **Nose and muzzle:** Most common for dogs that sniff an adder at close range. Dogs are naturally inquisitive and will investigate a snake with their nose, triggering a defensive bite.
- **Face and head:** If bitten near the mouth, lips, or around the eye.
- **Legs and paws:** If a dog steps on a basking or resting adder.
- **Neck:** Less common but very serious.

PDSA, Vets Now, and New Forest vet Alice McLeish all confirm that nose, face, and lower leg are the most common bite sites. Three dogs bitten in the New Forest in May 2021 - two were bitten on the nose, one on the leg.

Bites to the face and neck are more serious because of proximity to the airway (facial swelling can obstruct breathing), greater blood supply, and the risk of venom reaching the bloodstream more quickly. Limb bites, while painful and causing significant local swelling, are generally less immediately life-threatening. (PDSA, Vets Now, New Forest Dog Owners Group, NFDOG)

### Are certain breeds more at risk?

No specific breed has been scientifically shown to be more susceptible to venom effects. However, body size is a clear factor:

- **Small dogs** (under approximately 10 kg) face disproportionate risk. Their smaller blood volume means venom reaches higher concentrations faster, causing greater tissue damage. Recovery is typically harder and hospitalisation more likely.
- **Large dogs** generally handle bites better, though any bite is still a veterinary emergency.
- **Hunting breeds and terriers** with strong prey instinct or "rummaging" behaviour may be more likely to encounter adders in undergrowth. Spaniels and similar working breeds with low-to-ground sniffing behaviour are noted in the literature as high-encounter-risk dogs.

One documented case involved a Staffordshire Bull Terrier that required leg amputation following a bite. Yorkshire Dales incident involved a four-year-old cocker spaniel named Flora who required overnight hospitalisation and a week of medication. (ManyPets, NFDOG, Richmondshire Today, Medivet)

### Symptoms of adder bite: full timeline

**Immediate (0-30 minutes):**
- Dog may yelp or cry out
- Visible two-puncture wound (though fur may make this hard to see)
- Dark, painful swelling begins forming around the bite site
- Restlessness or distress

**Within 2 hours (most cases):**
- Swelling increases, may become dramatic especially on the face
- Lethargy and depression
- Limping if bitten on a leg
- Pale or white gums
- Drooling
- Vomiting and/or diarrhoea
- Elevated heart rate (tachycardia)
- Elevated temperature (hyperthermia)
- Panting

**Severe cases (can develop rapidly, especially face/neck bites):**
- Swelling obstructs breathing
- Collapse
- Tremors or convulsions
- Blood clotting problems and bruising
- Kidney stress
- In the most severe cases - sudden death from cardiovascular effects

The Scottish SPCA notes symptoms can sometimes take up to 24 hours to fully develop, though VPIS data shows most cases present within 2 hours. About two-thirds of dogs develop both local and systemic effects; one-third show only local effects. (VPIS study, Scottish SPCA, PDSA, Vets Now)

### How serious is an adder bite?

97% of bitten dogs show clinical signs. Mortality rate is 4.6% (approximately 1 in 22 dogs). Most dogs - around 96% - make a full recovery within 5 days with treatment. Average recovery time is 5 days; range is 24 hours to 30 days in more complex cases.

The main factors affecting severity:
1. Location of bite (face/neck far more serious than limb)
2. Size of dog (small dogs at much greater risk)
3. Speed of treatment (faster treatment = better outcome)
4. Whether venom entered the bloodstream directly

(VPIS study, Borrowmydoggy/vet advice, Vets Now)

### Veterinary treatment

Vets typically provide:
- Strong pain relief (analgesia)
- Intravenous fluids to support organs and treat shock
- Antihistamines to reduce swelling and allergic response
- Monitoring with ECG and blood tests for at least 24 hours
- Antibiotics if infection is a concern
- Surgery in rare cases to remove dead tissue around the bite site

**Antivenom:**
Antivenom was used in 55.9% of VPIS-reported cases. It significantly reduces swelling duration - from an average of 94 hours to 47 hours. However, there is no licensed veterinary antivenom product in the UK. Vets must import human antivenom (European Viper Antiserum from Biomed, Poland) using a Special Treatment Certificate from the Veterinary Medicines Directorate (VMD).

Not all vet practices keep antivenom in stock. The VPIS runs a ToxBox scheme - participating practices hold emergency antivenom. Call VPIS on 020 7305 5055 to locate the nearest stocking practice. Emergency suppliers include Raman Pharma (01252 260460) who can offer same-day emergency dispatch.

(VPIS global website, VPIS antivenom supply page, VPIS retrospective study)

**Note on conflicting information:** Some sources (e.g. Vets Now, Medivet) describe antivenom as available for severe cases without mentioning the licensing complexity. The VPIS and Veterinary Practice journal are clearer that no licensed UK veterinary product exists and import is required. Dog owners should not assume their nearest vet stocks antivenom - calling ahead is advisable.

## 3. What Walkers Should Do

### Immediate first aid steps if a dog is bitten

1. **Stay calm.** Panic communicates distress to your dog and increases their heart rate, accelerating venom spread.

2. **Stop your dog moving.** Movement increases blood flow and speeds venom distribution. Do not let the dog run or walk if you can possibly avoid it.

3. **Carry the dog.** Pick your dog up and carry them to your vehicle or towards the nearest point of vet access. If you cannot carry the whole dog, at minimum prevent them from running. Keep the bitten limb elevated above the heart level if a leg is bitten.

4. **Leave the bite alone.** Do not squeeze, cut, or suck the wound. Do not apply a bandage or tourniquet. Do not apply ice or cold packs.

5. **Do not give antihistamines orally.** They absorb too slowly to be of significant benefit and should not delay getting to the vet. (NFDOG)

6. **If you can, photograph the snake from a safe distance** - do not approach, handle or harm it. Note the pattern and colour if possible. Adders are legally protected. This helps vets confirm the species.

7. **Call your vet immediately.** Tell them you are bringing in a dog with a suspected adder bite. This gives them time to prepare treatment, locate antivenom if needed, and clear a treatment bay. If your regular vet is closed, call the out-of-hours number.

8. **Get to the vet as fast as possible.** The sooner treatment begins, the better the outcome.

(PDSA, Vets Now, Scottish SPCA, Medivet, New Forest Dog Owners Group)

### What NOT to do

- **Do not suck or cut the wound** - this is ineffective, can introduce infection, and wastes critical time.
- **Do not apply a tourniquet or tight bandage** - this concentrates venom and can cause additional tissue damage. The Scottish SPCA notes adder venom "breaks down blood and tissue" so trapping it in one area causes more harm.
- **Do not apply a pressure immobilisation bandage** - this is sometimes recommended for other venomous bites but is not appropriate for adder bites.
- **Do not let your dog run or exercise** - movement circulates venom faster.
- **Do not try to catch, kill, or handle the snake** - adders are legally protected and a person attempting to handle a snake risks being bitten themselves.
- **Do not give oral antihistamines alone as a substitute for vet treatment.**
- **Do not wait to see if symptoms develop before going to the vet** - seek treatment even if the dog appears fine initially.

(PDSA, Scottish SPCA, NFDOG, Vets Now)

### How quickly does a dog need to reach a vet?

As quickly as possible - ideally within the hour. Adder bites are classified as a veterinary emergency. The VPIS study shows most clinical effects develop within two hours, but in severe cases (especially face/neck bites in small dogs) deterioration can be rapid. Calling ahead lets the vet prepare. Even if your dog seems mildly affected, early intervention significantly improves outcomes.

### Carrying the dog vs. letting them walk

All major sources agree: carry the dog where possible. PDSA, Vets Now, and Medivet all state that carrying the dog (with bitten area elevated) reduces venom spread by minimising circulation. This is especially important for limb bites. If the dog is too large to carry, keep them as calm and still as possible and minimise the distance they walk.

### How to find the nearest emergency vet mid-walk

- **Save your vet's out-of-hours number in your phone before any countryside walk** - all vet practices in the UK are required to provide an emergency out-of-hours service.
- **Vets Now** operates over 60 night and weekend clinics across the UK - use their website or app to find the nearest: vets-now.com
- **RSPCA Vet Finder**: rspca.org.uk/adviceandwelfare/pets/findavet
- **Google Maps**: search "emergency vet near me" for the most geographically relevant result.
- **What3Words**: the free app gives you a precise 3-word address for your location that emergency services and the RSPCA can use. Download before walking in remote areas. Works offline.
- **Animal PoisonLine:** 01202 509000 - for poisons advice including adder bites.
- **VPIS (for vets):** 020 7305 5055

(PDSA, RSPCA, what3words, Animal PoisonLine, VPIS)

## 4. Prevention for Dog Walkers

### Reducing the risk of encounters

**Keep your dog on a lead in known adder habitats.** This is the single most effective prevention measure. All major sources - PDSA, Vets Now, Medivet, New Forest NPA, Dartmoor NPA - recommend lead use in heathland, moorland, and coastal grassland habitats between March and October, or at least during peak season (April to August). Dogs on leads can be quickly pulled away. Off-lead dogs that rush into undergrowth or push through heather are far more likely to disturb a resting adder.

**Stick to established paths** where ground is clear and adders are more visible and more likely to move away from foot traffic. Adders rely on vibration to detect approach - walking on a clear path gives them more warning and they will typically move away before you arrive. Pushing through unmaintained vegetation dramatically increases encounter likelihood.

**Keep dogs close to you** in vegetation zones rather than ranging far ahead through long grass, bracken, or heather.

**Scan the path ahead** before your dog enters an area - especially warm, sun-exposed sections, south-facing banks, log piles, and rock edges. An adder basking in a sunny patch may be visible if you look before your dog runs into it.

**Make noise.** Adders detect vibration through the ground. Walking with purpose (not tiptoeing) and talking normally helps alert adders to your approach so they can move away. Some sources suggest whistling when approaching known risk areas.

**Walk at lower-risk times.** Very early morning (before 9 am) and evening (after 7 pm in spring/autumn) carry lower risk. High summer midday heat also reduces activity. The most dangerous window is mild spring/autumn days between roughly 9 am and 5 pm at temperatures of 10-17 degrees C.

**Plan around the season.** April to July is peak risk. Consider extra vigilance, shortening routes through heather and bracken, or keeping dogs on leads for the full walk during this period.

**Research your walk location.** The National Biodiversity Network (NBN Atlas) holds reptile sighting records. Local wildlife trusts and amphibian and reptile groups (ARGs) can advise on known adder sites. Some walking blogs and local dog walking groups also flag known hotspots.

### Training approaches

**Solid recall** is the most important skill in adder country. A dog that returns immediately when called can be pulled away from an investigation before it becomes a bite. Practice recall in progressively distracting environments. Blue Cross provides detailed recall training guidance at bluecross.org.uk.

**"Leave it" command** - train your dog to disengage from something on the ground when asked. Start with food and toys in low-distraction environments, then proof the behaviour against progressively more interesting distractions. This command can theoretically interrupt a dog investigating a snake, though it must be very well established to work reliably in a high-arousal situation.

**Collar bell** - some sources suggest attaching a bell to your dog's collar to give adders warning of approach. However, the Scottish SPCA notes that snakes do not hear like mammals and do not detect airborne sound - they detect ground vibration. A bell may have limited additional benefit beyond what normal dog movement already provides. It does not substitute for a lead in high-risk areas.

(Ladies Working Dog Group, Blue Cross recall training, Dog Friendly Cornwall, Scottish SPCA)

### What to look for on the path ahead

- A small (50-80 cm), thick-bodied snake lying still in a sunny patch - adders are not fast movers in cooler temperatures and may appear to be a stick or shadow
- The distinctive dark zigzag down the back - grey (male) or brown/copper (female) with the zigzag in black or very dark brown
- The V or X head marking
- On path edges where sunlit ground meets vegetation cover
- Near south-facing banks, log piles, stone walls, and heathland tussocks
- Move slowly if you spot one and give it space to move away

Occasionally all-black (melanistic) adders are found - these look like a uniformly dark snake and the zigzag may not be visible. Treat any small snake in heathland or moorland habitat with caution regardless of markings.

## 5. Sniffout Walk Connections

### Walk risk assessment methodology

Risk assessment is based on: (a) documented adder presence in the habitat type and region, (b) specific source confirmation where available, (c) habitat characteristics of each walk. "High risk" means adders are confirmed present and the habitat is primary adder habitat. "Moderate risk" means adders are present in the broader area and/or the habitat is suitable but the specific terrain may be less optimal. "Lower risk" means habitat is less typical for adders though encounters remain possible.

---

**1. Hindhead Common and Devils Punch Bowl - Surrey heathland**

**Risk level: HIGH**

Hindhead Commons is part of the Wealden Heaths National Nature Reserve (2,765 hectares), described by the National Trust as home to rare and endangered species including adders. The NNR specifically lists adders as one of the protected species of concern on the heaths. Surrey has the second-largest adder habitat area in the UK (after Devon). The heathland at Hindhead is classic adder habitat: dry, south-facing heather and gorse with woodland edges. The Wealden Heaths are a known adder stronghold in Surrey. Dogs should be kept on leads throughout the walk from April to August at minimum.

Sources: National Trust Wealden Heaths NNR page, Surrey Wildlife Trust adder mapping

---

**2. Frensham Great Pond - Surrey sandy heathland**

**Risk level: HIGH**

Frensham Common is one of only two Surrey sites where all six native reptile species are present, including adders. It is a Site of Special Scientific Interest, Special Protection Area, and Special Area of Conservation. The heathland at Frensham - purple heather, gorse, bracken, and sandy tracks - is prime adder habitat. This is one of the most confirmed adder sites in this set of walks. Dogs should be kept on leads in heather and off-path areas throughout adder season.

Sources: National Trust Frensham Little Pond page, Surrey Hills National Landscape, ramblingsnscribblings.com sand lizard survey

---

**3. Leith Hill and Coldharbour - Surrey mixed woodland and heath**

**Risk level: MODERATE-HIGH**

Leith Hill is within the Surrey Hills AONB and contains heathland and woodland glades - both suitable adder habitat. Surrey Wildlife Trust confirms adders inhabit Surrey's heathland and woodland glades throughout the county. Leith Hill's heathland sections carry adder risk. The woodland itself is lower-risk than open heath, but rides and glades at woodland edges are suitable basking habitat. Surrey's second-largest adder habitat area in the UK means the probability of adder presence across Surrey heathland is elevated.

Sources: Surrey Wildlife Trust, National Trust Leith Hill page (habitat description)

---

**4. Box Hill Summit Loop - Surrey chalk downland and woodland**

**Risk level: MODERATE-HIGH**

Surrey ARG and Wild Surrey confirm adders are present in Surrey's chalk grassland habitats including Box Hill. Kent Wildlife Trust notes that adders frequent chalk downland and grassland with south-facing slopes, which describes Box Hill's terrain. The Surrey Wildlife Trust adder mapping project covers the county broadly, and Box Hill's open chalk grassland and woodland edge habitat is suitable. Numbers may be lower than on heathland but adder presence is documented.

Sources: Wild Surrey, Surrey ARG, Kent Wildlife Trust chalk downland guide

---

**5. Seven Sisters Cliffs - East Sussex chalk clifftop**

**Risk level: MODERATE**

The Seven Sisters are part of the South Downs, where adders are recorded in grassland and woodland areas. The chalk grassland clifftop at Seven Sisters - with its open, wind-exposed terrain - is less optimal adder habitat than sheltered heathland or valley scrub. However, adders do inhabit chalk grassland habitats and are recorded in the South Downs. The lower-lying grassland behind the clifftop and any scrubby south-facing banks carry more risk than the exposed cliff edge itself. The site was declared a National Nature Reserve and supports diverse wildlife. Risk is real but likely lower than heathland walks.

Sources: A-Z Animals (South Downs identified as snake habitat), ARC Trust (chalk/sand free-draining soils noted as preferred substrate)

---

**6. Haytor and Hound Tor - Dartmoor moorland**

**Risk level: HIGH**

Dartmoor is a well-documented adder stronghold. The Dartmoor National Park Authority explicitly warns: "During warm weather there is a risk of adders in the undergrowth, so we advise keeping dogs on leads and sticking to footpaths." Adders are a characteristic species of Dartmoor's upland heathland, basking in sheltered sunny patches. Documented incidents include a German Shepherd bitten on the tongue at Roborough Down and a dog bitten near Postbridge (both 2014). The area also has a documented melanistic (black) adder population. Haytor and Hound Tor moorland - open heather, bracken, and rocky ground - is ideal adder habitat.

Sources: Dartmoor NPA visitor dog guidance, Legendary Dartmoor article (documented incidents), A-Z Animals

---

**7. Stanage Edge and Hathersage Moor - Peak District gritstone and moorland**

**Risk level: MODERATE-HIGH**

Adders are specifically confirmed at Stanage Edge. The description from Lets Go Peak District states: "Stanage Edge's rocks are home to shy common lizards and adders." The Derbyshire Times reported adders emerging at Curbar and Stanage after warm weather. The Peak District's Eastern Moors - which includes the Stanage area - is the main adder stronghold in the national park, estimated at around 300 individuals. The heather moorland and gritstone edge habitat is suitable. The PDNP population is considered nationally significant given overall UK adder declines.

Sources: Lets Go Peak District (Stanage page), Peak District CCVA report, Derbyshire Times (adder emergence report)

---

**8. Grasmere Lake Circuit - Lake District lakeside and meadow**

**Risk level: LOW-MODERATE**

Adders are present in Cumbria - the Cumbria Amphibian and Reptile Group (CARG) monitors multiple sites and reported increased sightings in 2023. However, no specific confirmed records for the Grasmere circuit were found. The lakeside and meadow terrain of the Grasmere circuit is less typical adder habitat than open heathland or moorland. Any adjacent moorland or bracken-covered slopes carry higher risk than the lakeside path itself. The Lake District overall is adder territory, but the specific Grasmere walk appears to be lower-risk compared to more heathland-dominated routes.

Sources: Cumbria Wildlife Trust, Cumbria Amphibian and Reptile Group (CARG) monitoring data, George Fisher/Tiso article (redirect - not retrieved)

---

**9. Burley Village and Forest Walks - New Forest ancient woodland**

**Risk level: HIGH**

The New Forest is one of the UK's most documented adder habitats. The New Forest NPA states adders are "common and widespread in the open areas of the New Forest." The best-known adder basking area is along the former railway line between Burley and Brockenhurst - directly adjacent to the Burley walk area. Vet Alice McLeish at a local practice confirmed three dog bites in a single month in May 2021. Adder bites to dogs are described as more common in the New Forest than bites to humans (5 or so human bites per year in the forest). The ancient woodland itself is lower-risk than open heathland, but the forest edges, rides, and heathy clearings adjacent to Burley are confirmed adder habitat.

Sources: New Forest NPA adder page, NFDOG adder bites article, advertiserandtimes.co.uk (2021 dog bite incidents), New Forest National Park website (snake bites dog article)

### Conflicting information between sources

**Peak bite season - ARC Trust vs. VPIS study:**
The VPIS retrospective study (the most rigorous clinical data source) identifies April to July as peak bite season (69.2% of cases). ARC Trust's website states "the majority of bites occur between April and September, with a marked peak in July-August." These are not directly contradictory but the emphases differ - VPIS data suggests bites begin rising sharply in April, while ARC Trust emphasises the July-August peak. Both are correct for different aspects of the pattern. Tom should use both: spring (April onwards) is when risk begins and is particularly dangerous due to venom potency; July-August is the statistical peak by absolute numbers.

**Antivenom availability:**
Some sources (Vets Now, Medivet, some online vet guides) discuss antivenom as a treatment option without noting the absence of a licensed UK veterinary product. VPIS and specialist veterinary literature (Veterinary Practice journal article) make clear that no licensed product exists for animals and vets must navigate import procedures. This is an important nuance. Tom does not need to go into licensing detail in a consumer-facing guide, but should not imply antivenom is routinely and instantly available at any practice.

**Symptoms timeline - "up to 24 hours" vs. "within 2 hours":**
Scottish SPCA states symptoms may take up to 24 hours to appear. VPIS study data shows initial clinical effects typically occur within two hours. These are not contradictory - the full picture is that most dogs show signs within two hours (VPIS study: this is the norm), but in some cases symptoms may develop more gradually. The practical guidance is the same: seek vet treatment immediately regardless, do not wait for symptoms to appear.

**Walking in hot weather:**
Dog Friendly Cornwall notes that above 17 degrees C adders are "alert and fast" and less likely to bite because they can escape. This is based on the temperature/activity relationship and is consistent with ARGUK information. Some other sources simply advise keeping dogs on leads in warm weather without this nuance. The 10-17 degrees C window as a specific danger zone is from the Cornwall and ARGUK sources - Tom could reference this as a useful nuance for experienced walkers without it contradicting other guidance.

**Collar bells:**
Ladies Working Dog Group suggests collar bells as a deterrent. Scottish SPCA contradicts this, noting adders do not hear like mammals and detect ground vibration rather than airborne sound. The bell is probably of limited benefit but is not harmful. Tom should probably not recommend it as a primary prevention strategy.
