## WALK 1: Chipping Campden and Dover's Hill

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Chipping Campden and Dover's Hill
- **Location:** Chipping Campden, Gloucestershire
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** GL55 6UW (Dover's Hill National Trust car park) or GL55 6HT (Chipping Campden School car park, weekends/school holidays only)
- **Latitude:** 52.0537
- **Longitude:** -1.8031
- **Approximate distance:** 6.6 km (4.1 miles circular)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate
- **Terrain type:** Mixed — grass and dirt paths, stony tracks, one main road crossing. Can be muddy after rain. Exposed at hilltop. No stiles but some steep sections. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Off-lead status:** On-lead only
- Dogs must be kept on a short lead at all times on Dover's Hill. The National Trust states the hill is grazed by livestock year-round. This is confirmed on the NT dog walking page for Dover's Hill. (HIGH)
- **Livestock present:** Yes, year-round. Sheep graze the escarpment. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Dogs must remain on lead throughout the NT land at Dover's Hill due to permanent livestock grazing. The car park notes dogs are welcome but facilities are limited (no dog water provided on site). The NT rates this a one-pawprint site.

**Note for Ailsa:** This is on-lead throughout the National Trust section. The route down through Chipping Campden village gives some relief from livestock fields but is a busy tourist village — still on-lead advisable in the main street. There are no sections where off-lead is appropriate on this specific route.

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Dover's Hill National Trust car park, GL55 6UW, National Trust free to members, charge for non-members (JustPark app accepted). Open dawn to dusk. Alternative: free on-street parking on Back Ends in Chipping Campden (short walk to start). Chipping Campden School car park (GL55 6HT) weekends and school holidays. Market Square car park — 2-hour maximum stay. (HIGH)
- **Toilets:** Yes, in Chipping Campden village — Sheep Street and tourist information office on High Street. None at Dover's Hill itself. (HIGH)
- **Cafe or pub:** Multiple dog-friendly options in Chipping Campden. The Noel Arms — dogs welcome in Dover's Bar, Coffee Shop, lounge and conservatory (confirmed dog-friendly). The Red Lion Tavern — dog-friendly confirmed. Most pubs in the village accept dogs. (HIGH)
- **Public transport:** Chipping Campden is served by Johnsons Coach and Bus from Stratford-upon-Avon and Moreton-in-Marsh. Limited service — check current timetables. (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **livestock** — Year-round. Sheep present on Dover's Hill at all times. Dogs must be on lead. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Long grass and scrub on the escarpment. (MEDIUM)
- **adders** — April to September. Gorse and scrub edges on open hillside. (MEDIUM)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Open grassland habitat on Dover's Hill SSSI. (MEDIUM)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Exposed stony tracks on the escarpment can heat up in direct sun. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** Top of the Cotswold escarpment with wide-open views — huge sniffing territory. The Cotswold Olimpick Games site gives a distinctive amphitheatre feel, open and spacious. Path through honey-coloured Chipping Campden village — varied textures, smells, and architecture. Good contrast between open escarpment and enclosed village lanes.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Wide open grassland at Dover's Hill (even on lead, plenty of space). Multiple dog-friendly pubs with water for dogs. Interesting variety of terrain: hilltop, field paths, village streets.
- **Honest practical note:** Dogs must remain on lead for the entire Dover's Hill section due to year-round livestock grazing. This is a non-negotiable National Trust rule, not just a seasonal request. Plan accordingly — the views are exceptional but off-lead running is not available here.

## WALK 2: Bourton-on-the-Water and the Windrush Valley

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Bourton-on-the-Water and the Windrush Valley
- **Location:** Bourton-on-the-Water, Gloucestershire
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** GL54 2LU (Bourton Vale car park) or GL54 2BN (Rissington Road car park)
- **Latitude:** 51.8848
- **Longitude:** -1.7580
- **Approximate distance:** 8.6 km (5.4 miles circular — Bourton to the Slaughters route)
- **Difficulty:** Easy
- **Terrain type:** Mainly dirt and grass paths, some quiet country roads, short section along a busier road with verge walking. One stile. Can be muddy in wet weather. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Partial off-lead.** Dogs must be on lead in Bourton-on-the-Water village itself (very busy tourist crowds, high pedestrian traffic, dogs reported to be unsafe off lead in the river area due to volume of visitors). Once clear of the village the route gives opportunities for off-lead in fields between the villages where no livestock present, though sections cross livestock fields requiring leads. (MEDIUM)
- **If partial or on-lead:** In the village, dogs must be kept on lead due to extreme tourist crowds, narrow riverside paths, and unpredictable foot traffic. The River Windrush in the village centre is accessible for paddling/swimming but should be supervised given crowds. On fields with livestock (present seasonally), leads required. Off-lead opportunities exist on open field sections away from livestock between villages. (MEDIUM)
- **Livestock present:** Seasonal — sheep and cattle on some field sections along the route between villages. Not in the village itself. (MEDIUM)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Dogs not permitted inside the Model Village attraction. The village itself is extremely busy in summer — visitor congestion on the riverside path makes off-lead impractical. Dog water bowls and drinking stations are set out throughout the village. (HIGH for village restrictions; MEDIUM for rural sections)

**Note:** The River Windrush in the village is shallow enough for dogs to paddle. Dogs reported to enjoy the water. However the river in the village runs alongside public greens that are packed with tourists in peak season. (MEDIUM)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Bourton Vale car park, GL54 2LU, £5 for 3 hours (Cotswold District Council operated). Rissington Road car park, GL54 2BN, charge applies (PayByPhone or card). Arrive early in summer as the village fills quickly. (HIGH)
- **Toilets:** Yes — public toilets at Rissington Road (GL54 2DR, 40p charge) and Church Rooms on corner of High Street and Rectory Lane (GL54 2AX, 40p). Not available along the rural sections of the route. (HIGH)
- **Cafe or pub:** Multiple dog-friendly options. The Mousetrap Inn — dog treats behind the bar, ground floor dog-friendly bedrooms available, explicitly dog-friendly. The Willow — dog treats and water bowls provided. Box Bush — dog-friendly cafe. Slaughters Country Inn at Lower Slaughter — traditional pub with riverside garden, good mid-route stop. (HIGH)
- **Public transport:** Pulhams 801 bus runs roughly hourly connecting Moreton-in-Marsh rail station to Bourton-on-the-Water (30 minutes). Connections to Cheltenham and Stow-on-the-Wold. (HIGH)

### 4. Hazards

- **livestock** — Seasonal, approximately April to November. Fields between Bourton and the Slaughters. (MEDIUM)
- **ticks** — March to October. Grass verges, riverside vegetation, field edges. (MEDIUM)
- **flooding** — October to March. The River Windrush is prone to flooding. Some path sections alongside the river and through the Slaughters village can become impassable after heavy rain. (MEDIUM)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Riverside and field margins. (LOW)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Hard village streets and exposed stony paths in hot weather. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The River Windrush running through the heart of the village — dogs can paddle in the shallow water. The route heads out to Upper and Lower Slaughter through genuinely quiet countryside once clear of the village crowds. The contrast between the busy honey-stone village and the still, empty fields is striking.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Windrush paddling in the village. Multiple dog-friendly pubs (the Slaughters Country Inn makes an excellent mid-route stop with riverside garden). Water bowls throughout the village. Good variety of terrain for dogs — river, grass, field paths, village lanes.
- **Honest practical note:** Bourton-on-the-Water is extremely busy in summer. The river in the village is best visited early morning or out of peak season — the narrow riverside paths become congested by mid-morning at weekends. Plan to arrive before 9am if visiting July to August.

## WALK 3: Broadway Tower and Fish Hill

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Broadway Tower and Fish Hill
- **Location:** Broadway, Worcestershire
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** WR12 7LB (Broadway Tower main car park) or WR12 7LD (Fish Hill Picnic Area car park — free, recommended start point)
- **Latitude:** 52.0242
- **Longitude:** -1.8357
- **Approximate distance:** 7.0 km (4.4 miles circular from Broadway village) or 4.5 km loop from tower car park
- **Difficulty:** Moderate
- **Terrain type:** Mixed — open grassland, field paths, some woodland edges. Exposed at hilltop. Some steep sections. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Partial off-lead.** Dogs are very welcome at Broadway Tower grounds but must be kept on lead within the estate grounds to safeguard the resident red deer herd. Once past the tower and out onto the open fields and Cotswold Way sections beyond the estate boundary, dogs can be off lead where no livestock is present. (HIGH)
- **If partial:** Dogs must be on lead throughout the 50-acre Broadway Tower estate due to the resident red deer herd. Off-lead is possible on public footpath sections away from the estate. Fish Hill car park (free) is the recommended start point for a walk that keeps dogs off lead for the majority of the route, visiting the tower as a short detour on lead. (HIGH)
- **Livestock present:** Deer on the Broadway Tower estate — year-round. Possible sheep/cattle on surrounding field sections. (HIGH for deer; MEDIUM for broader route livestock)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Dogs not permitted inside the Tower Museum. Dogs welcome in the grounds. Dog-friendly seating areas at both the Morris and Brown cafe and the Tower Barn cafe. (HIGH)

**Note on Broadway Tower ownership:** Broadway Tower is NOT a National Trust property. It is privately owned and operated (Broadway Tower Ltd). The surrounding fields and Cotswold Way sections pass over public rights of way and open access land managed separately. (HIGH — clarifies earlier brief question)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Fish Hill Picnic Area car park, WR12 7LD — free, approximately 20 cars, dawn to dusk, 15-minute walk to Broadway Tower. Recommended for dog walkers wanting more off-lead time. Broadway Tower main car park, WR12 7LB — £3 charge (payable on arrival), open to non-visitors to the Tower. (HIGH)
- **Toilets:** Available at Broadway Tower estate. None at Fish Hill car park. (MEDIUM)
- **Cafe or pub:** Morris and Brown Cafe at Broadway Tower — dog-friendly outdoor seating confirmed. Tower Barn Espresso Bar — dog-friendly outdoor seating confirmed. Both on the Broadway Tower estate. (HIGH)
- **Public transport:** Broadway village is served by Johnsons Coach and Bus service. Broadway is approximately 2.5 km downhill from the tower — the village walk adds to the route for those arriving by bus. (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **deer** — Year-round. Red deer herd resident on the Broadway Tower estate. Dogs must be on lead. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Long grass and woodland edges on field sections. (MEDIUM)
- **livestock** — Seasonal, April to November. Surrounding fields on the broader circular route. (MEDIUM)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Exposed hilltop and stony Cotswold Way sections in direct sun. (MEDIUM)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Open grassland sections. (LOW)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The open hilltop gives spectacular 360-degree views — up to 62 miles on a clear day — with a huge wind-scoured plateau ideal for a good run (on the Cotswold Way sections off the estate). The distinctive Gothic tower rising from the escarpment is unlike anything else in the Cotswolds. Red deer encounter on the estate gives a memorable wildlife moment even on lead.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** The Fish Hill approach maximises off-lead time before the estate section. Dog-friendly cafes with outdoor seating mean a proper rest stop mid-walk. The open Cotswold Way grassland is ideal for energetic dogs.
- **Honest practical note:** Dogs must be on lead throughout the Broadway Tower estate due to the resident red deer — this covers the central 50-acre section of the walk. Start from Fish Hill car park rather than the tower car park to keep the on-lead section contained and short.

## WALK 4: Cleeve Hill and Cleeve Common

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Cleeve Hill and Cleeve Common
- **Location:** Cleeve Hill, Gloucestershire (near Cheltenham)
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** GL52 3PW (Quarry Car Park near Golf Club — the only legal parking on the common)
- **Latitude:** 51.9199
- **Longitude:** -2.0072
- **Approximate distance:** 9.7 km (6 miles circular from Quarry Car Park — the longer National Trail circular) or 5 km shorter circular
- **Difficulty:** Moderate
- **Terrain type:** Open limestone grassland, some stony paths, golf course crossing on established tracks only. Exposed at 330m. Undulating with some steep sections. Gorse and scrub throughout. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Partial off-lead.** Cleeve Common is CRoW Act open access land. Under the CRoW Act, dogs must be kept on a lead of no more than 2 metres from 1 March to 31 July on open access land (not on public rights of way). Year-round, dogs must be on lead whenever livestock is present. From 1 August to 28 February, dogs can be off lead on open access land away from livestock. (HIGH — confirmed from Cleeve Common Trust's own website)
- **If partial:** On lead 1 March to 31 July (CRoW Act). On lead year-round when near livestock. Off-lead permissible 1 August to end of February on open access land. Sheep present April to November; cattle year-round. Given cattle are present year-round, effectively on lead for much of the year. (HIGH)
- **Livestock present:** Yes — sheep April to November, cattle year-round. Over 20 sheep die annually on the common from dog attacks. Cleeve Common Trust is explicit about this. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Golf course crossing — use only the wide established track through, never cross fairways, greens or tees. Car park — dogs must exit on leads. No commercial dog walking without a licence (max 6 dogs per licensed walker). Adders present in gorse and scrub — significant hazard for off-lead dogs in spring/summer. (HIGH)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Quarry Car Park, GL52 3PW (off narrow lane signed to golf club, past cattlegrid). £2 for 24 hours. This is the only legal parking on the common. Parking elsewhere on the common is illegal. (HIGH)
- **Toilets:** Golf club toilets are the only public facilities on the common. No dedicated public toilets on the open common. (HIGH)
- **Cafe or pub:** No cafe on the common itself. Golf club may provide refreshments. Nearest options are in Bishop's Cleeve village or Cheltenham. (MEDIUM)
- **Public transport:** Stagecoach Service W (Cheltenham to Winchcombe) stops near Cleeve Common. Check current timetables. (MEDIUM)

### 4. Hazards

- **livestock** — Cattle year-round, sheep April to November. High risk — fatalities on this common documented. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to September. High density in gorse and scrub. Cleeve Common explicitly warns about adders — seek immediate vet care if bitten, keep dog calm and carry if possible. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Present throughout. Common Trust directs visitors to NHS tick guidance. (HIGH)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Open grassland SSSI. Coincides with CRoW Act lead requirement. (HIGH)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Limestone paths and stony tracks in direct sun. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The highest point in the Cotswolds (330m) gives genuinely huge views west over Cheltenham, the River Severn, and into Wales. The common has a wild, open character unlike the enclosed valley walks of the main Cotswolds — more like moorland than manicured countryside. The lone beech tree at 317m is a distinctive landmark. A real feeling of space and exposure.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Vast open grassland with real freedom when livestock-free (August to March). The wild, scrubby terrain provides exceptional scent variety. The views are the best in the Cotswolds.
- **Honest practical note:** This common has a serious livestock history — over 20 sheep are killed by dogs here annually. Leads are required from March to July (CRoW Act) and whenever livestock is present year-round. Cattle roam year-round so leads are effectively required for most visits. Adders are active in the gorse from April onwards — keep dogs to main paths and away from scrub edges, particularly in spring.

## WALK 5: Symonds Yat Rock and the Wye Valley

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Symonds Yat Rock and the Wye Valley
- **Location:** Symonds Yat, Herefordshire/Gloucestershire border (Wye Valley AONB)
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** GL16 7NZ (main Symonds Yat Rock car park, Forestry England) — confirmed as the primary start point for Forestry England trails
- **Latitude:** 51.8373
- **Longitude:** -2.6342
- **Approximate distance:** 8.5 km (5.3 miles — circular walk from Symonds Yat Rock car park down to the river, east bank to Symonds Yat East village, rope ferry crossing, west bank return)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate
- **Terrain type:** Mixed — wide surfaced stone tracks and narrower unsurfaced woodland paths. Some steep descents from the rock to the river. Undulating riverside paths. (HIGH)

### 2. Dog Access

- **Partial off-lead.** Most of the route is on Forestry England managed land and public rights of way through the Wye Valley AONB. Dogs are welcome and can be off lead on woodland sections where no livestock is present. Dogs must be on lead near cliff edges (significant drop at Symonds Yat Rock viewpoint), near the river, and wherever livestock is encountered. The route is primarily woodland and riverside with no significant livestock sections. (HIGH for welcome; MEDIUM for precise off-lead status)
- **If partial:** Lead required at Symonds Yat Rock viewpoint (cliff edge, significant drop). Lead recommended on the steep descent from the rock. Off-lead appropriate on woodland and riverside sections. Standard close control near the rope ferry and riverside. (MEDIUM)
- **Livestock present:** No regular livestock on the main woodland and riverside route. Occasional livestock on some adjacent field paths. (MEDIUM)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** Rope ferry (hand-pulled chain ferry between Symonds Yat East and West) — dogs are permitted. Multiple sources confirm dogs can cross on the ferry. Ferry operates year-round except in adverse river conditions. (HIGH for ferry dog access)

**East vs West bank:** The east bank (starting from the main Forestry England car park) is the more popular approach with the viewpoint at Symonds Yat Rock. The west bank is quieter with fewer visitors. A classic circular combines both via the rope ferry and suspension bridge at Biblins. East bank better for the peregrine viewpoint and the cafe. (MEDIUM)

**Peregrine falcons:** Peregrine falcons nest at Coldwell Rocks at Symonds Yat from approximately April to August. The RSPB has operated watches at the site since 1982. The viewing point is at Symonds Yat Rock — dogs are welcome at the viewpoint but should be kept close. There are no formal dog exclusion zones reported for the peregrine nesting area based on available sources. The viewing is from the rock above — dogs do not approach the nesting cliff face. (MEDIUM — no formal restrictions found; recommend flagging for the Editor to verify with Forestry England directly)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Symonds Yat Rock main car park, GL16 7NZ (Forestry England). £3 minimum for 2 hours. Cash recommended (poor phone signal for pay-by-phone). Steep single-track road with passing places — drive cautiously. Free public toilets at the car park. (HIGH)
- **Toilets:** Yes — free public toilets at the main car park. (HIGH)
- **Cafe or pub:** Symonds Yat Rock Cafe (log cabin cafe at the viewpoint, open from 10am daily, teas, coffees, sandwiches, ice creams) — dog-friendly outdoor seating. Saracens Head Inn at Symonds Yat East — dog-friendly, riverside location, accessible via the rope ferry, confirmed dog-welcoming. Ye Old Ferrie Inn at Symonds Yat West — also dog-friendly. (HIGH)
- **Public transport:** Limited. The area is best accessed by car. Some seasonal shuttle services in the Forest of Dean — check Traveline. (LOW)

### 4. Hazards

- **cliff** — Year-round. Symonds Yat Rock viewpoint has a significant drop. Keep dogs on lead at the viewpoint and on cliff-edge paths. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Dense woodland throughout the route. High tick risk in the Wye Valley wooded gorge. (HIGH)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Woodland and scrub areas. Peregrine falcons nest nearby — keep dogs to main paths during nesting season. (MEDIUM)
- **flooding** — October to March. The River Wye floods regularly and can make riverside paths impassable. Check conditions before visiting in autumn/winter. The ferry may also be suspended in high water. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to September. Scrub and sunny woodland clearings. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The deep wooded gorge of the Wye Valley with the river winding 150m below the rock is one of the most dramatic settings in England for a dog walk. The hand-pulled rope ferry across the Wye is a memorable crossing (dogs permitted). Dense ancient woodland gives exceptional scent variety. The peregrine viewpoint adds genuine wildlife theatre.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** The rope ferry is a dog-friendly highlight — a short hand-pulled crossing that dogs take in their stride. The wooded riverside path along the east bank is largely traffic-free and excellent for dogs. Multiple dog-friendly pubs accessible on foot from the route.
- **Honest practical note:** The main car park involves a steep, narrow single-track road with passing places — take it slowly. Phone signal is very poor at Symonds Yat Rock. The rope ferry cannot operate in adverse river conditions; check before relying on it for a circular route (a long alternative via the B4432 road exists).

## WALK 6: Malvern Hills — Worcestershire Beacon Circuit

### 1. Basic Data

- **Full name:** Malvern Hills — Worcestershire Beacon Circuit
- **Location:** Great Malvern, Worcestershire
- **Nearest postcode for parking:** WR14 4EH (Beacon Road car park — closest to Worcestershire Beacon, confirmed by Visit the Malverns and Malvern Hills Trust)
- **Latitude:** 52.0912
- **Longitude:** -2.3400
- **Approximate distance:** 9.7 km (6 km for the shorter circuit; a longer 9.7km circuit takes in more of the ridge. The standard Worcestershire Beacon circular is approximately 6 km / 3.7 miles)
- **Difficulty:** Moderate (some hard sections on ridge approach)
- **Terrain type:** Open hilltop, steep ridge paths, some woodland sections, well-worn paths. The ridge is exposed and can be very windy. Good underfoot on main paths but rough on off-piste sections. (HIGH)

**Note on distance:** Multiple routes exist. The standard Worcestershire Beacon circuit from Beacon Road is 6 km / 3.7 miles. A longer ridge walk incorporating more of the Malvern Hills range can reach 9.7 km. For the page, use the standard circuit of 6 km with an option note.

### 2. Dog Access

- **Partial off-lead.** Walkers have a right of access across all land under the care of the Malvern Hills Trust. Dogs are welcome and popular on the hills. Dogs must be kept under close control at all times. On lead near livestock — the Trust operates a Stockwatch service showing where electric-fenced livestock enclosures are currently sited. On lead in areas signed for ground-nesting birds (March to July). The open ridge sections away from livestock enclosures offer genuine off-lead freedom. (HIGH)
- **If partial:** Lead required whenever livestock is present (check Stockwatch before visiting at grazingmap.nofence.no). Lead required near ground-nesting bird signs (March to July). Off-lead on open ridge sections clear of stock. (HIGH)
- **Livestock present:** Yes — free-roaming livestock across the hills. Roe deer in woodland sections. The Trust operates temporary electric-fenced enclosures across Northern, Central, Southern, Old Hills, and Commons areas — location changes seasonally. (HIGH)
- **Specific dog restrictions:** No specific exclusion zones beyond livestock and nesting bird areas. Dog waste must be picked up — either remove or use provided bins. Owners must ensure dogs are wormed (parasite transmission risk to livestock). (HIGH)

### 3. Practical Information

- **Parking:** Beacon Road car park, WR14 4EH — pay and display. New charges from April 2026: £6.50 day ticket, £3.75 evening (after 6pm). Annual pass available at £59/year. Card payment only at some machines — Beacon Road accepts both cash and card. (HIGH — charges verified from Malvern Hills Trust parking page as of April 2026)
- **Toilets:** Facilities available at some Malvern Hills Trust car parks. Not confirmed specifically at Beacon Road — recommend checking on site. Cafe H2O (GeoCentre) en route provides facilities. (MEDIUM)
- **Cafe or pub:** St Ann's Well cafe midway on the route — confirmed refreshments available, dog-friendly outdoor area. Cafe H2O (GeoCentre) at the base of the hills. Multiple dog-friendly pubs in Great Malvern town. (MEDIUM)
- **Public transport:** Great Malvern railway station — on the Hereford to Worcester line. Regular trains from Birmingham. The walk can be joined from Great Malvern town centre (approx 30-40 minute walk up to the ridge). (HIGH)

### 4. Hazards

- **livestock** — Year-round via rotating enclosures, plus free-roaming stock in some areas. Check Stockwatch before visiting. (HIGH)
- **ground-nesting-birds** — 1 March to 31 July. Skylarks and meadow pipits nest on open hillside. Signs erected in known nesting areas. Keep dogs to paths in marked zones. (HIGH)
- **ticks** — March to October. Woodland sections and long grass on lower slopes. Roe deer population elevates tick risk. (HIGH)
- **adders** — April to September. Bracken and scrub areas, particularly on lower slopes and woodland edges. (HIGH — confirmed from multiple sources)
- **paw-burn** — June to August. Exposed rocky ridge in direct sun. Light-coloured quartzite rock reflects heat. (MEDIUM)
- **deer** — Year-round. Roe deer in woodland sections — can trigger chasing behaviour. (MEDIUM)

### 5. Ailsa Description Notes

- **Key features distinctive for dogs:** The Malvern Hills are a narrow, dramatic ridge rising abruptly from the flat Worcestershire plain — the profile is distinctive and unlike the broad rolling Cotswolds. Worcestershire Beacon at 425m is the highest point, with views extending to the Black Mountains of Wales and, on clear days, to the Cotswold escarpment. The ridge walking is exposed and exhilarating. Ancient woodland on the lower slopes adds scent variety.
- **Dog-friendly highlights:** Open ridge walking with sweeping views. Good livestock management system (Stockwatch) allows pre-visit planning. Multiple access points from Great Malvern allow route customisation by fitness level. St Ann's Well cafe makes a natural mid-walk stop.
- **Honest practical note:** Parking at Beacon Road can fill very quickly on sunny weekends. Arrive before 10am at weekends in summer. The ridge is genuinely exposed and windy — cold even on summer days. Check the Malvern Hills Trust Stockwatch before visiting to know where livestock enclosures are sited.

### 6. FAQ Suggestions

1. Are dogs allowed on the Malvern Hills?

## RESEARCHER NOTES

### Significant findings for the Editor

**1. Dover's Hill (Walk 1) — on-lead throughout, not partial**
The National Trust confirms that dogs must be on a short lead at all times on Dover's Hill due to year-round livestock grazing. This is confirmed on the National Trust's dedicated dog-walking page for the site. The brief should classify this as on-lead only, not partial. This affects the offLead field in the WALKS_DB entry and on the walk page. (HIGH confidence)

**2. Broadway Tower — NOT National Trust**
The brief tasks the researcher to "verify dog access on National Trust land." Broadway Tower is not a National Trust property. It is privately owned by Broadway Tower Ltd. The dog access rules (leads required due to resident deer herd) are the estate's own policy, not NT policy. This distinction matters for the walk page copy — do not describe it as a National Trust site. Surrounding fields and Cotswold Way sections are public rights of way. (HIGH confidence)

**3. Cleeve Hill — effectively on lead most of the year due to year-round cattle**
The CRoW Act lead requirement (1 March to 31 July) combined with year-round cattle grazing means that in practice, dogs are on lead for the vast majority of visits. The offLead field should be "Partial" but the copy must be honest that cattle year-round means leads are required for most of the open common area on most visits. The August to February window without the statutory lead requirement still requires leads whenever stock is present. This is a genuinely complex site for the off-lead field. Recommended: flag as Partial with a clear explanation.

**4. Symonds Yat — peregrine restrictions unclear**
No formal dog exclusion zones around the peregrine nesting site at Coldwell Rocks were found in research. The RSPB has operated watches here since 1982 but no official dog access restriction (equivalent to a seasonal no-dog zone) was located. The viewing is done from the rock above the nesting cliff, not from the cliff face itself. However, this should be flagged for the Editor to verify directly with Forestry England or the local RSPB group before publication, as informal restrictions may exist that are not published online. Recommend flagging as (LOW confidence) on peregrine restrictions specifically and noting it as a query for the Editor.

**5. Symonds Yat — technically Gloucestershire/Herefordshire border**
The Symonds Yat Rock car park (GL16 7NZ) is technically in Gloucestershire (Coleford area, Forest of Dean district). Symonds Yat village is in Herefordshire. The Wye here forms the county boundary. For the walk page, "Herefordshire" is the conventional description used by most sources and by Visit Herefordshire. Using "Herefordshire" on the page is consistent with how the location is commonly presented. (HIGH)

**6. Malvern Hills parking price increase April 2026**
The Malvern Hills Trust increased parking charges from 1 April 2026. The new rates are £6.50 day ticket and £3.75 evening. The previous rate (£55 annual pass mentioned in earlier research) has been updated to £59 annual pass (£30 second vehicle). The Developer should use the April 2026 prices. (HIGH confidence — from the Malvern Hills Trust parking page)

**7. Bourton-on-the-Water crowd management**
Multiple sources confirm this is one of the most visited villages in the Cotswolds. Summer weekends see extreme congestion. The walk page copy should include a clear practical note about arriving early. The off-lead status on the village section is effectively on-lead due to crowds even where no formal restriction exists — the copy should reflect this honestly without overstating any restriction.

**8. CRoW Act 1 March to 31 July rule applies to Walks 4 (Cleeve) and portions of Walk 6 (Malverns)**
Both Cleeve Common and parts of the Malvern Hills are CRoW Act open access land. The standard lead requirement (dogs on lead max 2m, 1 March to 31 July on open access land) applies to both. The Developer should ensure the hazard notices for these two walks reflect this rule. This is site-specific confirmed information, not a general assumption.
