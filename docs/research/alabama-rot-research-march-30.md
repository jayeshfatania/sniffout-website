## Section 1 - What Is Alabama Rot

### Full Name and Origin

- Full name: Cutaneous and Renal Glomerular Vasculopathy (CRGV)
- Alabama rot: named after an outbreak in racing greyhounds in the US state of Alabama in the 1980s. The US cases affected greyhounds specifically. The UK disease is now understood to be the same condition but affects dogs of all breeds.
- First UK cases: suspected November 2012. The earliest confirmed UK cluster was in and around the New Forest, Hampshire, which is why the disease is sometimes called "New Forest disease" in UK media.

### UK Case Tracking

Anderson Moores Veterinary Specialists (Winchester) lead the UK clinical research into CRGV. The Alabama Rot Research Fund (ARRF) at www.arrf.co.uk maintains the official confirmed case list and a live map. The companion site alabama-rot.co.uk also hosts an interactive case map. These are the authoritative UK sources.

**Important note for Tom:** The ARRF confirmed case numbers are based on histologically confirmed post-mortem diagnoses. Clinical cases (diagnosed in living dogs) may be higher than the confirmed case total, but unconfirmed clinical cases are not included in the official count.

### UK Case Numbers - Verified

Cumulative confirmed cases from November 2012 to early 2025: approximately 334 (multiple sources; exact figure fluctuates slightly between sources as cases are added).

Annual confirmed case figures from ARRF data:

| Year | Confirmed Cases |
|------|-----------------|
| 2018 | 52 |
| 2019 | 29 |
| 2020 | 47 |
| 2021 | 28 |
| 2022 | 8 |
| 2023 | 4 (ARRF confirmed-only figure; some sources cite higher clinical estimates) |
| 2024 | 19 (full year estimate across multiple sources) |
| 2025 | 6 (partial year, as of late January 2025) |

**Note on 2023 discrepancy:** ARRF records 4 confirmed cases in 2023 based on histological confirmation. Other sources cite higher numbers for 2023 that may include clinically suspected but not histologically confirmed cases. The ARRF figure is the verified minimum.

**Framing note for Tom:** The UK averages roughly 10 to 50 confirmed cases per year depending on the year. By comparison, millions of dogs are walked in UK countryside annually. The RSPCA describes the overall risk as "very low." This context is essential to include in the article.

## Section 2 - Cause

### Current Status - Confirmed

The cause of CRGV in UK dogs remains unknown as of 2025. This has been the position since 2012 and has not changed despite sustained research. ARRF, RVC, and Anderson Moores all state this explicitly.

Verbatim from the RVC FAQ (last updated 2024): "It is not yet known what causes the disease or why some dogs suffer more than others."

Verbatim from ARRF: "It is not yet known what causes the disease or why some dogs suffer more than others."

### What Has Been Ruled Out - Confirmed

- Contagion between dogs: no indication it spreads from dog to dog. ARRF: "Research to date shows no indication that it can be passed from dog to dog."
- Human transmission: no cases in humans, including owners of affected dogs.
- Other species: not observed in cats or other animals.
- Water supply contamination: investigated and excluded.
- E. coli (as a direct UK cause): histological examination of UK cases has not found E. coli. The original US greyhound cases had a proposed toxin/E. coli mechanism but this has not been replicated in UK post-mortems.
- Seasonal Canine Illness (SCI): ARRF explicitly distinguishes CRGV from SCI. These are separate conditions with different presentations and should not be conflated.

### What Has Not Been Ruled Out - Investigational Only

An environmental trigger is considered possible by Anderson Moores and ARRF but has not been proven by testing to date. The specific nature of any environmental trigger remains entirely unknown.

**Hypotheses that have been suggested but not confirmed (flag for Tom - treat with caution):**

- Aeromonas hydrophila (a bacterium found in waterlogged soil and still water): suggested as a possible trigger in some veterinary commentary but not confirmed in peer-reviewed literature.
- A toxin produced by environmental micro-organisms: suggested but not evidenced.
- The RVC's 2018 work established a clinical parallel with thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA) in humans, which is treated with plasma exchange (see Section 4 on treatment). This tells us about the mechanism of disease (tiny blood clots forming in blood vessels) but not about the original cause.

**Safe framing for Tom:** "Despite more than a decade of research, the cause of Alabama rot remains unknown. Environmental factors - possibly something in wet or muddy woodland soil - are considered possible but have not been identified or confirmed."

## Section 3 - UK Geography and Seasonality

### Seasonal Pattern - Confirmed

Multiple authoritative sources confirm a seasonal distribution. Most cases occur between November and May. July and August see very few cases. The RVC FAQ states: "A seasonal distribution is reported with most cases occurring between November and May each year."

The association with wet weather and wetter ground conditions is observed but the causal mechanism is unknown.

### Geographic Distribution - Verified

Cases have been confirmed across 47 of the UK's counties. No single county or region can be declared safe. That said, geographic patterns have been identified.

- **South West England** has historically had the highest proportion of cases relative to the dog population. Devon in particular had the highest single-county case count between 2018 and 2020 (10 cases). Cornwall, Wiltshire, Somerset, and Gloucestershire also feature.
- **South East England** has significant case numbers: Surrey, Hampshire (especially the New Forest), Berkshire, Oxfordshire, Sussex.
- **North West England:** Greater Manchester, Lancashire, Cheshire.
- **Wales:** Cases confirmed across several counties including Rhondda Cynon Taf, Carmarthenshire, and Powys.
- **Midlands:** Staffordshire, Herefordshire, Warwickshire, Worcestershire, Nottinghamshire.

**No county is definitively high-risk or low-risk.** ARRF does not recommend avoiding specific areas. The interactive map at arrf.co.uk/confirmed-cases.html and the live map at alabama-rot.co.uk allow owners to check case history near them.

### Woodland/Muddy Walk Association - Status

This is frequently stated across consumer-facing sources. The precise framing matters.

**What is confirmed:** A statistical association between CRGV cases and dogs walking in muddy woodland areas has been noted by Anderson Moores and others. Most affected dogs had walked in wet or muddy environments.

**What is not confirmed:** A causal relationship. The disease appears to occur more commonly in dogs that walk in muddy woodland, but it has not been proven that the woodland or mud itself is the trigger. It is equally possible that dogs most at risk simply tend to be dogs that are frequently walked in these environments.

**Safe framing for Tom:** "Most recorded cases have occurred in dogs walked in muddy woodland during the wetter months. The connection is documented but the causal mechanism is not understood."

## Section 4 - Signs and Progression

### Skin Lesions - Confirmed

Primary presentation in all verified cases is unexplained skin lesions. Key characteristics:

- Location: most commonly on the lower legs, below the knee or elbow. Also on the paws, pads, and between the toes. Less commonly on the face, muzzle, tongue, flank, or belly.
- Appearance: red patches, swellings, or ulcers that are not explained by a known injury. They may appear as a sore, a circular hairless patch, or a wound that is not healing. Dogs often lick the affected area.
- Key distinguishing factor: the lesions are non-traumatic (no known injury to explain them).

**Safe framing for Tom:** Any unexplained skin lesion on the lower legs during winter or spring, following a muddy woodland walk, should be assessed by a vet promptly. Most unexplained lesions are not CRGV. But CRGV lesions look like ordinary skin sores and cannot be distinguished by appearance alone without veterinary assessment.

### Timeline from Lesion to Kidney Involvement - Confirmed

RVC FAQ: "Kidney involvement typically occurs within 1-9 days of first noticing the skin ulcer(s)."

Vets Now: kidney failure can emerge within approximately 10 days of the skin lesion appearing.

Signs of kidney involvement: lethargy, decreased appetite, nausea, vomiting, increased thirst, decreased urination.

**Critical point for Tom:** The window between skin lesion and kidney involvement is short. Speed of veterinary assessment after spotting a lesion is the most important factor.

### Prognosis - Verified Figures

Two figures are in circulation and are both valid but refer to different populations:

1. **Vets Now / general guidance:** approximately 80-85% of dogs that develop kidney failure do not survive.
2. **RVC Queen Mother Hospital (March 2024):** approximately 30% of dogs clinically diagnosed with CRGV have survived.

These figures are not contradictory. The RVC figure covers all clinically diagnosed cases, including dogs that do not progress to kidney failure (who generally recover fully). The 80-85% figure refers specifically to dogs that develop acute kidney failure.

**Key framing:** Dogs that develop only skin lesions, without kidney involvement, generally recover. The fatality rate applies to dogs that develop kidney failure - which, based on case data, is a significant proportion of confirmed cases.

**Safe framing for Tom:** "Dogs that develop only the skin lesions, without kidney involvement, are expected to recover. For dogs that progress to kidney failure, the prognosis is serious and the majority do not survive. Approximately 30% of dogs clinically diagnosed with Alabama rot at the Royal Veterinary College have survived - a figure that includes specialist treatment including plasma exchange."

### Treatment - Confirmed

No disease-specific treatment exists because the cause is unknown.

Treatment is supportive: wound management of skin lesions, aggressive fluid therapy for kidney support, and monitoring of kidney function.

At the RVC Queen Mother Hospital, Therapeutic Plasma Exchange (TPE/plasmapheresis) is available for dogs with CRGV. A 2018 study published in Frontiers in Veterinary Science (Cortellini et al.) described two of six dogs surviving with TPE. This was the first documented survival of dogs so severely affected. TPE is a specialist treatment not available at most practices.

## Section 5 - Breed and Risk Factors

### Breed - Peer-Reviewed Data Available

A peer-reviewed study published in Veterinary Record in 2018 (Walker et al., "Signalment risk factors for cutaneous and renal glomerular vasculopathy (Alabama rot) in dogs in the UK") identified breed associations using case-control methodology.

Key findings:
- Hounds and gundog breeds had the highest risk compared with terriers.
- English springer spaniels, whippets, flat-coated retrievers, and Hungarian Vizslas had the highest confirmed case rates.
- Staffordshire bull terriers, Jack Russells, and German shepherds had the lowest rates.
- Toy breeds were absent from confirmed cases.

**Important caveat (from the study itself):** The breed associations may reflect where these breeds tend to be walked (in woodland/rural areas) rather than a true genetic susceptibility. It is not possible to conclude from the data that certain breeds are inherently more susceptible.

**Safe framing for Tom:** "Cases have been recorded across many breeds and no breed is definitively protected or definitively at high risk. The disease has occurred in both large and small dogs, across a wide age range and both sexes."

### Age and Sex

No specific age or sex predisposition has been confirmed in consumer-facing guidance. The Walker et al. 2018 study did not identify age as a significant independent risk factor.

## Section 6 - What Owners Should Do

### If a Skin Lesion Is Found - Confirmed Guidance

All authoritative sources agree on this:

Go to the vet promptly if an unexplained skin lesion appears, especially following a muddy woodland walk during November to May.

Verbatim from ARRF: "Contact your vet immediately."
Verbatim from Vets Now: "Time is of the essence and by far the best chance of success is early, aggressive veterinary treatment."
Verbatim from RSPCA: "We urge that you contact your vet immediately for further advice."

The vet will assess the lesion, may treat the wound, and will monitor kidney function with blood and urine tests. Diagnosis of CRGV during life is clinical (based on presentation). Definitive histological confirmation requires kidney tissue analysis post-mortem.

**Safe framing for Tom:** Any unexplained skin lesion on the lower legs, following a muddy woodland walk between November and May, warrants same-day vet assessment. Do not wait to see if it heals.

### Paw Washing - Status

This is widely recommended but the evidence base must be clearly stated.

**What is confirmed:** Paw washing after muddy walks is recommended as a reasonable precaution by multiple sources.

**What is not confirmed:** There is no scientific evidence that washing paws after muddy walks reduces the risk of CRGV. The RVC FAQ states this explicitly: bathing feet and legs post-walk is "often recommended" but "there is no evidence to prove this is of benefit."

ARRF also notes that paw washing may aid early detection of lesions by making inspection easier.

**Safe framing for Tom:** "Washing mud from [dog name]'s legs and paws after a muddy woodland walk in winter or spring is widely recommended. There is no proof that this prevents Alabama rot - the cause remains unknown. But it is a sensible habit regardless, and it makes a post-walk inspection easier."

### Is There Anything Proven to Reduce Risk

No. Because the cause is unknown, there is no proven prevention. ARRF, RVC, and Anderson Moores all state this.

What is advised as reasonable precaution (without proven efficacy):
- Wash mud from lower legs and paws after muddy walks in the at-risk season.
- Check [dog name] for unexplained skin lesions regularly, especially in the winter/spring season.
- Seek prompt vet assessment for any unexplained skin sore.

## Section 7 - Framing Note for Tom

This is an important section. Alabama rot generates anxiety among dog owners that is disproportionate to the actual case numbers. The article must be honest about both facts: the disease is serious when it does occur, and it is rare.

**The numbers:**
- Approximately 334 confirmed cases over 13 years in the UK.
- Roughly 10 to 50 confirmed cases per year in recent years.
- Millions of dogs are walked in UK countryside annually.
- The RSPCA describes the overall risk as "very low."
- Even in the highest-risk region (South West England), the rate is approximately 3.85 cases per 100,000 dogs.

**What to say and not say:**
- Do not amplify the risk. Alabama rot is real and serious when it occurs. It is also very rare.
- Do not use "deadly" as a standalone description. The disease has a high fatality rate when it reaches kidney failure - but it does not always reach that stage.
- Do not tell owners to avoid specific areas. No areas are officially identified as high-risk and ARRF does not recommend avoiding specific locations.
- Do say: if an unexplained sore appears following a muddy woodland walk in winter or spring, act fast and see a vet.
- The interactive map at arrf.co.uk allows owners to look up cases near them - this is a useful reference to direct readers to.
