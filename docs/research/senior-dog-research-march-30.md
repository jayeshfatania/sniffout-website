## Section 1 - At What Age Is a Dog Considered Senior?

### By Size and Breed - Verified

There is no single age threshold. Senior status varies significantly by size and breed, and this is confirmed across PDSA, RSPCA, and Kennel Club guidance.

The most widely used framework by UK sources:

| Size | Typical Senior Age |
|------|-------------------|
| Small breeds (under approximately 10kg) | Around 10-12 years |
| Medium breeds (approximately 10-25kg) | Around 8-10 years |
| Large breeds (approximately 25-40kg) | Around 7-8 years |
| Giant breeds (over approximately 40kg) | Around 5-7 years |

- RSPCA uses age seven as a general reference point: "at around seven your dog might benefit from gradually moving onto a diet designed for senior dogs."
- The Kennel Club does not give a specific age threshold but confirms the size-based pattern.
- PDSA's senior exercise guidance does not define a specific age; it uses functional and behavioural indicators rather than a calendar threshold.
- Purina UK confirms: "Large and giant breeds are usually considered to be 'seniors' by the time they're five, while medium-sized dogs won't reach old age until they're seven, and small and toy breeds reach seniority around the age of 10."

**Safe framing for Tom:** Do not give a single age. Use size-based guidance: giant and large breeds age faster; small breeds may not show senior characteristics until 10 or older. Seven is a reasonable general reference point but comes with caveats.

## Section 2 - How Exercise Needs Change with Age

### Core Principle - Confirmed (PDSA, RSPCA, Kennel Club)

The consistent message across all UK sources is: do not stop, but adapt. Shorter, more frequent walks are better than less frequent longer ones.

**PDSA verbatim:** "little and often is best as joints get stiffer when they've not been used for a bit."

**RSPCA:** Short walks little and often; maintain daily outdoor access for sniffing and mental stimulation; avoid stopping exercise altogether.

**Kennel Club verbatim:** "Maintain a constant level of daily exercise rather than random strenuous activity, as random strenuous activity often guarantees soreness the following day."

### What to Change

- Reduce duration of individual walks rather than frequency.
- Replace one long walk with two or three shorter ones where possible.
- Allow more time for rest stops and sniffing rather than maintaining pace throughout.
- Reduce high-impact activity: no ball chasing, jumping, or running on uneven ground.
- Keep routes familiar and predictable; senior dogs benefit from known terrain.

### What Not to Change

- Daily outdoor access: senior dogs still need to get out every day.
- Mental stimulation through walks: sniffing and environmental variety remain important.
- Consistency: irregular bursts of exercise after inactivity cause more soreness than gentle regular walks.

**Note for Tom:** No authoritative UK source gives a specific minute-count for senior dog walks. Guidance is qualitative (shorter, gentler, more frequent) rather than quantitative. Avoid stating specific durations unless caveated as approximate. One non-UK source (PetMD) suggests 30-60 minutes daily for seniors; this has not been verified from a UK source and should not be stated without caveat.

## Section 3 - Signs a Walk Is Too Long or Strenuous

### Confirmed Signs (PDSA, Kennel Club, Blue Cross, general vet sources)

During the walk:
- Dog lies down mid-walk and refuses to continue.
- Lagging behind, losing pace significantly.
- Excessive panting that is not weather-related.
- Limping or altered gait during or after the walk.
- Legs visibly trembling or shaking.

After the walk:
- Stiffness or difficulty getting up after resting (particularly the morning after a longer walk).
- Increased reluctance to move or get up from their bed.
- Lameness that was not present before the walk.
- More sleep than usual following the walk.

**PDSA verbatim:** "If they stop chasing a toy or lie down on a walk, they need to rest." and "Don't rush them or try to make them exercise for longer than they are able."

**Kennel Club:** "Random strenuous activity often guarantees soreness the following day." This is the key diagnostic: if a dog is stiffer the morning after than usual, the previous day's exercise was too much.

### The Morning-After Test

The morning after a walk is often more diagnostic than during the walk itself. A dog that moves normally after completing a walk but is stiff and slow to rise the following morning has likely been over-exercised. This pattern is confirmed by the Kennel Club's guidance and is consistent with arthritis progression.

**Safe framing for Tom:** "If [dog name] is stiffer the morning after a walk than usual, that walk was probably too much. The walk that causes no immediate signs can still show up as discomfort the next day - particularly as dogs tend not to show pain clearly while moving."

## Section 4 - Joint Conditions Common in Older Dogs

### Arthritis (Osteoarthritis) - PDSA and Blue Cross Confirmed

Arthritis is extremely common in older dogs and is one of the leading causes of reduced mobility. The RSPCA identifies arthritis as among the leading causes of death in older dogs, citing a figure of 12.4%.

**Signs of arthritis in dogs (PDSA confirmed):**
- Stiffness, especially after rest or after walks.
- Limping or lameness (may be subtle at first).
- Slower pace on walks; reluctance to keep up.
- Difficulty rising from lying down.
- Reluctance to climb stairs or jump into cars.
- Muscle wastage, particularly in rear limbs.
- Behavioural changes: grumpiness, reduced play interest, increased sleeping.
- Visibly swollen or enlarged joints.

**Note for Tom:** Arthritis symptoms often develop slowly and owners may attribute early signs to "slowing down" rather than pain. This is worth flagging - stiffness in an older dog is worth a vet assessment rather than acceptance as normal ageing.

**Exercise for arthritic dogs (PDSA confirmed):**
- Regular short walks each day, unless the vet advises otherwise.
- Avoid sudden increases in activity.
- Avoid jumping, skidding, ball-chasing, and running on uneven ground.
- Controlled, consistent movement is better than inactivity.
- Slippery surfaces indoors (tiles, hardwood) are hazardous; rugs and anti-slip mats recommended.

### Hip Dysplasia - PDSA Confirmed

Hip dysplasia is a developmental condition where the hip joints do not fit together correctly, causing instability, pain, and arthritis over time. It may be present from puppyhood but often first shows symptoms around 5-6 months of age; it worsens as dogs age.

**Signs (PDSA confirmed):**
- "Bunny hopping" gait (both hind legs moving together when running).
- Limping or lameness, particularly in the hind legs.
- Difficulty rising, lying down, or climbing stairs.
- Reduced interest in walks.
- Weak or visibly reduced muscle mass around the hips.

**Exercise guidance for hip dysplasia (PDSA confirmed):**
- Regular, short lead walks are recommended unless the vet advises otherwise.
- Avoid jumping, skidding, chasing, racing, and long-duration exercise.
- Hydrotherapy (water exercise) is a low-impact alternative recommended by PDSA and Blue Cross.

**Breeds with known hip dysplasia predisposition:** Labrador Retrievers, German Shepherds, Golden Retrievers, Rottweilers, and many large-breed dogs. The Kennel Club/BVA run a hip scoring scheme for affected breeds. This is breed-specific information; Tom does not need to list breeds unless writing for a breed guide.

### Cold, Damp Weather and Joint Conditions

The Kennel Club explicitly notes that joint stiffness is worse "in colder, damper weather." This is a consistent pattern confirmed by both the Kennel Club and PDSA senior guidance. Walks in cold, wet conditions may be more uncomfortable for arthritic dogs than the same distance in dry conditions, even at the same temperature.

## Section 5 - Benefits of Continuing to Exercise Senior Dogs

### Physical Benefits - Confirmed (PDSA, RSPCA, Kennel Club)

- Prevents obesity, which puts additional strain on joints and the cardiovascular system.
- Maintains muscle mass, which supports joint function and reduces injury risk.
- Maintains joint mobility; prolonged inactivity stiffens joints further.
- Contributes to healthy weight management.
- Keeps nails worn down (noted by RSPCA as particularly relevant since senior dogs may exercise less).

### Cognitive Benefits - Peer-Reviewed Research Available

**Source:** "Associations between physical activity and cognitive dysfunction in older companion dogs: results from the Dog Aging Project." Published in GeroScience (PMC9886770, 2023). Study of 11,574 companion dogs aged 6-18.

**Key findings:**
- Higher levels of physical activity were associated with significantly lower levels of cognitive dysfunction.
- Among dogs of the same age, health status, breed type, and sterilization status, dogs who were not active had 6.47 times higher odds of reaching a clinical threshold for canine cognitive dysfunction (CCD) compared to very active dogs.
- Benefits were observed even in dogs who had not yet reached clinical dementia thresholds, suggesting physical activity may have a protective effect at earlier stages of cognitive ageing.

**Critical caveat (from the study authors):** This is an observational study and cannot prove causation. Dogs experiencing cognitive decline may naturally become less active - reverse causation cannot be excluded. Longitudinal intervention studies are needed to confirm whether increasing exercise directly prevents cognitive problems.

**Safe framing for Tom:** "Research from the Dog Aging Project, a study of over 11,000 dogs, found an association between higher physical activity levels and lower rates of cognitive dysfunction in older dogs. The researchers note that causality has not been established - but the association is strong enough to be worth noting when considering whether to keep a senior dog active."

Do not state this as proven causation. The association is strong and peer-reviewed but the study design prevents causal conclusions.

## Section 6 - Terrain Considerations for Older Dogs

### What to Avoid - Confirmed

- **Slippery surfaces:** PDSA and RSPCA both flag slippery floors (tiles, hardwood) as a fall hazard for dogs with reduced muscle control and arthritis. Outdoors, icy paths and wet smooth stone pose the same risk.
- **Uneven ground:** PDSA confirms that running on uneven ground should be avoided for dogs with hip dysplasia; general sources extend this to arthritic dogs. Uneven ground requires more muscular effort to stabilise and increases fall risk.
- **Steep gradients:** Not explicitly addressed by UK sources consulted but consistent with reduced impact exercise principles and the general guidance to avoid strain on joints.
- **High-impact surfaces:** Long distances on hard tarmac or concrete are harder on joints than softer surfaces.

### What Works Well - Confirmed

- **Flat, familiar paths:** PDSA recommends sticking to familiar, shorter, flatter routes.
- **Soft ground:** Grass and compacted earth paths are gentler on joints than tarmac.
- **Consistent terrain:** Predictable ground reduces the cognitive and physical demands on a senior dog compared to constantly varying surfaces.
- **Water-based exercise:** Hydrotherapy and swimming are recommended by PDSA and Blue Cross for dogs with arthritis and hip dysplasia as low-impact alternatives that maintain muscle strength without joint loading.

**Note for Tom:** UK-specific terrain guidance is limited in the sources consulted. The above is derived from condition-specific guidance (arthritis, hip dysplasia) applied to general terrain. Tom may apply this as general practical advice rather than attributed guidance.

## Section 7 - Weather Considerations for Older Dogs

### Cold Weather - Vets Now Confirmed

Vets Now identifies older dogs as a vulnerable group in cold weather, alongside small breeds and thin-coated dogs.

- **7 degrees Celsius:** Vets Now identifies this as the threshold at which small, thin-coated, and elderly dogs may need a coat.
- **1 to -4 degrees Celsius:** Vets Now describes this range as "potentially unsafe" for elderly dogs and recommends limiting walk duration and monitoring for hypothermia signs.
- Signs of cold stress: shivering, whining, pulling paws back, tucking the tail, reluctance to go outside.
- Icy and slippery paths increase fall risk for dogs with reduced muscle strength.
- Cold, damp conditions worsen arthritis and joint stiffness (Kennel Club confirmed).

**Practical guidance confirmed by PDSA:** Consider a dog coat for cold weather. Shorten walk duration in very cold conditions.

### Hot Weather - PDSA and RSPCA Confirmed

- Older dogs do not cope well with high temperatures, particularly breeds with underlying respiratory or cardiovascular conditions.
- PDSA: "You may need to walk during cooler times of day if it's hot."
- The same early morning / evening timing guidance that applies to brachycephalic breeds applies to senior dogs generally.
- Heat regulation becomes less efficient with age, increasing heatstroke risk.

**Framing note for Tom:** The heatstroke guide covers summer heat in detail. This article can cross-reference that guidance rather than duplicating it.

## Section 8 - When to Consult a Vet About Exercise

### Clear Triggers for a Vet Visit - Confirmed (PDSA, RSPCA, Kennel Club)

Contact your vet if the dog shows:
- Lameness or limping that is new or worsening.
- Stiffness on rising that is getting progressively worse.
- Reluctance to go on walks they previously enjoyed.
- Exercise intolerance (tiring quickly or refusing to continue earlier than before).
- Obvious pain response when touched or handled.
- Any rapid behavioural change: increased grumpiness, withdrawal, sleep disruption.

**PDSA verbatim:** "Always check with your vet that the level of exercise that they're getting is okay and isn't going to cause them any problems."

**Kennel Club:** "Dogs showing lameness or discomfort should be checked by your vet, as a range of treatments may be recommended."

### Proactive Vet Engagement - Confirmed (RSPCA)

The RSPCA recommends twice-yearly vet checks for senior dogs rather than the annual schedule appropriate for younger dogs. Some vet practices offer senior-specific clinics. This is proactive monitoring rather than crisis response.

**Safe framing for Tom:** A vet visit when a dog reaches senior age (or shows early signs of slowing down) is worth doing proactively - not to confirm something is wrong but to get a baseline and discuss appropriate exercise levels for that individual dog.

## Section 9 - Practical Adaptations for Walking Senior Dogs

### Shorter, More Frequent Walks - Confirmed (PDSA, RSPCA, Kennel Club)

The consistent recommendation is multiple shorter walks rather than fewer long ones. This maintains joint mobility, prevents the stiffness that comes from inactivity, and reduces the risk of overexertion on any single outing.

### Harness vs Collar - Guidance Available

A harness is preferable to a collar for dogs with neck, spine, or joint issues. Collars concentrate lead tension on the neck; harnesses distribute pressure across the chest and reduce strain on the cervical spine and forelimb joints. This is particularly relevant for dogs that pull or for owners who need to support the dog on uneven ground.

**Source note for Tom:** The specific harness guidance comes from veterinary sources outside the core UK charity set (PDSA, Blue Cross etc.) but is consistent with the general principle of reducing joint strain. It is safe to include as practical advice without over-attributing.

A harness with a handle on the back is particularly useful for dogs with significant mobility issues, allowing the owner to assist the dog over obstacles or up steps without lifting by the collar.

### Rest Stops - Confirmed (PDSA)

Allow the dog to stop and rest when they want to. PDSA specifically states "If they stop chasing a toy or lie down on a walk, they need to rest." Forcing a senior dog to continue past the point they have chosen to stop is counterproductive.

### Car Access

Getting in and out of cars can become difficult for older dogs. RSPCA and Kennel Club both mention ramps or being lifted as useful adaptations. This is not directly a walking adaptation but is relevant to accessing walks.

### Walk Timing

For dogs with arthritis or joint stiffness, the first walk of the day may be the most challenging as joints are stiff from overnight rest. A shorter initial outing to warm up joints, followed by a more substantial walk later in the day once the dog has loosened up, can work well.

**Source note for Tom:** The warm-up walk principle is not stated verbatim in the UK sources consulted but is consistent with the Kennel Club's "joints get stiffer when they've not been used for a bit" guidance and is clinically reasonable. Frame as practical advice, not attributed guidance.

### Scent Work and Mental Stimulation

RSPCA and Kennel Club both note that scent-based activities and puzzle toys are valuable for senior dogs who may not be able to manage the same physical exercise as before. A shorter walk with more sniffing time can provide mental stimulation equivalent to a longer walk at pace. Allowing more time for sniffing on walks is a low-cost adaptation that benefits both cognition and engagement.
