## WALK 1: Headley Heath and Nower Wood

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 2.6 miles | PLAUSIBLE - NT circuit is approx 2 miles; 2.6 miles consistent with a medium loop of the full 530-acre site |
| Difficulty | Easy | CORRECT - largely flat heathland with sandy paths, no significant climbs |
| Off-lead | Full | INCORRECT - see section 2 |
| Livestock | None | INCORRECT - belted Galloway cattle graze year-round in rotating zones A, B, C with on-site signage |
| Enclosed | No | CORRECT |

**Critical flag:** Nower Wood is a Surrey Wildlife Trust educational nature reserve - NOT open to the public, NO dogs permitted. The walk name implies access to Nower Wood. The route passes adjacent to Nower Wood on National Trust land only; it does not go through the reserve. The website page must make this clear.

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Headley Heath is owned and managed by the National Trust. It is a SSSI.

Off-lead status:
- Dogs must be on leads near the belted Galloway cattle grazing zones at all times. Zones rotate through areas A, B, and C; current zone is marked on-site and on the NT website.
- 1 March to 31 August: dogs must be on leads across the whole site to protect ground-nesting birds (nightjar, woodlark). Standard NT Surrey heathland policy.
- Outside the ground-nesting season and away from cattle zones: dogs may be off-lead under close control.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Adders: Confirmed present. Active March to October. Keep dogs on paths and away from long grass and bracken. If bitten, seek immediate vet treatment.
- Ticks: Standard Surrey heathland hazard. Check dogs thoroughly after every visit. Peak season April to October.
- Cattle: Belted Galloways are docile but dogs must be on leads near them. If caught between a dog and cattle, release the lead.
- Ground-nesting birds: Lead restrictions apply 1 March to 31 August.
- Heat: Open heathland with limited shade. Bring water for dogs in summer.
- Winter: Sandy paths drain reasonably well. Boots recommended October to April.
- No toilet facilities on site.
- Phone signal is poor - download JustPark before arriving.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Main car park: Headley Common Road, Headley Heath, KT18 6NN (OS grid ref: TQ 204 538)
- Two overflow car parks on west and east sides
- Charges: £2 up to 1 hour / £4 up to 2 hours / £6 up to 4 hours / £9 all day. National Trust members park free.
- Payment via JustPark app or on-site machine. Poor phone signal - set up JustPark app before arriving.

**Facilities:**
- Food van on-site most days, approx 9am-4:30pm (hot food, sandwiches, drinks)
- Dog waste bins provided
- No toilets

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Cock Inn, Church Lane, Headley, KT18 6LE - dog-friendly village pub close to the heath, water bowls and homemade dog treats
- Headley Village Stores and Tea Room - small cafe, tea, cakes, reportedly offers dog biscuits

### 5. FAQs

**Q: Can I let my dog off the lead at Headley Heath?**
A: It depends on the time of year. Between 1 March and 31 August, dogs must be on leads across the whole heath to protect ground-nesting birds including nightjar and woodlark. Outside those months, dogs can be off-lead on most of the heath but must go back on leads near the belted Galloway cattle grazing zones, which are clearly marked on-site.

**Q: Is the car park free?**
A: No. Parking costs £2 for up to 1 hour, rising to £9 for a full day. National Trust members park free. Payment is via JustPark app or on-site machine - note the signal is poor on the heath, so set up JustPark before you leave home.

**Q: Are there adders at Headley Heath?**
A: Yes. Adders are confirmed at the site and active from March through October. Keep your dog on paths and away from long grass and bracken, particularly in spring when adders bask after coming out of hibernation. If your dog is bitten, take it to a vet immediately.

**Q: Can I walk through Nower Wood with my dog?**
A: No. Despite the walk name, Nower Wood is a private Surrey Wildlife Trust educational nature reserve with no public access. Dogs are not permitted. The route stays on National Trust land adjacent to the reserve.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Best time to visit: Late April to early June (spring heather and woodland flowers before peak ground-nesting restrictions); October for autumn colour
- Summer: Bring water - open heathland gets hot. Early mornings are best in July and August. Note the full-site lead restriction is in force until 31 August.
- Worst conditions: Ground-nesting season peak (April to June) if you want unrestricted off-lead walking. Winter is fine with boots.
- Avoid: Weekends in school holidays if you want quiet paths

## WALK 2: Bookham Common and Fetcham Downs

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 3.1 miles | PLAUSIBLE - NT trails on Bookham Commons range from 1.5 to over 4 miles; 3.1 miles consistent with a combined loop |
| Difficulty | Easy | CORRECT - largely flat oak woodland and grassland, no significant elevation |
| Off-lead | Full | INCORRECT - seasonal restrictions and livestock-related lead requirements apply |
| Livestock | None | INCORRECT - cattle graze The Plains section |
| Enclosed | No | CORRECT |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Bookham Commons (Great Bookham Common and Little Bookham Common) is owned and managed by the National Trust. It is a SSSI (originally notified 1961, approximately 370 acres). Fetcham Downs is a separate chalk downland nature reserve managed by Surrey Wildlife Trust.

Off-lead status:
- Dogs are generally welcome off-lead across the commons.
- Dogs must be on leads where cattle are grazing on The Plains. This section is signed.
- 1 March to 31 July: NT requests leads in heathland sections and that walkers stay on paths to protect ground-nesting birds.

Dog waste bins are at Tunnel Car Park and Merritts Cottage.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Ticks: Bookham Common is in a tick-risk area. The mix of long grass, woodland edge and heathland provides ideal tick habitat. Check dogs and yourself thoroughly after every visit. Peak season April to October.
- Adders: Present in heathland sections of the Surrey commons area. Active March to October. Keep dogs from long vegetation and bracken.
- Livestock: Cattle on The Plains require leads in that section.
- Winter mud: Little Bookham Common has poor drainage with WWII bomb craters and gunpits holding water. Expect very boggy conditions November to March. Tufted hair-grass dominates wet grassland sections and can conceal waterlogged ground.
- Blue-green algae: Check any ponds in warm weather (July to September) before allowing dogs to swim.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Tunnel Car Park: Church Road, Great Bookham, KT23 3LT. Approximately 150m east of Bookham station. Free to NT members; non-members pay via JustPark.
- Hundred Pound Bridge: Free roadside parking (limited spaces)
- Mark Oak Gate: Free roadside parking (limited spaces)
- Note: Some motorists park in residential roads to avoid car park charges - inconsiderate and a source of local friction.

**By train:** Bookham station (London Waterloo to Effingham Junction line) is approximately 150m from Tunnel Car Park - an excellent rail-accessible option.

**Facilities:**
- Dog waste bins at Tunnel Car Park and Merritts Cottage
- No cafe or toilets on site

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Old Crown, High Street, Great Bookham - dog-friendly gastropub in the village centre with a dedicated dog menu and landscaped garden
- Crazy Bean Cafe, 23 High Street, Great Bookham - vegan cafe explicitly welcoming to dogs, water bowls provided
- The Royal Oak, Great Bookham - traditional pub, dog-friendly, real ales, beer garden

### 5. FAQs

**Q: Is Bookham Common good for dogs year-round?**
A: Generally yes. In winter, bring waterproof boots and expect significant mud, particularly on Little Bookham Common which has poor drainage. In summer, the ancient oak woodland provides excellent shade. Keep dogs on leads when the cattle are grazing on The Plains.

**Q: Is there parking at Bookham Commons?**
A: The main car park is Tunnel Car Park (KT23 3LT), roughly 150m from Bookham train station. NT members park free; others pay via JustPark. There is limited free roadside parking at Hundred Pound Bridge and Mark Oak Gate.

**Q: Are there ground-nesting bird restrictions at Bookham Commons?**
A: Yes. Between 1 March and 31 July, the National Trust asks walkers to keep dogs on leads in heathland sections and to stay on paths. The site is a SSSI partly designated for its breeding bird populations.

**Q: Can I get here by train?**
A: Yes - one of the most train-accessible dog walks in Surrey. Bookham station (London Waterloo to Effingham Junction line) is approximately 150m from Tunnel Car Park. The walk starts essentially from the station.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Best time to visit: May to June for butterfly populations (the site is noted for purple emperor and white admiral); October for autumn colour in the ancient oak woodland
- February to April: Snowdrops in February, bluebells in April
- Worst conditions: December to February - Little Bookham Common becomes waterlogged and very muddy
- Summer: Good shade from oak canopy; carry water for dogs on hot days

## WALK 3: St Martha's Hill and Chilworth

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 3.7 miles | PLAUSIBLE - consistent with a Chilworth circular including descent and return |
| Difficulty | Moderate | CORRECT - approx 120m elevation gain on steep sandy ascent, multiple ascents and descents |
| Off-lead | Full | NEEDS CAVEAT - SCC requires close control on paths; leads strongly advised in churchyard |
| Livestock | None | NOT CONFIRMED EITHER WAY - no specific livestock references found for this route |
| Enclosed | No | CORRECT |
| Terrain | Muddy | CORRECT - particularly lower paths near Chilworth and Tillingbourne valley |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Land managed by Surrey County Council as a countryside site. Within the Surrey Hills National Landscape. The North Downs Way National Trail, Greensand Way, and Pilgrims Way all pass through the area.

St Martha's Church at the summit (OS grid ref: TQ 028 483) is a working Anglican church. Dogs should be on leads in the churchyard.

SCC visitor information states: "Dogs allowed. Please keep dogs under close control and on paths." There is no formal off-lead designation - dogs must stay on paths under close control throughout.

**App data flag:** "Off-lead full" overstates freedom here. SCC specifically requires dogs to stay on paths and under close control.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Steep terrain: The main sandy ascent is steep and becomes slippery in wet conditions. The most commonly reported hazard. Boots with grip are essential October to April.
- No bins on site: Dog waste must be carried out. This is important to flag for dog walkers.
- Tillingbourne River: Accessible for dogs to paddle at Chilworth near the old gunpowder mills. Shallow and gentle. However, the Tillingbourne has a history of industrial pollution from former mills - check water clarity after heavy rain.
- Winter mud: Paths in the Chilworth valley section become significantly muddy October to April. The sandy summit paths drain better but become slippery when wet.
- Sun exposure: Summit is fully exposed with no shade. Dogs can overheat in summer. Bring water.
- Mountain bikers: Bridleways on the Chantries (Greensand ridge section) are shared with cyclists.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Halfpenny Lane Car Park: Halfpenny Lane, Guildford, GU4 8PZ. Free. Height restriction: max 1.9m.
- Guildford Lane Car Park: Guildford Lane, Chilworth, GU5 9BQ. Free. Same height restriction.
- Neither car park has toilets or facilities.
- By train: Chilworth station (Guildford to Redhill line) allows a rail-to-trail start.

**Facilities:**
- No refreshments, no toilets, no bins on site
- Picnic areas and benches at the summit

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Percy Arms, 75 Dorking Road, Chilworth, GU4 8NP - dogs welcome in the bar (not restaurant). Beer garden with the Tillingbourne running through the grounds. Listed on the SCC visitor information page as the nearest amenity. Views towards the North Downs.

### 5. FAQs

**Q: Is St Martha's Hill part of the North Downs Way?**
A: Yes. The North Downs Way National Trail passes through the site. The Greensand Way and Pilgrims Way also converge near the hill, making it one of Surrey's great walking crossroads. Views on clear days extend over eight counties.

**Q: How steep is the ascent to St Martha's Church?**
A: The main sandy path involves approximately 120m of elevation gain over around 700m - steep enough to require care in wet conditions. The sandy surface becomes slippery when wet. Boots with grip are essential. Most moderate-fitness walkers will feel this ascent.

**Q: Can my dog drink or paddle in the Tillingbourne?**
A: Dogs can paddle at Chilworth near the base of the hill. The river is shallow and normally accessible. Be aware that the Tillingbourne has a history of industrial pollution from the former gunpowder and other mills; check water clarity after heavy rain when silt may be disturbed.

**Q: Is there parking at the church?**
A: No parking at the summit. Use Halfpenny Lane Car Park (GU4 8PZ) or Guildford Lane Car Park (GU5 9BQ), both free but with a 1.9m height restriction. There are no facilities at either car park.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Best time to visit: Late September to November for heather and autumn colour; March to May for clear views before leaf cover and for heather on the sandy heath
- Summer: Heather blooms on the summit; bring water as the hilltop is fully exposed
- Worst conditions: January and February after sustained rain - valley paths near Chilworth become deeply muddy, sandy summit path becomes slippery. Frost can make the summit path icy.
- The summit church holds occasional outdoor services in summer - check ahead if you want to avoid a crowd

## WALK 4: The Hurtwood Ridge Walk

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 4.3 miles | PLAUSIBLE - Hurtwood and Holmbury Hill circuits range from 3.6 to over 8 miles; 4.3 miles consistent with an Ewhurst-based ridge route |
| Difficulty | Moderate | CORRECT - sustained ascents to Pitch Hill (257m) and Holmbury Hill (261m), not technical but definitely felt |
| Off-lead | Full | BROADLY CORRECT with caveats - no formal livestock, no formal lead requirements. However, mountain bikers require vigilance. |
| Livestock | None | CORRECT - no livestock grazing confirmed at the Hurtwood |
| Enclosed | No | CORRECT - 60+ miles of paths, multiple access points |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

The Hurtwood is a privately owned estate (multiple landowners), but public access has been dedicated since 1926 when Reginald Arthur Bray opened the land - one of the first private estates in England to do so. Friends of the Hurtwood (FotH) is a registered charity that manages public access and employs a ranger to maintain the site.

Dogs are welcome throughout. No formal on-lead requirement. However:
- Keep dogs on leads or very close when encountering horse riders (courtesy requirement)
- Mountain bikers can appear suddenly on narrow paths - keep dogs in sight at all times and consider leads on known bike trail sections (particularly near Car Park 3)
- No livestock restrictions confirmed

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Mountain bikers: The primary and most distinctive hazard at the Hurtwood. The site is one of Surrey's busiest mountain biking destinations. Cyclists travel at speed on narrow woodland tracks and can appear suddenly. This is not a standard shared-use caution - it is a defining characteristic of this location that significantly affects dog safety.
- Adders: Confirmed present on heathland sections of Pitch Hill and Holmbury Hill. Active March to October. Keep dogs from long grass, bracken, and rocky sunbathing spots.
- Ticks: High-risk area. The mix of woodland, heathland edge and bracken is ideal tick habitat. Use vet-recommended tick prevention. Check thoroughly after every visit.
- Tree roots and uneven terrain: Exposed roots on forest tracks. Watch footing on descents.
- Winter mud: Lower valley tracks become muddy October to April. Ridge paths drain better.
- Summit exposure: Pitch Hill and Holmbury Hill tops are open and exposed. Bring water for dogs on warm days; no shade at summits.
- Car park gates: On rare occasions the Hurtwood car park gates are locked. Alternative roadside parking exists on nearby lanes.
- Narrow approach roads: Lanes to the car parks are single-track.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Hurtwood Car Park 1: Near Peaslake village, Walking Bottom, approx RH5 6NU - main car park close to the village
- Hurtwood Car Park 3 (Pitch Hill/The Quarry): approx GU6 7NL - popular mountain bike hub and walking start point, free, can have potholes
- Holmbury Hill car park: Off Radnor Road near Holmbury St Mary, approx RH5 6NU area - smaller car park
- All Hurtwood car parks are free of charge

**Facilities:**
- Peaslake Store, Walking Bottom, Peaslake, GU5 9RR - close to Car Park 1, serves sandwiches, cake, tea and coffee, popular with walkers and cyclists
- No toilets at Hurtwood car parks

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Hurtwood Inn, Walking Bottom, Peaslake, GU5 9RR - award-winning dog-friendly pub at the foot of Holmbury Hill. Open fire, large outdoor seating area. Serves Italian and English food with local beef and lamb from Coverwood Farm. Won a Rover Dog-friendly Pub Award.

### 5. FAQs

**Q: Who manages the Hurtwood and is it genuinely open to the public?**
A: The Hurtwood is privately owned but has been open to the public since 1926, when Reginald Arthur Bray dedicated the land in what was one of the first such acts by a private landowner in England. Friends of the Hurtwood (FotH) is a charity that manages access, employs a ranger and maintains the 60+ miles of paths. Access is free.

**Q: Is it safe for dogs given the mountain bikers?**
A: Manageable, but the mountain biking here is more intensive than at most Surrey walks. The Hurtwood is one of Surrey's main mountain biking destinations and cyclists can travel at speed on narrow woodland paths. Keep dogs in sight at all times, and use a lead near Car Park 3 (Pitch Hill) where bike traffic is heaviest.

**Q: Are adders a real risk at The Hurtwood?**
A: Yes. Confirmed adder populations on the heathland sections of Pitch Hill and Holmbury Hill. They are active March through October. Keep dogs away from long grass, bracken, and sunny rocky areas. Seek immediate vet attention if your dog is bitten.

**Q: Is there water for dogs on the ridge?**
A: Limited. The summit areas are dry. Carry water for your dog on warm days. There are occasional boggy dips and seasonal streams in valley sections below the ridge, but these are not reliable.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Best time to visit: Late September to November for purple heather on the hilltops and vivid autumn colour in the mixed woodland. Views from Holmbury Hill over the Weald and towards the South Downs are exceptional on clear autumn days.
- Spring (April to May): Woodland flowers including bluebells; paths may still be muddy but drying.
- Summer: The ridge itself is good but exposed summits are hot. Midweek visits are quieter - mountain biking peaks at weekends in good weather. Early mornings are best in July and August.
- Winter (November to February): Lower woodland tracks become muddy. Ridge paths drain better. Generally still walkable with appropriate boots.

## WALK 5: Epsom Common and Stew Ponds

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 2.4 miles | VERIFIED - confirmed by Walks with Rena and AllTrails routes in the area |
| Difficulty | Easy | CORRECT - flat throughout, London clay substrate, no elevation |
| Off-lead | Full | NEEDS QUALIFICATION - SSSI requires close control throughout; no formal off-lead designation |
| Livestock | None | INCORRECT - cattle have grazed the common for conservation since 1997, seasonal temporary electric fencing |
| Enclosed | No | CORRECT - multiple access points |
| Terrain | Muddy | CORRECT and significant - London clay creates exceptional mud November to March |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Managed and owned jointly by Epsom and Ewell Borough Council (EEBC) and the Epsom Common Association. It is a SSSI (first notified 1955, re-notified 1984) and was designated a Local Nature Reserve in 2001 - the largest LNR in Surrey at 177.4 hectares (438 acres).

As the owner of a SSSI, EEBC has a statutory duty to further the conservation and enhancement of the site. Dog walkers are welcome but must keep dogs under close control throughout. There are no formally designated off-lead zones. Dogs should be kept away from the Great Pond (designated protected wildlife reserve).

Cattle have grazed the common since 1997 for conservation management. During summer grazing periods, temporary electric fencing is erected. Dogs must be on leads near cattle and must not enter fenced grazing areas.

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- Winter mud: The primary practical hazard. London clay holds water and the common becomes extremely muddy between October and April. A hard-surfaced "all-access" perimeter path remains usable year-round. Grass tracks and woodland paths become waterlogged. Boots are essential.
- Blue-green algae: As a still-water site, Stew Pond carries a seasonal algae risk in summer (July to September). Look for green or blue-green scum on the water surface. Blue-green algae can be rapidly fatal to dogs. If in doubt, keep your dog out of the water.
- Cattle and electric fencing: Temporary in summer. Keep dogs away from fenced areas and on leads near cattle.
- Ticks: Long grass, woodland and bracken create tick habitat. Check dogs thoroughly after every visit. Peak season April to October.
- Shared-use paths: The 3-mile hard-surfaced loop is shared with cyclists and horse riders.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Stew Ponds Car Park: Off Christ Church Road, Epsom, KT19 8JA. Free. The primary access point for this walk. (Note: postcode directs to the general area; look for the entrance off Christ Church Road.)
- Also accessible from Dorking Road, Wilmerhatch Lane and Woodcote Side.

**Facilities:**
- Dog waste bins at Stew Ponds Car Park and around Stew Pond
- Information boards at entrance
- Benches throughout
- No cafe or refreshments on site

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The Cricketers Inn, near Epsom Common - dog-friendly in bar and garden, water bowls and biscuits, described as the natural post-walk option
- The Jolly Coopers, Wheelers Lane, Epsom - 18th-century pub with microbrewery, dog treats and water provided

### 5. FAQs

**Q: Who manages Epsom Common and why are there restrictions?**
A: Epsom Common is jointly managed by Epsom and Ewell Borough Council and the Epsom Common Association. It has been a SSSI since 1955 and is the largest Local Nature Reserve in Surrey. The SSSI status means the council has a legal duty to protect the habitat, which is why dogs must be kept under close control throughout.

**Q: Can my dog swim in the Stew Ponds?**
A: Dogs can access the water's edge. However, as a conservation site, swimming in the ponds is not actively encouraged. In summer (July to September), check for blue-green algae before allowing your dog near the water - look for a green or blue-green scum on the surface. Blue-green algae can be rapidly fatal to dogs. If in doubt, keep your dog out.

**Q: How muddy does it get in winter?**
A: Very muddy. London clay holds water exceptionally well and the grass paths become waterlogged between October and April. The hard-surfaced perimeter path is usable year-round. Boots are essential for the inner paths in winter. Expect to wash your dog after a wet-season visit.

**Q: Is there livestock on Epsom Common?**
A: Yes. Cattle have grazed the common since 1997 as part of conservation management. During summer grazing periods, temporary electric fencing may restrict some areas. Dogs must be on leads near cattle and must not enter fenced enclosures.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Best time to visit: April to June before the ground dries out but after the worst mud; September to October for fungi and autumn colour; June for the common's notable invertebrate populations
- Summer (July to August): Hard path stays usable; monitor Stew Pond for algae; cattle grazing may be active
- Worst conditions: November to February - London clay waterlogging makes grass sections extremely muddy. Even in well-maintained walking boots, the inner paths can be challenging.
- Notable: The common borders Ashtead Common (a National Nature Reserve managed by the Corporation of London). Combined walks are possible.

## WALK 6: Shere Village and Abinger Roughs

### 1. Verified Data

| Field | App Says | Verified |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Distance | 3.5 miles | PLAUSIBLE - NT Abinger Roughs to Gomshall circuit is approximately 3 to 4 miles; a Shere-based loop of 3.5 miles is consistent |
| Difficulty | Moderate | CORRECT - some elevation change through woodland, not severe but not flat |
| Off-lead | Partial | CONFIRMED CORRECT - partial is accurate |
| Livestock | None | INCORRECT - livestock grazing confirmed inside Abinger Roughs, marked by latched gates |
| Enclosed | No | CORRECT |

### 2. Access Rules and Off-Lead Status

Abinger Roughs and Netley Park is owned and managed by the National Trust. 211 acres of woodland with views over the Tillingbourne Valley. NT car park at White Down Lane, Abinger Hammer, RH5 6QS (0.5 miles up White Down Lane from the A25).

Off-lead partial is confirmed accurate:
- Abinger Roughs woodland: dogs can be off-lead for most of the route
- Livestock grazing areas within Abinger Roughs: dogs on leads. Confirmed by NT with latched gates at entry and exit of livestock sections.
- Shere village: dogs on leads on village streets and narrow lanes
- A25 road crossing: dogs on leads. This is a fast, busy A-road - the primary safety hazard of this walk.
- Farm tracks: "regularly used by farm vehicles" - take care and listen for vehicles

### 3. Hazards and Seasonal Warnings

- A25 road crossing: The A25 (Guildford to Dorking) is a fast A-road with limited visibility. There is no pedestrian crossing at the crossing point used by this route. Dogs must be on leads. Wait for a clear gap in traffic. This is the most significant hazard on the route.
- Livestock gates: Multiple latched gates mark livestock sections inside Abinger Roughs. Dogs must be on leads inside these areas.
- Farm vehicles: Tracks crossing the route are used by farm vehicles. Listen for approaching vehicles.
- Ticks: Woodland and long grass sections carry tick risk. Check dogs and yourself after the walk. Peak season April to October.
- Visitor pressure in Shere: The village is a film location for "The Holiday" (2006) and other productions. This generates significant visitor pressure especially at weekends and during Christmas and New Year. Village can become congested and parking fills quickly.
- Winter mud: Woodland paths can become muddy November to March.

### 4. Practical Details

**Parking:**
- Abinger Roughs National Trust Car Park: White Down Lane, Abinger Hammer, RH5 6QS. Free (NT members and non-members). 0.5 miles up White Down Lane on the left, north of the A25. This is the recommended start point.
- Shere Village Car Park: Upper Street/London Lane, Shere, GU5 9HE. Charges £1.00 per hour, 9am to 6pm, 7 days a week (except Christmas Day), free for first 30 minutes. Charges introduced June 2024. Fills quickly at weekends.

**Facilities:**
- Dog waste bins at Abinger Roughs car park
- Benches on the route
- Tillingbourne river in Shere accessible for dogs

**Nearest dog-friendly pub/cafe:**
- The White Horse, Shere Lane, Shere, GU5 9HS - Grade II-listed former farmhouse (1475). Explicitly dog-friendly in the bar, with a dedicated dog station with water bowls and treats. Featured in "The Holiday" as the first date location. Part of the Chef and Brewer group. Rated 4.1/5 on TripAdvisor.
- The Dabbling Duck Tea Room, Shere - dog-friendly tearoom in the village, good for post-walk breakfast and cake

### 5. FAQs

**Q: Why is this walk "off-lead partial" rather than fully off-lead?**
A: Three sections require leads: (1) livestock grazing enclosures within Abinger Roughs, marked by latched gates; (2) Shere village roads and lanes; (3) the A25 road crossing. The majority of the Abinger Roughs woodland is off-lead.

**Q: Is the A25 crossing safe?**
A: It requires care. The A25 is a fast, busy A-road without a pedestrian crossing at the point this route crosses. Dogs must be on leads. Wait for a clear gap before crossing - do not rush this section. It is the route's most significant hazard.

**Q: Is Shere village too busy to enjoy?**
A: It depends when you visit. Shere is genuinely charming but draws large crowds, particularly at weekends and in December when fans of "The Holiday" make pilgrimages. Weekday visits are much quieter. The village has the Tillingbourne running through it, which dogs love paddling in. Arrive early for parking.

**Q: Can my dog paddle in the Tillingbourne in Shere?**
A: Yes. The River Tillingbourne runs through Shere village and is shallow and accessible in normal conditions. A popular spot for dogs. Avoid after heavy rain when the flow increases significantly.

### 6. Seasonal Notes

- Best time to visit: February for snowdrops at Abinger Roughs (the NT runs specific snowdrop walks here); April to May for bluebells and rhododendrons; autumn for woodland colour
- "The Holiday" season: December and early January bring a significant uplift of film-location visitors to Shere. Parking becomes very difficult. The White Horse is busy. Midweek visits strongly recommended in December.
- Spring (March to May): Abinger Roughs woodland at its best. Wildlife active, bluebells in April.
- Summer: Good shade in the woodland; Shere village busy at weekends but the Tillingbourne paddling is popular.
- Worst conditions: Winter weekends when film tourists and dog walkers compete for village parking.
