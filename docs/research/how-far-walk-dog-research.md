## Section 1: The Core Question - Distance or Time?

**Important framing point for Tom:** The search query is "how far should I walk my dog" but published guidance from the Kennel Club, PDSA, and Blue Cross consistently uses **time**, not distance. Dogs walk at different paces. A Basset Hound may cover 1.5 miles in 40 minutes while a Husky covers 4 miles in the same period.

**Recommendation:** Answer the "how far" question honestly in the opening - acknowledge that guidance is time-based, and that a useful estimate is 20-25 minutes per mile for an average-paced walk. Tom should not try to force distance figures throughout the article. Time is the relevant unit.

**The short answer Tom needs to deliver early:** Most adult dogs need between 30 minutes and 2 hours of exercise per day, split across at least two walks. The right amount depends primarily on breed, age, and health - not simply size, though size is a useful rough guide.

## Section 2: General Daily Walk Guidance

### UK Source Consensus

**PDSA:** "Some breeds need a minimum of one to two hours of exercise per day, but others, such as working breeds, need significantly more." At a minimum, at least one walk per day unless a vet advises otherwise. (Source: PDSA Exercise Guide)

**Blue Cross:** "One good walk every day is the minimum." Exercise needs depend on breed, age, and individual personality. (Source: Blue Cross puppy exercise page)

**Kennel Club:** Uses a four-category system for breed exercise needs, applied to every breed listed in their Breeds A to Z. Tom should frame the categories as follows (these reflect the KC system):
- Up to 30 minutes per day
- Up to 1 hour per day
- 1 to 2 hours per day
- More than 2 hours per day

The KC system is a useful framework for the article. Tom can reference it directly without needing to list every breed - a table by size/type is more useful.

### By Size (Rough Guide - Flag That Breed Matters More Than Size)

Data from a large UK survey (PMC5465859, 12,314 UK dog owners) found KC exercise recommendations correlated with size as follows:
- Small breeds: KC recommends up to 30 minutes (30% of small breeds) or up to 1 hour (70% of small breeds)
- Medium breeds: up to 1 hour (63%) or more than 2 hours (37%)
- Large breeds: up to 1 hour (13%) or more than 2 hours (87%)

**Flag for Tom:** Size is a rough proxy, not a rule. A Jack Russell (small) typically needs 60-90 minutes. A Great Dane (giant) needs around 30-60 minutes as an adult. The article should say this clearly.

### By Age

#### Puppies

**Critical finding:** PDSA has explicitly stated that "there is no scientific evidence behind" the 5-minutes-per-month-of-age rule, "and although it might work for some, it's not appropriate for most puppies." (Source: PDSA puppy exercise page)

**Origin of the rule:** The 5-minute rule appears to have originated on a US working gundog forum and was adopted by UK owners as an easy-to-remember shorthand. It was never derived from clinical research. Multiple UK canine consultants and trainers now describe it as informal guidance, not a clinically validated protocol.

**What the evidence does support:**
- Growth plates must fuse before high-impact or prolonged forced exercise. Premature loading risks permanent joint damage, arthritis, and hip dysplasia.
- Small and medium breeds: growth plates typically close around 12 months
- Large breeds (Labradors, Greyhounds, Dobermans): up to 18 months
- Giant breeds (Great Danes, Irish Wolfhounds): up to 24 months

**Blue Cross guidance** still references the 5 min/month rule as a practical starting point for on-lead walks, with the caveat that large and giant breed puppies should consult a vet and the guide does not apply to free play. (Source: Blue Cross puppy exercise)

**Tom's recommended framing:** Present the 5-minute rule as a widely used practical guideline while acknowledging PDSA's caveat that it lacks scientific evidence. The core principle - start gently, build gradually, avoid high-impact until growth plates close - is sound even if the specific formula is uncertain.

**What puppies should be doing instead of long walks:** Short lead walks for toilet training and exploration, play at home, mental stimulation through puzzle feeders and training sessions. Free play in a garden is less risky than forced on-lead marching. PDSA explicitly recommends avoiding running and cycling with puppies until full maturity.

### Brachycephalic Breeds

Tom should write 2-3 sentences acknowledging flat-faced breeds as a distinct category requiring shorter, cooler, gentler walks, then link to the French Bulldog guide for full detail. The brachycephalic guide is currently a placeholder.

Key point to include: Brachycephalic breeds cool themselves by panting and their compressed airways make this less efficient. This means heat and exercise combine as a risk in a way that does not apply to other breeds. The threshold for caution is around 15-20C for these breeds compared to 20-22C for average dogs.

### Giant Breeds and Joint Considerations

**Adults:** Despite their size, giant breed adults typically need moderate rather than extreme exercise - 30 minutes to 1-2 hours depending on individual fitness. Their size makes it tempting to assume they need the most exercise of any dog, but this is incorrect.

**Puppies (critical):** Giant breed puppies are the group most at risk from over-exercise during development.
- Growth plates in Great Danes and Irish Wolfhounds do not fully close until approximately 18-24 months
- High-impact or forced exercise before closure can cause osteochondrosis dessicans (OCD) - a cartilage defect with genetic and mechanical components, common in Irish Wolfhounds
- Experienced giant breed breeders allow free play in open spaces but specifically advise against long coaxed lead walks, roadwork, or extended play sessions with puppies
- PDSA recommends avoiding high-intensity activities and discouraging jumping and skidding for breeds predisposed to joint problems (Source: PDSA puppy exercise)

**Tom should flag:** For giant breed puppies, the vet conversation about exercise timing is more important than for other dogs. This is not alarmism - it is practical guidance.

## Section 4: Signs of Too Much or Too Little Exercise

### Signs of Over-Exercise

Observed during or immediately after a walk:
- Limping or uneven gait
- Lying down mid-walk and refusing to continue
- Excessive panting unrelated to temperature
- Slowing significantly behind their usual pace
- Holding up a paw
- Licking or biting paws after returning home

Observed the following day (the "morning-after test" - already named in senior dog guide, can be referenced briefly):
- Stiffness rising from lying down
- Reluctance to go on the next walk
- Lameness or tenderness when touched on joints

From the senior dog guide (brief reference only): "If [dog name] is noticeably stiffer when getting up the morning after a walk than on a normal morning, that walk was probably too much."

**Sore paw pads:** reddened, cracked, or sensitive pads indicate excessive wear. Dogs on hard surfaces (tarmac, gravel) show this more than those walking on grass. (Source: Pooch and Mutt; PDSA limping guidance)

**When to call the vet:** Any new lameness that persists more than 24 hours, any joint swelling, any lameness that appears suddenly. PDSA notes that dogs are often good at hiding pain - if something seems off, it is worth a call rather than waiting.

### Signs of Too Little Exercise

Behavioural rather than physical, typically:
- Destructive behaviour at home (chewing, digging)
- Restlessness and inability to settle
- Excessive barking or whining
- Pacing
- Hyperactivity indoors - jumping, mouthing, not responding to usual commands
- Difficulty sleeping, or waking the household

Physical:
- Weight gain (link to the obesity statistic below)

**UK context stat:** 38% of UK dog owners walked their dog for 30 minutes or less each day. Vet professionals estimate 46% of UK dogs are overweight or obese (PDSA PAW 2024). Exercise and diet both contribute, but insufficient exercise is a significant factor.

## Section 5: Weather and Seasonal Factors

### Heat

Tom should write a short paragraph that makes the core point and links to the temperature guide:

The heat threshold for most adult dogs starts at 20C. At 22C, Pedigree UK and others name it as potentially too hot for flat-faced, elderly, or overweight dogs. At 25C, walking any dog in direct sun is a paw burn hazard. At 28C+, no dog should be walked. The full breakdown - by breed, temperature band, and what to do - is in the temperature guide. Link directly to it.

**One practical note to add that's not in the temperature guide:** On hot days, distance should be shorter regardless of time. Dogs walking on hot tarmac cover the same distance in less mental energy. A 30-minute walk on hot pavement is more taxing than a 45-minute walk on cool grass.

### Cold and Winter

Covered briefly in the senior dog guide. Tom should write one short paragraph for the general audience:

- Most healthy adult dogs tolerate UK winters without issue, including rain and cold
- Below approximately 7°C, elderly dogs, small short-coated dogs, and brachycephalic breeds benefit from a coat and shorter outings (Vets Now threshold, cited in senior guide)
- Ice is a specific hazard for dogs with joint issues or senior dogs - reduce distance and avoid slippery paths
- Rain itself is not a risk factor; wet coats and wet paws are manageable. The real winter consideration for walkers is daylight and visibility (use a lit collar or high-vis lead)

**UK-specific note:** The UK's damp climate means "muddy walks" is a practical concern even where cold is not. Wet grass and mud increase the risk of grass seeds embedding in paws from late spring through autumn.
