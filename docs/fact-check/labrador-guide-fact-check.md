### FLAG 1 - 5-minute puppy rule
**Result: CORRECTION REQUIRED**

**Article text (puppy section):**
> "The standard guidance is 5 minutes of structured exercise per month of age, twice daily."

**Issue:**
Tom calls the rule "standard guidance," which is not the same as presenting it as clinical fact - so this does not meet the FAIL threshold. However, the brief requires the rule to be framed as "practical guideline with uncertain evidence." Tom's current framing omits the fact that there is no scientific evidence behind it. The PDSA is explicit on this point, and the research pack flags it as "an established practical consensus" that "is not based on a peer-reviewed study."

The article presents the rule as straightforward guidance without any caveat, which could mislead readers into treating it as evidence-based clinical advice.

**Correction required:**
Add a brief caveat after the rule is stated. Suggested insert (Editor to refine):

*"There is no peer-reviewed evidence behind this rule - it is practical consensus, cited consistently across UK vet organisations and breed authorities rather than derived from a controlled study."*

This can be added as a sentence immediately following the initial statement of the rule, before the garden-play clarification.

### FLAG 2 - 8% Labrador stat
**Result: CORRECTION REQUIRED**

**Article text (exercise section):**
> "Research published in 2018 found only 8% of UK Labrador owners were meeting Kennel Club exercise guidelines."

**Issues:**

1. **Wrong year.** The source is Westgarth et al. (2017), BMC Public Health (PMC5465859). The article says 2018. The research pack's own tone notes section says 2018 (an internal error in the pack), but the authoritative citation in the research pack's stats section gives the correct date as 2017. The published paper is a 2017 study. Correction: "2018" should be "2017."

2. **Missing attribution.** The source (Westgarth et al., 2017) is not named in the article. "Research published in 2017" alone is sufficient for a consumer article, but attribution would strengthen the claim. Editor may add "(Westgarth et al., 2017)" or similar.

**Correction required:**
Change "Research published in 2018" to "Research published in 2017" (Westgarth et al., BMC Public Health).

### Coat colour study - evidence framing
**Result: CORRECTION REQUIRED**

**Article text:**
> "The evidence does not support the popular assumption that black Labs overheat faster than yellow Labs. A 2019 study testing both coat colours on the same walk found no meaningful difference in temperature or respiration rate. A separate 2021 analysis reached a similar conclusion."

**Issue:**
The 2019 study referenced is a bioRxiv preprint (DOI: 10.1101/639757), not a peer-reviewed publication, and with only 16 dogs is severely underpowered. The authors themselves stated that adequate statistical power would require 500+ dogs. The research pack explicitly notes this and advises that the study "should not be cited as settling the question."

Tom's current framing - "the evidence does not support" - is the correct conclusion the research pack directs him to reach. However, presenting a 16-dog preprint without noting its limitations gives readers a false impression of the evidence base. The framing currently implies stronger evidence than exists.

**Correction required:**
Add a brief caveat to the coat colour section acknowledging the study's limitations. Suggested approach (Editor to refine):

*"The main study on this - a 2019 trial comparing black and yellow Labs on the same walk - found no meaningful difference, though it was small (16 dogs) and is not yet peer-reviewed. A separate 2021 analysis supports the same conclusion."*

The overall framing ("the evidence does not support the assumption") can remain - this is the correct and honest conclusion. The caveat ensures the article does not overstate the certainty.
