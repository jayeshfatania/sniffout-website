## Flag items

### FLAG-01: Treatment window -- "few hours" vs PDSA's "12 hours"

**Article text:** "Time matters here more than with most poisoning cases because treatment must begin within a few hours of ingestion to be effective."

**What PDSA says:** "Your dog has a higher chance of surviving if treatment is started within 12 hours of eating or drinking ethylene glycol."

Tom's "within a few hours" is more conservative than PDSA's stated 12-hour window. It is not inaccurate in the sense that earlier treatment is always better, and "a few hours" is consistent with the urgency of the situation. However, it understates the window PDSA gives owners. The practical effect of Tom's phrasing is to prompt faster action, which is the right outcome.

**Note:** Flagged for editorial awareness. Not a FAIL because the error is in the direction of more caution, not less. Consider aligning with PDSA's "within 12 hours" for precision while retaining the urgency framing.

---

### FLAG-02: Two-stage vs three-stage poisoning model

**Article text:** Tom uses a two-stage model -- Stage 1 (neurological, initial symptoms) and Stage 2 (kidney failure, 12-24 hours).

**Sources:** PDSA uses a two-stage model consistent with Tom's. Vets Now describes three stages (neurological / apparent recovery / kidney failure). The two-stage model is a simplification of the three-stage model that collapses Stage 1 and the apparent recovery period into a single stage. Tom does describe the apparent recovery phenomenon within Stage 1: "During this stage, symptoms may ease and the dog may seem to improve." This captures the key safety point.

**Note:** The two-stage model is defensible and aligns with PDSA. No fix required. Flagged for completeness.

---

### FLAG-03: Risk period -- "October through February" vs year-round framing

**Article text:** "Risk is highest in the colder months - October through February - when antifreeze is being topped up in car cooling systems more frequently across the UK."

**Article also states:** "But the risk is not purely seasonal. Any time a car is being serviced or a coolant reservoir is being handled nearby, the exposure risk exists."

The article correctly notes year-round risk after giving the seasonal framing. The October-February range is a reasonable UK seasonal estimate. No source contradicts it. However, the specific months are not attributed to any source.

**Note:** Acceptable as editorial judgement. No fix required.
