## Section 1: When are ticks active in the UK?

| Claim | Verdict | Fix required |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| Main tick season runs from March to October | Correct | None |
| Busiest months being May and June | Flag | PHE/UKHSA data describes the spring peak as April-June, not May-June. April is a significant month of rising tick activity. Omitting it is a factual narrowing of the peak window. Fix: change to "with the busiest months typically running from April through June" |
| A second smaller rise in activity tends to happen in September | Correct | None |
| Risk does not disappear entirely in mild winters | Correct | None |
| High-risk areas listed (Scottish Highlands, New Forest, Exmoor, Dartmoor, Lake District, Surrey Hills, South Downs, Yorkshire Moors, Thetford Forest) | Correct | None |
| Any area with deer, sheep, rabbits, or hedgehogs is potential tick territory | Correct | None |

## Section 4: How to remove a tick correctly

This section is safety-critical. Assessed with highest scrutiny.

| Claim | Verdict | Fix required |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| Tick removal hook is the right tool | Correct | None |
| "Slide under the tick and lift it free without squeezing the body" | Correct | None - accurate description of hook tool function |
| Cost around five to eight pounds | Correct | None - consistent with UK retail pricing for O'Tom Tick Twister and equivalents |
| Rotate the tool gently - two or three turns is usually enough | Correct | None - O'Tom Tick Twister official instructions explicitly state "rotate 2 or 3 times." The rotation instruction is correct for hook tools. This is distinct from tweezers, where you pull steadily without twisting. The article does not say "do not twist" at any point, and correctly instructs rotating for the hook tool. |
| The tick releases on its own | Correct | None - consistent with O'Tom instructions |
| Check bite site to confirm whole tick has come away | Correct | None |
| Drop tick in sealed bag and dispose of it | Incomplete | Minor: some NHS and UKHSA guidance for humans suggests keeping the tick in a sealed container in case a doctor needs to examine it. The article instructs disposal. This is not wrong for a dog-focused audience, but a brief note that human walkers may wish to keep the tick is defensible. Low priority fix. |
| Clean bite area with antiseptic wipe and wash hands | Correct | None |
| Do not squeeze the tick's body - risks pushing stomach contents into the bite | Correct | None - accepted mechanism, cited by UKHSA and NHS |
| Do not burn, apply Vaseline, nail varnish, or alcohol while tick is attached | Correct | None - all these are explicitly warned against by UKHSA, NHS, RSPCA |

**Section verdict: PASS**

Note: The twist/rotate distinction is handled correctly. "Rotate the tool gently" is the right instruction for a hook tool. The article does not contain the dangerous error of telling users NOT to rotate a hook tool, nor does it tell users to twist tweezers.

## Section 5: What diseases can ticks carry?

| Claim | Verdict | Fix required |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| Lyme disease is caused by bacteria carried by the common sheep tick | Correct | None - Borrelia burgdorferi sensu lato, carried by Ixodes ricinus |
| Most tick bites do not result in Lyme disease | Correct | None - not all ticks carry Borrelia; not all bites result in transmission |
| Transmission generally requires the tick to be attached for more than 36 hours | Flag | The 36-hour figure is widely repeated and comes from human/mouse transmission studies (Piesman et al.). However: (1) UKHSA and NHS guidance do not specify a 36-hour threshold - they state that the longer a tick is attached, the higher the risk; (2) some transmission can occur before 36 hours, at lower probability; (3) framing it as a requirement may cause owners to feel safe if a tick has been attached for 30 hours. Fix: rephrase to "the risk is significantly lower when a tick is removed quickly - the longer it feeds, the greater the chance of transmission" rather than implying a threshold of safety |
| Lyme disease in dogs can cause joint swelling, lameness, lethargy, loss of appetite | Correct | None - consistent with BVA and RSPCA guidance on canine Lyme disease |
| Treatable with antibiotics and responds well when caught early | Correct | None |
| Babesiosis is a blood infection caused by a microscopic parasite | Correct | None - Babesia canis is a protozoan |
| Carried by the ornate dog tick | Correct | None - Dermacentor reticulatus is the confirmed vector for UK Babesia canis cases per APHA |
| Identified mainly around Harlow in Essex | Correct | None - APHA confirmed autochthonous cases in this area; it remains geographically limited |
| Signs include lethargy, pale or yellowed gums, and weakness | Correct | None - accurate clinical signs for canine babesiosis |
| Early removal is best protection; same-day removal carries much lower risk | Correct | Consistent with overall evidence, appropriately framed |

**Section verdict: FLAG** (one claim requires more careful framing on Lyme transmission window)

## Section 7: When to call the vet

| Claim | Verdict | Fix required |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| Retained tick parts under skin | Correct | None |
| Infected, swollen, or non-healing bite site | Correct | None |
| A circular or ring-shaped rash in dogs - less visible through the coat but worth checking | Flag | Erythema migrans (bull's eye rash) is a well-established early indicator of Lyme disease in humans. In dogs, this rash is not a reliable or commonly documented clinical sign - BVA and BSAVA guidance does not list erythema migrans as a typical diagnostic indicator in dogs. The article hedges by saying "less visible through the coat" but implies owners should check their dogs for this rash, which may create false reassurance if no rash is visible (because dogs rarely show one). The advice to check yourself after a walk is correct and should stay. Fix: remove the implication that a ring rash is a meaningful thing to check for in dogs. Replace with advice to watch for systemic signs (lameness, lethargy, swollen joints) rather than a visible rash. The self-check line can remain. |
| Lethargy, loss of appetite, swollen joints in weeks following tick bite | Correct | None |
| Mention Harlow/Essex location specifically to vet | Correct | None - appropriate and clinically useful detail for babesiosis context |
| Early treatment makes a significant difference | Correct | None |

**Section verdict: FLAG** (erythema migrans framing in dogs needs correction)

## Overall verdict: FLAG

The article is well-researched, appropriately calibrated in tone (not alarmist), and the safety-critical removal section is correct. The rotation instruction for hook tools is accurate and correctly distinct from tweezer guidance. The babesiosis geographic information is accurate.

Three items require amendment before the article is fully publication-ready. Two are moderate: the Lyme transmission window framing and the ring-rash-in-dogs advice. One is minor: the spring peak month range. None are fails - no dangerously wrong information, no content that would cause direct harm to an animal.
