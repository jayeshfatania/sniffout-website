## Section 2: Why heat is a serious concern for Shar Peis

| Claim | Verdict | Fix required |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| Shar Peis are brachycephalic, meaning they have a shorter, flatter muzzle than most breeds | Correct | None - Shar Peis are classified as brachycephalic by the Kennel Club and BVA. The article does not overstate the degree of brachycephaly, which is appropriate: Shar Peis are moderately brachycephalic, not in the most extreme category alongside Pugs or French Bulldogs. The framing here is proportionate. |
| Shar Peis regulate body temperature less efficiently than dogs with a longer airway | Correct | None - direct consequence of brachycephalic conformation, consistent with BVA guidance |
| "considerably more folded coat" contributing to overheating | Flag | "Folded coat" is imprecise and potentially misleading. Shar Peis have wrinkled skin (skin folds), not a folded coat. Their coat is characteristically short and harsh (or, in Bear Coat variants, longer but still a distinct coat from the folds). The skin folds do contribute to heat retention and reduced ability to dissipate heat, but the mechanism should be described as wrinkled skin or skin folds, not folded coat. Fix: replace "considerably more folded coat" with "considerably more wrinkled skin" or similar. |
| They overheat faster than their composed exterior often suggests | Correct | None - consistent with BVA and RSPCA guidance on brachycephalic heat risk |
| They can show fewer obvious warning signs before the situation becomes serious | Correct | None - consistent with BVA brachycephalic risk guidance on reduced visible distress signals |
| Signs of overheating listed (heavy panting, excessive drooling, slowing of pace, reluctance to continue) | Correct | None - consistent with standard heat stress indicators across PDSA and RSPCA guidance |

**Section verdict: FLAG** (one imprecise phrase: "folded coat" should be "wrinkled skin" or "skin folds")

## Section 5: Walking with other dogs

| Claim | Verdict | Fix required |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| Shar Peis have a deserved reputation for being reserved around unfamiliar dogs | Correct | None - Kennel Club and PDSA both note that Shar Peis can be wary or selective around other dogs. "Reserved" is an appropriately calibrated description that does not overstate aggression. |
| This is normal for the breed rather than a sign of anything wrong | Correct | None - framing is responsible and consistent with breed guidance |
| They were historically bred as guard dogs and their caution around strangers is deeply ingrained | Incomplete | The guard dog description is accurate but incomplete as an explanation for dog-to-dog wariness specifically. Shar Peis were historically bred for multiple purposes in China, including dog fighting - a purpose documented by the Kennel Club and directly more relevant to their caution around unfamiliar dogs than the guard role. The guard dog attribution is not wrong, but using it as the sole historical explanation for this specific trait omits the more direct cause. Fix: consider adding "and, in some historical breeding lines, for dog fighting" or similar to give a fuller picture. This is a minor point rather than a critical error. |
| On-lead handling, calm management of encounters, and distance introductions are the right approach | Correct | None - appropriate, responsible guidance |
| Many Shar Peis settle comfortably around familiar dogs they have grown up with while remaining watchful around strangers | Correct | None - consistent with breed temperament descriptions from KC and RSPCA |

**Section verdict: FLAG** (minor: historical purpose attribution is incomplete - dog fighting history is more directly relevant to dog-dog wariness than guard dog heritage alone, and the Kennel Club acknowledges it)

## Section 6: What to watch for on walks - SPAID and Shar Pei Fever

This section carries the highest factual weight and was assessed with the closest scrutiny.

| Claim | Verdict | Fix required |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| Familial Shar Pei Fever (FSF) is a periodic fever syndrome that causes sudden high temperature and swelling in the hocks | Correct | None - this is an accurate clinical description of FSF |
| The hock is the joint just above the paw | Correct | None - the hock (tarsus) is correctly described |
| FSF is now understood as part of a broader cluster of autoinflammatory conditions specific to Shar Peis, grouped under the name Shar Pei Autoinflammatory Disease (SPAID) | Correct | None - SPAID is the current accepted umbrella term encompassing FSF and related autoinflammatory presentations. This framing is accurate and reflects current clinical understanding. |
| Research by Dr Linda Tintle at Cornell University has been instrumental in advancing the medical understanding of SPAID | Wrong | The "at Cornell University" designation is factually incorrect. Dr Linda Tintle is a veterinarian in private practice (Wurtsboro Veterinary Clinic, Wurtsboro, New York). She has collaborated with Cornell University researchers and co-authored publications involving Cornell affiliates, but she is not based at or employed by Cornell University. Describing her as being "at Cornell" misrepresents her institutional affiliation. Fix: remove "at Cornell University." The correct framing is simply "Research by Dr Linda Tintle has been instrumental in advancing the medical understanding of SPAID" or, if the Cornell connection is important to retain, "Dr Linda Tintle, whose research and collaborations with Cornell University researchers have been instrumental..." |
| Walk signs: sudden reluctance to continue, limping without obvious cause, stopping | Correct | None - consistent with clinical presentations of FSF during exercise |
| Swelling around one or both hock joints on examination | Correct | None - bilateral or unilateral hock swelling is the characteristic physical sign of FSF |
| FSF episodes generally respond well to appropriate treatment | Correct | None - with anti-inflammatory management, FSF episodes can be controlled. The article correctly avoids promising a cure and flags the need for veterinary management. |

**Section verdict: FAIL on one claim** (Dr Tintle's institutional affiliation stated as Cornell University is factually wrong - she is in private practice and collaborates with Cornell but is not based there)
