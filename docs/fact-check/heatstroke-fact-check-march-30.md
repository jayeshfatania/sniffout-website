## Overall verdict: FAIL

Two safety-critical errors in the cooling advice section. The article instructs readers not to use ice packs, which directly contradicts current PDSA guidance. The attribution of the 20-degree/25-degree temperature thresholds to PDSA is also incorrect. Both must be corrected before publication.

## Fail items

### FAIL-01: "Not ice packs" contradicts PDSA guidance (SAFETY-CRITICAL)

**Article text:** "Apply cool wet towels to the neck, armpits, and groin. Not ice packs."
**Article text:** "do not use ice or very cold water. It causes blood vessels near the skin to constrict, which slows cooling rather than speeding it up."

**What PDSA actually says:**
"wrap an ice pack/frozen veg in a towel and place it in between their thighs."
PDSA also confirms: "Gently pour cold water (water from the cold tap is fine) over their whole body."

The article tells owners not to use ice packs. PDSA specifically recommends ice packs wrapped in a towel placed between the thighs as a first-aid cooling measure. These are directly contradictory instructions on a safety-critical point.

The vasoconstriction explanation Tom provides ("It causes blood vessels near the skin to constrict, which slows cooling rather than speeding it up") does not appear in PDSA, Blue Cross, or Vets Now guidance. Vets Now first-aid guidance recommends cold water combined with a fan as the most effective cooling method. The vasoconstriction warning applied to dogs appears to come from human or equine heatstroke guidance and is not reflected in current UK veterinary sources for dogs.

**Fix required:**
Remove the "Not ice packs" instruction and the vasoconstriction explanation. Align with PDSA guidance: cold tap water poured over the dog is recommended; ice packs wrapped in a towel applied to the thigh area are recommended by PDSA. Remove the claim that cold water is harmful.

### FAIL-02: Temperature thresholds attributed to PDSA incorrectly

**Article text:** "PDSA guidance is a useful reference point. Above 20 degrees Celsius warrants extra caution, particularly in direct sun. Above 25 degrees is high risk for most dogs."

**What PDSA actually says:**
PDSA's heatstroke guidance does not state ambient temperature thresholds of 20°C or 25°C for walking decisions. PDSA uses the dog's body temperature (40°C+) as the clinical marker for heatstroke onset. PDSA says to walk "in the early morning/evening when it's cool" but does not give a specific ambient temperature figure.

The 20°C threshold for walking comes from Blue Cross ("temperatures over 20 degrees celsius can put dogs at higher risk of heatstroke") and Vets Now ("even at temperatures as low as 20°C dogs are at risk of heat stroke"), not from PDSA.

Attributing both thresholds to PDSA is incorrect. The 20°C threshold is supported by Blue Cross and Vets Now. The 25°C "high risk" threshold is not confirmed in any of the source pages consulted.

**Fix required:**
Change "PDSA guidance is a useful reference point" to "Blue Cross and Vets Now both note that". Remove the "above 25 degrees is high risk" claim or source it specifically. The 20°C threshold is supportable from Blue Cross and Vets Now but not PDSA.

### FAIL-03: Wet towels advice may contradict PDSA

**Article text:** "Apply cool wet towels to the neck, armpits, and groin."

**What PDSA says:**
"Do not cover the dog with wet towels as this will make them even hotter."

Tom's advice is targeted application to specific areas (not covering the whole dog), which is different from what PDSA warns against (covering). However, the principal cooling method in PDSA guidance is to pour water over the dog, not to apply wet towels to specific areas. The two approaches differ, and PDSA's specific warning against wet towels creates a risk that an owner reading Tom's article will cover the dog with wet towels rather than pour water.

**Fix required:**
Change step 3 and 4 to align with PDSA. Replace "Apply cool wet towels to the neck, armpits, and groin" with "Pour cold water over the dog, concentrating on the neck, armpits, and groin. Do not cover the dog with wet towels."

## Flag items

### FLAG-01: Evening walk timing inconsistent with Vets Now

**Article text:** "evening walks after 7pm mean cooler air, cooler ground, and less direct sun."

**Sources:** Vets Now advises avoiding the 8am-8pm window, implying walks should be after 8pm rather than after 7pm. Blue Cross and PDSA give no specific evening clock time. Tom's "after 7pm" is less conservative than Vets Now's implied after-8pm.

**Fix:** Either remove the specific evening time, or change to "after 8pm" to align with Vets Now's guidance. The morning "before 8am" is consistent with available sources.

---

### FLAG-02: Pavement test method inconsistent with PDSA

**Article text:** "hold the back of your hand flat on the tarmac for five seconds."

**PDSA says:** "If you can comfortably place the palm of your hand on it for 7 seconds it's okay."

Tom says five seconds with the back of the hand; PDSA says seven seconds with the palm. The back of the hand is more sensitive than the palm, so a shorter duration may be appropriate for that method. However, the specific figure of five seconds does not come from PDSA. Different sources use different durations and hand positions.

**Fix:** Either attribute the five-second back-of-hand version to a specific source, or align with PDSA ("palm of your hand for seven seconds").

## Ambiguous items

### AMB-01: "A dog left in a parked car on a warm day can reach dangerous temperatures in a matter of minutes"

PDSA data shows that on a 22°C day, a car can reach 47°C within 60 minutes. The "matter of minutes" framing is more alarmist than the evidence supports (significant heat rise occurs within the first 20-30 minutes but the phrase "matter of minutes" is imprecise). This is a commonly used safety phrase by multiple charities (Dogs Trust, RSPCA) even if the specific timeline is imprecise.

**Note:** Acceptable as editorial framing given broad use across UK animal welfare charities. Flag to editor for precision.
