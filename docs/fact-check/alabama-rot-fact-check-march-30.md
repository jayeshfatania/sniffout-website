## Overall verdict: FAIL

Three em dash violations: two in frontmatter (title and description) and one in the article body. The article is otherwise well-sourced and the core cause/causation framing is accurate. Five flag items require attention before publication, including a significant omission in the prognosis section (survival and fatality rate figures that the research file explicitly provides) and an inaccuracy in the geographic attribution of Hampshire to the South West.

## Flag items

### FLAG-01: Prognosis section omits survival and fatality rate figures (SIGNIFICANT)

**Article text:** "Dogs that develop only skin lesions, without kidney involvement, have a better outlook. The prognosis becomes more serious if kidney failure develops."

**What the research file specifies:**
The research file explicitly provides verified prognosis figures and a recommended safe framing that includes them:

- Approximately 80-85% of dogs that develop kidney failure do not survive (Vets Now, general guidance)
- Approximately 30% of dogs clinically diagnosed with Alabama rot have survived (RVC Queen Mother Hospital, March 2024, including dogs receiving specialist treatment)

The research file's safe framing for Tom: "Dogs that develop only the skin lesions, without kidney involvement, are expected to recover. For dogs that progress to kidney failure, the prognosis is serious and the majority do not survive. Approximately 30% of dogs clinically diagnosed with Alabama rot at the Royal Veterinary College have survived -- a figure that includes specialist treatment including plasma exchange."

Tom's current text ("have a better outlook"; "prognosis becomes more serious") is vague to the point of being misleading. "More serious" does not convey that the majority of dogs that develop kidney failure do not survive. For owners whose dog has progressed to kidney involvement, this framing significantly understates the situation. The research file distinguishes clearly between the two populations; the article conflates them.

**Fix required:** Align the prognosis section with the research file's safe framing. Include the 80-85% kidney failure fatality framing (or equivalent: "the majority do not survive") and the 30% overall RVC survival figure, attributed explicitly to the RVC Queen Mother Hospital.

### FLAG-02: "Definitive confirmation requires tissue analysis" -- "post-mortem" omitted

**Article text (section "What should you do if you notice a suspicious lesion?"):** "A diagnosis of CRGV during the dog's lifetime is based on clinical presentation. Definitive confirmation requires tissue analysis."

**What the research file states:** "Definitive histological confirmation requires kidney tissue analysis post-mortem."

The omission of "post-mortem" changes the meaning materially. As written, the article could be read to imply that definitive diagnosis is available to a living dog through biopsy or tissue sampling during veterinary assessment. This is not the case. Histological confirmation requires kidney tissue obtained after death. An owner reading the article may reasonably expect that their vet can confirm CRGV definitively while the dog is alive; this creates a false expectation about what a vet visit will establish.

**Fix required:** Add "post-mortem" to make the limitation explicit: "Definitive histological confirmation requires kidney tissue analysis and is only possible post-mortem."

---

### FLAG-03: Hampshire grouped geographically with South West England

**Article text:** "The South West of England has historically had the highest proportion of cases relative to the dog population, with Devon, Cornwall, and Hampshire accounting for a significant share."

**Research file:** Hampshire appears explicitly under the South East England geographic cluster alongside Surrey, Berkshire, Oxfordshire, and Sussex. Devon and Cornwall appear under South West England. Hampshire is not listed under the South West.

Hampshire (including the New Forest) is historically significant in CRGV case history as the location of the first confirmed UK cluster. However, it is geographically in the South East / South Central region of England, not the South West. Grouping it with Devon and Cornwall in a sentence introducing South West England is a geographic inaccuracy.

**Fix required:** Either remove Hampshire from the South West sentence and note it separately (as the site of the first confirmed UK cases), or restructure the geographic paragraph to avoid placing Hampshire in the South West grouping. Example: "Devon and Cornwall have among the highest case counts in the South West. Hampshire, home to the New Forest where the first UK cases were identified, also accounts for a significant proportion of confirmed cases."

### FLAG-04: Skin lesion location list -- muzzle, tongue, and flank omitted

**Article text:** "Lesions can also appear on the paws, pads, between the toes, and less commonly on the face or belly."

**Research file:** "Less commonly on the face, muzzle, tongue, flank, or belly."

Tom's article omits muzzle, tongue, and flank from the less common locations. Muzzle is a distinct and recognisable location that owners can check. The research file lists it explicitly. Omitting it reduces the usefulness of the inspection guidance.

**Fix required:** Add muzzle and flank to the less common location list. Tongue is less relevant as an owner-observable inspection point and its omission is acceptable. Suggested: "less commonly on the face, muzzle, flanks, or belly."

---

### FLAG-05: Vet urgency framing -- "same day" is less urgent than ARRF guidance

**Article text:** "Contact your vet on the same day."

**Research file:** ARRF verbatim: "Contact your vet immediately." Vets Now verbatim: "Time is of the essence and by far the best chance of success is early, aggressive veterinary treatment." The research safe framing for Tom says "same-day vet assessment."

"On the same day" is consistent with the research's safe framing but is less urgent than the source guidance. Given the one-to-nine-day window from skin lesion to kidney involvement, and the severity of outcome once kidney failure sets in, the softer "on the same day" framing is adequate but sits at the lower end of urgency. There is no factual error here; the safe framing in the research file does use "same-day." Flagged for editorial awareness.

**Note:** No change strictly required. Editor may wish to strengthen to "Contact your vet promptly -- the same day you notice the lesion" to retain the practical framing while signalling more urgency.

## Ambiguous items

### AMB-01: Pull quote "Around 10 to 50 cases per year" -- recent confirmed figures fall below 10

**Pull quote:** "Around 10 to 50 cases of Alabama rot are confirmed in UK dogs each year."

**Research file data:** 2022: 8 confirmed cases; 2023: 4 confirmed (histological minimum); 2025: 6 (partial year). The "10 to 50" framing appears in the research file itself as the recommended range for Tom ("The UK averages roughly 10 to 50 confirmed cases per year"). However, the confirmed ARRF figures for 2022, 2023, and the partial 2025 data are all below 10.

The article body handles this correctly: "Annual figures vary considerably. Some years have seen over 50 confirmed cases; others fewer than 10." This is accurate. The pull quote's "10 to 50" range technically understates the low end in recent years.

**Note:** The "10 to 50" framing derives from the research file's own safe framing. The article body is more precise. The pull quote is broadly acceptable. Flagged for editorial awareness in case the research file is updated with more recent confirmed case data.

## Summary

The Alabama rot article is well-structured and the cause/causation framing is the strongest aspect of the piece: the cause is clearly stated as unknown, no hypotheses are presented as confirmed, and the seasonal pattern is correctly distinguished from a causal link. Three em dash violations require correction. The prognosis section is the most significant editorial gap: it omits the survival and fatality rate figures that the research file explicitly provides and flags as required content. The omission of "post-mortem" from the definitive confirmation sentence and the geographic misclassification of Hampshire as South West England are straightforward fixes. Once these items are resolved the article is ready for publication.
