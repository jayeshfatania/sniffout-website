### FAIL-02: Swimfo map does not monitor blue-green algae (ACCURACY)

**Article text:** "For current warnings in England, the Environment Agency's Swimfo map is the most reliable single source. Checking it before a summer walk near any lake or reservoir takes about thirty seconds."

**What Swimfo actually monitors:** The Environment Agency's Swimfo site (swimfo.environment-agency.gov.uk) monitors designated bathing water quality for human swimmers at designated bathing sites. It measures bacterial contamination (E. coli, intestinal enterococci) at sites formally designated under the Bathing Water Regulations 2013. It is not a blue-green algae monitoring tool and does not cover most inland lakes and reservoirs used by dog walkers.

**What PDSA recommends:** PDSA advises checking local council websites and reporting suspected blooms to the Environment Agency. Blue Cross advises checking local authority and Environment Agency social media for current bloom warnings.

Tom's recommendation to use Swimfo as "the most reliable single source" for blue-green algae warnings is incorrect. Most lakes and reservoirs where dog walkers encounter BGA are not designated bathing sites and will not appear on Swimfo. An owner who checks Swimfo and finds no warning for a lake may incorrectly conclude the water is safe.

**Fix required:** Replace the Swimfo recommendation with an accurate alternative. Suggested fix: direct readers to check local council websites, the Environment Agency's social media, and UKHSA for bloom notifications. Mention that local authority warning signs at the water body remain the most immediate indicator, while noting that smaller, unmonitored water bodies may have no signs even when blooms are present.

## Flag items

### FLAG-01: Bloom season -- "May to September" vs "June to September"

**Article text:** "typically May to September"

**Sources:** PDSA, Blue Cross, and Vets Now most commonly cite June to September as the primary bloom season, noting that warm, still, nutrient-rich conditions are required. Some sources include late May in warm years. UKHSA guidance notes blooms are most common "during periods of warm, sunny, calm weather, typically from June to September."

Tom's "May to September" is slightly broader than the consensus. It is not wrong in warm years where May temperatures allow blooms to form, but it is less precise than most UK sources.

**Note:** Acceptable as editorial framing if the word "typically" is retained, as it signals variability. No fix required, but editor may wish to align with "June to September" for closer source fidelity.

---

### FLAG-02: Symptom onset window -- "fifteen to sixty minutes"

**Article text:** "signs can appear within fifteen to sixty minutes"

**Sources:** Blue Cross states "within 15 minutes to an hour." PDSA states symptoms can develop "within 15 minutes to a few days depending on the toxin." Tom's "fifteen to sixty minutes" matches Blue Cross but is narrower than PDSA's range, which includes slower-developing hepatotoxic presentations (liver damage) that may take days to manifest.

**Note:** The narrower range creates urgency, which is appropriate. However, owners reading this framing may not recognise a delayed presentation (12-24 hours later) as BGA poisoning. Consider whether to widen to "within 15 minutes to several hours" or to add a note that some cases present more slowly.

---

### FLAG-03: "No antidote" -- accuracy

**Article text:** "There is no antidote."

**Sources:** PDSA, Blue Cross, and Vets Now all confirm there is no antidote for cyanotoxin poisoning in dogs. Treatment is supportive. The claim is accurate.

**Note:** No fix required. Included here for completeness.

## Ambiguous items

### AMB-01: "Can kill a dog within hours"

**Pull quote:** "Blue-green algae can kill a dog within hours."

PDSA states symptoms can develop within 15 minutes and that the condition can be fatal. The "within hours" framing is consistent with the faster-onset neurotoxic presentations. Hepatotoxic cases may take longer. The pull quote is an appropriate summary of the acute risk without being inaccurate.

**Note:** Acceptable as editorial framing. No fix required.

---

### AMB-02: "Most lakes on most summer days are fine"

**Article text:** "The honest thing to say about the risk level is this: most lakes on most summer days are fine."

No UK source quantifies bloom prevalence at this level of generality. The statement is a calibrated reassurance, not a cited fact, and the framing ("the honest thing to say") signals editorial judgement rather than a source claim. Consistent with the hazard tone guide.

**Note:** Acceptable as editorial framing. No fix required.

The blue-green algae article handles the hazard well in tone and structure. Two corrections are required before publication.
