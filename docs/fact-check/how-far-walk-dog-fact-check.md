### Priority 1: 5-minute puppy rule framing

**RESULT: MINOR ERROR - correction needed**

Tom has correctly framed the 5-minute rule as a guideline rather than clinical fact, and has stated that PDSA notes no scientific evidence behind it. However, there is a wording error in the paraphrase.

**Tom writes:** "it is not appropriate for all puppies"

"Most" is a meaningfully stronger statement than "all" in this context. PDSA's actual wording makes the limitation broader. Tom's paraphrase softens the PDSA position.

**Correction:** Change "not appropriate for all puppies" to "not appropriate for most puppies." (One word change.)

### Priority 2: 8% Labrador stat

**RESULT: FAIL - three errors in one sentence**

The article states: "A 2017 survey of 12,314 UK dog owners found that only 8% of Labrador owners reported meeting their dog's Kennel Club exercise guidelines - the worst compliance rate of any popular breed in the study."

**Error 1 - "12,314 UK dog owners":**
The 12,314 figure refers to pedigree **dogs** in the final dataset, not dog owners. The study draws from 17,028 dogs initially. The study does not separately report a participant count of 12,314 owners. The sentence as written misrepresents the sample.

**Error 2 - "the worst compliance rate of any popular breed":**
FALSE. The paper explicitly shows multiple breeds with lower compliance rates than the Labrador's 8%: Bloodhound 0%, Giant Schnauzer 0%, Foxhound 0%, Great Dane 3%, Bullmastiff 4%. The Labrador's 8% was highlighted in the paper as notable because of the breed's popularity, not because it was the lowest rate overall. "Worst compliance rate of any popular breed" is not what the paper says or implies.

**Error 3 - source description:**
The research pack attributes this study to BMC Public Health. The study was published in the **Journal of Nutritional Science**. If Tom has cited BMC Public Health in any notes or if the source line at the bottom of the article implies this, it needs correcting. The PMC reference (PMC5465859) is correct; the journal name is not BMC Public Health.

**What the paper actually says:** "Even in popular breeds like the Labrador retriever only 8% were reported to meet the guidelines for activity." The 8% figure itself is accurate and citable. The surrounding claims are not.

**Correction needed:** Revise the sentence to read approximately: "A survey of 12,314 dogs from UK owner-reported data found that only 8% of Labradors were reported to meet their Kennel Club exercise guidelines." Remove "the worst compliance rate of any popular breed" entirely or replace with: "a figure the authors highlighted given the breed's widespread popularity." Correct journal attribution to Journal of Nutritional Science if it appears in the article.

### Priority 3: Growth plate closure timings

**RESULT: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED - minor issue**

The article states: small and medium breeds around 12 months, large breeds (Labradors, Retrievers, German Shepherds) up to 18 months, giant breeds (Great Danes, Irish Wolfhounds) up to 24 months.

**What PDSA actually states:** "Most dogs are fully grown by one year of age, but larger breeds such as Greyhounds, Labradors, Dobermans and Great Danes can take as long as 18 months to 2 years to reach full size."

PDSA does not use the article's three-tier breakdown. PDSA groups Labradors and Great Danes together in a single "up to 18 months to 2 years" bracket - it does not separately distinguish large breeds (18 months) from giant breeds (24 months). The article's more granular breakdown is a reasonable veterinary interpretation but is presented as fact without a source that states it in this exact form.

**Assessment:** The three-tier breakdown is broadly consistent with veterinary consensus and is a reasonable synthesis. It is not wrong. However, it should not be framed as sourced directly from PDSA, because PDSA does not separate it in this way. This is a sourcing precision issue rather than a factual error.

**Correction needed:** The growth plate closure list does not need to change. Remove any implication that this three-tier breakdown comes directly from PDSA. It can be noted as "broadly consistent with UK vet guidance" without attributing the specific tier structure to PDSA.

### Priority 4: PDSA PAW Report figures

**RESULT: FAIL - wrong year and wrong figures**

The article states: "A 2024 PDSA survey found that 38% of UK dog owners walk their dog for 30 minutes or less each day, and vet professionals estimate that 46% of UK dogs are overweight or obese. (PDSA PAW Report 2024)"

**Error 1 - the 46% figure:**
The 46% vet professional estimate for dogs being overweight or obese does not appear in the PAW Report 2024. This figure originates from the **PAW Report 2019** and was repeated in later editions. The 2024 report does not contain this statistic. Attributing it to PAW 2024 is factually wrong.

**Error 2 - the 38% figure:**
The 38% figure for dog owners walking their dog for 30 minutes or less does not appear in any PDSA PAW Report. The closest figure in the PAW 2019 report is "44% of owners walk their dog for less than 30 minutes at a time." The 38% figure does not match any verified PAW report statistic for dog walking duration. Its source is unconfirmed.

**Correction needed:** Both statistics require verified sourcing before the article can be published. Options:
- If the 44% (PAW 2019) figure is used instead of 38%, the year attribution must change to 2019.
- If the 46% vet estimate figure is used, it should be attributed to the PAW Report edition in which it actually appeared (2019, with note that the figure has been cited in subsequent reports).
- Alternatively, remove both statistics and replace with verified 2024 figures, or remove the statistics altogether. Tom's article functions well without them - they are context rather than load-bearing claims.

### Priority 5: Breed exercise table - KC A to Z

**RESULT: FAIL - multiple errors**

The article's breed exercise table and the related FAQ claim contain several direct contradictions of the KC A to Z.

**Critical error - KC framework itself:**

The article presents a **four-category** KC framework:
1. Up to 30 minutes per day
2. Up to 1 hour per day
3. 1 to 2 hours per day
4. More than 2 hours per day

**The KC A to Z uses three categories only:**
1. Up to 30 minutes per day
2. Up to 1 hour per day
3. More than 2 hours per day

There is no "1 to 2 hours per day" KC category. Any breed the article places in this tier is misclassified per the actual KC system.

**Breed-specific errors:**

| Breed | Article states | KC A to Z | Issue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cocker Spaniel | 1.5 to 2 hours | Up to 1 hour per day | WRONG - article overstates by one full tier |
| Jack Russell Terrier | 60 to 90 minutes | Up to 1 hour per day | WRONG - 90 minutes exceeds KC upper limit of 60 minutes; article overstates |
| Great Dane | Implied less than Jack Russell (see FAQ below) | More than 2 hours per day | WRONG - see detailed note |
| Shih Tzu | 20 to 40 minutes (grouped with 30 min breeds) | Up to 30 minutes per day | MINOR - 40 minutes slightly exceeds KC maximum of 30 minutes |
| Bichon Frise | 20 to 40 minutes | Up to 30 minutes per day | MINOR - 40 minutes slightly exceeds KC maximum of 30 minutes |

**Confirmed correct per KC A to Z:**
Border Collie (2+), English Springer Spaniel (2+), Labrador (2+), Husky (2+), Weimaraner (2+), Vizsla (2+), German Shepherd (2+), Golden Retriever (2+), Dalmatian (2+), Staffordshire Bull Terrier (up to 1 hour), Greyhound (up to 1 hour), Whippet (up to 1 hour), Cavalier King Charles Spaniel (up to 1 hour), Basset Hound (up to 1 hour), Chihuahua (up to 30 min), French Bulldog (up to 1 hour), Pug (up to 1 hour), English Bulldog (up to 1 hour), Miniature Schnauzer (up to 1 hour).

**The Great Dane / Jack Russell FAQ claim - specific error:**

The FAQ answer for "Do small dogs need less exercise?" states: "A Jack Russell Terrier typically needs 60 to 90 minutes daily - more than an adult Great Dane."

Per KC A to Z:
- Jack Russell Terrier: Up to **1 hour** per day
- Great Dane: **More than 2 hours** per day

The article's claim is the reverse of KC guidance. The KC says Great Danes need more exercise than the KC category for Jack Russells, not less. This is a direct factual error citing the KC as the implied authority.

**Note on context:** The research pack also listed Great Dane as 30-60 min (adult), which is inconsistent with the KC A to Z. This appears to be an error that originated in the research pack and was carried through into the article. The fact that the research pack was wrong does not change the article's obligation to match the KC A to Z, which is the primary source cited in the brief and the article.

**Corrections needed:**

1. Revise the KC framework in the article from four categories to three. Remove the "1 to 2 hours per day" tier.
2. Move Cocker Spaniel from the "1.5 to 2 hours" row to the "up to 1 hour" KC tier.
3. Revise Jack Russell figure from "60 to 90 minutes" to "up to 1 hour."
4. Revise or remove the Great Dane comparison in the FAQ. The claim that Great Danes need less exercise than Jack Russells contradicts the KC A to Z directly. If the Jack Russell vs Great Dane comparison is retained for rhetorical effect, either use a different low-exercise breed that is not KC "More than 2 hours per day", or revise the comparison to a breed the KC does assign to the low-exercise tier (e.g., Chihuahua vs Border Collie).
5. The Shih Tzu and Bichon Frise "up to 40 minutes" figures are minor - they are close enough to the KC "up to 30 minutes" category that the Editor may choose to tighten to "20 to 30 minutes" for the KC 30-minute tier breeds.

### Heat thresholds (20C caution, 22C+ serious concern)

**RESULT: MINOR INCONSISTENCY with the Sniffout temperature guide**

The article states: "Above 20 degrees Celsius, shorten walks... Above 22 degrees, the risk becomes more serious for elderly, overweight, or flat-faced dogs."

The Sniffout temperature guide (content/guides/temperature-guide.md) states at the 22-24C band: "High caution for **all** dogs; flat-faced breeds should not walk" and describes the 22-24C threshold as 6/10 risk for "any dog, not just vulnerable ones."

The article's framing that 22C "becomes more serious for elderly, overweight, or flat-faced dogs" implies the risk at 22C is primarily for vulnerable groups. The temperature guide is clearer: at 22C, healthy adult dogs of any breed are also in a high-caution zone - the restriction is not limited to vulnerable groups.

This is not a dangerous error given the article links directly to the temperature guide for the full breakdown. The framing is an understatement relative to the Sniffout temperature guide rather than a contradiction of an external source.

**Assessment:** Acceptable for a summary paragraph whose primary function is to direct readers to the temperature guide. No mandatory correction, but Editor may wish to revise to: "Above 22 degrees, all dogs are in a high-caution zone - flat-faced breeds should not walk at this temperature" to align with the temperature guide.
