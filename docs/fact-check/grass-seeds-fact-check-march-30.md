## Overview

The article is well-researched and closely follows the research file. The vast majority of factual claims are correct or acceptably worded. ## Frontmatter

### Pull quote

**Claim:** `"90% of grass seed cases happen between June and September - but a five-minute post-walk check can prevent most of them."`

**Issues:**

**F-01 | INCOMPLETE | Attribution and rounding**
The research file records the exact figure as **90.6%** (Brant et al. Preventive Veterinary Medicine 2021). Tom rounds to "90%" in the pull quote. Rounding to a whole number in a pull quote is acceptable editorial practice, but the downstream body-text attribution (see S2-01 below) must give the exact figure and the correct citation. The pull quote alone is acceptable if the body text is corrected.

**F-02 | FAIL | Em dash in pull quote**
The phrase "between June and September - but a five-minute post-walk check" uses ` - ` (space-hyphen-space) as an em dash substitute. The brief prohibits em dashes and em dash substitutes in all user-facing copy. This is the pull quote -- it is the most prominent user-facing string in the article. Fix required.

Fix: Replace with a full stop or a comma. Suggested: "90.6% of grass seed cases happen between June and September. A five-minute post-walk check can prevent most of them."

## Section 6 -- When to call the vet -- and when to go immediately

**Section heading: FAIL | Em dash**
"When to call the vet - and when to go immediately" uses ` - ` as em dash substitute.

---

**Claim S6-01:** The section heading promises "when to go immediately" but the first subsection is labelled "Go to a vet the same day if:" -- and covers ear, eye, and nose presentations.

**S6-01 | FAIL | SAFETY-CRITICAL: "Same day" vs "immediately"**

This is the most serious factual error in the article. The research is explicit: "Must go to vet immediately (all sources agree): Any seed in or near the ear canal; Any seed in or near the eye; Any seed that has entered via the nose."

Tom labels these cases as "Go to a vet the same day if:" -- which could reasonably be interpreted as "go today at some point" rather than "go right now." For a dog shaking its head violently, squinting acutely, or sneezing repeatedly with blood, the correct message is: go immediately, not same day. A "same day" framing may lead an owner to wait until evening surgery when the correct action is to go at once.

The section title promises to tell readers "when to go immediately" but never delivers a category using the word "immediately." The result is a safety guide that uses weaker language than all its sources.

Fix required: The ear, eye, and nose presentations should be labelled as requiring immediate vet attention. Suggested restructure:

"Go immediately (do not wait for a routine appointment) if:
- The dog is shaking its head violently or pawing at an ear
- There is sudden squinting or discharge from an eye
- Violent repeated sneezing has started, particularly with nasal discharge

These locations need sedation and specialist removal. Do not attempt to remove seeds from ears, eyes, or nose at home.

Go to a vet the same day if:
- There is visible swelling, heat, or discharge at any site on the body
- The dog is in obvious discomfort but not showing the acute signs above"
