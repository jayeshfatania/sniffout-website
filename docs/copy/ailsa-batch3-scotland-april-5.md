## Arthur's Seat and Holyrood Park

Arthur's Seat sits in the middle of Edinburgh, an ancient volcanic ridge that most visitors treat as a quick summit tick but rewards a slower circuit. The outer path around the base of the hill is a consistent favourite with dog walkers, keeping you clear of the main summit crowds while taking in the crags, the three lochs, and the long views south.

Leads are required near the three lochs (Dunsapie, St Margaret's, and Duddingston) and along the Salisbury Crags section between April and July, when peregrines are nesting on the cliff face. The path below the crags is gravel and can be loose underfoot on the descent.

The lochs are off-limits for swimming. Dogs do better here on a cooler day when the paths are quiet and the walk can be taken at their own pace rather than against weekend foot traffic.

The Sheep Heid Inn at Duddingston, a short detour from the east side of the park, is Scotland's oldest pub and welcomes dogs. Holyrood 9A is about 15 minutes from the north gate and also welcomes dogs.

## Threipmuir and Harlaw Reservoirs

Threipmuir and Harlaw sit in the Pentland Hills Regional Park south of Edinburgh, two reservoir loops joined by open hillside and quiet farm tracks. It is an unpretentious walk: wide paths, long sight lines, room to move. One of the most popular dog walks on the edge of the city for exactly those reasons.

The Scottish Outdoor Access Code applies, which means dogs can be off-lead under proper control for most of the circuit. Cattle graze the hillside sections at certain times of year. When they are present, keep your dog on a lead. The cattle here are large and can be assertive, and standing your ground calmly works better than moving quickly away.

The reservoirs are Scottish Water supply. Dogs cannot swim in them. Ticks are a consistent hazard across the Pentlands from spring through autumn. Check your dog carefully after every visit.

Bavelaw car park at the foot of the hills charges around two pounds. The Balerno Inn is the nearest dog-friendly option, about a mile from the car park.

## The Hermitage and Black Linn Falls

The Hermitage at Dunkeld is a National Trust for Scotland site built around the River Braan gorge, where the river drops through Black Linn Falls and is viewed from Ossian's Hall, a folly perched directly above the drop and restored to its original painted interior.

The giant Douglas firs on this walk are among the tallest trees in Britain, some standing at 50 to 60 metres. In strong winds, debris falls from them without warning. The car park and main path pass beneath them, which is worth bearing in mind before setting out on a stormy day.

Dogs need to be on leads along the gorge path and at the falls viewpoint. The drop is sheer and unguarded in places. Away from the gorge, off-lead is fine. Dogs can swim in the calmer sections of the Braan upstream from the falls. The water is cold and clear.

Red squirrels are present in the canopy. Keep dogs under close control in the woodland to reduce disturbance to them.

The Taybank Hotel in Dunkeld has a beer garden on the River Tay and welcomes dogs.

## Tentsmuir Forest and Beach

Tentsmuir is a stretch of Scots pine and ancient coastal dune on the Fife coast, backing onto one of Scotland's most significant seal colonies at Tentsmuir Point. The walk combines forest paths with a wide beach foreshore and can be done comfortably in a half day.

Common seals pup here in June and July. Grey seals arrive from October. NatureScot asks that dogs are kept on leads throughout the National Nature Reserve sections year-round to protect the colony. Signs mark the NNR boundaries. Getting too close to hauled seals, even unintentionally, can cause a stampede that injures pups. In the main forest interior, off-lead under proper control is generally fine.

The tide at Tentsmuir comes in quickly across wide sandflats. Do not walk far onto the tidal sand toward Tentsmuir Point. Check tide times before visiting. Leuchars military base is immediately adjacent and low-flying aircraft are a regular occurrence, worth noting if your dog is noise-sensitive.

There are no facilities at the car park. The Larick Cafe at Tayport, about four miles away, is dog-friendly. The Rhynd in Leuchars is the nearest dog-friendly pub.

## Balmaha and Loch Lomond Shore

The lochside walk from Balmaha to Milarrochy Bay on the eastern shore of Loch Lomond is one of the most straightforward introductions to walking dogs in Scotland. The path stays flat, the loch is immediately beside you for most of the route, and Milarrochy Bay is a consistently calm spot for a swim.

The Scottish Outdoor Access Code applies. Off-lead is reasonable on the lochside sections, with dogs kept closer through the village and near the car park where paths are busiest.

Midges are significant here from May through September in still, humid conditions. They affect dogs and owners equally. In summer the loch carries considerable boat traffic, so keep dogs clear of the main channel when swimming.

Balmaha is a major West Highland Way waypoint and the 177-space car park fills before mid-morning on sunny summer weekends. An early start or a weekday visit makes the whole thing considerably more enjoyable.

The Oak Tree Inn is directly at the start of the walk, dogs welcome in the outdoor areas. It is a Scotland's Top Pub award winner and a reliable post-walk option at any time of day.

## Kinnoull Hill and Tay Valley Views

Kinnoull Hill rises above Perth on the east bank of the Tay, a ridge of beech and oak woodland designated Scotland's first Woodland Park in 1991. The walk to Kinnoull Tower takes under an hour from the Jubilee car park, and the view from the top over the broad arc of the River Tay, with the Ochil Hills and Strathearn visible on a clear day, is consistently regarded as one of the finest accessible viewpoints in the country for the effort involved.

The cliff on the south face drops approximately 150 metres, partly screened by mature woodland. It is easy to be closer to the edge than you realise. Dogs must be on leads for the entire section near and around Kinnoull Tower. Away from the cliff, the woodland is excellent off-lead territory.

Red squirrels and roe deer are both present. Autumn is an outstanding season here, with beech and oak turning gold through the canopy. Paths can be boggy on lower sections after prolonged rain, and icy near the cliff in cold spells.

Both car parks are free. No facilities on site. Willowgate Cafe in Perth has riverside outdoor seating on the Tay, about 1.5 miles away, and welcomes dogs outside.

## Elterwater and Langdale Valley

**Note: This walk is in the Lake District, Cumbria, England. CRoW Act applies, not SOAC.**

Elterwater village sits in the Great Langdale valley in the Lake District, a tarn and riverside walk that follows Great Langdale Beck through the lower valley before returning via Baysbrown Wood and Skelwith Bridge. It is an easy route with the Langdale Pikes as a backdrop throughout, well suited to a dog that does not need serious elevation.

There are sheep throughout the valley including on Elterwater's village common, and the CRoW Act requires dogs on a short lead near livestock. Between 1 March and 31 July on open access land, a short lead is required during bird nesting season as well. In practice this walk is largely on-lead.

Great Langdale Beck has accessible swimming spots in the lower valley where it broadens and slows. Keep dogs on leads near Skelwith Force by Skelwith Bridge, where the falls drop into a fast gorge below. Several stiles on the route may require larger dogs to be lifted.

The valley car parks fill before 9am on summer weekends. The Britannia Inn in Elterwater village is one of the most reliably dog-friendly pubs in the Lake District, dogs welcome in the bar.
