## New Forest

Burley Common puts you in the middle of ancient woodland, open heathland, and gravel tracks that stretch further than most dogs can manage in a day. The New Forest is genuinely good for dogs, but the rules here are not optional. Ponies, cattle, donkeys, and pigs roam freely throughout the park. Dogs must be on a lead near all livestock at all times.

## Yorkshire and the Peak District

Stanage Edge and Malham Cove are two of the most distinctive dog walking destinations in the north of England, and neither is a gentle stroll. Gritstone moorland, exposed ridgelines, and steep limestone gorges make this serious walking country. Weather changes fast on the tops. Sheep graze on most of the open land, so lead awareness matters on farmland and during lambing.

## Sussex

Seven Sisters is one of the most recognisable stretches of walking on the South Downs - white chalk cliffs, rolling downland, and long views out to sea. Dogs need to be kept on a lead or under very close control near the cliff edges; the drops are serious. The South Downs Way runs through the area, opening up longer routes away from the cliff line.

## Lake District

Grasmere is one of the gentler ways into the Lake District, with lakeside paths and valley meadows rather than fell scrambles. The fells elsewhere in the park are worth knowing about for the future, but they ask more of a dog than many are ready for. Sheep are everywhere up here. Keep the lead close, and watch the forecast - weather comes in fast.

## Dartmoor

Haytor and Hound Tor give you Dartmoor at its most open - granite ridgelines, tussock grass, and views that stretch as far as the weather allows. Dogs can run free on much of the moor, but Dartmoor ponies, cattle, and sheep graze freely across the same ground, so the lead is always close by. Adders are active on the moorland from spring. The terrain is rough underfoot and fog rolls in fast.
